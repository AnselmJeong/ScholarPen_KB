## V. Lived Experiences: Patients and Staff

The ethical and philosophical debates examined in the preceding sections acquire a different dimension when considered through the lens of those directly affected by coercive practices. Patients who undergo chemical restraint and the staff who administer it share a common burden of distress that abstract policy arguments often overlook.

### 5.1 Patient Perspectives and Re-traumatization

Qualitative research consistently documents that patients experience chemical restraint as a profound violation. Butterworth et al. [93], in a systematic review of 21 qualitative studies across 11 countries, found that patients' experiences of restrictive practices were overwhelmingly negative, with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) rates of 25-47% among those who had been subjected to restraint. Patients described feeling punished rather than helped, stripped of agency, and treated as objects rather than persons.

For patients with trauma histories—a substantial proportion of psychiatric inpatients—the experience of chemical restraint can constitute re-traumatization. Hennessy et al. [94] synthesized qualitative evidence demonstrating that patients with prior trauma are re-traumatized by coercive practices in inpatient settings, yet little systematic effort is made to identify these patients or modify approaches accordingly. The involuntary administration of medication, the physical holding required for injection, and the subsequent loss of consciousness can mirror the dynamics of earlier abuse: loss of control, physical intrusion, and the inability to make oneself heard.

Guzman-Parra et al. [95] examined perceived coercion, PTSD symptoms, and treatment satisfaction across different coercive measures, finding that different forms of coercion produce distinct psychological impacts—a finding relevant to the proportionality debate. If some coercive measures are associated with less psychological harm than others, this evidence supports efforts to identify and implement the least harmful interventions rather than treating all coercive practices as equivalent.

Perspectives from non-Western settings add important nuance. Asayesh et al. [96], studying patient experiences in Iranian psychiatric settings, found that patients identified preventable factors contributing to coercion and perceived chemical restraint as preferable to mechanical restraint—suggesting that the hierarchy of coercive measures may vary by cultural context. Paksarian et al. [97] similarly documented cross-cultural variation in how patients interpret and respond to coercive treatment.

Steinert et al. [98], surveying international trends in seclusion and restraint, documented wide variation in coercion rates across countries—variation that cannot be explained by differences in patient populations alone, pointing instead to institutional and cultural factors that shape how and how often chemical restraint is used.

### 5.2 Staff Perspectives and Moral Injury

Healthcare workers who administer chemical restraint are not immune to its psychological effects. Lamoureux et al. [111], in a systematic review of moral distress among acute mental health nurses, found that moral distress is prevalent and is specifically associated with the use of coercive measures. Involuntary admissions and restraints were identified as key triggers. Nurses reported feeling compelled by duty to act against their own moral judgment, a state that accumulated over time into burnout and Compassion fatigue.

The concept of moral injury—originally developed in military contexts—has recently been applied to psychiatric nursing. Ohnishi et al. [112] studied psychiatric nurses in Japan who had resigned due to moral distress, finding that nurses described feeling complicit in harmful practices, including what they perceived as unjustified restraints. A parallel study from Switzerland [113] documented the spectrum and impact of moral injury among mental health nurses, providing European comparative data on how staff experience being both agents of and witnesses to coercive treatment.

Knibbs et al. [114], using a phenomenological approach, explored mental health nurses' lived experiences with chemical restraint during behavioral emergencies, capturing the complex ethical decision-making involved, feelings of moral compromise, and the tension between professional duty and respect for patient autonomy. Staff in Butterworth et al.'s [93] review similarly reported guilt, self-doubt, and a sense of having failed at their job—documenting the moral distress dimensions that accompany coercive practice.

These findings carry implications that the human rights critique often neglects. When staff are criticized as rights violators, the effect compounds their existing moral distress. The result is a workforce that feels attacked from all sides: condemned by human rights advocates for using restraint, yet held responsible by institutions and the public for failing to prevent harm when restraint is not used.

### 5.3 The Relational Impact of Coercion

Beyond individual effects, chemical restraint damages the therapeutic relationship—a form of harm that neither the proportionality nor the absolutist framework adequately captures. Tingleff et al. [35] found that patients who experienced coercion reported lasting damage to trust in healthcare providers, reduced willingness to seek future treatment, and diminished engagement with therapeutic plans. Muir-Cochrane et al. [36] documented a feedback loop in which staff fear and blame contributed to restrictive practices, which in turn increased patient fear and resistance, further escalating the cycle.

Pelto-Piri et al. [90] demonstrated that informal coercion—subtle pressures such as offering rewards for behavioral compliance or persuading patients to take oral medication to avoid injections—pervades psychiatric practice. This observation is significant because it challenges the assumption that eliminating formal coercive measures would eliminate coercion itself. If informal coercion persists even when chemical restraint is removed, the ethical calculus shifts: the question becomes not whether coercion exists, but which forms of coercion cause the least harm while producing the most benefit.

## VI. Non-Pharmacological Alternatives and De-escalation

### 6.1 Verbal De-escalation and Environmental Modification

The principle of least restrictive intervention demands that non-pharmacological approaches be attempted before chemical restraint whenever clinically feasible. Richmond et al. [99], in a landmark consensus statement from the American Association for Emergency Psychiatry Project BETA, established verbal de-escalation as the first-line response to agitation. The consensus defined 10 domains of de-escalation, including respecting personal space, avoiding coercive language, offering choices, and listening actively. These techniques aim to reduce agitation through therapeutic engagement rather than pharmacological suppression.

Evidence supports the efficacy of structured de-escalation training. Ye et al. [101] conducted a cluster randomized controlled trial demonstrating that CRSCE-based de-escalation training significantly reduced physical restraint use in psychiatric hospitals. Lavelle et al. [102] identified predictors of effective de-escalation, including staff communication style, patient characteristics, and environmental factors—findings that inform implementation by specifying what makes de-escalation succeed or fail.

Knox and Holloman [104], in a companion consensus to the BETA de-escalation guidelines, addressed strategies for avoiding seclusion and restraint through environmental modification, sensory approaches, and restructuring staff responses to agitation. The "avoid when possible" standard established in these consensus documents provides the evidence base supporting a non-pharmacological-first approach to acute agitation.

### 6.2 Sensory Approaches and Alternative Interventions

Sensory rooms—dedicated spaces equipped with soothing stimuli such as weighted blankets, ambient lighting, calming music, and tactile objects—have been implemented as alternatives to restraint in several countries. Tunde-Ayinmode et al. [103], in a systematic review of sensory rooms in adult psychiatric inpatient settings, found that both staff and patients perceived sensory rooms as valuable alternatives, though evidence regarding their effect on seclusion and restraint rates was mixed. The variability in outcomes likely reflects differences in implementation fidelity, staffing levels, and patient populations.

Baker et al. [100], in the COMPARE systematic mapping review of non-pharmacological interventions to reduce restrictive practices, classified intervention components using behavior change technique taxonomy and identified which techniques showed the most promise. The review found that multi-component interventions—combining environmental modification, staff training, leadership engagement, and patient involvement—were more effective than single-component approaches.

These findings align with the broader principle that reducing reliance on chemical restraint requires systemic change rather than simple substitution of one intervention for another. Environmental design, staffing ratios, organizational culture, and patient engagement all influence whether de-escalation succeeds or whether escalation to chemical restraint becomes unavoidable.

### 6.3 Psychiatric Advance Directives as a Middle Ground

Psychiatric advance directives (PADs)—also known as Ulysses contracts—represent a potentially promising approach to reconciling autonomy with medical necessity. A PAD is a legal document completed by a person with decision-making capacity that specifies treatment preferences for future mental health crises, including consent to or refusal of specific interventions such as chemical restraint [115].

Swanson et al. [117], in a foundational empirical study, demonstrated that PADs effectively reduce coercive crisis interventions. Less coercion led to greater autonomy and self-determination, suggesting that prior consent—expressed when the person is competent—can preserve autonomy even during periods of incapacity. Swanson et al. [119] subsequently conducted a randomized controlled trial published in JAMA Psychiatry showing that peer-facilitated PADs reduced coercion and improved clinical outcomes—the strongest experimental evidence to date for PADs as a practical alternative to involuntary treatment.

Brenna et al. [115], in a comprehensive integrative review, cataloged the literature on Ulysses contracts, analyzing shared features, consent requirements, and utilization outcomes. The review identified consistent findings: PADs that include self-binding clauses (the "Ulysses clause," whereby a person instructs providers to override their future refusal of treatment) appear to resolve the philosophical tension between autonomy and medical necessity, because the person's competent self binds their incompetent self—a qualitatively different moral act from external coercion.

Vergallo and Gulino [118] argued that the CRPD's framework fails to consider advance directives and proposed specific legal criteria for PADs that could reconcile CRPD autonomy requirements with clinical necessity. Gloeckler et al. [116], in an international comparison of PAD policy across eleven jurisdictions, found wide variation in legal recognition and binding force—some jurisdictions treat PADs as fully binding, others as advisory, and many lack any legal framework. This variability limits the generalizability of PAD-based approaches and highlights the need for legislative reform.

Summary of Theme 6: Non-pharmacological alternatives—de-escalation, environmental modification, sensory approaches—can reduce reliance on chemical restraint but have not eliminated it. Multi-component interventions show the most promise. PADs offer a theoretical resolution to the autonomy-medical necessity conflict, but their practical impact depends on legal recognition, implementation infrastructure, and patient willingness to engage in advance planning.

## VII. Pharmacological Developments in Managing Acute Agitation and Violence

While human rights advocates seek to prohibit all forms of coercive treatment, the core goal of protecting patients' rights and autonomy remains valid. Even if moral absolutists continue to reject pharmacological management on principle, the development of less invasive and less coercive pharmacological treatments represents meaningful progress in maintaining ethical psychiatric care [37,38].

### 7.1 Alternative Routes of Administration

Expecting an agitated patient to willingly take oral medication is often unrealistic. Conversely, coercing parenteral injections raises human rights concerns. These circumstances have driven exploration of alternative routes of administration, including inhalers, nasal delivery, orally disintegrating tablets, syrups, and sublingual formulations. Formulations that bypass first-pass metabolism—inhaled, intranasal, and sublingual—offer faster absorption, higher bioavailability, and reduced invasiveness compared to injections, potentially reducing patient resistance and risk of accidents [73].

**Inhaled Loxapine (ADASUVE).** Loxapine, a typical antipsychotic, is available as an inhalable formulation. When the patient places the device in their mouth and inhales, a built-in sensor detects airflow, rapidly heating and vaporizing the coated loxapine. The inhaled vapor condenses into particles approximately 2μm in diameter and deposits in the alveoli, with delivery beginning within one second of inhalation [74]. Efficacy has been demonstrated in three large-scale randomized clinical trials. In patients with schizophrenia, 5-10mg administration produced significant differences in agitation compared to placebo. In bipolar disorder, significant efficacy appeared as early as 10 minutes [75]. ADASUVE was approved in the United States and Europe in 2012. However, it can cause bronchospasms leading to severe respiratory distress, and in the United States is provided only through the REMS (Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies) program [74]. It has not been widely adopted due to side effects and cost, and was withdrawn from the Korean market due to poor sales performance.

**Intranasal Olanzapine.** Two intranasal olanzapine formulations (INP105 and NRL-4) are under development [38]. INP105 uses Precision Olfactory Delivery (POD) technology, which releases the drug by expelling stored gas—allowing administration even if the patient is uncooperative [76]. NRL-4 combines olanzapine with dodecyl maltoside (DDM), a permeation enhancer previously used in sumatriptan and diazepam nasal sprays. NRL-4 is administered through a simple disposable device requiring neither patient inhalation nor complex manipulation [77].

**Sublingual Asenapine.** Asenapine, acting on 5-HT2A/D2 receptors, received FDA approval in 2009 for schizophrenia and bipolar disorder. Although its oral bioavailability remains below 2%, sublingual administration achieves approximately 35% bioavailability. While not specifically indicated for acute agitation, Pratts et al. [79] demonstrated significant differences from placebo beginning 15 minutes after sublingual administration, with minimal adverse reactions during the 2-hour observation period.

**Dexmedetomidine Sublingual Film (IGALMI).** Dexmedetomidine, a highly selective α2-adrenergic receptor agonist, received new FDA approval in 2022 as a sublingual film specifically targeting acute agitation in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder [80,81]. Clinical trials demonstrated significant agitation reduction with effects appearing as early as 20 minutes after a single dose of 180μg or 120μg. Because dexmedetomidine does not act on dopamine receptors, it does not cause extrapyramidal side effects. As a sublingual film, it is expected to increase patient acceptance and reduce the sense of shame patients may experience with injections [82]. However, questions remain about whether its α2-adrenergic mechanism addresses underlying psychotic symptoms and whether its higher price is justified by improved efficacy and safety.

An umbrella review of pharmacological management of acute agitation [121] confirms that inhaled loxapine 10mg is superior to 5mg, and provides updated evidence synthesis including dexmedetomidine. Recent developments include BioXcel Therapeutics' SERENITY At-Home Phase 3 trial, expanding the dexmedetomidine sublingual film indication to community-based agitation management—a shift from inpatient-only to outpatient settings that could reduce the need for emergency department presentations and involuntary treatment.

### 7.2 Remote Passive Monitoring

Intervention typically begins after an incident has occurred. Since agitation tends to escalate from minor triggers, early intervention is essential. Assessment tools such as the Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC) and Dynamic Appraisal of Situational Aggression (DAMA) have been used, but feasibility and reliability concerns persist [83].

Passive remote monitoring technologies overcome some of these limitations [84]. Wearable sensors monitor physiological indicators—electrodermal activity, heart rate variability—in real-time without significant patient burden. Machine learning analysis of these data can help predict impending aggressive behavior [85,86]. Motion sensors and audio recorders can detect aggressive verbal expressions or behaviors in advance [88]. While still experimental, these techniques are being applied in forensic psychiatric settings [87]. However, data security, patient consent for continuous monitoring, and the reliability of automated analysis systems remain unresolved challenges [89].

## VIII. Cross-National Legal Frameworks

### 8.1 International Variation in Coercive Legislation

Legal frameworks governing coercive psychiatric treatment vary substantially across nations, reflecting different balances between rights protection and clinical discretion. Savage et al. [106], in a comparative analysis of coercive practices across nine countries (including Japan, Australia, England, Germany, Ireland, and New Zealand), identified significant variability in both rates and duration of coercion. Japan's comparatively high rates of physical restraint contrast with European countries' greater reliance on chemical interventions—a pattern shaped by legal, cultural, and institutional factors.

Fistein et al. [109], analyzing 32 Commonwealth Mental Health Acts, found widespread deviation from human rights standards, with some jurisdictions using "need for treatment" criteria while others relied solely on "dangerousness" thresholds. Lepping et al. [110] compared restraint data from four European countries, revealing large variations in the type of coercive measure used despite similar overall incidence—suggesting that local institutional culture and legal frameworks, rather than clinical need, often determine which form of coercion is applied.

The FOSTREN network (COST Action CA19101), a major European collaborative initiative from 2020-2024, developed standards and guidelines for minimizing coercion across European mental health services [105,122]. FOSTREN's survey of involuntary mental health legislation across 30 European countries found that no government has fully implemented the UNCRPD's recommendation to eliminate coercion—a finding that underscores the gap between aspirational rights frameworks and operational clinical realities [105].

### 8.2 Korean Mental Health Law and Reform

South Korea's 2016 revision of the Mental Health and Welfare Act restricted involuntary admission criteria, reflecting the global trend toward rights-based mental health legislation. Park et al. [108], using interrupted time series analysis, examined the effects of this legal reform on healthcare utilization patterns, finding significant changes in admission and discharge patterns following the legislation. The Korean context is particularly relevant because the Tuberculosis Prevention Act—which mandates forced isolation treatment for patients who may transmit infection—has remained largely unchallenged despite bearing striking resemblance to involuntary psychiatric admission [46]. This double standard may stem from the visibility and microbial etiology of tuberculosis compared to the ambiguity that still surrounds mental illness.

### 8.3 Reform Trends and Persistent Gaps

Despite decades of legislative reform, coercion rates have not consistently declined. Flammer et al. [123] documented a 24.6% increase in coercive measures in psychiatric hospitals, with increasing cumulative duration per patient. More recently, an ecological data study from six European countries (Austria, England, Germany, Norway, Portugal, and Switzerland) found that coercion rates have generally increased despite reform efforts—for example, a 37.79% increase in England between 2020 and 2023 [124]. These findings challenge the narrative that legislative reform alone can reduce coercion and suggest that systemic factors—staffing levels, institutional culture, available alternatives—may be more determinative than legal frameworks.

The TCI Asia Pacific Bali Declaration [125] documented that new mental health laws across 21 Asia-Pacific countries retain core provisions for involuntary admission and treatment, resulting in some of the highest rates of psychiatric institutionalization globally. Dey et al. [107], comparing legislation across four South Asian countries, identified barriers including inadequate legal safeguards, insufficient human rights protections, and resource deficiencies.

Summary of Theme 8: Cross-national comparison reveals that no jurisdiction has resolved the tension between medical necessity and rights protection. Legal reform alone has not reduced coercion rates; in several countries, rates have increased despite legislative changes aimed at restriction. The gap between UNCRPD aspirations and clinical reality remains wide across all regions.

## IX. Cross-Cutting Synthesis

### 9.1 Convergent Findings

Several findings emerge consistently across the themes examined in this review. First, the historical legacy of psychiatric coercion continues to shape contemporary perceptions of chemical restraint, regardless of how modern practice has evolved [3,4]. Second, the Geneva Impasse between proportionality and absolutist interpretations of human rights remains unresolved at the international level, with no jurisdiction having fully implemented the CRPD's call for elimination of coercive treatment [28,105]. Third, both patients and staff experience significant psychological harm from coercive practices—re-traumatization and moral injury, respectively—suggesting that the current system serves neither group well [93,111]. Fourth, non-pharmacological alternatives and pharmacological innovations can reduce the invasiveness and frequency of chemical restraint but have not eliminated the need for it in acute emergencies [99,121].

### 9.2 Divergent Findings and Debates

The most significant divergence lies between the absolutist position—that all coercive treatment is inherently a human rights violation and must be prohibited [23,24]—and the proportionality position—that coercion may be justified when necessary, proportionate, and minimally intrusive [6,28]. This divergence extends to practical questions: whether PADs can genuinely reconcile autonomy with medical necessity [115,119]; whether de-escalation and environmental modification can sufficiently reduce coercion to make elimination feasible [100,101]; and whether legal reform translates into reduced coercion in practice [123,124].

A secondary divergence concerns the interpretation of patient experience data. Absolutists point to consistently negative patient experiences as evidence that chemical restraint is inherently harmful and should be prohibited [93,94]. Proportionality advocates argue that different coercive measures produce different levels of harm, supporting efforts to identify the least harmful interventions rather than abolishing all of them [95,98]. Both positions draw selectively on the same evidence base.

### 9.3 Methodological Observations

The literature is dominated by qualitative studies (patient and staff experience), legal analyses, and expert consensus documents. Randomized controlled trials are scarce, particularly for non-pharmacological interventions. Cross-national comparisons are limited by differences in how coercion is defined, measured, and recorded. Many studies originate from Western European and North American settings; data from low- and middle-income countries, including much of Asia and Africa, remain sparse. The absence of standardized outcome measures for coercion-related harm limits the comparability of findings across studies and settings.

## X. Conclusion

Can patients who struggle to control their behavior possess the competency to decide on effective treatment methods? While scholars advocate for prior discussion about all available options, doubts arise as to how many patients would willingly opt for coercive interventions in advance. Certain scholars argue that all forms of pharmacological treatment inherently contain elements of coercion and human rights violations, advocating instead for humanistic and psychosocial approaches. Apart from their ineffectiveness in emergencies, this perspective overlooks that psychological interventions can also contain coercive elements. Offering rewards for behavior modification or persuading patients to take oral medication to avoid injections constitutes informal coercion [90]. If we were to eliminate all forms of coercive treatment, the therapist's role would be reduced to providing unconditional validation and emotional support, potentially compromising effective treatment.

Patients with severe mental illness retain their fundamental human rights and autonomy, which must be protected. The importance of patient engagement for successful long-term treatment outcomes cannot be overstated. However, when a patient poses an immediate risk to themselves or others, the question of how far clinical intervention should be limited by human rights concerns has no simple answer.

Mental health professionals have embraced human rights principles by eliminating closed wards, minimizing involuntary admissions, and significantly reducing hospitalization periods. They have strived to listen to patients' voices and incorporate them into treatment plans, focusing on long-term compliance and recovery. Being barred from intervening in emergencies for the sake of protecting patients' human rights is very difficult to accept.

Moral absolutists fear that allowing exceptions to universal ethical principles will lead down a slippery slope to routine human rights violations. Yet if they continue to deprecate pharmacological treatments in this way, we risk going down a different slippery slope where all scientific advancements are dismissed as inhumane remnants of imperialism. The current effort to eliminate the term "chemical restraints" from medical discourse is merely euphemism. Using "pharmacological management of acute agitation and violence" instead of "chemical restraint" may seem to sidestep the core ethical dilemma—it does not.

Finding the right balance requires continued dialogue that acknowledges the validity of both clinical realities and rights-based concerns, rather than absolutist positions from either side. Psychiatric advance directives, alternative delivery methods, de-escalation training, and systemic institutional reform all contribute to reducing the frequency and severity of chemical restraint. None of these alone resolves the fundamental tension. The debate has real, immediate consequences for everyday psychiatric practice, and those consequences demand engagement rather than impasse.

### Limitations

This review has several limitations. First, as a narrative thematic review rather than a systematic review with formal quality assessment, it may be subject to selection bias in source identification. Second, the literature is predominantly from Western settings; findings may not generalize to low- and middle-income countries. Third, the absence of standardized outcome measures for coercion-related harm limits cross-study comparability. Fourth, some of the new pharmacological agents discussed are still under investigation, and their long-term safety and real-world effectiveness remain uncertain.
