# least restrictive alternative

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical and legal standards for involuntary psychiatric interventions  
- Guides clinical decision-making in emergency and coercive treatment contexts  
- Directly impacts patient autonomy and human rights in mental health care  
- Balances clinical necessity with legal and ethical constraints on liberty restrictions  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Ethical priority on patient preferences | Individual service users' preferences should determine the least restrictive restraint type, overriding rigid hierarchies | [[2024 - Hempeler et al. - Preferences of Individual Mental Health Service Users Are Essential in Determining the Least Restrictive Type of Restraint]] |
| Legal justification framework | Involuntary hospitalization justified by police power (danger to self/others), not parens patriae, and must comply with least restrictive principles | [[Clinical Handbook of Psychiatry and Law - 02]] |
| Limited right to treatment | Right to treatment ensures only minimally adequate care, not optimal or chosen treatment, with limited real-world impact due to judicial and systemic factors | [[Clinical Handbook of Psychiatry and Law - 03]] |
| Human rights challenge to coercion | UN CRPD asserts persons with psychosocial disabilities retain legal capacity and must consent to all interventions, rejecting capacity-based justifications | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 09]] |
| Impossibility of universal standards | No objective, universally applicable best practice standards for coercive treatment due to limitations of ethics, evidence, and consensus approaches | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 06]] |
| Evolving but contested justification | Coercion justification shifted from social control to clinical-ethical frameworks but remains ethically and legally contested due to autonomy vs. beneficence conflicts | [[Human Rights in Psychiatry - 06]] |
