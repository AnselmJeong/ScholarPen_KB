# ethical conflict

## Why It Matters
- Highlights core tensions in psychiatric practice between utilitarian legal frameworks and rights-based approaches
- Reveals persistent ethical dilemmas in the use of coercion despite professional acknowledgment of its violation of rights
- Demonstrates how ethical conflicts shape clinical decision-making, policy implementation, and patient experiences
- Underscores the uniqueness of psychiatry’s ethical landscape compared to other medical specialties

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Psychiatry is ethically torn | Operates under competing legal frameworks: utilitarian Mental Health Act vs. rights-based Human Rights and Mental Capacity Acts | [[2008 - Lepping - Is psychiatry torn in different ethical directions]] |
| Coercion acknowledged as rights violation but deemed necessary | Despite recognizing coercion as a violation of rights, many professionals view it as indispensable and beneficial in practice | [[2021 - Morandi et al. - Mental health professionals feelings and attitudes towards coercion]] |
| Attitudes toward coercion are multidimensional | Professional attitudes structured around internal pressure, emotional impact, external pressure, and relational involvement | [[2021 - Morandi et al. - Mental health professionals feelings and attitudes towards coercion]] |
