# emergency exception

## Why It Matters
- Addresses legal and ethical limits on overriding patient autonomy during crises  
- Highlights tension between beneficence and informed consent in psychiatric emergencies  
- Reveals gaps in standardization across jurisdictions for emergency interventions  
- Connects to broader debates on coercion, capacity, and human rights in mental health care  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Emergency exceptions override standard consent | Incapacitated patients may receive treatment without consent in life-threatening situations to prevent serious harm | [[Clinical Manual of Psychiatry and Law - 04]] |
| Emergency exceptions are narrowly defined | Must involve imminent danger, inability to consent, and no reasonable alternatives; not a blanket waiver of rights | [[Clinical Manual of Psychiatry and Law - 04]] |
| State laws govern emergency exceptions | Legal standards vary significantly by jurisdiction, affecting when and how emergency treatment can be administered | [[Clinical Manual of Psychiatry and Law - 04]] |
