# decision-making capacity
## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical decisions about coercion and restraint in psychiatry  
- Determines capacity to consent or refuse treatment  
- Guides application of least restrictive alternatives  
- Shapes legal and clinical standards for substituted decision-making  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Context-dependent ethical principles | Ethical principles vary across cultural, religious, economic, and political settings | [[2020 - Atakora, Asampong - Ethical Principles in Psychiatric Practice; A Systematic Review]] |
| Coercion permissible only in exceptional circumstances | Ethically allowed only when serving higher values like long-term autonomy after transparent evaluation | [[2021 - Chieze et al. - Coercive Measures in Psychiatry A Review of Ethical Arguments]] |
| Structured decision algorithm required | Ethical decisions on coercion should follow algorithm based on capacity, benefit-harm, and biopsychosocial context | [[2023 - Steinert - Using coercion in mental disorders or risking the patients death An analysis of the protocols of a clinical ethics committee]] |
| Patient preferences determine least restrictive restraint | Must prioritize individual service users' preferences over rigid hierarchies of restrictiveness | [[2024 - Hempeler et al. - Preferences of Individual Mental Health Service Users Are Essential in Determining the Least Restrictive Type of Restraint]] |
| Coercion requires strict conditions | Justification depends on necessity, proportionality, procedural justice; some argue never justifiable under human rights | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]] |
