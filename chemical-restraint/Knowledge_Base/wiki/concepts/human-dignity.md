# human dignity

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical debates on coercion and autonomy in psychiatric care  
- Frequently invoked but contextually interpreted in conflicting ways across clinical and legal settings  
- Linked to international human rights frameworks such as the CRPD  
- Highlights tensions between individual rights and clinical safety in involuntary treatment  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Insufficiency of dignity alone | Appeals to autonomy and human dignity are insufficient to justify eliminating coercive measures, as they can support or oppose seclusion depending on interpretation | [[2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry]] |
| Dignity as achieved through support | Autonomy under CRPD Article 12 is best understood as an achievement requiring internal competencies and external support, integrating civil-political and socio-economic rights | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -04]] |
| Dignity violations in coercive practices | Coercive practices vary globally, banning one method often increases others, and causes measurable patient distress and dignity violations | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 07]] |
