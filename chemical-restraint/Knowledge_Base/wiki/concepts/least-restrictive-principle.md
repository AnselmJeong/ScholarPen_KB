# least restrictive principle
## Why It Matters
- Addresses ethical tension between safety and autonomy in psychiatric care
- Guides clinical decision-making to minimize harm and preserve dignity
- Central to human rights frameworks and legal standards in healthcare
- Informs protocols for restraint reduction and de-escalation strategies
## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| First-line de-escalation | Non-coercive interventions should precede any restrictive measure | [[2020 - Guerrero et al. - Physical and Chemical Restraints (an Update)]] |
| Last resort justification | Coercive measures may be necessary only when safety is imminently threatened | [[2022 - O'Donovan et al. - Current trends in restrictive interventions in psychiatry; a European perspective]] |
| No universally least restrictive option | Banning one restrictive method may increase use of others, causing harm | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 07]] |
| Context-dependent justification | Mechanical restraint can be ethically justified in high-secure settings under strict governance | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 22]] |
