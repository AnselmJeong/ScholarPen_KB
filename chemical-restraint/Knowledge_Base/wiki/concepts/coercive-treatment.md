# coercive treatment

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical debates on autonomy and liberty in psychiatric care  
- Highlights tensions between clinical necessity and human rights protections  
- Underscores systemic issues like epistemic injustice and inconsistent enforcement  
- Reflects evolving legal and moral frameworks around consent and coercion  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Human rights absolutist | CRPD Article 14 prohibits all liberty deprivation, making coercive care unlawful under any circumstances | [[2019 - Martin, Gurbai - Surveying the Geneva impasse; Coercive care and human rights]] |
| Critical bioethics focus | Altering consciousness via drugs constitutes a greater liberty violation than unconsciousness, challenging ethical hierarchies of restraint | [[2024 - Birks - Chemical Restraints and the Basic Liberties]] |
| Patient experience priority | Ethical analysis must center lived experience to counter drug-induced consent undermining and epistemic injustice | [[2024 - Dougherty et al. - The Lived Realities of Chemical Restraint; Prioritizing Patient Experience]] |
| Systemic failure view | Despite progress, coercive practices persist in Europe due to legal fragmentation, weak enforcement, and reinstitutionalization | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 11]] |
| Historical persistence claim | Coercive practices endured for decades in Germany via biological reductionism, even with humane alternatives available | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 18]] |
| Historical context lens | Emergence of rapid tranquilization tied to antipsychotic development, reflecting broader shifts in psychiatric practice | [[2014 - Allison, Moncrieff - ‘Rapid tranquillisation’; an historical perspective on its emergence in the context of the development of antipsychotic medications]] |

## Open questions
- How can clinical urgency in psychiatric emergencies be balanced with absolute human rights prohibitions on liberty deprivation?  
- What mechanisms ensure that patient lived experience genuinely shapes ethical guidelines on chemical restraint, beyond token inclusion?  
- Why do humane, non-coercive alternatives like Soteria fail to scale despite evidence of efficacy and ethical superiority?  
- To what extent do current legal frameworks enable "reinstitutionalization" through opaque or inconsistently applied coercive practices?
