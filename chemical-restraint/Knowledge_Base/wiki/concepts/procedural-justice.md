# procedural justice

## Why It Matters
- Central to evaluating the legitimacy of coercive practices in mental healthcare  
- Addresses fairness in decision-making processes, independent of outcomes  
- Highlights stakeholder conflicts over what constitutes just procedures  
- Serves as a ethical and legal safeguard against arbitrary use of power  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Procedural justice as a condition for justifying coercion | Coercion may be permissible only if fair procedures (e.g., necessity, proportionality, voice) are followed, even under harm principle or soft paternalism | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]] |
| Stakeholder divergence on procedural fairness | Service users view coercion as rights violation; staff and caregivers see it as necessary despite acknowledging harm to dignity and relationships | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 04]] |
| Limits of procedural justice | Some argue coercion can never be justified under human rights frameworks, regardless of procedural safeguards | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]] |

## Open questions
- How can procedural justice be meaningfully measured or assessed in high-pressure psychiatric emergency contexts?  
- What weight should be given to service user experiences of procedural fairness versus institutional assessments of necessity?  
- Are there viable alternatives to coercion that satisfy both safety concerns and procedural justice ideals?  
- To what extent do current legal and institutional procedures actually uphold procedural justice, or merely ritualize compliance?
