# basic liberty

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical debates on restraint in psychiatric and medical contexts  
- Challenges assumptions about physical vs. chemical restraint intrusiveness  
- Highlights the role of subjective experience and conscious state alteration  
- Underpins arguments for patient-centered, preference-sensitive care  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Physical restraint is less intrusive than consciousness-altering chemical restraint | Chemical restraints affecting consciousness may violate basic liberty more than physical restraint by disrupting broader mental states | [[2024 - Marco et al. - On the Relative Intrusiveness of Physical and Chemical Restraints]] |
| Conscious state alteration is a greater liberty infringement than unconsciousness | Altering desires or awareness constitutes a deeper violation of basic liberty than rendering someone unconscious | [[2024 - Birks - Chemical Restraints and the Basic Liberties]] |
| Patient preferences should determine least restrictive restraint | Ethical restraint decisions must prioritize individual service users’ choices over standardized hierarchies | [[2024 - Hempeler et al. - Preferences of Individual Mental Health Service Users Are Essential in Determining the Least Restrictive Type of Restraint]] |
| Ethical chemical restraints must minimally restrict liberty | Interventions should limit liberty only as necessary, be medically appropriate, involve consent (or emergency exception), and preserve rational capacity post-intervention when consciousness is unaffected | [[2022 - Crutchfield, Redinger - The Conditions for Ethical Chemical Restraints]] |

## Open questions
- How should basic liberty be weighed against therapeutic benefit in emergency contexts?  
- Can liberty be meaningfully preserved when interventions alter a person’s core values or desires?  
- Are current hierarchies of restrictiveness ethically valid if they ignore subjective experience?  
- How do cultural and relational factors influence perceptions of liberty in psychiatric care?
