# rapid tranquilization

## Why It Matters
- Central term in debates about chemical restraint and psychiatric emergency interventions  
- Reflects clinical practice patterns for managing acute agitation  
- Highlights tensions between efficacy, safety, and ethical use of pharmacological interventions  
- Frequently conflated with related terms, complicating research and policy  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Clinical preference for antipsychotics in acute agitation | Haloperidol alone or with anticholinergics is preferred due to stronger evidence and better risk profile than benzodiazepines alone | [[2019 - Zareifopoulos et al. - Treatment Options for Acute Agitation in Psychiatric Patients Theoretical and Empirical Evidence]] |
| Definitional ambiguity hinders practice and research | "Rapid tranquilization" is used interchangeably with "chemical restraint" and "therapeutic sedation," creating confusion in clinical, legal, and ethical contexts | [[2020 - Muir-Cochrane - A wicked problem Chemical restraint towards a definition]] |
| Risks necessitate minimization and oversight | Psychiatric restraints (including pharmacological) pose significant harms and should be reduced via legal safeguards, de-escalation, and monitoring | [[2024 - Paul et al. - Exploring psychiatric patient restraints Balancing safety, ethics, and patient rights in mental healthcare]] |

## Open questions
- How can rapid tranquilization be clearly distinguished from chemical restraint and therapeutic sedation in clinical guidelines?

- To what extent does the use of rapid tranquilization violate principles of autonomy and informed consent in emergency contexts?  
- How do legal and ethical frameworks govern the use of rapid tranquilization across different jurisdictions?
