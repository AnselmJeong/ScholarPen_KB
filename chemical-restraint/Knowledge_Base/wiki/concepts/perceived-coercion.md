# perceived coercion

## Why It Matters
- Explores subjective experience of coercion, distinct from objective measures or legal definitions  
- Highlights variation in how patients perceive coercive interventions, including potential for neutral or positive experiences  
- Reveals cultural and relational factors shaping perceptions of coercion in psychiatric care  
- Informs ethical debates on paternalism, autonomy, and the justification of coercive practices  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Paternalism insufficient justification | Paternalism based on assumptions of incompetence does not justify psychiatric coercion; dangerousness alone fails to justify any coercion | [[2012 - Seo et al. - Coercion in psychiatric care Can paternalism justify coercion]] |
| Cultural shaping of coercion | Coercion is culturally constructed: Western contexts emphasize individual autonomy, Eastern emphasize family decision-making, affecting patient experience despite universal rights frameworks | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 03]] |
| Stakeholder divergence | Service users experience coercion as rights violation and trauma; staff and caregivers often justify it as necessary for safety despite acknowledging harm to dignity | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 04]] |
| Structural embedding | Coercion is embedded in institutional structures and social processes, shaping patient experience through loss of autonomy and identity, not limited to specific interventions | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 04]] |
| Complex patient experiences | Patient experiences of coercion are not uniformly negative; some report safety or benefit when accompanied by respectful communication and therapeutic relationships | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 18]] |

## Open questions
- How can respectful communication and therapeutic relationships mitigate negative perceptions of coercion, and under what conditions might coercion be experienced as beneficial?  
- What are the cross-cultural determinants of perceived coercion, and how do family-centered versus individual autonomy models shape patient experience?  
- To what extent do structural and institutional factors in psychiatric settings influence subjective experiences of coercion beyond specific interventions?  
- How should ethical frameworks reconcile stakeholder disagreements—particularly between service users reporting trauma and staff justifying coercion for safety?
