# rights-based ethics

## Why It Matters
- Highlights ethical tension in psychiatry between utilitarian legislation (e.g., Mental Health Act 2007) and rights-based frameworks (e.g., Human Rights Act 1998, Mental Capacity Act 2005)
- Reveals systemic inconsistency in how ethical principles are applied across medical specialties, with psychiatry uniquely split
- Underscores challenges in balancing autonomy with beneficence in involuntary treatment due to power imbalances and inconsistent jurisdictional application
- Connects to broader debates on legal capacity, coercion, and human rights in mental health care

## Open questions
- How can psychiatry reconcile its dual ethical obligations under utilitarian mental health legislation and rights-based human rights instruments in practice?
- What mechanisms exist to ensure consistent application of autonomy-respecting criteria for involuntary treatment across different jurisdictions and clinical settings?
- To what extent do power imbalances between clinicians and patients undermine the validity of consent and assent in psychiatric care, even when formal procedures are followed?
- How might supported decision-making models be operationalized within existing legal frameworks for mental health care to better uphold rights-based principles?
