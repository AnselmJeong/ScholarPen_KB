# human rights

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical debates on psychiatric care, particularly regarding coercion and capacity  
- Framed as essential for equitable mental health treatment and protection against abuse  
- Central to international legal frameworks like the CRPD, shaping domestic policy and practice  
- Highlights tensions between biomedical models and rights-based approaches in psychiatry  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Human rights as essential framework for mental health | Mental health must be reframed through a human rights lens, prioritizing legal capacity, informed consent, and social inclusion | [[2005 - Hunt - Report of the Special Rapporteur on the Right of Everyone to the Enjoyment of the Highest Attainable Standard of Physical and Mental Health, Paul Hunt]] |
| Critique of paternalism in psychiatric coercion | Paternalism unjustifiably assumes incompetence; dangerousness does not justify coercion; impairment only justifies extreme measures as last resort | [[2012 - Seo et al. - Coercion in psychiatric care Can paternalism justify coercion]] |
| Global variability in coercion practices | Coercion is widespread but takes legalized forms in high-income countries and extreme, unregulated forms (e.g., pasung) in low/middle-income countries | [[2017 - Molodynski et al. - Key international themes in coercion]] |
| Defense of psychiatry against anti-psychiatry bias | UN Special Rapporteur's report unfairly blames biomedical psychiatry while ignoring treatment efficacy and systemic gaps | [[2018 - Dharmawardene et al. - Responding to the UN Special Rapporteur's anti-psychiatry bias]] |
| Reliance on international ethical standards | European psychiatric associations favor international codes over national ones, supporting shared guidelines via EPA | [[2023 - Samochowiec et al. - Ethical dilemmas in contemporary psychiatry; Findings from a survey of National Psychiatric Associations in Europe]] |
| Need for systemic reform centered on human rights | Ethical challenges in psychiatry require reform focused on coercion, digital tech, early intervention, and lived experience involvement | [[2024 - Galderisi et al. - Ethical challenges in contemporary psychiatry an overview and an appraisal of possible strategies and research needs]] |
| CRPD Article 12 supports both supported and substitute decision-making | Article 12 should be interpreted via indivisibility and dignity to allow supported decision-making as primary, substitute as last resort | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07]] |

## Open questions
- How can human rights frameworks be operationalized in clinical practice without undermining clinical judgment or safety?  
- What is the appropriate balance between supported decision-making and substitute decision-making under CRPD Article 12 in cases of severe cognitive impairment?  
- To what extent does biomedical psychiatry contribute to human rights violations, versus systemic underfunding and social determinants?  
- How should emerging technologies (e.g., digital phenotyping, AI in psychiatry) be governed to prevent new forms of coercion or rights infringements?
