# competence

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical and legal debates about involuntary treatment and patient autonomy  
- Determines when a patient's refusal of medication can be overridden in psychiatric settings  
- Links to foundational principles of informed consent and decision-making capacity  
- Highlights tensions between clinical judgment and constitutional rights in mental health care  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Qualified right to refuse | Patients have a constitutional right to refuse antipsychotics unless found incompetent or dangerous | [[1980 - Brooks - The constitutional right to refuse antipsychotic medications.]] |
| Autonomy requires capacity | Respect for autonomy depends on intentionality, understanding, and absence of coercion | [[Principles of Biomedical Ethics 04]] |
