# coercion

## Why It Matters
- Coercion is a recurring and contested practice in mental health care across global contexts, raising ethical and human rights concerns.
- It intersects with legal capacity, informed consent, and the legitimacy of psychiatric interventions.
- Its presence challenges trust in therapeutic relationships and reflects broader societal values around autonomy and care.
- Understanding coercion requires analyzing its forms, cultural variations, and the tensions between safety and rights.

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Coercion as inherently contradictory to care | Coercion in psychiatry fundamentally undermines trust, genuine care, and human rights, making it an ethical contradiction. | [[2015 - Donskoy - Coercion and trust in psychiatry the ultimate contradiction]] |
| Coercion as globally pervasive but context-shaped | Coercion varies by economic context: legalized forms in high-income countries and extreme, unregulated practices (e.g., pasung) in low/middle-income countries. | [[2017 - Molodynski et al. - Key international themes in coercion]] |
| Coercion as potentially acceptable under constraints | Coercion may be justified only when balanced against individual rights and public safety through legal and ethical limits. | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 09]] |
| Coercion as experienced and mitigable in care | Service users report mixed experiences; safety, improved treatment quality, and participation can reduce coercion despite ongoing concerns about forced medication and distrust. | [[2023 - Brekke et al. - Patients' experiences with coercive mental health treatment in Flexible Assertive Community Treatment a qualitative study]] |
| Coercion requires precise definition for accountability | Chemical restraint should be defined by intent to control behavior, avoidance of physical restraint/seclusion, or lack of consent, integrating structure, process, and intent. | [[2024 - Hupe et al. - Defining chemical restraint A preliminary step towards measurement and quality assessment]] |
| Coercion linked to systemic violence and underreporting | Staff-perpetrated violence and abuse in UK mental health settings occur with concerning frequency but are systematically underreported due to power imbalances and stigma. | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 02]] |
| Coercion shaped by cultural decision-making norms | Western contexts emphasize individual autonomy; Eastern contexts emphasize family decision-making, affecting patient experience despite universal human rights frameworks. | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 03]] |
| Voluntariness in consent depends on influence legitimacy | Voluntariness in research consent hinges on whether influences are external, intentional, illegitimate, and causally effective—not mere pressure or incentives. | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 22]] |
| Human rights require reframing mental health care | Mental health must be viewed through a human rights lens, prioritizing legal capacity, informed consent, and social inclusion over the biomedical model. | [[2005 - Hunt - Report of the Special Rapporteur on the Right of Everyone to the Enjoyment of the Highest Attainable Standard of Physical and Mental Health, Paul Hunt]] |

## Open questions
- How can coercion be minimized while ensuring safety in acute psychiatric crises, especially given varying cultural and resource contexts?
- What are the long-term psychological and relational impacts of coercive interventions on service users, trust in mental health systems, and help-seeking behavior?
- How should legal and ethical frameworks evolve to better uphold autonomy and informed consent in light of persistent coercive practices globally?
- In what ways do power imbalances, systemic stigma, and institutional culture perpetuate underreporting and normalization of coercion and associated violence?
