# substitute decision-making

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical and legal debates on mental health care under the CRPD
- Challenges traditional models of paternalism in psychiatric treatment
- Highlights tension between autonomy protection and harm prevention
- Informs policy on coercion, consent, and legal capacity reforms

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Substitute decision-making as last resort | Permitted only when necessary to uphold equality and dignity for persons with severe cognitive impairment | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -03]], [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -06]], [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07]] |
| Supported decision-making as norm | Legal capacity must be exercised with support; substitution is exceptional and must respect will and preferences | [[2017 - Szmukler - The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 'Rights, will and preferences' in relation to mental health disabilities]], [[2019 - Puras et al. - Mental health and human rights in the 21st century]] |

| Distinguish enduring will from transient preferences | Honoring prior expressed will may justify overriding current preferences without constituting impermissible substitution | [[2017 - Szmukler - The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 'Rights, will and preferences' in relation to mental health disabilities]] |

## Open questions
- How can "enduring will" be reliably identified and distinguished from changing preferences in fluctuating mental states?
- What safeguards are necessary to prevent misuse of substitute decision-making as a guise for paternalism?
- To what extent does supported decision-making eliminate the need for substitution, even in severe cognitive impairment?
- How should legal systems balance Article 12 of the CRPD with public safety and parens patriae concerns?
