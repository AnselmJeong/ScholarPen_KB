# police power

## Why It Matters
- Central to legal justification for involuntary psychiatric interventions  
- Defines limits of state authority over individual bodily autonomy in mental health contexts  
- Balances public safety concerns with protection of civil liberties  
- Underpins frameworks for civil commitment and forced treatment  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Limited state power to override treatment refusal | Involuntary medication requires finding of incompetence or dangerousness; privacy rights create qualified right to refuse | [[1980 - Brooks - The constitutional right to refuse antipsychotic medications.]] |
| High burden for civil commitment | Commitment demands "clear and convincing" evidence of mental illness and dangerousness or grave disability | [[Psychiatry and the Law - 06]] |
