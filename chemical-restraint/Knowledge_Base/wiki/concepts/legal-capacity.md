# legal capacity

## Why It Matters
- Central to human rights frameworks like the UN CRPD, particularly Article 12, which affirms equal recognition before the law  
- Challenges traditional models of substitute decision-making in psychiatry by emphasizing supported decision-making  
- Directly impacts ethical and legal debates around coercion, informed consent, and involuntary treatment  
- Key to advancing disability rights and dismantling discriminatory practices in mental health systems  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Legal capacity is inherent and non-deniable | Persons with psychosocial disabilities retain legal capacity and must consent to all medical interventions | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 09]], [[Human Rights in Psychiatry - 05]] |
| Supported decision-making as the norm | Legal capacity requires support systems; substitute decision-making should be a last resort | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -06]], [[Human Rights in Psychiatry - 15]] |
| Will and preferences must be respected | Distinguish enduring will from transient preferences; prior valid will may override current wishes without violating rights | [[2017 - Szmukler - The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 'Rights, will and preferences' in relation to mental health disabilities]] |
| Substitute decision-making as exceptional | May be permissible only as a last resort to promote equality for some with severe cognitive impairment under CRPD | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -03]], [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07]] |
| Systemic reform needed | Legal and policy frameworks must shift from biomedical to human rights models to uphold dignity and autonomy | 2005 - Hunt - Report of the Special Rapporteur, 2024 - Galderisi et al. - Ethical challenges in contemporary psychiatry |
