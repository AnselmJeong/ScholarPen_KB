# Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

## Why It Matters
- Centralizes legal capacity and decision-making rights in disability rights discourse  
- Challenges biomedical models of mental health by centering human rights and autonomy  
- Sparks debate over compatibility of involuntary treatment with international human rights law  
- Drives global shifts toward supported decision-making and coercion reduction in psychiatry  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Abolitionist stance | Involuntary psychiatric interventions violate CRPD and must be eliminated | [[2019 - Puras et al. - Mental health and human rights in the 21st century]] |
| Conditional permissibility | Involuntary treatment may be allowed under strict safeguards as last resort | [[Is Involuntary Placement and Non-Consensual Treatment Ever Compliant with UN Human Rights Standards_]] |
| Supported decision-making priority | CRPD requires supported decision-making; advance directives overridden by current wishes | [[2019 - Scholten et al. - Psychiatric Advance Directives Under the Convention on the Rights of Persons With Disabilities Why Advance Instructions Should Be Able to]] |
| Pluralistic model of dignity | Dignity in CRPD includes five dimensions: equal worth, autonomy, interdependence, embodiment, indivisibility | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -05]] |
| Indivisibility allows substitute decision-making | Article 12 permits substitute decision-making as last resort for severe cognitive impairment | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -03]], [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07]] |
| Systemic barriers limit reform | Service user education fails to change practice due to hierarchy and powerlessness | [[2021 - Every‐Palmer et al. - Applying Human Rights and Reducing Coercion in Psychiatry following Service User-Led Education; A Qualitative Study.]] |
| Inadequate protection in crisis contexts | Existing laws fail in low-resource and conflict zones; need new frameworks | [[2022 - Miller - Mental health and the law What else is needed for particularly vulnerable contexts facing armed conflict and development obstacles]] |
| Medical model inadequate | Mental health must be reframed through human rights lens, not biomedical model | [[2005 - Hunt - Report of the Special Rapporteur on the Right of Everyone to the Enjoyment of the Highest Attainable Standard of Physical and Mental Health, Paul Hunt]] |
| Balance model for cognitive disability | Effective frameworks must recognize embodied impairment to avoid undermining autonomy | [[Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -02]] |
