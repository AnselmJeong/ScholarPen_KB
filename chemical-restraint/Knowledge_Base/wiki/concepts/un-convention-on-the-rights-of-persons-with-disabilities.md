# UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities

## Why It Matters
- The CRPD provides a binding human rights framework that challenges involuntary psychiatric treatment and coercive practices.
- Its ratification has increased pressure to align mental health laws with autonomy and consent standards globally.
- Article 12 of the CRPD is central to debates on legal capacity and supported decision-making in psychiatry.
- Despite widespread ratification, coercive practices like involuntary admission persist, revealing implementation gaps.

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| CRPD supports abolition of coercion | Involuntary treatment violates CRPD rights to liberty, autonomy, and legal capacity | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]], [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 10]] |
| CRPD permits coercion under strict conditions | Coercion may be justified if necessary, proportionate, and with procedural safeguards | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]], [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 03]] |
| CRPD implementation reduces coercion | Psychiatric advance directives and joint crisis plans lower coercive admissions by upholding autonomy | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 10]] |
