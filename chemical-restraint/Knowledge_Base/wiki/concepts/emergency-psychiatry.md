# emergency psychiatry

## Why It Matters
- Represents a critical intersection of clinical urgency and legal authority in psychiatric care
- Highlights tensions between patient autonomy and state power during acute crises
- Involves high-stakes decisions about involuntary treatment and restraint
- Reflects evolving standards for balancing safety, ethics, and human rights in emergencies

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Legal justification focus | Involuntary hospitalization is justified primarily by police powers (danger to self/others), not parens patriae | [[Clinical Handbook of Psychiatry and Law - 02]] |
| Historical evolution | Rapid tranquilization emerged alongside antipsychotic development as a clinical response to agitation | [[2014 - Allison, Moncrieff - ‘Rapid tranquillisation’; an historical perspective on its emergence in the context of the development of antipsychotic medications]] |
| Procedural safeguards | Emergency psychiatric interventions must comply with procedural standards and least restrictive principles | [[Clinical Handbook of Psychiatry and Law - 02]] |
