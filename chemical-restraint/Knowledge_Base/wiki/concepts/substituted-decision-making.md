# substituted decision-making

## Why It Matters
- Challenges traditional justifications for overriding legal capacity in mental health care  
- Central to debates on compliance with UN CRPD Article 12 and human rights standards  
- Highlights tension between paternalism and autonomy in psychiatric settings  
- Drives interest in alternatives like advance directives and supported decision-making  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Abolitionist (CRPD-aligned) | Persons with psychosocial disabilities retain full legal capacity; substituted decision-making violates CRPD Article 12 | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 09]] |
| Conditional permissibility | Involuntary placement/treatment may comply with human rights standards only under strict safeguards | [[Is Involuntary Placement and Non-Consensual Treatment Ever Compliant with UN Human Rights Standards_]] |
| Pragmatic reform | Despite CRPD ratification, involuntary admission persists, but psychiatric advance directives and joint crisis plans can reduce coercion | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 10]] |
