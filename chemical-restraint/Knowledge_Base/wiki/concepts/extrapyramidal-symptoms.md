# extrapyramidal symptoms

## Why It Matters
- Represents a serious adverse effect of antipsychotic medications
- Relevant to clinical decision-making in acute psychiatric care
- Highlights ethical tensions in medication administration
- Informs risk-benefit assessments in psychiatric emergencies

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Clinical preference | Haloperidol alone or with anticholinergics is often preferred for acute agitation due to evidence base, despite EPS risk | [[2019 - Zareifopoulos et al. - Treatment Options for Acute Agitation in Psychiatric Patients Theoretical and Empirical Evidence]] |
| Ethical concern | Covert antipsychotic use can reduce harmful behaviors but may cause EPS, raising autonomy vs. beneficence conflicts | [[2024 - Razzak et al. - Ethical Dilemmas in Covert Prescribing A Case Report on Medication Administration for Noncompliant Psychiatric Patients]] |
| Guideline gap | Limited guidance exists for managing persistent agitation after first-line medication failure, increasing EPS risk from polypharmacy | [[2021 - Searles Quick et al. - Which Emergent Medication Should I Give Next Repeated Use of Emergent Medications to Treat Acute Agitation]] |
| Safety emphasis | De-escalation is first-line; restraints require strict protocols to avoid complications including neurological adverse effects | [[2020 - Guerrero et al. - Physical and Chemical Restraints (an Update)]] |
