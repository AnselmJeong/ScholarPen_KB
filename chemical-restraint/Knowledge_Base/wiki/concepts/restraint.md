# restraint

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical debates on coercion in psychiatric care  
- Legally and clinically regulated as a last-resort intervention  
- Shapes patient experience, autonomy, and therapeutic relationships  
- Intersects with legal, human rights, and clinical practice frameworks  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Ethically permissible only in exceptional circumstances | Coercion is justified only when it serves higher values like long-term autonomy or safety after transparent evaluation | [[2021 - Chieze et al. - Coercive Measures in Psychiatry A Review of Ethical Arguments]] |
| Legally permissible only as last resort to prevent imminent harm | Seclusion and restraint require due process, timely review, continuous monitoring under federal/state regulations | [[Clinical Manual of Psychiatry and Law - 06]] |
| Staff attitudes shaped by perceived efficacy and views of aggression | Staff support coercion when they view aggression as intentional, not based on objective safety or dignity | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 16]] |
| Patient experiences are complex and not uniformly negative | Some patients report safety or benefit when coercion includes respectful communication and therapeutic relationships | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 18]] |

## Open questions
- How can respectful communication and therapeutic relationships mitigate negative experiences of restraint?  
- What objective criteria determine when restraint is truly a "last resort" versus a habitual response?  
- Do staff perceptions of patient intent introduce bias that undermines ethical and legal safeguards?  
- How do legal standards for restraint align with patient-reported experiences of safety or harm?
