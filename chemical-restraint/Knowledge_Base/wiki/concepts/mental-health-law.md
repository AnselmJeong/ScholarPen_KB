# mental health law

## Why It Matters
- Central to ethical tensions in psychiatry between public safety and individual rights  
- Shapes clinical practice through legal frameworks governing coercion and consent  
- Highlights gaps between domestic laws and international human rights standards  
- Drives reform efforts in low-resource and conflict-affected settings  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Utilitarian legal dominance | Mental health law is primarily utilitarian (Mental Health Act 2007), conflicting with rights-based laws | [[2008 - Lepping - Is psychiatry torn in different ethical directions]] |
| Advance directives reduce coercion | Prior expressed wishes via advance directives can align care with autonomy and reduce coercion | [[2012 - Bonnie - Advance Directives A Tool for Reducing Coercion]] |
| Human rights framework for reducing coercion | Integrating CRPD and local context (e.g., family) can define and reduce coercive measures | [[2013 - Lepping, Raveesh - The Mysore Declaration]] |
| Distinguishing will from preferences | Respect for CRPD requires honoring enduring will over transient preferences, even if it overrides current choice | [[2017 - Szmukler - The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities 'Rights, will and preferences' in relation to mental health disabilities]] |
| Coercion as exceptional and justified | Coercion is only ethical in rare cases when it serves higher values like safety or long-term autonomy after rigorous review | [[2021 - Chieze et al. - Coercive Measures in Psychiatry A Review of Ethical Arguments]] |
| Need for new frameworks in fragile contexts | Existing international law fails in conflict/low-resource settings; new laws needed balancing autonomy and protection | [[2022 - Miller - Mental health and the law What else is needed for particularly vulnerable contexts facing armed conflict and development obstacles]] |
| Data comparison requires depth | Cross-jurisdictional comparison of mental health and capacity law must include lived experience and social determinants | [[2024 - Davidson et al. - Comparing mental health and mental capacity law data across borders; Challenges and opportunities]] |
| No UN consensus on involuntary care | UN bodies disagree: some ban involuntary treatment, others allow it only under strict safeguards | [[Is Involuntary Placement and Non-Consensual Treatment Ever Compliant with UN Human Rights Standards_]] |
