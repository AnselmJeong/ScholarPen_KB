# civil commitment

## Why It Matters
- Central to debates about liberty versus treatment in mental health law  
- Highlights tensions between legal standards and real-world care quality  
- Connects to human rights frameworks challenging coercive practices  
- Intersects with autonomy, consent, and disability rights discourse  

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Legal standard for commitment | Requires "clear and convincing" evidence of mental illness and dangerousness or grave disability | [[Psychiatry and the Law - 06]] |
| Right to treatment limitation | Guarantees only minimally adequate care, not optimal or chosen treatment, for involuntarily committed patients | [[Clinical Handbook of Psychiatry and Law - 03]] |
| Human rights critique | U.S. mental health law unjustly violates bodily autonomy by permitting coercive treatment while denying access to voluntary, culturally responsive care | [[2020 - Bergan - The Right to Choose and Refuse Mental Health Care: A Human The Right to Choose and Refuse Mental Health Care; A Human Rights Based Approach to Ending Compulsory Psychiatric Intervention]] |

## Open questions
- How can legal standards for commitment be reformed to better align with human rights obligations and cultural responsiveness?  
- What mechanisms ensure the "right to treatment" delivers meaningful, person-centered care rather than minimal compliance?  
- Under what conditions might non-coercive alternatives achieve equivalent safety and therapeutic outcomes?  
- How does the application of civil commitment vary across demographic groups, and what disparities exist in access to voluntary care?
