# coercive measures
## Why It Matters
- Coercive measures in psychiatry are ethically contested, with debates centering on whether they can ever be justified despite potential harm to mental health and dignity  
- Human rights-based approaches show promise in reducing coercion while supporting staff and patient empowerment, though implementation outcomes vary  
- Cultural context significantly influences perceptions and practices, challenging universal definitions of "least restrictive" or "best practice"  
- Systemic factors like understaffing and cost pressures often drive coercive use more than clinical necessity  
## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Ethical permissibility under strict conditions | Coercion may be justified only in exceptional cases when it serves higher values like long-term autonomy or safety, following transparent evaluation | [[2021 - Chieze et al. - Coercive Measures in Psychiatry A Review of Ethical Arguments]] |
| Insufficiency of autonomy/dignity arguments alone | Appeals to autonomy and human dignity cannot definitively justify eliminating coercion, as these principles can support or oppose seclusion depending on context | [[2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry]] |
| Framework for reduction via human rights and context | The Mysore Declaration provides a model to reduce coercion by integrating human rights with family involvement and legal reform in Indian mental health care | [[2013 - Lepping, Raveesh - The Mysore Declaration]] |
| Harm to mental health outcomes | Exposure to coercive measures is associated with a small but significant worsening of mental health at discharge (1.91-point HoNOS increase) | [[2024 - Baggio et al. - Effect of coercive measures on mental health status in adult psychiatric populations a nationwide trial emulation]] |
| Limited effectiveness of guideline interventions | The PreVCo RCT improved guideline adherence but did not significantly reduce coercive measure use, suggesting implementation gaps | [[2023 - Steinert et al. - Implementation of guidelines on prevention of coercion and violence (PreVCo) in psychiatry a multicentre randomised controlled trial]] |
| Role of systemic drivers over clinical need | Coercive practices are often driven by understaffing and cost-cutting rather than clinical necessity, making them ethically indefensible from a patient perspective | [[2024 - Laureano et al. - Ethical Issues in Clinical Decision-Making about Involuntary Psychiatric Treatment A Scoping Review]] |
| Cultural shaping of acceptability | In traditional societies, coercive measures may be socially acceptable when aligned with family/religious values, contrasting with Western autonomy-focused norms | [[Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 13]] |
| Staff role conflict and coping mechanisms | Healthcare workers in high-security settings experience conflict between caregiving and security roles, leading to emotional detachment as a coping strategy | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 17]] |
