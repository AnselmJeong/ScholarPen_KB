# informal coercion
## Why It Matters
- Explains psychological pressure without explicit threats
- Central to understanding patient experiences in mental healthcare
- Challenges traditional distinctions between voluntary and involuntary treatment
- Highlights ethical tensions in care delivery under moral or systemic pressures

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Contextual model of informal coercion | Occurs when refusing treatment is perceived to worsen one's situation relative to a moral baseline, with no reasonable alternative, even without explicit threats | [[2023 - Hempeler et al. - When Treatment Pressures Become Coercive A Context-Sensitive Model of Informal Coercion in Mental Healthcare]] |
| Patient experience in flexible care | Service users report varied experiences; safety, improved quality, and participation reduce coercion, but forced medication and systemic distrust remain concerns | [[2023 - Brekke et al. - Patients' experiences with coercive mental health treatment in Flexible Assertive Community Treatment a qualitative study]] |
| Formal-informal continuum | Coercion spans formal (legally regulated, physical) and informal (psychological) forms; justification depends on context via harm principle or soft paternalism, subject to necessity, proportionality, procedural justice | [[Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08]] |
