# neuroethics

## Why It Matters
- Explores ethical limits of chemical restraints in psychiatric care
- Challenges traditional views on bodily autonomy and consent
- Highlights gaps in principlist approaches to clinical ethics
- Informs policy debates on coercive treatment and human rights

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Chemical restraints altering consciousness are greater liberty violations | Changing desires or conscious states constitutes a deeper infringement on basic liberty than unconsciousness | [[2024 - Birks - Chemical Restraints and the Basic Liberties]] |
| Sedating chemical restraints are more ethically problematic | Restraints that undermine consciousness are worse than those altering specific states due to necessary basic liberty infringement | [[2024 - Crutchfield, Redinger - Basic Liberties, Consent, and Chemical Restraints]] |
| Principlism is inadequate for chemical restraint ethics | Overemphasizes autonomy, lacks value theory for conflict resolution, ignores collective moral impact | [[2024 - Mangal, Scheiner - The Limitations of Principlism]] |
