# substituted judgment
## Why It Matters
- Addresses ethical decision-making for incapacitated patients in psychiatric contexts
- Balances individual autonomy with clinical necessity in restraint and treatment decisions
- Informs legal standards for consent when capacity is impaired
- Guides least restrictive interventions through substituted judgment frameworks

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Ethical priority on individual preferences | Mental health service users' preferences should determine least restrictive restraint types over rigid hierarchies | [[2024 - Hempeler et al. - Preferences of Individual Mental Health Service Users Are Essential in Determining the Least Restrictive Type of Restraint]] |
| Legal standard for incapacitated patients | Informed consent requires functional competency assessment; substituted consent applies when capacity is lacking, subject to state laws | [[Clinical Manual of Psychiatry and Law - 04]] |
