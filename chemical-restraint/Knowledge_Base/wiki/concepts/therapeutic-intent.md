# therapeutic intent
## Why It Matters
- Central to distinguishing treatment from restraint in psychiatric emergencies
- Key ethical and legal criterion in evaluating coercive practices
- Subject of ongoing ambiguity despite regulatory efforts to define it
- Influences clinical decision-making in acute care and forensic settings

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Therapeutic intent as distinguishing factor | Treatment is justified by therapeutic intent, while restraint lacks it; regulatory efforts aim to uphold this distinction | [[2003 - Currier - The Controversy over “Chemical Restraint” in Acute Care Psychiatry]] |
| Ambiguity in application | Despite efforts, therapeutic intent is poorly defined and often conflated with treatment, leading to ethical and clinical uncertainty | [[2003 - Currier - The Controversy over “Chemical Restraint” in Acute Care Psychiatry]] |
| Coercive practices persist regardless | Banning one coercive measure increases use of others, indicating limited impact of intent-based distinctions on actual practice | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 07]] |
| Patient harm remains | Coercive measures cause measurable distress and dignity violations, regardless of stated therapeutic intent | [[The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 07]] |
