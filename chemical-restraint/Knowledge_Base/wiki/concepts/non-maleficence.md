# non-maleficence
## Why It Matters
- Central to evaluating ethical limits of coercive interventions in psychiatry
- Challenges assumptions that autonomy or dignity alone justify eliminating coercion
- Highlights risks of defensive medicine prioritizing clinician self-interest over patient safety
- Underpins ethical analysis of covert medication and hidden harm in treatment

## Key Positions
| Position | Core claim | Key sources |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Non-maleficence limits autonomy-based arguments against coercion | Appeals to autonomy and dignity cannot uniformly justify eliminating coercive measures, as they may support or oppose seclusion depending on context | [[2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry]] |
| Defensive medicine violates non-maleficence | Prioritizing clinician self-interest through deception and harm without benefit breaches core medical ethics, including non-maleficence | [[2020 - Bester - Defensive practice is indefensible how defensive medicine runs counter to the ethical and professional obligations of clinicians]] |
| Non-maleficence requires structured harm-benefit analysis in coercion decisions | Ethical use of coercion must follow algorithms assessing capacity, benefit-harm balance, and biopsychosocial context, not rely on principles alone | [[2023 - Steinert - Using coercion in mental disorders or risking the patients death An analysis of the protocols of a clinical ethics committee]] |
| Covert medication risks violating non-maleficence | Hidden antipsychotic administration may reduce specific harms (e.g., foreign object ingestion) but causes new harms (e.g., extrapyramidal symptoms) without resolving psychosis, raising non-maleficence concerns | [[2024 - Razzak et al. - Ethical Dilemmas in Covert Prescribing A Case Report on Medication Administration for Noncompliant Psychiatric Patients]] |

## Open questions
- How can non-maleficence be objectively measured when harms are psychological, delayed, or contested?
- To what extent should clinician intent factor into non-maleficence assessments in coercive or covert interventions?
- Can non-maleficence coexist with paternalism in psychiatry, or are they inherently conflicting?
- Does reducing one harm (e.g., suicide risk) via coercion automatically satisfy non-maleficence if other harms emerge (e.g., trauma, distrust)?
