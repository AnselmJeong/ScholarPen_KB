# On the concept of restraint in psychiatry

## Summary
This article provides a concise clarification of the concept of "restraint" in the psychiatric context, with particular reference to official sources. It argues that restraint in psychiatry refers to measures that restrict an individual's freedom of movement and comprises various types, with an essential characteristic being its fundamentally coercive nature. The author analyzes definitions from regulatory bodies such as the U.S. Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS), the Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organization (JCAHO), the Italian National Bioethics Committee (NBC), and the Mental Capacity Act (2005) to establish a consensus that restraint involves both the restriction of freedom of movement and an element of coercion. The article introduces the novel concept of "physical psychological restraint" to capture coercive non-contact practices such as intimidation or psychological pressure used to enforce patient compliance, thereby completing the concept of physical restraint. Additional types discussed include chemical restraint (medication used to manage behavior rather than treat a condition), environmental restraint (restriction of access to parts of the environment), psychological restraint (withholding privileges or activities), and psycho-environmental restraint (a combination of environmental and psychological measures). The author emphasizes that precise terminology is crucial for research, monitoring, and reduction efforts, particularly given the heterogeneity and lack of internationally agreed-upon definitions across jurisdictions.

## Key Entities
- **European Commission** (org) — Funded the Eunomia project on restraint in psychiatry across ten European countries, which informed the author’s consideration of coercion in involuntary patient care.
- **American Association for Emergency Psychiatry** (org) — Created the "Ten domains of de-escalation" via Project BETA Workgroup, referenced as an alternative to restraint.
- **CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services)** (org) — Provided regulatory definitions of physical and chemical restraint used throughout the analysis; already represented in the wiki as [[concepts/centers-for-medicare-and-medicaid-services-cms]].
- **Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organization (JCAHO)** (org) — Cited for its definition of restraint as any method restricting freedom of movement, physical activity, or normal body access.
- **Italian National Bioethics Committee (NBC)** (org) — Provided a definition of restraint focusing on limitation of autonomous movement.
- **Académie Suisse des Sciences Médicales** (org) — Defined coercive acts as actions carried out against a patient’s stated or presumed will or causing resistance, underpinning the coercive nature of psychiatric restraint.

## Key Concepts
- **Restraint (in psychiatry)** — Measures restricting freedom of movement, fundamentally coercive in nature; central to the article and defined using official sources (CMS, JCAHO, NBC, Mental Capacity Act 2005).
- **Physical psychological restraint** — Use of intimidation, command, or psychological pressure by staff to force patient compliance (e.g., ordering to stay in a position or corner); introduced by the author to complete the concept of physical restraint.
- **Chemical restraint** — Drug used to manage behavior or restrict movement, not standard treatment for the patient’s condition (per CMS); must be considered full-fledged restraint due to its coercive nature and restriction of spontaneous movement.
- **Environmental restraint** — Restricts free access to all parts of a person’s environment (e.g., locked doors, outdoor access limits); a less invasive but still coercive restrictive measure.
- **Psychological restraint** — Therapeutic regimes involving withholding privileges or activity access due to non-compliance (per Pennsylvania Code); a recognized form in some jurisdictions highlighting non-physical control.
- **Psycho-environmental restraint** — Measure affecting environment and/or applying psychological pressure to limit freedom of movement or choice (e.g., restricted schedules, object limitations, social interaction bans); an integrative category for less obvious but impactful controls.
- **Coercive act** — Action carried out against patient’s stated/presumed will or causing resistance (per Académie Suisse des Sciences Médicales); essential characteristic defining restraint in psychiatry versus other medicine.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The concept of restraint in psychiatry must be clearly defined as fundamentally coercive and encompassing multiple types (physical, chemical, environmental, psychological, and physical psychological restraint) to avoid terminological confusion in research, practice, and policy.
  - **Evidence**: Analysis of official sources (CMS regulations, JCAHO, Italian NBC, Mental Capacity Act 2005, Académie Suisse) showing consensus on restraint involving freedom restriction + coercion; historical context (Tuke, Conolly, no-restraint wards) showing controversy over necessity; clinical distinctions: restraint in psychiatry is typically non-consensual vs. consensual use in other medicine (except geriatrics/ICU); author’s introduction of "physical psychological restraint" to capture coercive non-contact practices (e.g., threats of restraint to enforce medication).
  - **Strength**: Strong for definitional clarity based on authoritative regulatory and ethical sources; moderate for historical claims (cited but not detailed); novel typology (physical psychological restraint) is logical but not empirically validated in this piece.
