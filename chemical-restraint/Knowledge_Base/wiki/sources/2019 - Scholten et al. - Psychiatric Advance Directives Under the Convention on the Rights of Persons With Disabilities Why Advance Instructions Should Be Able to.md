# Psychiatric Advance Directives Under the Convention on the Rights of Persons With Disabilities: Why Advance Instructions Should Be Able to Override Current Preferences

## Summary
This article examines how the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) impacts the effectiveness of Psychiatric Advance Directives (PADs). The authors analyze two competing interpretations of the CRPD: a "radical" model endorsed by the Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities that requires absolute respect for current preferences, and a "combined supported decision making model" that integrates supported decision making with competence assessment and substitute decision making. Through clinical vignettes involving schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, they demonstrate that the radical CRPD model renders PADs ineffective precisely when they are most needed—during mental health crises where patients express clear but conflicting treatment preferences. The combined model better supports PAD functionality by allowing advance directives to guide decisions when decision-making capacity is impaired, even if some communication ability remains. The authors conclude that while the radical interpretation undermines PAD utility, a more flexible approach that incorporates capacity assessment can preserve both autonomy and effective crisis intervention.

- **Combined Supported Decision Making Model** (type: model) — Proposed alternative framework integrating supported decision making, competence assessment, and substitute decision making to better support PAD effectiveness

## Key Concepts
- **Psychiatric Advance Directives (PADs)** — Documents allowing individuals to state treatment preferences for future mental health crises; central focus of analysis regarding effectiveness under CRPD interpretations
- **Radical CRPD Model** — Interpretation requiring absolute respect for current preferences during mental health crises, rejecting substitute decision making and competence assessment; argued to render PADs ineffective when clear but conflicting preferences emerge
- **Combined Supported Decision Making Model** — Framework integrating supported decision making with competence assessment and substitute decision making when capacity remains impaired despite support; proposed to better support PAD effectiveness by prioritizing advance directives during incapacity
- **Supported Decision Making** — Interventions to enhance decision-making ability without removing legal capacity; promoted by UN bodies but critiqued for potential accountability gaps in acute psychosis
- **Substituted Judgment Standard** — Surrogate decision-making approach where choices reflect what the person would have wanted if competent; presented as ethically superior to best interest standard for honoring advance directives
- **Best Interest Standard** — Surrogate decision-making approach choosing what is objectively best for the person; contrasted with substituted judgment as potentially overriding PADs
- **Functional Approach to Competence** — Assessment of decision-making ability via understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and communication; used in combined model but rejected by radical CRPD model as discriminatory
- **Legal Capacity** — Includes legal standing (holding rights) and legal agency (exercising rights); basis of radical CRPD claim that all humans inherently possess it, negating need for capacity assessment
- **Ulysses Contract / Competence-Insensitive Self-Binding Directive** — Advance directive binding future actions regardless of current wishes; mentioned as CRPD committee’s proposed solution but critiqued as insufficient and undesirable
- **Compromise / Autonomy in Mental Health** — Ethical principle emphasizing self-determination and life planning according to values; debated how best to respect it during crises when current preferences conflict with advance directives

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The radical interpretation of the CRPD by the UN Committee undermines the effectiveness of Psychiatric Advance Directives (PADs) precisely when they are most needed—during mental health crises where individuals express clear but conflicting treatment preferences.
  - Evidence: Two clinical vignettes illustrate this: (1) A schizophrenia patient refuses antipsychotics during crisis despite prior PAD requesting low-dose atypical antipsychotics; under radical CRPD model, current refusal overrides PAD, forcing unwanted treatment or no treatment. (2) A bipolar disorder patient demands discharge during mania despite PAD agreeing to hospitalization; under radical model, current preference must be followed, risking harm.
  - Strength: Well-supported by logical analysis and clinical illustration; limitation is that vignettes are hypothetical but grounded in common clinical scenarios.
- **Core claim**: The combined supported decision making model (supported decision making + competence assessment + substitute decision making when needed) better supports PAD goals by allowing advance directives to guide decisions when capacity is impaired, even if some communication ability remains.
  - Evidence: Theoretical analysis showing how the model prioritizes advance directives during incapacity while still providing support; consistent with empirical data showing 67% consistency between PADs and care when accessed.
  - Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in medical ethics and disability rights; limitation is lack of direct empirical testing of the model itself.
- **Core claim**: Concerns remain about accountability of support persons in supported decision making arrangements; competence assessment helps mitigate this risk.
  - Evidence: Authors note that without accountability mechanisms, support may not adequately protect the person's values; competence assessment provides objective check on when support suffices.
  - Strength: Reasonable concern based on practical implementation challenges; limitation is speculative without empirical data on support systems.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The article challenges any assumption in the wiki that the CRPD universally supports or strengthens PADs, showing instead how a dominant UN interpretation undermines their effectiveness in mental health crises
- Internal tension exists between respecting autonomy via advance planning versus current preferences during impaired decision-making; authors argue that ignoring incapacity harms the very autonomy PADs aim to protect
- Tension between the desire for non-discriminatory approaches (rejecting capacity assessment) and the need for mechanisms to distinguish when decision-making support is sufficient versus when substituted judgment is needed
- Authors reject Ulysses contracts (competence-insensitive binders) as conceptually and ethically problematic, yet note they are the CRPD committee's own proposed solution, highlighting a gap between theoretical ideals and practical implementation

## Open Questions
- How can support persons in decision-making arrangements be made accountable without undermining the trust-based nature of support relationships in mental health care?
- Is there a way to interpret the CRPD that allows for time-limited, capacity-sensitive authority of advance directives without violating non-discrimination principles regarding disability?
- What specific safeguards prevent competence assessments from being used discriminatorily while still allowing them to function as ethical tools to determine when decision-making support is sufficient versus when substituted guidance is needed?
