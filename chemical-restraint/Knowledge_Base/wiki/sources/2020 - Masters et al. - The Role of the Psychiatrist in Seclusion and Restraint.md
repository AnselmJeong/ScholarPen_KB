# The Role of the Psychiatrist in Seclusion and Restraint

## Summary
This article examines the evolving role of psychiatrists in managing seclusion and restraint (S-R) in psychiatric inpatient and residential settings. Historically, psychiatrists have been involved in S-R oversight for over 200 years, but their specific responsibilities in prevention have remained undefined and inconsistent across facilities. The authors argue that psychiatrists should take a more active, leadership-oriented role in S-R reduction by engaging with the evidence-based Six Core Strategies to Prevent Conflict, Trauma, and Violence (6CS) model. This framework provides concrete opportunities for psychiatrists to lead organizational change, use data to guide practice, train staff, apply prevention tools, include patients and families in decision-making, and conduct therapeutic debriefings after S-R events. The article highlights successful implementations, such as the Pennsylvania State Hospital’s reduction in S-R hours and New York’s PARS program achieving 50–80% reductions over four years, as evidence that psychiatrist-led involvement within structured models like 6CS can improve safety and reduce harm.

## Key Entities
- **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)** (org): Sets federal standards for S-R use, including the "one-hour rule" requiring physician review after S-R events; referenced as a regulatory benchmark that psychiatrists must meet.
- **Joint Commission** (org): Healthcare accreditation body that sets standards for S-R use but does not specify a defined role for psychiatrists in prevention; noted as not assigning preventive responsibilities to psychiatrists.
- **Six Core Strategies to Prevent Conflict, Trauma, and Violence (6CS)** (tool): Evidence-based model comprising leadership, data use, workforce development, use of prevention tools, patient and family inclusion, and debriefing; presented as a framework for psychiatrist-led S-R reduction.
- **Pennsylvania State Hospital S-R program** (org): Cited as a real-world example where psychiatric leadership contributed to reducing seclusion from 4.2 to 0.3 and restraint from 3.5 to 1.2 hours per 1,000 patient days between 1990 and 2000.
- **New York State Office of Mental Health’s Positive Alternatives to Restraint and Seclusion (PARS) program** (org): Example of successful S-R reduction in youth programs using 6CS, achieving 50–80% decreases in S-R use over four years while expanding alternative interventions.

## Key Concepts
- **Seclusion and Restraint (S-R)**: Interventions that restrict patient movement or behavior in psychiatric settings; described as potentially harmful or fatal, often resulting from systemic failures, and in need of preventive strategies led by psychiatrists.
- **Treatment failure**: A conceptual reframing of S-R as indicative of inadequate or insufficient care rather than an inevitable or necessary intervention; attributed to Charles Currie’s 1997 advocacy to shift focus toward preventive, supportive alternatives.
- **Public health prevention model**: A three-tier framework organizing prevention into primary (e.g., trauma-informed environments), secondary (e.g., de-escalation, safety plans), and tertiary (e.g., post-event analysis, repair, debriefing); serves as the organizational foundation for the 6CS model.
- **Trauma-informed care**: An approach that integrates understanding of trauma into environmental and interpersonal practices to avoid retraumatization; identified as a core component of primary prevention within the 6CS model.
- **Debriefing**: A structured post-S-R process in which clinicians review events with patients and staff to understand triggers, apologize when appropriate, repair therapeutic alliances, and identify prevention strategies; highlighted as a key opportunity for psychiatrist-led learning and relationship repair.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Psychiatrists should assume a more active leadership role in all phases of S-R management—particularly prevention—through engagement with the Six Core Strategies model, as their current involvement is largely reactive and underutilized despite their ethical and clinical responsibility.
- **Evidence**: Success stories from the Pennsylvania State Hospital (significant reductions in S-R hours) and New York’s PARS program (50–80% S-R reduction over four years) demonstrate that psychiatrist leadership within structured models like 6CS correlates with improved outcomes; CMS reports of low S-R in monitored facilities further support the effectiveness of systemic approaches.
- **Strength**: The evidence is grounded in real-world implementations and aligns with established public health principles, offering practical, scalable guidance for psychiatric leaders; the multi-component nature of 6CS allows for measurable impact across organizational, clinical, and interpersonal domains.
- **Limitations**: The conclusions rely on program-level outcomes and expert consensus rather than randomized controlled trials; it is difficult to isolate the psychiatrist’s specific contribution from other systemic factors (e.g., staff training, policy changes), and there is a noted lack of standardized psychiatric training in S-R prevention, which may hinder consistent application of the proposed roles.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- While CMS requires only a physician review within 24 hours of an S-R event (which may be conducted by a trained nurse with psychiatric follow-up), the article argues that this minimal standard underutilizes psychiatrist expertise and fails to leverage their potential role in prevention—creating a tension between regulatory compliance and optimal clinical leadership.
- The authors acknowledge an internal limitation: psychiatrists often lack uniform, standardized training in S-R prevention, as most training is facility-specific and inconsistent, which could impede reliable implementation of the proactive roles outlined in the 6CS model (e.g., in data use, debriefing, or workforce training).
