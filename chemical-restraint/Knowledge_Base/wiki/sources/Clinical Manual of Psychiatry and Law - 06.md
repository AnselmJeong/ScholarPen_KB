# Chapter 6: Seclusion and Restraint

## Summary
This chapter examines the legal and clinical framework governing the use of seclusion and restraint in psychiatric settings. It defines seclusion as involuntary confinement alone in a room and restraint as the application of physical force—including drugs—to restrict movement. The chapter emphasizes that these interventions must be used only as a last resort when less restrictive measures fail and to prevent imminent harm to self or others, not for staff convenience or punishment. It details the overlapping regulatory landscape involving CMS, JCAHO, state laws, and professional guidelines, highlighting requirements such as physician evaluation within one hour (per CMS), documentation, continuous monitoring, and due-process procedures including notice and impartial review. Landmark cases like Youngberg v. Romeo are discussed, acknowledging that restraint may rarely be justified for training but emphasizing the primacy of safety. The chapter also outlines clinical indications, contraindications (e.g., in delirium or dementia), and risks of misuse, supported by malpractice cases such as Fleming and Pisel, which illustrate liability for improper use.

## Key Entities
- **Center for Medicare Services (CMS)**: organization — sets federal conditions of participation for Medicare/Medicaid hospitals, including 1-hour physician review requirement for seclusion/restraint.
- **Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organizations (JCAHO)**: organization — accredits healthcare organizations; sets standards limiting seclusion/restraint to imminent risk scenarios and enforces timely evaluation.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)**: organization — issues clinical guidelines (e.g., Task Force reports) that inform best practices and have influenced federal and accreditation standards.
- **Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1987**: tool (legislation) — establishes stringent rules for restraint use in nursing homes receiving federal funds, including limits on chemical restraint and requirements for less restrictive alternatives.
- **Youngberg v. Romeo (1982)**: dataset (legal case) — U.S. Supreme Court decision recognizing a limited right to restraint for "needed training" in addition to safety, though safety remains the primary justification.

## Key Concepts
- **Seclusion**: Involuntary confinement of a person alone in a room where they are physically prevented from leaving; defined as a regulated intervention requiring strict adherence to last-resort and imminent harm standards.
- **Restraint**: Direct application of physical force (human touch, mechanical devices, or drugs) to restrict freedom of movement; includes chemical restraint but excludes therapeutic medication use for underlying psychiatric conditions.
- **Due-process procedures**: Legal requirements involving notice, hearing, and impartial decision-making before implementing seclusion/restraint; flexibility exists based on circumstances (e.g., time of day) but core protections remain.
- **Last resort principle**: The requirement that seclusion and restraint be used only after less restrictive interventions have failed and only to prevent imminent harm; a cornerstone of both legal and ethical use.
- **Chemical restraint**: Use of psychotropic medications to immobilize a patient; prohibited unless treating an underlying psychiatric disorder that contributes to dangerous behavior.
- **Therapeutic alliance**: Collaborative relationship between patient and staff that can reduce reliance on seclusion/restraint by fostering trust and cooperation, particularly during crisis stabilization.
- **Risk-benefit analysis**: Clinical evaluation weighing benefits (e.g., violence prevention) against harms (physical/emotional trauma) of seclusion/restraint; recommended for ongoing justification of continued use.
- **Imminent risk**: Threshold for emergency use under JCAHO and CMS standards, referring to immediate danger of self-harm or harm to others; distinguishes emergency use from convenience or punitive applications.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Seclusion and restraint are legally permissible only as a last resort to prevent imminent harm, not for convenience or punishment, and are subject to stringent federal, state, and accreditation regulations (CMS, JCAHO, state laws) that require due process, timely physician review, and continuous monitoring.
  - **Evidence**: Citations of CMS regulations (42 CFR 482.13), JCAHO standards, state statutes, and case law including Youngberg v. Romeo (acknowledging limited training justification), Fleming v. Prince George’s County (liability for failure to assess cause), and Pisel v. Stamford Hospital (liability for unsafe environments).
  - **Strength**: Strong — based on authoritative federal regulations, accreditation standards, and judicial precedent. The synthesis of multiple legal sources creates a coherent framework, though real-world application may vary due to interpretation and clinical judgment.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension**: While Youngberg v. Romeo acknowledges restraint may be used for "needed training," the chapter later emphasizes seclusion/restraint must never be for staff convenience, creating ambiguity about what constitutes legitimate training versus misuse.
- **Internal caveat**: Notes that staff may request seclusion due to their own anxiety or helplessness rather than patient need, indicating a gap between policy and practice that is not fully resolved.
- **Procedural flexibility**: Describes due process as less formal at night, which could be pragmatic but also opens potential for inconsistent application without clear safeguards.
