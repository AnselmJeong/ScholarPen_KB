# The Right to Choose and Refuse Mental Health Care: A Human Rights Based Approach to Ending Compulsory Psychiatric Intervention

## Summary
This article examines how U.S. mental health law creates exceptions to bodily autonomy rights for people with psychosocial disabilities, permitting involuntary psychiatric treatment while simultaneously restricting access to voluntary, culturally responsive care. Through historical, legal, and human rights analysis, Bergan demonstrates that psychiatric labels have been historically used to pathologize marginalized groups—including Black, Indigenous, poor, queer, and disabled communities—and that civil commitment laws function as tools of social control rather than genuine medical intervention. The article contrasts the strong constitutional protections for refusing somatic medical treatment (e.g., in Cruzan v. Director) with the lack of equivalent protections in psychiatric contexts, arguing that this disparity stems from epistemic injustice and sanism. Drawing on international human rights frameworks like the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR), the author contends that forced psychiatric treatment violates rights to liberty, bodily integrity, and health. The article concludes that ensuring parity between the right to refuse somatic and psychiatric care, expanding access to community-based alternatives, and centering the lived experiences of people with psychosocial disabilities are essential to ending coercive practices and advancing health justice.

## Key Concepts
- **Bodily autonomy** — The right to govern what happens to one’s own body without external coercion; central to the argument that people with psychosocial disabilities are unjustly denied this right in mental health contexts.
- **Informed consent** — The legal and ethical requirement for patient agreement to treatment after understanding risks and benefits; presented as routinely overridden in psychiatric care despite being upheld in somatic medicine.
- **Epistemic injustice** — A form of unfairness where someone is wronged specifically in their capacity as a knower, often due to prejudice; used to explain how psychiatric labels undermine the credibility of individuals’ self-reports and treatment preferences.
- **Structural inequality** — Systemic disparities embedded in social, economic, and political institutions; identified as a root cause of distress that is often ignored when psychiatric labeling individualizes and pathologizes marginalized populations.
- **Sanism** — Bias and discrimination against people with mental illness, analogous to racism or sexism; described as underpinning justifications for forced treatment through assumptions of incompetence and danger.
- **Civil commitment** — The legal process for involuntary psychiatric hospitalization or treatment; critiqued as being misused to deprive rights under the guise of treatment, with historical ties to eugenics and social control.
- **Cultural responsiveness** — A healthcare approach that respects patients’ cultural, linguistic, and social needs; proposed as a key alternative to coercion that could reduce the perceived need for forced treatment by meeting individuals where they are.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Current U.S. mental health law unjustly violates the bodily autonomy of people with psychosocial disabilities by permitting coercive treatment while denying access to voluntary, culturally responsive care, and this violates both domestic constitutional principles and international human rights law.
- **Evidence**: 
  - Historical analysis shows psychiatric diagnoses have been used to pathologize Black, Indigenous, poor, queer, and disabled communities, reinforcing systems of oppression.
  - Legal comparison reveals courts protect the right to refuse somatic care (e.g., Cruzan v. Director) but routinely deny equivalent rights in psychiatric cases, despite similar liberty interests.
  - International human rights frameworks (CRPD, ICESCR) are violated by forced psychiatric treatment, which may constitute inhumane treatment or torture under UN standards.
  - Scholarly and advocacy sources document the harms of institutionalization and the effectiveness of community-based, peer-led alternatives in reducing crisis and promoting autonomy.
- **Strength of evidence**: Strong—combines doctrinal legal analysis, historical documentation, international human rights law, and empirical public health research. The argument is supported by case law, treaty obligations, and a growing consensus in disability rights and public health literature on the harms of coercion and benefits of voluntary care.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **With existing assumptions**: If the wiki currently treats civil commitment as a neutral or benevolent tool for public safety, this source directly challenges that by showing its historical and ongoing use in social control, eugenics, and discrimination against marginalized groups.
- **Internal tension**: The author acknowledges the state’s interest in preventing harm but argues it is rarely genuine in psychiatric contexts, often relying on stereotypes rather than evidence-based risk assessment, creating a tension between paternalistic ideals and autonomy rights.
- **Conceptual tension**: While there is no affirmative constitutional right to healthcare in the U.S., the article argues for a strong negative right to refuse treatment; this raises questions about how to frame healthcare access as a justice issue without relying on unsubstantiated entitlements.

## Open Questions
