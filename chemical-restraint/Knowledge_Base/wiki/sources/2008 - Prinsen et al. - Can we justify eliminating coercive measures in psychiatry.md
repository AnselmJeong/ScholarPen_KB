# Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry?

## Summary
This paper critically examines whether ethical appeals to autonomy and human dignity can justify the complete elimination of coercive measures, particularly seclusion, in psychiatric practice. The authors argue that both autonomy and dignity are not singular, unambiguous principles but admit multiple interpretations—such as relational versus individualistic autonomy and inherent versus contextual dignity—that can be marshaled to either support or oppose the use of seclusion. Drawing on philosophical analysis and clinical observation, they contend that without evidence of benefit, appeals to these principles fail to resolve the ethical debate. The paper highlights the absence of randomized controlled trials evaluating seclusion’s efficacy or harm, citing a 2000 Cochrane review that found no such studies, and concludes that ethical arguments must be supplemented with empirical evidence before policy changes can be justified. The authors advocate for continuing seclusion only within rigorously designed research frameworks, such as cluster-randomized trials, to determine whether it offers net benefit compared to alternatives like forced medication.

## Key Entities
- **Zwaluwen Poort Institute for Mental Health West-Overijssel** (org): Affiliation of the first author; provides the Dutch clinical context for the analysis of coercive practices in psychiatric inpatient care.
- **Julius Center for Health Sciences and Primary Care, University Medical Center Utrecht** (org): Affiliation of the second author; contributes methodological and ethical expertise, strengthening the paper’s critique of evidence gaps.
- **Cochrane Library** (tool/database): Referenced for its 2000 systematic review concluding no controlled studies exist on the value of seclusion or restraint in serious mental illness; central to the argument about lack of evidence.
- **Council of Europe** (org): Cited regarding its stance on restraint and the right to treatment in the least restrictive environment; used to contextualize legal and ethical standards in European mental health care.
- **Citizens Commission on Human Rights** (org): Referenced for publishing a report on harms associated with restraint and seclusion; used to support claims about adverse psychological and physical effects.
- **Seclusion and restraint** (tool/method): The clinical interventions under review; defined as involuntary isolation or physical/mechanical restraint used to manage acute behavioral disturbance; central to the ethical and empirical analysis.

## Key Concepts
- **Autonomy** (principle): The capacity for self-determination; discussed as multidimensional, including relational autonomy, challenging the assumption that it uniformly opposes coercion.
- **Human dignity** (principle): The inherent worth of individuals; analyzed as having both universal (inherent) and context-dependent (individualistic) dimensions, allowing conflicting interpretations in seclusion debates.
- **Beneficence/non-maleficence** (principles): Ethical duties to do good and avoid harm; framed as in tension with autonomy, but undermined as a justification for seclusion due to lack of proven benefit.
- **Relational autonomy** (theory): The idea that autonomy develops through relationships and social context; used to argue that coercion might, in some cases, restore rather than destroy autonomy.
- **Paternalism** (ethical approach): Overriding a person’s choices for their perceived benefit; critiqued as unjustifiable without evidence that seclusion confers such benefit.
- **Precautionary principle / primum non nocere** (methodological/ethical guideline): The obligation to avoid harm when evidence is lacking; applied to conclude seclusion should not be used outside research until proven beneficial.
- **Controlled trial / evidence-based medicine** (methodological standard): The gold standard for assessing intervention efficacy; its absence in seclusion research is presented as a critical flaw in current practice.
- **Least restrictive environment** (legal/clinical standard): The principle that interventions should limit freedom as little as possible; cited from Council of Europe to frame seclusion as a measure of last resort, though its application remains unclear without comparative data.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Respect for autonomy and human dignity alone cannot justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry, as these principles are interpretable in ways that both support and oppose seclusion.
  - **Evidence**: Philosophical distinction between individualistic and relational autonomy; inherent versus individualistic dignity; clinical observations of mixed patient experiences (some report benefit, most report harm, but only 22% support full elimination); reference to Cochrane review and absence of RCTs on seclusion.
  - **Strength**: The ethical analysis is conceptually robust and highlights important nuances in moral reasoning. However, the empirical foundation is weak due to the near-total absence of controlled trials, which the authors repeatedly emphasize as a decisive gap. The argument’s strength lies in showing that ethical debate cannot be resolved without data on outcomes.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The paper tensions with abolitionist positions that frame seclusion as an inherent violation of autonomy and dignity, showing these values can be interpreted to permit or even require coercion in certain contexts.
- It creates an internal tension between the precautionary principle (avoid harm due to unknown risks) and clinical necessity (need to manage imminent danger), ultimately resolving in favor of research-based use rather than blanket permission or prohibition.
- If the wiki currently treats seclusion as ethically indefensible based on autonomy or dignity violations, this paper complicates that view by demonstrating the interpretive flexibility of these principles.

## Open Questions
- Under what specific conditions, if any, might seclusion or restraint be justified compared to alternatives such as forced medication or de-escalation techniques?
- What would a rigorously designed ethical and clinical trial (e.g., cluster-randomized) comparing seclusion, forced medication, and non-coercive interventions look like, and what outcomes should it measure?
