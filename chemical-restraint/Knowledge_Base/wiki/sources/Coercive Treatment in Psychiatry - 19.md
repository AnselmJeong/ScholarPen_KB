# Coercion – point, perception, process

## Summary
This study examines the experience of coercion among individuals with serious mental illness under New York's Kendra's Law, which mandates assisted outpatient treatment (AOT). Researchers compared perceived coercion between those court-ordered into AOT and those receiving voluntary outpatient treatment (OPT), using the Perceived Coercion Scale (PCS) alongside qualitative interviews. Contrary to expectations, no significant difference in perceived coercion was found between groups. The PCS demonstrated strong reliability and validity, correlating with poor insight, prior involuntary hospitalizations, stigma, poor therapeutic alliance, and low quality of life. Qualitative analysis revealed three key themes: the ubiquity of prior coercive experiences making court orders feel incremental, a tendency to choose compliance over confrontation, and the valuing of services like housing and case management that can reframe coercion as supportive. The findings suggest that subjective coercion is shaped more by relational context and service access than by legal status alone.

## Key Concepts
- **Perceived coercion** — Subjective experience of feeling forced or pressured into treatment; central outcome measured by the PCS.
- **Objective coercion** — External, observable events like court orders or involuntary hospitalization; contrasted with perceived experience.
- **Treatment adherence** — Extent to which patients follow prescribed treatment; underlying rationale for AOT policies.
- **Therapeutic alliance / Working alliance** — Collaborative relationship between patient and provider; found to be negatively associated with perceived coercion.
- **Insight** — Awareness of having a mental illness and need for treatment; low insight linked to higher perceived coercion.
- **Stigma (mental illness)** — Perceived devaluation/discrimination; positively associated with perceived coercion.
- **Quality of life** — Multi-domain life satisfaction; negatively associated with perceived coercion.
- **Leverage in treatment** — Use of incentives (housing, money, legal status) to encourage adherence; reframes coercion as instrumental support.
- **Culture of coercion** — Normalization of coercive experiences in mental health system; explains why court orders add little perceptible difference.
- **Benevolent manipulation / Compliance over confrontation** — Framing coercion as acceptable when tied to valued services; highlights procedural justice and trust.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Despite objective coercion (court order under Kendra’s Law), patients under AOT do not report significantly higher perceived coercion than those receiving voluntary outpatient treatment.
  - **Evidence**: Quantitative analysis showed no significant difference in PCS scores between AOT (mean 1.44) and OPT (mean 1.33) groups (p > 0.05). The PCS demonstrated strong internal consistency (α = 0.86) and validity, correlating as expected with poor insight (r = -0.236), prior involuntary hospitalizations (r = 0.331), stigma (r = 0.260), poor working alliance (r = -0.339), and low quality of life (r = -0.223). Qualitative interviews revealed themes of coercion ubiquity, compliance over confrontation, and valued services reducing negative experience.
  - **Strength**: Moderate for quantitative null result (well-powered but limits causal inference); strong for qualitative explanation (purposive sampling, triangulation, rich context). Overall, findings are credible and shift focus from legal status to relational and contextual factors in coercion experience.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The null finding contradicts the common assumption that court orders inherently increase feelings of coercion, instead aligning with literature suggesting coercion is experienced relationally and contextually.
- While the PCS is valid and reliable, it fails to detect group differences—suggesting either the scale misses key aspects of coercion or lived experience is so pervasive that legal adds no incremental burden.
- Findings may not generalize to first-episode or less-exposed populations due to sample characteristics (high baseline trauma, chronic illness).

## Open Questions
- Does the null finding persist in first-episode psychosis or populations with fewer prior coercive experiences?
- How do cultural factors influence the experience of coercion under AOT across different demographic groups?
- Can interventions reduce perceived coercion by enhancing case manager empathy, shared decision-making, and procedural justice, even under court order?
