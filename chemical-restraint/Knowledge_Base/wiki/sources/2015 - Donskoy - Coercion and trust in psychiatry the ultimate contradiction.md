# Coercion and trust in psychiatry: the ultimate contradiction

## Summary
This paper presents a survivor-centered critique of coercion in psychiatry, arguing that forced treatment is fundamentally incompatible with trust, genuine care, and human rights. Drawing on philosophical, legal, and empirical evidence, the author contends that coercion violates autonomy and dignity, undermines the therapeutic alliance, and contradicts international human rights standards such as the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD). The analysis critiques paternalism as an ethically flawed justification for overriding patient consent and highlights systemic abuses documented in psychiatric settings worldwide. The paper emphasizes survivor knowledge as a valid epistemology and calls for a paradigm shift toward supported decision-making and non-coercive crisis alternatives.

## Key Concepts
- **Coercion in psychiatry** — Refers to forced psychiatric treatment or admission administered despite a person’s objection, central to the paper’s argument as incompatible with trust and human rights.
- **Therapeutic alliance** — The collaborative, trust-based relationship between clinician and patient; presented as undermined by coercion and essential for genuine care.
- **Paternalism** — The justification for overriding autonomy in the name of the patient’s “best interest”; critiqued as ethically invalid when applied to competent individuals and as a core driver of coercive practices.
- **Legal capacity** — The right to make decisions about one’s life, including medical treatment; affirmed by the UN CRPD as non-derogable, even in crisis, challenging mental health laws that permit override.
- **Supported decision-making** — An alternative to substituted decision-making that provides assistance while preserving autonomy; implied as a CRPD-consistent approach contrasted with coercion.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion in psychiatry is fundamentally incompatible with trust, genuine care, and human rights, making it an "ultimate contradiction."
  - **Evidence**: Philosophical critique from Stuart Mill shows paternalism cannot justify overriding autonomy for the individual’s own good; empirical examples (e.g., Japan cage bed death, Czech Republic, UK staff abuse, France political incarceration) demonstrate systemic harm; critique of research notes over-reliance on quantitative methods and misinterpretation of post-coercion gratitude; reference to UN CRPD General Comment No. 1 (2014) affirms legal capacity cannot be denied, rendering forced treatment incompatible with international law.
  - **Strength**: Strong theoretical and normative grounding; moderate empirical support based on documented cases and secondary literature. The author’s survivor perspective adds experiential weight but may be viewed as anecdotal by some. Critique of research methodology is well-supported by citations.
