# Reducing Involuntary Admissions

## Summary
This chapter examines involuntary admission in mental health settings through legal, ethical, clinical, and experiential lenses. It analyzes how the practice persists despite widespread ratification of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which many interpret as prohibiting deprivation of liberty based on disability. The authors review global variation in admission rates, pathways to detention, and subjective experiences of patients, staff, and caregivers, highlighting tensions between public safety, clinical judgment, and human rights. The chapter evaluates reform efforts, particularly psychiatric advance directives (PADs), joint crisis plans (JCPs), and supported decision-making, presenting empirical evidence that these approaches can reduce reliance on coercion while maintaining safety and treatment engagement.

## Key Entities
- **United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (org) — central framework used to critique involuntary admission as discriminatory and a violation of legal equality
- **United Nations Human Rights Committee (UNHRC)** (org) — provides alternative interpretation allowing for proportionate and necessary deprivation of liberty under strict safeguards
- **World Health Organization (WHO)** (org) — developer of the QualityRights Initiative to align mental health law and practice with CRPD standards
- **Psychiatric Advance Directives (PADs)** (tool) — legal documents enabling individuals to state future treatment preferences during periods of competence, shown to reduce involuntary admissions
- **Joint Crisis Plans (JCPs)** (tool) — collaborative plans between patients and clinicians to guide crisis response, associated with reduced compulsory admissions through increased voluntariness

## Key Concepts
- **Involuntary admission** — detention in a mental health facility against a person’s will; central to the chapter’s analysis of coercion, rights violations, and reform efforts
- **Coercive shadow** — phenomenon where patients feel compelled to accept voluntary treatment due to fear of coercion if they refuse; challenges the binary view of voluntariness
- **Supported decision-making** — process where individuals receive assistance to make their own decisions without others deciding for them; positioned as CRPD-compliant alternative to substituted decision-making
- **Substituted decision-making** — decisions made by others (e.g., family, clinicians, courts) based on perceived best interests; critiqued by CRPD as violating autonomy and legal capacity
- **Functional approach to capacity** — assesses decision-making ability based on context-specific functional abilities; rejected by CRPD as discriminatory when applied to people with psychosocial disabilities
- **Psychiatric advance directives (PADs)** — advance statements of treatment preferences; shown to reduce involuntary admissions when supported by peer workers
- **Joint crisis plans (JCPs)** — collaborative crisis planning tools; associated with shifting admissions from involuntary to voluntary without reducing overall admissions
- **Fusion Law** — proposed legal framework applying capacity-based detention to all people regardless of disability origin; criticized by CRPD as still violating rights due to disability-based detention
- **Proportionality reasoning** — framework evaluating whether an intervention has legitimate aim, contributes to aim, is necessary, and balances reasons for/against; proposed as potential middle ground for justifying some interventions under CRPD
- **QualityRights Initiative** — WHO program to build capacity, support community services, and reform law/policy in line with CRPD; presented as practical effort to reduce coercion

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Involuntary admission remains widespread despite CRPD ratification due to tensions between public safety, clinical judgment, and human rights; however, alternatives like advance directives, joint crisis plans, and supported decision-making show promise in reducing reliance on coercion.
  - **Evidence**: International data show wide variation in admission rates (e.g., Italy: 14.5/100,000; Austria: 282/100,000); qualitative accounts emphasize loss of dignity and powerlessness, mitigated by information and familiar staff; RCTs show PADs with peer support reduced involuntary admissions by 32%; meta-analysis shows JCPs reduced involuntary admissions by 25% by shifting toward voluntary care.
  - **Strength**: Moderate to strong for specific interventions (PADs, JCPs); weaker for systemic reform due to entrenched legal frameworks, inconsistent CRPD interpretation, and lack of sustained policy change.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The chapter acknowledges that involuntary admission can be experienced as therapeutic or protective (e.g., providing "sanctuary") by some patients, yet frames it predominantly as harmful and rights-violating, creating tension between subjective experience and objective human rights violations.
- There is a fundamental tension between abolitionist interpretations of CRPD Article 14 (which prohibit any deprivation of liberty based on disability) and consequentialist views (e.g., UNHRC) that allow for necessary and proportionate interventions with safeguards.
- If the wiki assumes mental health systems generally comply with human rights standards, this source contradicts that by showing widespread non-compliance with CRPD despite near-universal ratification.
- **(note: internal caveat)** Supported decision-making is not immune to critique, as supporters may exert undue influence, and the model depends on accessible, trusted support networks that may not be available to all.

## Open Questions
- Can involuntary admission ever be compatible with the CRPD, or must it be abolished to comply with Article 14’s prohibition on disability-based deprivation of liberty?
- How can supported decision-making models be safeguarded against undue influence from family, clinicians, or appointed supporters?
- Why do reforms introducing alternatives like PADs and JCPs often fail to sustain reductions in involuntary admission rates over time, even when initially effective?
