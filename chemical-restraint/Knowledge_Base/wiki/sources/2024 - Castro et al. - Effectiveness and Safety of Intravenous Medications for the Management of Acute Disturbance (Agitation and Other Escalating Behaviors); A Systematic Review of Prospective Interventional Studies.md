# Effectiveness and Safety of Intravenous Medications for the Management of Acute Disturbance (Agitation and Other Escalating Behaviors); A Systematic Review of Prospective Interventional Studies

## Summary
This systematic review evaluated the effectiveness and safety of intravenous (IV) medications for managing acute disturbance—defined as escalating behaviors such as agitation, aggression, and violence—based on prospective interventional studies. The review included 15 studies across emergency departments (10), intensive care units (2), palliative care (2), pre-hospital (1), and psychiatric inpatient (1) settings. Data were synthesized narratively due to significant heterogeneity in outcome measures across studies. Findings indicated mixed but positive effectiveness for IV dexmedetomidine, lorazepam, droperidol, and olanzapine, with more limited evidence for haloperidol, ketamine, midazolam, and valproate. While most studies reported favorable adverse event profiles, the authors noted these were likely underpowered to detect rare serious events. Evidence quality varied, with GRADE ratings ranging from high (1 study) to very low (10 studies), underscoring the need for larger, standardized trials.

## Key Concepts
- **Acute disturbance** — a broad term referring to escalating behaviors secondary to a change in mental state, such as agitation, aggression, and violence; central focus of the review
- **Intravenous (IV) medication** — administration of medication via the intravenous route, noted for rapid onset and complete bioavailability; primary intervention under review
- **Narrative synthesis** — method of data analysis used due to significant variation in primary outcome measures across studies, precluding statistical pooling; explains why results are descriptive rather than quantitative
- **Effectiveness** — measure of how well IV medications achieve sedation or reduce agitation/aggression in acute disturbance; core outcome evaluated
- **Safety** — assessment of adverse events associated with IV medication use, including respiratory depression, hypotension, QTc prolongation, etc.; critical for risk-benefit analysis
- **Heterogeneity** — variability in study designs, populations, settings, outcome measures, and medications across the included studies; major limitation preventing meta-analysis and reducing confidence in pooled estimates
- **General hospital settings** — clinical environments (e.g., emergency departments, ICUs, palliative care) where IV medication use may be more feasible than in psychiatric units due to better access to equipment and monitoring; suggested as a more suitable context for IV use

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: IV medications (particularly dexmedetomidine, lorazepam, droperidol, and olanzapine) show mixed but positive effectiveness for managing acute disturbance, with generally favorable safety profiles, though evidence is limited by low/mixed quality and heterogeneity.
  - **Evidence**:
    - Droperidol: significantly outperformed placebo in sedation and reduction of additional sedative needs; average sedation time of 8 minutes; minimal side effects.
    - Olanzapine: shorter time to adequate sedation vs. droperidol in one study; otherwise equivalent; favorable safety, no QTc concerns.
    - Lorazepam: effective as adjunct to haloperidol in palliative care; inferior to droperidol when used alone.
    - Midazolam: median time to sedation 6.5 minutes; 55–57% adequately sedated at 10 minutes; notable for airway management needs.
    - Dexmedetomidine: high rates of adequate sedation in refractory delirium; treatable cardiac AEs noted but confounded by pretreatment.
    - Ketamine, haloperidol, valproate: limited data; valproate showed efficacy but 30% AE rate.
    - Most studies reported favorable AE profiles, but likely underpowered to detect rare serious events.
    - Quality assessment: NIH checklist — 7 good, 2 fair, 6 poor; GRADE — 1 high, 4 moderate, 10 very low.
  - **Strength**: Low to moderate due to methodological heterogeneity, variable outcome measures, small sample sizes, and lack of comparative effectiveness studies. Findings are suggestive but not conclusive; highlight need for standardized, large-scale, multi-arm RCTs.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension**: While IV route offers rapid onset and complete bioavailability, its use in psychiatric units may be limited by lack of equipment and trained staff — creating a gap between pharmacological potential and practical feasibility.
- **Internal caveat**: Positive safety findings may be misleading due to underpowered studies; rare but serious adverse events (e.g., arrhythmia, respiratory depression) may be undetected.
- **Contradiction with existing views**: Historical concerns about droperidol and QTc prolongation are not supported by the included studies, which found no significant cardiac issues — contrasting with legacy warnings.
- **Conceptual tension**: The review treats "acute disturbance" as a unitary construct, but includes heterogeneous presentations (agitation, aggression, violence, delirium), which may limit clinical applicability of findings.

## Open Questions
- How does IV medication use impact downstream outcomes like length of hospitalization, need for restraint, or discharge disposition?
- Are there specific subpopulations (e.g., delirium vs. psychosis vs. intoxication) for whom IV route is particularly beneficial or risky?
- What are the cost-effectiveness implications of IV vs. IM/oral routes when accounting for monitoring requirements?
