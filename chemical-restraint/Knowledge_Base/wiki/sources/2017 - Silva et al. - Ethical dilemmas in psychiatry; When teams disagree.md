# Ethical dilemmas in psychiatry; When teams disagree

## Summary
This paper examines a case involving a patient with treatment-resistant schizophrenia who refused oral medication, leading to a proposal for nasogastric clozapine administration under physical restraint. Despite legal authorization under the Mental Health Act 1983 and ethical justification via weak paternalism and the doctrine of double effect, the clinical team experienced significant internal disagreement rooted in emotional responses such as fear, disgust, and moral conviction. The authors argue that ethical decision-making in such contexts is not primarily driven by rational deliberation but by intuitive, emotion-laden judgments, as described by Haidt’s social intuitionist model. Resolution emerged not through new evidence but through reflective practice, shared deliberation, and social persuasion—particularly when senior figures reframed inaction as harmful. Over time, repeated exposure and team reflection reduced initial resistance, making similar interventions less contentious. The case underscores that ethical legitimacy does not eliminate moral distress and that effective ethical reasoning in teams requires attending to group dynamics, emotional awareness, and value pluralism.

## Key Concepts
- **Weak paternalism** — Justifying override of a patient’s wishes when they lack decision-making capacity, based on best interests; legally permitted under UK mental health and capacity legislation.
- **Principle-based ethics** — An ethical framework relying on principles like beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, and justice; shown to be insufficient alone in resolving team disagreement due to conflicting applications.
- **Consequentialism** — Judging actions by their outcomes; used to argue that inaction (not treating) carries moral weight equivalent to action, as it risks death or deterioration.
- **Virtue-based ethics** — Focusing on the character and intentions of actors (e.g., compassion, courage); aligned with NHS “6Cs” and used to justify intervention despite foreseeable harm via double effect reasoning.
- **Double effect** — The principle that an action with both good and bad effects may be permissible if the intention is good, the bad effect is not a means, and proportionality applies; invoked to justify distress during restraint as a foreseen but unintended consequence.
- **Moral psychology** — The study of how psychological processes shape moral judgment; central to explaining why teams disagreed despite ethical justification.
- **Social intuitionist model** — The theory that moral judgments are rapid, intuitive responses driven by emotion, with reasoning serving post hoc justification; used to explain the team’s initial resistance.
- **Dual process theory of moral reasoning** — Distinguishes fast, emotion-driven intuition from slow, deliberate reasoning; supported by neuroscience showing different brain activation patterns.
- **Reflective practice** — A structured process for individuals or teams to examine experiences, assumptions, and biases; presented as key to resolving ethical conflict by increasing self-awareness.
- **Moral conviction** — A strong, inflexible belief that something is morally right or wrong; explained persistent dissent among some staff who viewed the intervention as “always wrong.”
- **Value pluralism** — The idea that multiple ethically defensible positions can exist in a situation; highlights the need to explore differing views rather than force false consensus.
- **Social persuasion** — The influence of one person’s judgment on another’s intuition via communication; demonstrated when the SOAD told anxious staff they should worry more about inaction than action.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Ethical decision-making in mental health teams is shaped more by emotions, intuitions, and social dynamics than by rational principles, and resolving disagreement requires addressing these non-rational influences through reflection and dialogue.
  - **Evidence**: The team’s emotional reactions (fear, disgust, moral conviction) persisted despite legal authorization and ethical justification; shifts in attitude occurred via reflective practice and social persuasion, not new data; neuroscience supports that intuitive and reasoned judgments involve different neural systems.
  - **Strength**: Strongly supported by detailed case description and alignment with established moral psychology literature; limited by single-case design, though mechanisms are plausible and consistent with broader research on healthcare ethics and group dynamics.
- **Core claim**: Inaction can be morally significant, and failing to treat a deteriorating patient may be as ethically problematic as causing harm through intervention.
  - **Evidence**: The team initially viewed action as riskier due to omission bias, but were persuaded by reframing inaction as likely to lead to death or severe deterioration; consequentialist reasoning was activated through discussion of outcomes.
  - **Strength**: Well-supported by clinical context and ethical analysis; challenges common intuition that “doing nothing” is neutral or safer.
- **Core claim**: Value pluralism means ethical disagreement may reflect genuine differences in moral weighting, not just error or bias, and teams should explore these differences rather than seek artificial consensus.
  - **Evidence**: Reference to Fulford (2008) on values-based practice; acknowledgment that some resistance stemmed from deeply held views about dignity and bodily integrity.
  - **Strength**: Moderately supported; conceptually sound but not empirically measured in this case.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension between legal/ethical justification and lived experience**: The intervention was legally authorized and ethically defensible, yet many staff experienced it as morally wrong—highlighting a gap between formal ethics and moral intuition.
- **Conflict between beneficence and non-maleficence**: The duty to improve the patient’s condition conflicted with the duty to avoid causing distress through restraint, with no clear hierarchy to resolve it.
- **Team autonomy vs. consensus**: While consensus was pursued, the paper acknowledges that some disagreement may reflect valid value pluralism, suggesting forced agreement can suppress legitimate ethical perspectives.
- **Inaction as morally significant**: The paper argues that omissions (not treating) can be as harmful as commissions, challenging intuitions that favor inaction as safer or more ethical.

## Open Questions
- How can institutions structurally support reflective practice in clinical teams without it becoming performative or superficial?
- What training helps clinicians distinguish between moral discomfort arising from genuine ethical concern versus bias (e.g., stigma, disgust, or fear of litigation)?
- Are there thresholds where a clinician’s moral conviction should override a legally authorized treatment plan, particularly in cases involving coercive interventions?
- How does repeated exposure to ethically challenging interventions affect long-term clinician well-being, moral resilience, and susceptibility to burnout or desensitization?
