## Main Arguments & Findings
- The four principles of biomedical ethics (autonomy, nonmaleficence, beneficence, justice) apply to solitary confinement, revealing ethical violations.
- Autonomy is severely restricted in solitary confinement due to lack of liberty and agency, yet prisoners retain capacity for meaningful choices (e.g., in research).
- Solitary confinement causes harm (violating nonmaleficence) through sensory deprivation, idleness, and mental health deterioration.
- Beneficence requires intervening to prevent harm (e.g., self-harm) rather than permitting assisted suicide; palliative care is preferred.
- Justice demands addressing racial inequities in incarceration and treating solitary confinement as unjust due to its disproportionate impact on Black individuals.
