# How to de-escalate a risk situation to avoid the use of coercion

## Summary
This chapter examines how de-escalation strategies can prevent aggression and reduce the need for coercive interventions in psychiatric settings. It draws on empirical research from nursing studies, organizational policies, and interpersonal conflict resolution fields such as crisis intervention, hostage negotiation, and business negotiation to outline a practical framework for managing volatile situations. The author emphasizes that most violent incidents in mental health care follow escalating interactions, making early intervention through nonphysical means critical. De-escalation is presented as a process involving awareness, emotional attunement, communication, and problem-solving, grounded in the understanding that both staff and environmental factors contribute to escalation. While evidence remains limited and largely adapted from other fields, the chapter argues that structured approaches — including risk assessment, rapport-building, active listening, and exploring alternatives — offer a viable path toward minimizing coercion by addressing root causes rather than reacting to violence after it occurs.

## Key Concepts
- **De-escalation** (Method/Technique): A process aimed at reducing tension and preventing violence through nonphysical means, particularly by managing emotions, building rapport, and addressing unmet needs. It is positioned as a preventive alternative to coercion in mental health care.
- **Escalation** (Phenomenon): The progressive increase in conflict intensity through reciprocal actions, where each party’s response raises the level of force or aggression. The chapter identifies it as a precursor to most coercive interventions.
- **Emotional Contagion** (Psychological Phenomenon): The tendency for emotions to spread between individuals, influencing group dynamics. In de-escalation, staff regulation of their own emotional state can help calm the patient through this mechanism.
- **Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA)** (Concept from Negotiation Theory): The minimum acceptable outcome if negotiations fail, used to guide decision-making during conflict resolution. It helps staff prepare realistic alternatives without escalating tension.
- **Active Listening** (Communication Technique): A practice of fully attending to, understanding, and responding to another’s communication, including verbal and nonverbal cues. It is highlighted as a core skill for building trust and reducing defensiveness in agitated individuals.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: De-escalation techniques grounded in emotional regulation, active listening, and relationship-building can prevent aggression and reduce reliance on coercive interventions by intervening early in the escalation process.
- **Evidence**: The chapter synthesizes qualitative research (e.g., Mary Johnson’s studies), observational data on ward dynamics, and insights from crisis intervention, hostage negotiation, and business negotiation. Organizational strategies such as staff training, structured risk assessment, and incident reporting are also cited as supportive measures.
- **Strength of evidence**: The evidence is described as promising but limited, with a lack of strong quantitative studies and psychiatry-specific theoretical frameworks. Much of the knowledge is adapted from other disciplines, suggesting plausible but indirect support. The author acknowledges gaps in empirical validation while maintaining that the logical alignment with human behavior and conflict dynamics justifies implementation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- A key tension exists between organizational priorities and therapeutic goals: while safety protocols (e.g., locked doors, mandatory treatments) are intended to protect staff and patients, they can be perceived by patients as coercive or threatening, thereby triggering the very aggression they aim to prevent.
- Another tension arises from the imbalance in training focus: despite evidence that prevention reduces coercion, many institutions have invested heavily in physical intervention training while neglecting systematic education in de-escalation techniques, reflecting a reactive rather than preventive approach to violence.
