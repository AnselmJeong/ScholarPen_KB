# Seclusion or Restraint

## Summary
This resource document from the American Psychiatric Association’s Patient Safety Work Group provides practical considerations for the use of seclusion and restraint in psychiatric care for adults in emergency and hospital settings. It synthesizes clinical guidelines, legal standards, and research evidence to emphasize that seclusion and restraint should be interventions of last resort, only used after less restrictive measures fail. The document highlights the disproportionate risks associated with physical restraint—such as deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and post-traumatic stress—compared to seclusion, and underscores the importance of patient preferences, psychiatric advance directives, trauma-informed care, cultural competency, de-escalation techniques, and environmental modifications in reducing reliance on these interventions. It also addresses systemic disparities in use linked to race, socioeconomic status, and homelessness, and calls for post-incident reviews to improve equity and safety.

## Key Entities
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (organization): The professional body that issued the resource document through its Patient Safety Work Group and Council on Quality Care to guide clinicians on seclusion and restraint practices.
- **Patient Safety Work Group (PSWG)** (organization): The APA subgroup responsible for developing this resource document, focusing on safety, ethics, and equity in psychiatric care.
- **Council on Quality Care** (organization): The APA body that oversees quality improvement initiatives and approved this document for clinical use.
- **Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)** (tool): A legal instrument allowing individuals to express treatment preferences in advance of a mental health crisis, highlighted as critical for respecting autonomy during seclusion or restraint decisions.
- **Joint Commission (TJC)** (organization): A healthcare accreditation body whose standards inform safety protocols around seclusion and restraint, particularly regarding monitoring and discontinuation timing.

## Key Concepts
- **Seclusion or Restraint (S/R)**: Involuntary confinement (seclusion) or physical/mechanical restriction (restraint) used as a last resort to manage severe agitation, carrying significant physical and psychological risks.
- **Psychiatric Advance Directive (PAD)**: A advance care planning tool enabling individuals to state preferences for mental health treatment, including seclusion vs. restraint, during periods of impaired decision-making.
- **Trauma-Informed Care**: An approach recognizing widespread trauma histories in psychiatric patients, emphasizing safety, trust, and empowerment to avoid re-traumatization during interventions.
- **Cultural Competency**: The ability to understand and respond effectively to cultural differences, essential for reducing racial and ethnic disparities in seclusion and restraint use.
- **De-escalation Techniques**: Verbal and behavioral strategies to reduce agitation without physical intervention, recommended as first-line treatment in managing behavioral crises.
- **Environmental Interventions**: Modifications to the clinical environment—such as quiet rooms, sensory tools, or reduced stimulation—to prevent escalation and minimize the need for seclusion or restraint.
- **Disparities in S/R Use**: Systemic biases leading to disproportionate use of seclusion and restraint among Black, male, non-Hispanic, uninsured, and homeless populations, driven by social determinants of health.
- **Risk of Harm**: The physical dangers (e.g., DVT, pulmonary embolism, asphyxiation) and psychological harms (e.g., PTSD, perceived coercion) associated with seclusion and restraint, particularly higher with restraint.
- **Least Restrictive Principle**: An ethical and legal standard requiring that less restrictive interventions be attempted before seclusion or restraint, and that any used be for the minimum necessary time.
- **Post-Incident Review (PIR)**: A structured review process following a seclusion or restraint event to assess effectiveness, safety, equity, and opportunities for improvement in care.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Seclusion or restraint should be used only as a last resort, guided by patient-centered, trauma-informed, and culturally competent principles, with strong consideration of patient preferences (e.g., via PADs), and prioritized avoidance of restraint due to higher risks.
  - **Evidence**: 
    - Clinical guidelines from CMS and TJC mandate less restrictive interventions first and require discontinuation at the earliest possible time.
    - Research shows physical restraint carries higher risks of deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, and injury, while seclusion is associated with lower physical harm but still psychological distress.
    - One systematic review found PTSD incidence rates of 25–47% after restraint events.
    - Patient preference consistently favors seclusion over restraint when informed and capable of choosing.
    - Disparities in S/R use are linked to systemic bias, with higher rates among Black, male, non-Hispanic, uninsured, and homeless individuals.
    - Environmental interventions and de-escalation techniques reduce the need for S/R when properly implemented.
  - **Strength**: Strong—based on consensus statements, systematic reviews, cohort studies, and clinical guidelines; limitations include underreporting and lack of national standardization, but the evidence supports clear clinical action.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- While the document frames seclusion and restraint as last-resort safety tools, it acknowledges they are often used due to systemic failures—such as inadequate staff training, poor environmental design, and implicit bias—revealing a gap between ethical intent and real-world practice.
- Some cited studies show no significant difference in adverse effects between seclusion and restraint, yet the document emphasizes higher risks with restraint, reflecting ongoing debate in the literature about comparative safety.
- The document states it is “not a guideline,” yet provides detailed, prescriptive recommendations (e.g., on PAD use, de-escalation, environmental design), which may create confusion about its authority and implementation expectations.
- Limited focus on pediatric, adolescent, or neurodevelopmental populations despite brief mentions; defers to other sources, leaving a gap in guidance for these vulnerable groups.
