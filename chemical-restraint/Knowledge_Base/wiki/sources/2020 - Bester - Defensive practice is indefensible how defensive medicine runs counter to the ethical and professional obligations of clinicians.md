# Defensive practice is indefensible: how defensive medicine runs counter to the ethical and professional obligations of clinicians

## Summary
This article examines defensive medicine through an ethical and professionalism lens, arguing that it is fundamentally incompatible with medical ethics regardless of motivations like fear of litigation. The author defines defensive medicine as clinical actions taken primarily to protect the clinician against litigation or adverse outcomes, rather than to benefit the patient, distinguishing it from prudent or evidence-based caution. Drawing on empirical data from surveys in the US, UK, Italy, and Israel, the paper notes widespread prevalence, such as 78% of UK doctors admitting to some form of defensive practice and 20.6% of US medical care deemed unnecessary due to malpractice fears. The core argument consists of five ethical critiques based on the Beauchamp and Childress principled framework: defensive medicine violates patient autonomy through deception and incomplete disclosure, breaches fiduciary duty by subordinating patient to physician interests, contravenes non-maleficence by exposing patients to harm without benefit, erodes trust in the medical profession, and violates justice by wasting healthcare resources. The author rejects potential justifications—such as protecting doctors to enable continued care or rare diagnostic benefits—as unlikely and ethically insufficient, emphasizing that any pragmatic benefit is outweighed by moral harm and may even increase litigation risk by undermining trust. The piece concludes that defensive medicine should be abandoned in favor of evidence-based, patient-centered practice, and calls for reforms in medical education, professional self-regulation, and clinical ethics consultation to address its ethical roots.

## Key Concepts
- **Defensive medicine** — clinical actions taken primarily to protect the clinician against litigation or adverse outcomes, rather than to benefit the patient
- **Positive defensive medicine** — ordering unnecessary tests or treatments (e.g., "covering all bases") to protect against litigation
- **Negative defensive medicine** — avoiding patients or clinical situations to reduce litigation risk (e.g., refusing to treat high-risk patients)
- **Fiduciary obligation** — the ethical duty of clinicians to prioritize patient interests over their own in the clinician-patient relationship
- **Patient autonomy** — the right of patients to make informed decisions about their care based on full disclosure of relevant information
- **Non-maleficence** — the duty to avoid harming patients; violated when defensive medicine exposes patients to risk without compensating benefit
- **Justice** — the obligation to distribute healthcare resources fairly; violated when defensive medicine wastes resources that could be used where benefit is possible
- **Trust in medical profession** — public and patient confidence in clinicians’ motives and competence; eroded when patients perceive clinicians as self-interested
- **Overuse of healthcare** — use of services where no benefit exists or harm exceeds benefit; distinguished from defensive medicine by the absence of self-protective intent
- **Underuse of healthcare** — failure to provide beneficial interventions; distinguished from defensive medicine when not motivated by litigation avoidance

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Defensive medicine is ethically indefensible and violates core medical ethical principles, regardless of motivations like fear of litigation.
  - **Evidence**: The author defines defensive medicine by intent (protecting clinician), cites prevalence data (e.g., 78% of UK doctors, 20.6% of US care deemed unnecessary due to malpractice fear), and presents five ethical arguments grounded in Beauchamp and Childress’ framework: (1) violates autonomy via deception/incomplete disclosure; (2) violates fiduciary duty by subordinating patient to physician interests; (3) violates non-maleficence by risking harm without benefit; (4) erodes trust in profession, potentially increasing litigation; (5) violates justice by wasting resources. Potential counterarguments (e.g., protecting doctors enables care, rare diagnostic benefits) are rejected as unlikely and ethically insufficient.
  - **Strength**: Strong conceptual and ethical argumentation; empirical support from surveys is suggestive but not causative. The logical coherence of the ethical critique is robust, though reliance on self-reported survey data introduces limitations. The claim that defensive medicine may increase litigation risk is plausible but not definitively proven.
