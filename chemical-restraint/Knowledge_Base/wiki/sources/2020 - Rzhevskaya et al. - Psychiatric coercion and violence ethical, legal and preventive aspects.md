# Psychiatric coercion and violence: ethical, legal and preventive aspects

## Summary
This review article analyzes the ethical, legal, and preventive aspects of coercion and violence in psychiatric hospitals based on a literature review of 832 articles from the past five years across major databases including PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and others. The study examines how psychiatric patients are subjected to various forms of coercion and violence by medical staff, impacting their rights, treatment effectiveness, and therapeutic alliance. Key findings indicate that involuntary admission rates range from 3-38% globally (up to 44.4 per 100,000 in Russia), with coercive treatment affecting 27.6% of psychiatric hospital patients and physical restraint used in 10-30% of emergency cases. The authors argue that insufficient legal frameworks, ambiguous clinical criteria, and disregard for bioethical principles enable systemic abuses, while advocating for a bioethical (contract) approach, psychoeducation, and psychosocial rehabilitation to improve patient compliance and therapeutic relationships.

## Key Concepts
- Psychiatric coercion: Defined as external mental, physical, material, or organizational impact on a person’s consciousness, will, and behavior within psychiatric settings.
- Psychiatric violence: Behavior intended to cause harm to physical or mental health, well-being, or to insult/humiliate, including cruel treatment, abuse, and restriction of legal rights.
- Involuntary admission: Hospitalization without patient consent, occurring in 3-38% of psychiatric hospitalizations globally, with higher rates documented in specific regions like Russia.
- Physical restraint: Measures such as immobilization or isolation applied during agitation, used in 10-30% of emergency psychiatric treatments and frequently associated with rights violations.
- Bioethical (contract) approach: A model emphasizing mutual agreement, patient autonomy, and shared decision-making in psychiatric care, presented as an alternative to paternalistic models.
- Psychosocial rehabilitation: A process aiming for consensus between psychiatrist and patient, destigmatization, and responsible illness management, recommended to improve compliance and therapeutic alliance.
- Therapeutic alliance: The collaborative relationship between patient and provider, which is undermined by coercive and violent practices in psychiatric care.
- Patient compliance: Adherence to treatment regimens, significantly reduced by coercive interventions, leading to treatment abandonment and symptom exacerbation.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- Core claim: Psychiatric patients experience widespread coercion and violence in hospitals due to inadequate legal frameworks, unclear clinical criteria for intervention, and systemic neglect of bioethical principles, resulting in violations of bodily integrity, freedom of movement, privacy, and deteriorated therapeutic outcomes.
  - Evidence: The review synthesizes data from 832 recent articles, citing global involuntary admission rates (3-38%), Russian-specific rates (44.4 per 100,000), coercive treatment prevalence (27.6%), physical restraint use (10-30% of emergencies), and patient-reported violations such as denial of medical records, forced labor, and restricted communication.
  - Strength: The evidence is moderately strong, drawing on international comparative data and mechanistic links between regulatory gaps and abuse. Limitations include reliance on secondary synthesis without new primary data and potential bias from self-reported patient experiences, which may not fully capture institutional perspectives or contextual nuances.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The paper acknowledges that some coercion may be clinically necessary (e.g., for managing acute psychomotor agitation) but argues it is often excessive and applied without sufficient justification, creating an unresolved tension between safety imperatives and rights protection.
- If the wiki assumes psychiatric care inherently prioritizes patient welfare and rights, this source directly challenges that assumption by documenting systemic patterns of rights violations and abusive practices in psychiatric hospitals across multiple jurisdictions.
- High rates of involuntary admission in certain regions may reflect legitimate public safety or clinical necessities rather than mere misuse, a nuance noted in the text but not deeply explored, leaving room for debate about appropriate thresholds for intervention.

## Open Questions
- How can legal and clinical frameworks be designed to effectively distinguish between necessary coercion for safety and excessive or punitive applications in psychiatric emergencies?
- What specific legal models or policy interventions have demonstrated success in reducing coercive practices without increasing risks to patients or others in psychiatric settings?
- How generalizable are the findings from this Russian-European-focused review to psychiatric systems in other global regions with different legal, cultural, and healthcare infrastructures?
- What are the most effective strategies for implementing bioethical or contractual approaches to psychiatric care in resource-constrained or high-coercion environments?
- How does the intersection of stigma, diagnostic labeling, and institutional power dynamics contribute to the persistence of coercive practices despite ethical guidelines and patient rights protections?
