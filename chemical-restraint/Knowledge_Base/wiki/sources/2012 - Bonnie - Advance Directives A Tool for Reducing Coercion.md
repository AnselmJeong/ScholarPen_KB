# Advance Directives: A Tool for Reducing Coercion

## Summary
This 2012 article by Richard J. Bonnie proposes psychiatric advance directives (PADs) as a strategic tool to reduce coercion in mental health care by enabling individuals to document treatment preferences in advance of decision-making incapacity. The author argues that widespread use of advance directives can shift psychiatric care from a model of coercion to one of contractual agreement, particularly when integrated into routine care and supported by systemic initiatives. The article highlights Virginia’s statewide effort to promote advance directive completion through collaboration among hospitals, community agencies, legal professionals, and consumer advocacy groups, supported by a dedicated informational website. Bonnie contends that as advance directive use becomes normalized, coercive interventions will be needed less frequently and, when used, will align with prior consent, reducing their perceived coerciveness. He further suggests that the proportion of individuals with executed advance directives could serve as a measurable indicator of progress toward a recovery-oriented mental health system.

## Key Concepts
- **Advance directives** — Legal documents allowing individuals to outline future healthcare preferences, including mental health treatment, to be followed if decision-making capacity is impaired; presented as a mechanism to extend informed consent into future crises.
- **Coercion** — The use of force or pressure to compel treatment against a person’s will, often in psychiatric contexts for safety reasons; the central problem the article seeks to mitigate through advance planning.
- **Recovery-oriented care** — A treatment model emphasizing patient autonomy, hope, self-direction, and collaborative care planning; positioned as the philosophical foundation for why advance directives reduce coercion by aligning care with prior wishes.
- **Informed consent** — The process of granting permission after understanding risks and benefits; extended via advance directives to allow individuals to consent to or refuse future treatments while competent.
- **Leveraged community treatment** — Agreements where community-based support is contingent on treatment adherence, mentioned as part of a broader shift “from coercion to contract” in mental health service models.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Advance directives are a promising tool for reducing coercion in mental health care by enabling individuals to express treatment preferences in advance, thereby aligning future care with prior consent and supporting a recovery-oriented system.
  - **Evidence**: Describes Virginia’s statewide initiative involving hospitals, community agencies, legal professionals, and advocacy groups, along with the provision of online resources via www.virginiaadvancedirectives.org. Argues that normalized use of advance directives will reduce the need for coercion and make necessary coercive actions more consistent with patient wishes.
  - **Strength**: The argument is conceptually strong and ethically coherent, grounded in principles of autonomy and recovery. However, it relies on logical extrapolation and descriptive examples rather than empirical data (e.g., no metrics on directive completion rates or actual reductions in coercion are provided), limiting its evidential strength.
- **Additional finding**: The proportion of individuals with executed advance directives could serve as a performance metric for measuring progress toward a recovery-oriented mental health system, offering a potential tool for system evaluation and accountability.
