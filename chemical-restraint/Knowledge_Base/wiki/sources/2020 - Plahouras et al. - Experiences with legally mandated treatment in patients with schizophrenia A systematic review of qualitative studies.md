# Experiences with legally mandated treatment in patients with schizophrenia: A systematic review of qualitative studies

## Summary
This systematic review synthesized qualitative data from 18 studies involving 401 patients with schizophrenia or related disorders across 10 countries to explore experiences with legally mandated treatment. The review found that patient satisfaction was closely tied to perceived respect for autonomy, with positive experiences including regained independence and recognition of treatment benefits such as symptom control and safety. Negative experiences centered on restrictions to freedom, privacy, and lifestyle, often likened to being jailed, and were exacerbated by poor communication, lack of information, and feeling excluded from decision-making. While many patients retrospectively acknowledged treatment as beneficial—particularly after stabilization—this did not negate contemporaneous feelings of coercion or distress. The findings highlight that ethical legitimacy of legally mandated treatment depends not just on clinical outcomes but on how it is implemented, especially regarding communication, autonomy support, and therapeutic relationships.

## Key Entities
- **Community Treatment Orders (CTO)** (tool/intervention) — A form of legally mandated treatment studied in multiple included papers (e.g., Gibbs et al., Stroud et al.), allowing patients to live in the community under conditions; associated with patient reports of gradual independence and responsibility when autonomy was respected.
- **Involuntary Admission** (tool/intervention) — The most frequently examined form of legally mandated treatment across studies; patients described it as restrictive and coercive but sometimes beneficial for safety and symptom control.
- **Outpatient Commitment** (tool/intervention) — Studied in the Norwegian context (Riley et al., 2014); similar to CTOs, involving mandated treatment while living in the community.
- **Guardianship Board** (tool/intervention) — Referenced in Australian studies (Adams & Hafner, 1991) as a legal mechanism for supervised discharge and treatment oversight; patients experienced it as infringing on rights and privacy.
- **European Psychiatric Association** (organization) — Publisher of the journal in which the review appeared; provides contextual credibility but did not directly contribute to the research.

## Key Concepts
- **Patient autonomy** — The right to make informed decisions about one’s care without undue influence; central to patient satisfaction, with violations (e.g., feeling controlled) causing distress and affirmations (e.g., via CTOs) supporting engagement.
- **Beneficence** — The ethical duty to act in the patient’s best interest; patients often recognized treatment as beneficial retrospectively (e.g., medication adherence, ECT, safety from self-harm), supporting a beneficence-based rationale for coercion.
- **Nonmaleficence** — The duty to avoid harm; restrictions on freedom, privacy loss, and perceived coercion were experienced as harmful, challenging whether mandated treatment upholds this principle.
- **Informed consent** — Voluntary agreement based on adequate information and capacity; frequently undermined by poor communication, lack of understandable information, and absence of consultation, even when decisional capacity appeared present.
- **Lived experience** — First-person perspectives of individuals undergoing psychiatric treatment; the review’s qualitative methodology prioritized these voices to reveal nuanced, often contradictory experiences of mandate.
- **Therapeutic alliance** — Collaborative patient-provider relationship built on trust and mutual goals; weakened when patients felt unheard or talked about “behind their back,” but strengthened by ordinary conversation and staff presence.
- **Coercion** — Use of pressure or force to compel treatment against a patient’s will; embedded in legally mandated treatment, with patients simultaneously resenting its imposition and appreciating its effects post-stabilization.
- **Recovery model** — An approach emphasizing hope, empowerment, and personal meaning beyond symptom reduction; supported by patient reports of regaining independence, forming friendships, and pursuing goals via CTOs.
- **Community treatment order** — A legal mechanism for mandated treatment in the community; associated with patient-valued autonomy when allowing gradual independence and responsibility, but experienced as restrictive when perceived as overly controlling.
- **Systematic review** — A rigorous method for synthesizing qualitative research; used here to identify consistent themes across diverse geographic and legal contexts, enhancing transferability of core findings about autonomy and communication.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Patients with schizophrenia undergoing legally mandated treatment report mixed experiences: they value autonomy and resent its restriction, yet often retrospectively recognize treatment as beneficial; dissatisfaction is strongly linked to poor communication and lack of information.
  - **Evidence**: Thematic analysis of 18 qualitative studies (401 patients) showed positive experiences in 14 studies (autonomy/independence in 8, benefit recognition in 12) and negative experiences in 15 studies (autonomy restrictions in 12, communication deficits in 10). Example quotes include patients valuing CTOs for “More freedom, responsibilities, choices” but resenting them as restricting liberty (“The CTO restricts my liberty… I’ve got no liberty”), and appreciating treatment for safety while criticizing unclear written information or being “talked about behind my back.”
  - **Strength**: Strong for a qualitative synthesis. The review applied rigorous methods (CASP quality assessment, dual reviewer coding, thematic analysis), with 17/18 studies rated high quality. Limitations include potential self-report bias (social desirability or dissatisfaction), geographic overrepresentation of Oceania and Europe, and binary coding that may oversimplify fluid, context-dependent experiences.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Patients simultaneously resented loss of autonomy and appreciated treatment benefits — reflecting an enduring ethical tension not resolved in the source.
- Retrospective benefit recognition often occurred only after stabilization, raising questions about the validity of contemporaneous consent and whether benefit justifies prior coercion.
- Despite valuing autonomy, many patients lacked basic information — suggesting autonomy cannot be meaningfully exercised without adequate support, challenging liberal interpretations of autonomy as mere non-interference.
- Geographic bias: overrepresentation of high-income countries (Oceania, Europe) limits generalizability; experiences in low-resource or non-Western settings may differ significantly.
- Researcher influence: acknowledgment that positivity/negativity in responses may be shaped by who conducted the study (e.g., clinicians vs. independent researchers), potentially affecting candor.

## Open Questions
- How do experiences of legally mandated treatment differ for patients with schizophrenia versus other diagnoses (e.g., bipolar disorder, severe depression)?
- Can supported decision-making models reduce the need for legally mandated treatment while preserving safety and beneficence?
- What specific communication practices (e.g., shared decision-making tools, peer advocacy) most improve patient experience under mandate?
- How might cultural conceptions of autonomy and authority influence patient experiences of legally mandated treatment in non-Western settings?
