# Physical Restraint: A Historical Review and Current Practice

## Summary
This article traces the 300-year history of physical restraint in psychiatric care, beginning with its justification under English vagrancy laws and the quid pro quo principle, through reform efforts by figures like Philippe Pinel and John Conolly, to 20th-century transformations driven by the patient rights movement and landmark litigation such as O’Connor v. Donaldson, Youngberg v. Romeo, and Wyatt v. Stickney. It highlights how growing evidence of harm—particularly the 1998 Hartford Courant report linking 140 deaths to restraint over a decade, many due to asphyxiation in children—prompted regulatory action by the Joint Commission and Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS). These agencies established strict standards limiting restraint to immediate danger scenarios, requiring continuous monitoring, prohibiting PRN orders, and mandating debriefing and data collection. The article further outlines prevention strategies including staff training in de-escalation, patient and family involvement, environmental modifications like comfort rooms, and specific models such as collaborative problem solving and trauma-informed care, positioning restraint as a last resort rather than a routine intervention.

## Key Entities
- **Joint Commission** (organization): Accrediting body that established guidelines for restraint and seclusion use in healthcare facilities, requiring monitoring, training, and data reporting.
- **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)** (organization): Federal agency that enforces regulatory standards (e.g., 482.13(e)) limiting restraint to immediate safety threats and banning use for discipline or convenience.
- **Physical restraint** (tool/method): Involuntary restriction of movement by staff; one of three forms of restraint discussed, subject to historical and regulatory analysis.
- **Mechanical restraint** (tool/method): Restriction using devices such as straps or boards; regulated similarly to physical restraint under current standards.
- **Chemical restraint** (tool/method): Use of medication to restrict movement; treated as a distinct intervention requiring separate orders and monitoring when combined with physical restraint.
- **Seclusion** (tool/method): Involuntary confinement alone; often discussed alongside restraint and subject to similar regulatory limits.
- **Collaborative problem solving** (program): Evidence-based approach to reduce restraint by addressing lagging skills and involving patients in problem-solving.
- **Trauma Informed Care (TIC) curriculum** (program): Organizational framework emphasizing safety, trust, and empowerment to reduce reliance on coercive practices.
- **Cornell University’s therapeutic crisis intervention** (program): Crisis intervention model focused on trauma-sensitive de-escalation and emotional regulation.
- **Crisis Prevention Institute’s nonviolent therapeutic crisis intervention** (program): Training program promoting verbal and non-physical strategies to manage crisis situations.

## Key Concepts
- **Quid pro quo principle**: Legal justification historically used to defend restraint by claiming it benefited the individual and improved behavior, despite lack of consent.
- **Patient rights movement**: 1960s advocacy effort in mental health that challenged coercive treatments and aligned with civil rights to reform institutional practices.
- **14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution**: Constitutional basis for due process and equal protection; invoked in cases affirming rights of psychiatric patients against unlawful confinement.
- **O’Connor v. Donaldson**: 1975 Supreme Court ruling affirming that individuals cannot be confined without treatment, establishing a legal foundation for challenging involuntary psychiatric hold.
- **Youngberg v. Romeo**: 1982 decision requiring that treatment for institutionalized individuals be directed by a licensed physician, reinforcing medical oversight.
- **Wyatt v. Stickney**: Case establishing a right to adequate treatment in psychiatric facilities, influencing standards that limit non-therapeutic use of restraint.
- **Debriefing post-restraint**: Structured review of restraint incidents with staff and patient to prevent recurrence by addressing triggers and revising care plans.
- **Continuous visual monitoring**: Regulatory requirement for uninterrupted observation during restraint to ensure safety and detect complications like asphyxiation.
- **PRN (pro re nata) orders**: "As needed" medication or restraint orders; prohibited under current standards due to lack of immediacy and oversight.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The use of physical restraint in psychiatric care has evolved from a widely accepted practice justified by historical legal doctrines (e.g., vagrancy laws, quid pro quo) to a strictly regulated last-resort intervention due to patient rights advocacy, landmark litigation, and documented harms.
  - **Evidence**: Historical progression from 1740s English laws to reform by Pinel and Conolly; citation of Hartford Courant (1998) report of 140 restraint-related deaths over 10 years, many involving pediatric asphyxiation; reference to Joint Commission and CMS standards restricting restraint to imminent danger, requiring monitoring, licensure, and prohibiting punitive use; description of preventive strategies including de-escalation training, comfort rooms, and models like collaborative problem solving and trauma-informed care.
  - **Strength**: Strong for documenting historical trajectory and regulatory response; moderate for causal claims linking specific reforms to outcome reductions due to lack of longitudinal or comparative data; reliance on a single investigative report for mortality data, while impactful, would benefit from corroboration with peer-reviewed epidemiological studies.
