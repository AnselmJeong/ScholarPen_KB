# Mental health care and patients’ rights – are these two fields currently compatible?

## Summary
This chapter examines the compatibility of mental health care and patient rights from a European perspective, focusing on whether human rights are adequately protected in psychiatric care settings. It reviews the evolution of mental health care over recent decades, noting shifts toward community-oriented services and evidence-based practices, but highlights ongoing concerns such as reinstitutionalization—increasing forensic beds and long-stay placements—that counteract progress toward deinstitutionalization. The author analyzes international human rights frameworks, particularly the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) and the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), to assess their implementation in mental health contexts. Findings indicate limited access to justice via the ECHR, with only 108 of ~118,000 decisions over 50 years concerning psychiatric commitment. Data from the EUNOMIA project reveal significant cross-national variation in involuntary hospitalization laws and patient perceptions of admission appropriateness, ranging from 39–71% at one month and 46–86% at three months across countries. The chapter evaluates emerging initiatives—reforms to mental health laws, best practice guidelines for coercive measures, and psychiatric advance directives—as potential improvements, but notes gaps in evidence, inconsistent implementation, and weak enforcement of oversight mechanisms. Ultimately, it argues that while legal frameworks exist, systemic barriers prevent meaningful protection of patient rights in practice.

## Key Entities
- **World Psychiatric Association (WPA)** (org): Originator of the "Psychiatry for the Person" concept and the WPA Code of Ethics; referenced as a key actor in promoting person-centered care and ethical standards in psychiatry.
- **European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)** (org): International treaty used to assess human rights protections for individuals with mental illness; analyzed for its role in enabling access to justice in psychiatric commitment cases.
- **UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (org): Described as the most advanced international instrument for protecting the rights of persons with disabilities, including those with mental illness; central to discussions on legal capacity and autonomy.
- **European Court of Human Rights** (org): Judicial body enforcing the ECHR; examined for its limited engagement with psychiatric cases and dependence on national democracy and legal access for effectiveness.
- **World Health Organization (WHO)** (org): Implied through context of the QualityRights initiative; recognized as a global actor in promoting mental health and human rights, though not explicitly named in the chapter.

## Key Concepts
- **Human rights-based approach to mental health** — A framework prioritizing dignity, autonomy, participation, and non-discrimination in mental health care, grounded in international human rights law such as the CRPD and ECHR; presented as essential for reducing coercion and improving system accountability.
- **Institutionalization vs. deinstitutionalization** — The historical shift from long-stay psychiatric hospitals to community-based care; the chapter notes a concerning trend of reinstitutionalization (rising forensic beds, long-stay placements) that undermines earlier progress and threatens patient rights.
- **Patient autonomy** — The right of individuals to make independent decisions about their care, treatment, and life; examined as a principle challenged by outpatient commitment laws and forensic practices, though supported by tools like advance directives and personal budgets.
- **Legal capacity** — The legal recognition of an individual’s ability to make decisions with rights and responsibilities; discussed in the context of CRPD Article 12, with ongoing debate over supported versus substituted decision-making models.
- **Coercive treatment** — The use of involuntary measures (e.g., medication, restraint) in psychiatry, often justified by safety or treatment necessity; analyzed throughout the chapter for its compatibility with human rights principles in European mental health systems.
- **Best practice guidelines for coercive measures** — Standardized protocols aiming to reduce harm and respect rights during use of restraint, seclusion, etc.; described as emerging but hindered by lack of evidence, inconsistent development across countries, and poor implementation in clinical settings.
- **Psychiatric advance directives (PADs)** — Legal documents allowing individuals to state future treatment preferences during periods of incapacity; highlighted as a promising initiative to align care with prior wishes and strengthen autonomy in mental health care.
- **Outpatient commitment** — Legally mandated treatment in the community (e.g., via community treatment orders); analyzed as a policy intended to reduce hospitalization but potentially threatening liberty, therapeutic relationships, and data privacy due to wide legal variation and limited evidence.
- **Mental health law reporting systems** — Standardized collection and publication of mental health service data (e.g., involuntary admissions); seen as vital for transparency and rights protection, but challenged by fragmentation, inconsistent standards, and data protection concerns across jurisdictions.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Despite progress in mental health care (deinstitutionalization, community services, evidence-based practices), the human rights of people with mental illness are insufficiently protected in Europe due to legal inconsistencies, weak enforcement, reinstitutionalization trends, and lack of standardized monitoring.
  - **Evidence**: 
    - ECHR data shows only 108 of ~118,000 decisions over 50 years concerned psychiatric commitment, indicating severely limited access to justice for psychiatric patients.
    - The EUNOMIA project found major cross-national differences in involuntary hospitalization laws (e.g., who can initiate, time limits, requirement for legal representation).
    - Patient satisfaction with involuntary admission varied widely: 39–71% found it appropriate at one month, and 46–86% at three months, with England least favorable and Slovakia/Germany most favorable.
    - No dedicated domain for human rights existed in institutional care quality indicators until the recent development of the QUALITATIVE: QuIRC toolkit, indicating historical neglect of rights-based metrics.
    - Outpatient commitment laws vary widely (e.g., only in criminal law in some countries, restricted in others), with limited evidence on effectiveness and notable risks to liberty and privacy.
    - Monitoring bodies (e.g., CPT, ombudsmen) lack visibility, accessibility, and practical impact due to confidentiality requirements, infrequent visits, and complex procedures.
  - **Strength**: Strong for descriptive claims (legal variation, low ECHR engagement, patient survey results); weaker for prescriptive claims (e.g., that reforms would improve outcomes), as the chapter notes a lack of evidence base for the real-world impact of legal reforms.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- There is a tension between the goals of outpatient commitment (reducing hospitalization) and its potential to restrict liberty in the community, raising concerns about whether it truly represents a less restrictive alternative.
- While the CRPD supports legal capacity and opposes substitute decision-making, some interpretations (elsewhere in the wiki) argue for its necessity in "hard cases," creating a tension between abolitionist and pragmatic views on decision-making in mental health care.
- A gap exists between policy and practice: although anti-torture conventions and human rights laws exist on paper, enforcement mechanisms like CPT reports are often ignored and fail to change clinical behavior.
- The trend of reinstitutionalization (increasing forensic beds and long-stay placements) contradicts recovery-oriented, community-based care principles, suggesting a reversal of earlier progress toward deinstitutionalization.

## Open Questions
- How can legal capacity be operationalized in mental health care to support autonomy without increasing risk of harm to individuals or others?
- What models of continuous or accessible monitoring (beyond periodic visits by bodies like the CPT) could effectively improve human rights protections in psychiatric institutions?
- Is the observed reinstitutionalization in European mental health care primarily driven by risk-averse policies, insufficient community resources, or shifting societal attitudes toward people with mental illness?
