# The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care

## Summary
This chapter examines coercive measures across the entire mental health care pathway in England and Wales, from community contact through inpatient care to community discharge. It analyzes both formal coercive mechanisms (detention under the Mental Health Act, physical/mechanical restraint, seclusion, rapid tranquilization) and less tangible forms of coercion such as threats of detention, blanket ward rules, and conditional leave arrangements. The authors present evidence of increasing use of coercive measures over time, including a sixfold rise in Section 136 police detentions and over 50,000 Mental Health Act detentions in 2012/13, while highlighting significant gaps in data collection for certain coercive practices. The chapter emphasizes that informal admission status does not guarantee absence of coercion due to structural and perceptual factors, with studies showing 22-33% of informal patients experiencing coercion equivalent to detained patients. It concludes that coercion exists on a continuum and is influenced by patient factors, service organization, and staff practices, with ongoing tension between legal frameworks like the Mental Capacity Act and Mental Health Act.

## Key Entities
- **Care Quality Commission (CQC)** (organization): Primary regulator publishing annual reports on detention, restraint, and seclusion usage in English mental health services; key data source for trends in coercive measures.
- **Mental Health Act (1983)** (legislation): Primary legal framework governing detention and treatment in England and Wales; enables Sections 2, 3, 135, 136, and Community Treatment Orders.
- **Mental Capacity Act (2005)** (legislation): Governs assessment of decision-making capacity; creates tension with Mental Health Act as capacitous individuals can still be treated against their will under mental health legislation.
- **Community Treatment Order (CTO)** (policy tool): Post-discharge community supervision with power of recall to hospital; introduced via 2007 Mental Health Act amendments to extend coercion into community settings.
- **Mind** (organization): Mental health charity that conducted Freedom of Information requests revealing wide variation in physical restraint use across NHS trusts, reporting nearly 40,000 incidents in 2013.

## Key Concepts
- **Informal Admission**: Hospital admission without formal detention under the Mental Health Act; patients may experience coercion through threat of detention, blanket rules, or conditional leave despite not being sectioned.
- **Covert Coercion**: Non-physical pressures influencing behavior (e.g., threat of detention if leave denied, ward-wide rules restricting movement); explains why informal patients may feel coerced without formal legal mechanisms.
- **Physical Restraint**: Manual holding of patient to prevent harm; most basic intervention in behavioral crisis; associated with injury risks and significant variation in use across services.
- **Seclusion**: Isolation of patient in bare room to manage high risk; considered last-resort intervention; monitored by CQC but associated with dignity concerns and environmental risks.
- **De-escalation**: Verbal/non-physical techniques to reduce agitation before resorting to coercive steps; positioned as preferred first-line approach in crisis management.
- **Least Restrictive Option**: Principle requiring consideration of least restrictive interventions first (e.g., informal admission before detention); central to UK mental health law but tensions arise when informal status becomes coercive in effect.
- **Community Treatment Order (CTO)**: Post-discharge community conditions with power of recall to hospital; extends coercion into community following hospital discharge under Section 3.
- **Section 136**: Police power to detain and take individuals suspected of mental disorder to a place of safety; sixfold increase in use raises concerns about appropriateness, especially in custody suites.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion in mental health care extends beyond formal detention to include informal admissions, community treatment, and subtle pressures, with rising use across the care pathway in England/Wales despite goals to reduce it.
  - *Evidence*: CQC data showing year-on-year increase in detentions (over 50,000 in 2012/13); sixfold rise in Section 136 use; 25% of informal admissions perceived as coerced; 40,000 physical restraint incidents recorded by MIND in 2013.
  - *Strength*: Strong for quantitative trends (CQC, MIND); moderate for qualitative patient experience (smaller studies like Katsakou et al., O’Donoghue et al.).
- **Claim**: Informal admission status does not guarantee absence of coercion due to structural factors (e.g., blanket rules, threat of formal detention, leave conditions).
  - *Evidence*: Qualitative studies (Katsakou et al. 2011; O’Donoghue et al. 2014) showing 22-33% of informal patients experience coercion equivalent to detained patients; CQC notes on ward-wide policies.
  - *Strength*: Moderate – based on patient-reported experience and observational critiques, but lacks large-scale national metrics.
- **Claim**: Section 136 police detentions have risen sharply, often involving inappropriate use (e.g., for intoxication) and risks in custodial settings.
  - *Evidence*: Sixfold increase cited (Keown 2013); 22,000 detentions in 2012/13; concerns about custody suites as inappropriate places of safety; lack of historical data improving.
  - *Strength*: Strong for incidence trends; moderate for appropriateness critiques (based on anecdotal/media reports and expert opinion).
- **Claim**: Physical restraint use varies widely and lacks national framework, with significant injury risks.
  - *Evidence*: MIND FOI request showing 38–3000 annual incidents per trust; ~40,000 total episodes in 2013; 3500 face-down; associated injuries documented.
  - *Strength*: Strong for documenting variation and harm; reliant on single charity survey but large response rate (87% of trusts).
- **Claim**: Coercive practices are influenced by patient factors (e.g., younger age linked to seclusion; psychosis linked to seclusion/restraint) but moderated by setting and staff practices.
  - *Evidence*: Synthesis of European studies (Bowers et al. 2014); EUNOMIA project; specific findings on psychosis, agitation, and diagnosis links.
  - *Strength*: Moderate – based on heterogeneous observational studies; authors note limitations in drawing firm causal conclusions.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Tension between Mental Capacity Act and Mental Health Act: The source notes that capacitous individuals can still be treated against their will under MHA despite MCA’s emphasis on consent, creating a conflict between legal frameworks that both aim to protect patient autonomy.
- Informal Admission as Potentially Coercive: Challenges the assumption that informal = voluntary; evidence shows 22-33% of informal patients experience coercion equivalent to detained patients through threats, blanket rules, and leave conditions.
- Effectiveness of CTOs: The source cites OCTET finding no significant reduction in admission rates, questioning the long-term justification of CTOs despite their intended purpose of reducing the "revolving door."
- Rise in Coercion vs. Stated Goals: Data show increasing use of coercive measures (detentions, restraint, Section 136) despite international goals to reduce coercion, highlighting a gap between policy aspirations and practice.
- Subjectivity in Defining Coercion: Authors acknowledge difficulty quantifying covert forms (e.g., negotiation over leave); a narrow definition focusing only on physical restraint or detention would miss significant aspects of patient experience.

## Open Questions
