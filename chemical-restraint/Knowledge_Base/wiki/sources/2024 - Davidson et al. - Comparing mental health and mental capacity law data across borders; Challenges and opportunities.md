# Comparing mental health and mental capacity law data across borders; Challenges and opportunities

## Summary
This article examines the opportunities and challenges for comparing mental health and mental health capacity law between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. It notes that despite similar geographical, demographic, and publicly funded health structures, meaningful cross-jurisdictional comparison is hindered by inconsistent data collection practices. The authors find that current data focuses on process metrics like numbers of assessments and involuntary admissions, with limited demographic analysis and minimal capture of service user or carer experiences. Drawing on international examples from Australia, Canada, and European nations, they argue that effective outcomes frameworks must include social determinants of health, holistic wellbeing indicators, and perspectives from service users and carers. The article concludes that a coordinated, inclusive approach to data harmonization—grounded in co-production—is essential to enable policy learning, improve services, and uphold human rights under frameworks like the UNCRPD.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core argument**: Despite parallel legal reforms in mental health and mental capacity law in Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland, meaningful cross-jurisdictional comparison and learning are blocked by fragmented, inconsistent, and underused data collection. A coordinated, inclusive approach to data harmonization—incorporating clinical, experiential, and social data through co-production—is necessary to inform policy, improve services, and uphold human rights.
- **Evidence**: Supported by a narrative review of current law and policy (Farrell et al., 2022), a rapid review of international outcomes approaches (conducted for NI Mental Health Strategy 2021–2031), and examples from literature showing unexplained cross-national variation in reported rates (e.g., Zinkler & Priebe, 2002; Sheridan Rains et al., 2019) that point to methodological differences rather than true population differences.
- **Key findings**: (1) Both jurisdictions have similar public health structures but differ in private insurance reliance and progress on capacity law implementation (ROI’s Act fully commenced in 2023; NI’s remains partial). (2) Current data collection focuses on process metrics (assessments, admissions, tribunal hearings) with limited demographic breakdown and little capture of user or carer experience. (3) International best practices show effective frameworks include social determinants, holistic wellbeing, and stakeholder perspectives. (4) Major barriers include lack of standardized definitions (e.g., of compulsory intervention) and inconsistent timing/methods in administrative data. (5) Existing data is often underused for policy due to poor integration, accessibility, and lack of stakeholder engagement.
- **Strength**: The argument is strong in logic and policy relevance, drawing on documented reforms, official reports, and comparative studies. However, reliance on narrative and rapid review methods (rather than systematic review or primary data analysis) limits empirical depth. Conclusions are consistent with known challenges in cross-jurisdictional health research and align with human rights-based imperatives for transparent, inclusive monitoring.
