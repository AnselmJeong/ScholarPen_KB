# Use of a Novel Standardized Administration Protocol Reduces Agitation Pro Re Nata (PRN) Medication Requirements: The Birmingham Agitation Management (BAM)

## Summary
This study evaluated the Birmingham Agitation Management (BAM) protocol, which links the Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC) to a standardized PRN medication order set in an inpatient psychiatric unit. Researchers compared PRN administration rates before and after implementing BAM across 377 patients (184 pre-BAM, 193 BAM) at a U.S. medical center. The intervention standardized care by linking BVC scores (0–6) to specific medication actions: no medication for score 0, low-dose benzodiazepine for 1, high-dose benzodiazepine for 2, and high-dose benzodiazepine plus antipsychotic for scores ≥3. Results showed a 42.5% reduction in total PRN administrations, with mean doses per patient dropping from 8.21 to 4.39 (p<0.001) and median doses also significantly reduced. Notably, 40% of BAM patients received zero PRNs versus 22.8% pre-BAM. Crucially, there were no significant increases in seclusion (p=0.124) or restraint (p=0.994) duration, indicating reduced medication use did not increase reliance on coercive measures. The study suggests that standardizing the process of PRN administration—through objective scoring and clear action links—may be more impactful than medication choice alone for reducing inappropriate use.

## Key Entities
- **Birmingham Agitation Management (BAM)** (tool/protocol) — The intervention studied; a standardized protocol linking BVC scores to PRN medication orders for agitation management.
- **Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC)** (tool) — A validated 6-item checklist used to assess agitation severity and guide medication decisions under BAM; administered every 4 hours.
- **Electronic Health Record (EHR)** (tool) — System where BVC scoring and BAM order sets were integrated, enabling standardization and consistent implementation across nursing staff.
- **PRN (pro re nata) medication** (concept/practice) — As-needed medication administration for agitation; the primary outcome measure targeted for reduction through protocol standardization.
- **University of Alabama at Birmingham Medical Center (UABMC)** (organization) — The clinical setting where the pilot study was conducted; provides context but is not the focus of analysis.

## Key Concepts
- **Standardized medication protocol** — A predefined approach tying objective clinical assessments (e.g., BVC scores) to specific medication actions; reduces variability in PRN use by standardizing both assessment and response.
- **Agitation severity scoring** — Quantifying behavioral risk using a validated scale (BVC 0–6) to guide clinical decisions (e.g., no med, low-dose benzodiazepine, high-dose benzodiazepine ± antipsychotic); enables objective, consistent nursing assessments.
- **PRN overuse** — Administration of as-needed medications without clear indication, contributing to polypharmacy, oversedation, or adverse effects; the problem BAM aims to solve through protocol standardization.
- **De-escalation hierarchy** — Clinical approach prioritizing verbal/environmental interventions before medication or restraint; BAM includes verbal de-escalation as the first step before medication administration.
- **Seclusion and restraint rates** — Measures of coercive interventions (time in isolation or physical restraint); monitored as safety outcomes to ensure reducing PRN use does not increase alternative coercive measures.
- **Electronic health record (EHR) integration** — Embedding clinical protocols into digital systems to support consistent implementation; critical for scalability and adherence in BAM.
- **Medication standardization** — Defining specific drugs, doses, and routes based on assessment scores; reduces ambiguity in PRN orders compared to vague or overlapping instructions.
- **Psychiatric agitation** — A state of heightened arousal and risk of violence requiring intervention; the clinical context for PRN use and BAM application in inpatient settings.
- **Inpatient psychiatry** — The acute care setting where the study occurred; characterized by high acuity, involuntary admissions, and frequent use of PRN medications for behavioral control.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Implementation of the BAM protocol reduced total PRN administrations for agitation by 42.5% compared to pre-intervention, without worsening seclusion or restraint rates.
  - *Evidence*: Mean PRNs per patient decreased from 8.21 (SD 11.3) to 4.39 (SD 8.7); 95% CI [1.68–5.75], p<0.001. Median PRNs also significantly reduced (p<0.001). 40% of BAM patients had zero PRNs vs. 22.8% pre-BAM. No significant change in seclusion (p=0.124) or restraint (p=0.994) duration.
  - *Strength*: Strong for primary outcome due to large sample (n=377), statistical significance, and consistent mean/median trends. Moderate for secondary outcomes (non-significant trends in seclusion/restraint). Limited by retrospective design, lack of randomization, and potential confounding (e.g., staffing changes). No assessment of medication efficacy or patient-centered outcomes.
- **Core claim**: The reduction in PRN use was driven by standardization of the administration process rather than specific medication choice.
  - *Evidence*: When analyzing only protocol-specific medications (haloperidol, lorazepam, etc.), mean administration did not reach significance (p=0.190), though median did (p=0.023), suggesting process standardization—not drug selection—drove the outcome.
  - *Strength*: Moderate; supported by comparison of mean vs. median effects but limited by post-hoc analysis and possible substitution effects.
- **Core claim**: BAM maintains verbal de-escalation as a first step, aligning with least-restrictive care principles.
  - *Evidence*: Protocol describes attempting verbal de-escalation before medication administration at all BVC levels.
  - *Strength*: Strong; explicitly built into protocol design and described in methods.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Some literature suggests PRN reduction may increase reliance on restraint/seclusion if alternatives are lacking; BAM shows no such increase, challenging this assumption.
- Prior studies using agitation scales (e.g., PANSS-EC) to guide PRN use showed no effect, possibly due to lack of medication standardization—BAM suggests standardizing both assessment and response is key.
- While mean PRN use for protocol-specific medications was not significant (p=0.190), median was (p=0.023), indicating possible inconsistent adherence or substitution effects that weaken the specificity of the medication effect.
- High exclusion rate post-intervention (67/260 screened) due to protocol violations (wrong dose/medication) highlights implementation challenges despite training and EHR integration.
- No data on adverse effects (e.g., oversedation, falls) or patient-centered outcomes (e.g., satisfaction, trauma) limit understanding of full impact beyond process metrics.

## Open Questions
- How sustainable are BAM’s effects long-term? Were reductions in PRN use maintained beyond the 15-month intervention period?
- What are nurses’ and patients’ lived experiences with BAM? Did perceived autonomy, safety, or satisfaction change under the protocol?
- Can BAM be adapted for emergency departments or outpatient psychiatric settings where agitation also presents?
- Does reducing PRN use via BAM lead to improved clinical outcomes such as shorter hospital stays, fewer readmissions, or better engagement in therapy?
- How does BAM compare to other standardized agitation protocols (e.g., Project BETA, VA initiatives) in terms of effectiveness, implementability, and impact on coercive practices?
