# Eastern and Western Approaches to Coercion in Mental Health

## Summary
This chapter examines coercion in mental health care through a cross-cultural lens, comparing Western and Eastern perspectives. It defines coercion as the exercise of power to influence behavior, potentially through force or coercion when persuasion fails, and situates it within global human rights frameworks like the UNCRPD. The authors present epidemiological data showing high rates of coercive practices internationally—ranging from 3% in Portugal to 60% in England for involuntary admission—and highlight that coercion remains prevalent despite ethical concerns. In India, 63% of psychiatric patients experienced some form of restraint, with 27% receiving intravenous injections when staff were the target of aggression. The chapter emphasizes that coercion damages therapeutic relationships and patient dignity, particularly through perceived coercion, which predicts poorer patient–therapist relationships and is influenced by factors such as gender, religion, income, and illness insight. It argues that reducing coercion requires sustained, multi-faceted efforts including organizational changes, staff training, risk assessment, environmental modifications, psychotherapy, debriefings, and advance directives. Notably, patients who regain insight often retrospectively justify involuntary treatment, suggesting that effective care can mitigate coercive experiences. The chapter concludes by discussing the Mysore Declaration (2013), which sought to balance individual rights with family involvement in Indian mental health care while affirming that disproportionate coercion violates human rights.

## Key Entities
- **United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD)** (type: document/treaty) — Central international human rights framework referenced throughout the chapter as a foundation for rights-based approaches to mental health care.
- **Mysore Declaration** (type: document/declaration) — Outcome of the 2013 international symposium in Mysore, India; defines coercive measures in the Indian context and outlines strategies to minimize coercion in mental health settings.

## Key Concepts
- **Coercion** — The act of exercising power where an agent intentionally influences the behavior of others, potentially through force, threats, or intimidation, especially when persuasion fails; central to the chapter’s analysis across cultural contexts.
- **Cultural Dependency of Coercion** — The idea that perceptions, acceptability, and practices of coercion vary significantly based on cultural norms—e.g., individual autonomy emphasized in Western cultures vs. family/community decision-making in Indian, Arab, African, and Japanese contexts; a core argument explaining regional differences in coercion use.
- **Perceived Coercion** — An individual’s subjective experience of being pressured by internal or external sources to accept mental health treatment, regardless of objective coercion; linked to poorer therapeutic relationships and influenced by demographic and clinical factors.
- **Therapeutic Relationship** — The collaborative and trusting relationship between a patient and mental health provider, influenced by communication, mutual respect, and perceived respect for autonomy; highlighted as negatively impacted by perceived coercion.
- **Informed Consent** — A process requiring patient competence, adequate information disclosure, and voluntariness in agreeing to treatment; discussed as ethically compromised under coercion, particularly in psychiatric contexts where capacity may be questioned.
- **Mental Health Care Settings** — Environments where psychiatric care is delivered, including inpatient wards, community care (e.g., via Community Treatment Orders), and general medical facilities; context for analyzing variations in coercion use across regions and income levels.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Coercion is culturally shaped**: Its expression and acceptability depend on societal values—individual autonomy in Western contexts versus family-centered decision-making in many Eastern cultures (e.g., India).  
  - Evidence: Comparative examples from Europe, North America, and India; cites studies showing differing attitudes and practices (e.g., family presence during hospitalization in India vs. West).  
  - Strength: Strongly supported by comparative epidemiological and attitudinal data; limitation lies in limited longitudinal data on long-term outcomes.
- **Coercion remains prevalent despite ethical concerns**: High rates reported globally—e.g., 60% of psychiatric admissions involuntary in England (2019–2020); in India, 63% of patients experienced restraint, with 27% receiving IV injections when targeting staff.  
  - Evidence: Cites Salize & Dressing (2004), Lepping et al. (2016), Raboch et al. (2010), and Danivas et al. (2016).  
  - Strength: Well-documented prevalence data; limitation includes inconsistent definitions and measurement across studies.
- **Coercion harms therapeutic relationships and patient dignity**: Perceived coercion predicts poorer patient-therapist relationships; linked to loss of autonomy and dignity, even when treatment is later viewed as justified.  
  - Evidence: Theodoridou et al. (2012), van den Hooff & Goossensen (2014), Gowda et al. (2018).  
  - Strength: Consistent findings across studies; limitation relies largely on self-reported perceptions.
- **Reducing coercion requires multi-faceted, sustained efforts**: Effective strategies include organizational changes, staff training, risk assessment, environmental modifications, psychotherapy, debriefings, and advance directives—especially when implemented with leadership commitment.  
  - Evidence: Hirsch & Steinert (2019) cited as identifying seven intervention types.  
  - Strength: Based on synthesized intervention research; limitation is lack of standardized implementation metrics.
- **Insight and recovery reduce coercion over time**: Patients who regain insight often report that involuntary treatment was justified, suggesting that effective treatment can mitigate coercive experiences.  
  - Evidence: Gowda et al. (2018); recovery linked to decreased coercion severity at discharge.  
  - Strength: Supported by longitudinal patient reports; limitation includes potential recall bias and lack of control groups.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension between safety and autonomy**: The chapter acknowledges that coercion may be seen as necessary for safety (e.g., during acute aggression) but stresses that this should not override dignity or lead to routine use.  
  - Manifests in: Staff and family caregivers in India agreeing coercion should not be used more frequently, yet nearly half of families wanting more coercion due to logistical burdens.  
- **Discrepancy between objective and perceived coercion**: Patients may report low objective coercion but high subjective distress, or vice versa—highlighting the importance of subjective experience.  
  - Supported by: Findings that perceived coercion predicts therapeutic relationship quality independently of objective measures.  
- **Cultural relativism vs. universal human rights**: While advocating for context-sensitive approaches, the chapter upholds the UNCRPD as a universal standard, creating potential tension when local norms (e.g., family authority) conflict with individual rights.  
  - Addressed by: The Mysore Declaration’s attempt to balance community involvement with individual dignity and rights.  
- **Lack of evidence on effectiveness**: Despite widespread use, evidence on whether coercion improves long-term outcomes (e.g., treatment adherence) is inconclusive.  
  - Cited: Cossu et al. (2023) systematic review on involuntary hospitalization and compliance.

## Open Questions
- How can mental health systems balance familial involvement (valued in many cultures) with the individual rights of persons with mental illness, especially when their preferences diverge? This remains underexplored in current literature and wiki.
