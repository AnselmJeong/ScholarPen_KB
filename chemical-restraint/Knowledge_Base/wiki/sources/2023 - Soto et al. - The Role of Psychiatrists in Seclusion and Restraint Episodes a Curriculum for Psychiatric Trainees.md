# The Role of Psychiatrists in Seclusion and Restraint Episodes: a Curriculum for Psychiatric Trainees

## Summary
This study developed and evaluated a 90-minute educational curriculum for psychiatry trainees focusing on their role in seclusion and restraint episodes. The curriculum included a didactic component covering historical context, regulatory requirements, and chemical restraint concepts; an open discussion on ethical considerations and racial disparities; and a case-based discussion. Delivered to psychiatry interns during adolescent psychiatry rotations at an academic hospital, the study used pre- and post-test surveys to assess changes in knowledge and comfort. Results showed significant improvements in both knowledge (from 40% to 87% correct) and self-rated comfort (from 2.7 to 3.9 on a 1–7 scale), with high participant satisfaction and perceived relevance to future practice. Qualitative feedback emphasized the value of safe dialogue and requested more case-based learning. The authors conclude that while the brief intervention shows promise for increasing awareness and readiness, sustained, multi-component training is needed to impact actual clinical practice and address systemic issues like racial disparities in seclusion and restraint use.

## Key Concepts
- **Seclusion and restraint** — Clinical interventions involving isolation (seclusion) or physical/chemical restriction (restraint) to manage imminent risk of harm; central focus due to associated physical and psychological harms and racial disparities
- **Chemical restraint** — Use of medication to control behavior or movement not justified by standard treatment, per CMS definition; highlighted as carrying similar risks to physical restraint but lacking equivalent monitoring and reporting
- **Racial disparities in seclusion and restraint** — Disproportionate application of seclusion and restraint on Black patients versus White patients; presented as exacerbating health inequities and intergenerational trauma
- **Psychiatry Milestones** — Competency framework from ACGME outlining expected skills for psychiatric trainees, including systems-based practice; used to justify the curriculum’s alignment with training goals
- **Therapeutic milieu** — The environmental and interpersonal aspects of inpatient psychiatric care that foster safety and healing; referenced as a component of effective programs to reduce seclusion and restraint use

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: A brief educational curriculum improved psychiatry trainees' knowledge and comfort regarding seclusion and restraint, while identifying the need for ongoing training.
  - **Evidence**: Knowledge scores increased significantly from 2.4/6 (40%) to 5.2/6 (87%) (p < 0.01); self-rated comfort rose from 2.7 to 3.9 on a 1–7 scale (p = 0.01); participants rated the session as useful (4.7/5) and likely to influence future practice (4.1/5); qualitative feedback praised the safe space for discussion and requested more case-based learning.
  - **Strength**: Moderate. Supported by pre/post design with statistical testing, but limited by small sample (n=14 for comparison), single-site delivery, and lack of long-term follow-up or behavioral outcome data. Results indicate feasibility and short-term gains, not proven changes in clinical practice.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Despite improvements in knowledge and comfort, post-intervention comfort levels remained relatively low (3.9/7), suggesting persistent unease—possibly appropriate given the ethically complex and potentially harmful nature of seclusion and restraint practices.
- The study acknowledges that brief educational interventions alone are unlikely to alter clinical outcomes, implying a need for systemic changes beyond training, such as policy reform, leadership engagement, and environmental modifications.
- No data were collected on actual changes in seclusion or restraint frequency, limiting conclusions about the curriculum’s impact on real-world clinical behavior.

## Open Questions
- Can sustained, longitudinal training reduce actual seclusion and restraint rates in adolescent psychiatric inpatient settings?
- How should competency in managing seclusion and restraint be assessed beyond knowledge and comfort metrics (e.g., clinical decision-making, patient outcomes)?
- What role should patient lived experience and advocacy play in the design and delivery of training on seclusion and restraint for psychiatry trainees?
