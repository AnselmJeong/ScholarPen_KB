# An introduction to clinical ethics in psychiatry

## Summary
This article examines ethical challenges in psychiatric practice through analysis of key legal cases (Re C, RM v St Andrew’s Healthcare, Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board) and builds upon the Royal College of Psychiatrists’ CPD module on clinical ethics. It critiques the limitations of the four principles approach (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) in psychiatry due to ambiguous interpretation, conflicting priorities, and difficulties in factual assessment—particularly regarding future risk predictions and personal identity. The authors argue that ethical dilemmas are exacerbated by questions of personal identity in chronic mental illness and the difficulty of distinguishing illness-expressed values from the patient’s authentic self. When clinical ethics committees are unavailable (noting only three exist in UK NHS Mental Health Trusts), psychiatrists must engage in reflective practice, recognize personal and institutional biases, prioritize dialogue, and use Socratic questioning to navigate value conflicts. The article emphasizes that informed consent requires not just risk disclosure but a genuine exploration of what matters to the patient, and that deception (e.g., covert medication) undermines trust and justice regardless of intent or outcome.

## Key Concepts
- **Four Principles Approach**: Biomedical ethics model (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) used as a baseline for analysis; critiqued for ambiguous interpretation and difficulty resolving conflicts in psychiatric contexts.
- **Values-Based Practice (VBP)**: Approach emphasizing exploration of value positions in ethical decisions; presented as essential when consensus fails and values remain "in play" despite disagreement.
- **Capacity to Consent**: Legal and clinical assessment of a patient’s ability to understand, retain, weigh, and communicate a decision; highlighted as complex in psychiatry due to fluctuating capacity and challenges in distinguishing illness-expressed views from authentic preferences.
- **Relational Autonomy**: Concept that autonomy is shaped by relationships and social context (drawing on Agich 1993); used to explain why isolated chronic patients pose unique ethical challenges in decision-making.
- **Personal Recovery**: Framework distinguishing clinical recovery (symptom remission) from personal recovery (meaning, purpose, identity); central to understanding treatment aims in chronic mental illness beyond symptom control.
- **Covert Medication**: Administration of drugs without patient knowledge or consent; discussed as ethically problematic due to deception, impact on autonomy, and erosion of trust, even when clinically beneficial.
- **Therapeutic Privilege**: Concept (critiqued in the article) where clinicians withhold information believing it benefits the patient; contrasted with modern informed consent standards requiring transparency.
- **Moral Distancing/Dehumanization**: Process where institutional culture (e.g., dark humor, "us vs. them") reduces empathy and enables unethical behavior; cited as a barrier to ethical practice in psychiatric settings.
- **Informed Consent**: Requires not only risk disclosure (per Montgomery) but a genuine dialogue exploring patient values; underscores that consent is a process, not just a disclosure event.
- **Identity**: The role of personal identity in psychiatric decision-making, particularly in chronic illness where long-term symptoms may obscure or reshape premorbid personality, complicating assessments of authentic wishes.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The four principles approach has limitations in psychiatry due to ambiguous interpretation of principles (e.g., beneficence vs. autonomy), conflicting priorities, and challenges in fact-assessment, especially future risk predictions.
  - Evidence: Analysis of Re C shows disagreement on whether amputation was beneficial (clinicians saw risk reduction; patient saw loss of identity/autonomy). Courts sided with patient’s interpretation of facts and values.
  - Strength: Strong — supported by legal precedent and philosophical analysis of principle ambiguity.
- **Core claim**: Ethical dilemmas in psychiatry are exacerbated by issues of personal identity (especially in chronic illness) and the difficulty of determining a patient’s "true" self versus illness-expressed values.
  - Evidence: Contrast between acute psychosis (where collateral history helps identify "usual self") vs. chronic schizophrenia (where illness may dominate identity presentation). Raises questions about treatment aims (symptom control vs. personal meaning).
  - Strength: Moderate — theoretically grounded (citing Glover, Slade) but relies on clinical illustration rather than empirical data.
- **Core claim**: When clinical ethics committees are unavailable, psychiatrists must engage in reflective practice, recognize biases (personal/institutional), prioritize dialogue, and use Socratic questioning to navigate value conflicts.
  - Evidence: Reference to Silva et al (2017) showing staff distress even after ethically justified coercive treatment; emphasizes that "least-worth" outcomes may still cause moral injury.
  - Strength: Moderate — based on case studies and ethical theory; practical guidance is sensible but not novel.
- **Core claim**: Informed consent requires not just risk disclosure (per Montgomery) but also a genuine dialogue exploring what matters to the patient; deception (e.g., covert medication) undermines trust and justice, even with good outcomes.
  - Evidence: Montgomery and RM cases show courts rejecting paternalism; stress on honesty as non-negotiable in trust-based relationships.
  - Strength: Strong — backed by evolving legal standards and consensus in medical ethics.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Tension exists between clinician- and patient-defined beneficence: clinicians may view preventing harm to others as beneficent (e.g., detaining a risky patient), but patients may experience this as harm (loss of liberty, trust), reflecting a fundamental disagreement over whose welfare counts in ethical calculations.
- The article challenges assumptions of a stable, pre-illness self underlying autonomy decisions, showing that in long-term psychosis (e.g., 40-year schizophrenia), illness may dominate identity expression, complicating recovery models that assume return to a "normal" self.
- While some ethical frameworks treat autonomy as individualistic, the article advocates for relational autonomy (per Agich), creating tension in cases like Re C where the patient’s values conflict with clinicians’ views of their best interest, yet those values emerge from limited social connections.
- The article rejects therapeutic privilege as ethically permissible, aligning with Montgomery and Sidaway, which may contrast with any wiki content that still presents deception as a nuanced or context-justified strategy in medicine.

## Open Questions
- How should clinicians assess "personal recovery" goals when a patient’s illness-long identity resists distinction between self and symptoms, particularly in chronic psychosis where the illness may have shaped decades of lived experience?
- What institutional safeguards can prevent moral distancing and dehumanization in high-coercion psychiatric settings (e.g., secure units), where routine practices may erode empathy and ethical sensitivity over time?
- Is there a role for advance statements or Ulysses contracts in chronic psychosis to preserve autonomy during periods of contested capacity, and how can such tools balance respect for past wishes with present clinical judgment?
