# Psychosocial Support Without Coercion: Consequences, Dilemmas and Possible Ways Out

## Summary
This chapter examines the systemic consequences of abolishing coercion in psychiatry as mandated by the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD). It analyzes four key domains: preference-based self-declaration of illness, psychosocial support without diagnostic requirements, managing harm to others, and addressing self-harm and dementia. The author argues that while coercion reduction has not increased compulsory admissions in cases like Italy’s deinstitutionalization, full abolition under CRPD would create significant challenges—particularly in forensic settings where individuals might face standard prosecution instead of insanity defenses, in suicide prevention where coercive interventions have poor predictive validity and may deter help-seeking, and in dementia care where advance directives may conflict with evolving capacity. The chapter concludes that achieving coercion-free care requires not only psychiatric reform but also fundamental changes in legal, penal, and social welfare systems.

## Key Entities
- **UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (org) — Central international treaty driving the abolition of coercion in psychiatry; frames the core debate and legal obligation examined.
- **International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF)** (tool/dataset) — Referenced as a framework requiring linkage to ICD diagnosis for healthcare reimbursement, illustrating systemic dependency on medicalization.
- **International Classification of Diseases (ICD)** (tool/dataset) — Implied basis for ICD-linked ICF assessments; represents the diagnostic infrastructure that would need reform to support diagnosis-independent support services.
- **Association of State Mental Health Programme Managers (US)** (org) — Cited for its 1999 position paper advocating reduction (not elimination) of seclusion and restraint, showing early professional alignment with coercion reduction goals.
- **Swiss healthcare system** (org) — Referenced as an example where forensic outpatient treatment and complementary medicine (e.g., homeopathy) are reimbursable, illustrating existing models for non-coercive, preference-based care.

## Key Concepts
- **Abolition of coercion in psychiatry** — The elimination of involuntary interventions such as seclusion, restraint, and forced medication; framed as a legal imperative under CRPD but associated with complex systemic trade-offs.
- **Epistemic injustice** — Harm done to individuals in their capacity as knowers, particularly when their experiences are dismissed due to mental health stigma; identified as a barrier to suicide prevention under coercive systems.
- **Quarantine model** — A non-punitive approach to managing risk, grounded in neuroexistentialism, that detains individuals who pose danger without assigning moral blame; proposed as an alternative to forensic hospitalization.
- **Advance directives** — Pre-existing legal documents allowing individuals to outline future care preferences; discussed in dementia care as insufficient due to potential conflict between "then-self" and "now-self" decisions.
- **Psychosocial support without coercion** — Non-compulsory community-based alternatives such as Open Dialogue, peer respites, and supported housing; presented as feasible interim steps toward coercion-free care under current laws.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Abolishing coercion in psychiatry (per UN CRPD) creates systemic dilemmas across judicial, healthcare, and social domains—particularly regarding forensic commitments, suicide prevention, and dementia care—requiring fundamental reforms beyond psychiatry alone.
  - **Evidence**: Italian deinstitutionalization reduced psychiatric beds without increasing compulsory admissions (natural experiment); without insanity defenses, individuals would face standard prosecution and longer prison sentences lacking psychosocial support; current coercive suicide interventions have poor predictive validity and may deter help-seeking due to stigma; dementia advance directives may be overridden by present-state wishes, requiring ongoing renegotiation.
  - **Strength**: Strong in identifying systemic interdependencies between law, healthcare, and social structures; weaker in providing validated alternatives at scale (e.g., admits quarantine model is untested broadly, peer respites lack generalizable outcome data).

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The author critiques Appelbaum’s opposition to CRPD but agrees his concern about unaddressed forensic and suicide risks is valid, revealing sympathy for abolition while acknowledging its potential harms.
- No clear solution exists for preventing suicide without coercion; the author’s suggestion to expand assisted suicide access remains controversial even among abolition advocates.
- Advance directives are promoted for dementia care but acknowledged as potentially inadequate due to possible disagreement between prior and current wishes, undermining a pure autonomy model.
- The quarantine model’s rejection of free will (via neuroexistentialism) may conflict with liberal assumptions about autonomy and responsibility prevalent in current mental health law discourse.

## Open Questions
- Can the quarantine model be implemented at scale without recreating institutional harms or becoming a vehicle for net-widening social control?
- How can non-medical psychosocial support be sustainably financed if decoupled from illness diagnosis and insurance billing requirements?
- What safeguards are needed to ensure that risk-based detention (even if non-punitive) does not disproportionately impact marginalized groups or expand social control under the guise of protection?
