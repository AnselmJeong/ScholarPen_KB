# Who’s the Boss? Ethical Dilemmas in the Treatment of Children and Adolescents

## Summary
This article examines ethical challenges in psychiatric care for children and adolescents through three clinical vignettes. It explores tensions between adolescent autonomy (via assent), parental authority, confidentiality, and legal obligations such as mandatory reporting. The authors analyze scenarios involving substance use disclosure, adolescent romantic relationships, and exposure to intimate partner violence, emphasizing the need for context-sensitive judgment grounded in professional ethics codes (e.g., AACAP), legal frameworks like HIPAA, and evolving societal norms. The piece highlights how emerging issues—such as teen sexting and diverse family structures—complicate traditional ethical boundaries, requiring clinicians to navigate conflicts between therapeutic trust and legal compliance.

## Key Entities

- **Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA)** (type: tool) — Federal law governing privacy of protected health information, applied to both minor and adult patients in psychiatric care.

## Key Concepts
- **Assent** — A minor’s agreement to undergo treatment, distinct from legal consent; reflects developing capacity and is ethically required alongside parental consent in pediatric care.
- **Confidentiality** — The duty to protect patient privacy, which may be overridden by safety concerns or legal mandates (e.g., abuse reporting), especially critical in adolescent mental health treatment.
- **Mandatory reporting** — Legal obligation for clinicians to report suspected child abuse or neglect; triggered in the vignette by witnessing intimate partner violence, regardless of direct harm to the child.
- **Sexting** — The exchange of sexually explicit digital content between minors; may constitute illegal distribution of child pornography under state law, creating ethical tension between legal compliance and therapeutic trust.
- **Mature minor doctrine** — Legal principle recognizing that some adolescents possess sufficient maturity to make independent medical decisions, varying significantly by jurisdiction.
- **Romeo and Juliet exceptions** — Legal provisions that reduce or eliminate penalties for consensual sexual activity between close-in-age minors, referenced as a mitigating factor in teenage relationships.
- **Informed consent** — Requires decision-making capacity and legal age; generally held by parents for minors, but assent from the minor should be sought when possible.
- **HIPAA** — Federal standard for health information privacy that applies uniformly to minors and adults, though state laws may impose additional disclosure requirements.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Ethical psychiatric care for minors depends on balancing adolescent assent, parental authority, confidentiality, and legal duties, especially amid evolving social and technological contexts.
  - **Evidence**: Three detailed vignettes illustrate conflicts: (1) adolescent cannabis use and family disclosure struggles; (2) adolescent romantic relationship progression to sexting; (3) exposure to intimate partner violence triggering mandatory reporting. Each is analyzed using AACAP ethics guidelines, HIPAA, and state-level minor consent laws.
  - **Strength**: Strong grounding in established ethics codes and legal frameworks; moderate due to reliance on expert opinion and case-based reasoning rather than empirical data or outcome studies.
