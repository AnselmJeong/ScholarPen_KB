# Human Rights and Adherence to Treatment in Patients with Severe Mental Illness: Mental Healthcare Act 2017 Perspective

## Summary
This prospective, comparative study examined the impact of India's Mental Healthcare Act (MHCA) 2017 on medication adherence and human rights awareness among patients with severe mental illness (SMI). Researchers assessed 65 hospitalized patients diagnosed with SMI, dividing them into independent admission groups (IAG) based on decision-making capacity and supported admission groups (SAG) for those lacking capacity or requiring high support. Baseline assessments included sociodemographic data, psychopathology (BPRS), functioning (GAF), insight (SAI-E), adherence (MARS and CRS), and human rights awareness via a Needs Assessment Questionnaire covering physical, emotional, social, religious, and ethical domains. Follow-up assessments occurred at discharge and weeks 1 and 6 post-discharge. The study found no significant differences in psychopathology or functioning between groups over time, but IAG demonstrated significantly higher insight and adherence at baseline. Crucially, the emotional needs domain of human rights awareness was significantly lower in SAG compared to IAG at both baseline and week 6 follow-up. A positive correlation emerged between medication adherence (MARS) and human rights awareness in IAG at baseline (r = 0.4, p < 0.05), while SAG showed a higher correlation coefficient (r = 0.7) that lacked statistical significance in reporting. The findings suggest that adherence may support human rights realization, particularly autonomy-related aspects, while highlighting persistent deficits in emotional support for those under supported admission despite legislative protections.

## Key Entities
- **Mental Healthcare Act (MHCA), 2017** (org): Legislative framework governing mental health care in India, defining independent and supported admissions based on patient capacity for treatment-related decisions.
- **Medication Adherence Rating Scale (MARS)** (tool): Self-reported scale used to measure patient adherence to psychotropic medication, with a score ≥6 indicating adherence.
- **Clinician Rating Scale (CRS)** (tool): Clinician-administered scale assessing medication adherence, with a score ≥5 indicating adherence.
- **Schedule for Insight-Expanded Version (SAI-E)** (tool): Instrument used to assess patient insight into illness, need for treatment, and social consequences of mental disorder.
- **Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale (BPRS)** (tool): Widely used scale to measure severity of psychopathology, including psychotic and affective symptoms.
- **Global Assessment of Functioning (GAF)** (tool): Historical scale assessing psychological, social, and occupational functioning on a 0–100 scale, used here as an inclusion criterion (GAF <50).
- **Needs Assessment Questionnaire** (tool): 58-item instrument assessing human rights awareness across physical, emotional, social, religious, and ethical domains, with Part B evaluating community human rights climate.

## Key Concepts
- **Medication Adherence**: The extent to which patients follow prescribed treatment regimens; central outcome linked to relapse prevention, quality of life, and human rights awareness in SMI under MHCA 2017.
- **Human Rights Awareness**: Recognition and perception of entitlements to dignity, autonomy, and care, particularly in mental health contexts; found deficient in SAG (emotional domain) and positively correlated with adherence in IAG.
- **Independent vs. Supported Admission (under MHCA 2017)**: Legal classification based on decision-making capacity regarding treatment; IAG for patients with capacity, SAG for those lacking capacity or with high support needs/risk to self/others.
- **Emotional Needs Domain (of human rights)**: Pertains to feelings of dignity, respect, emotional support, and interpersonal connection; showed statistically significant deficits in SAG at baseline and follow-up, indicating unmet psychosocial needs.
- **Insight into Illness**: Patient’s awareness of having a mental illness, need for treatment, and social consequences; higher in IAG at baseline and linked to admission type and adherence.
- **Severe Mental Illness (SMI)**: Chronic, highly disabling conditions including schizophrenia, bipolar affective disorder, and severe depression; population of focus due to high disability and human rights vulnerability.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: There is a positive correlation between medication adherence and human rights awareness, particularly in the independent admission group (IAG) at baseline, and adherence is an important determinant of human rights awareness in SMI under MHCA 2017.
  - **Evidence**: At baseline, IAG showed a significant correlation between MARS scores and human rights awareness (r = 0.4, p < 0.05). SAG demonstrated a higher correlation (r = 0.7) at baseline but lacked statistical significance in reporting. Emotional needs domain of human rights was significantly lower in SAG vs. IAG at baseline (p = 0.01) and week 6 follow-up (p = 0.002). IAG had significantly higher insight (SAI-E: 12.80 ± 7.26) vs. SAG (8.18 ± 6.96) at baseline (p = 0.01). No significant differences in psychopathology (BPRS) or functional outcomes between groups over time.
  - **Strength**: Moderate. The study employs a prospective, comparative design with standardized scales but is limited by a small sample size (N=65), short follow-up (max 6 weeks post-discharge), and potential confounding (e.g., baseline psychopathology influencing insight). Correlation does not imply causation; the direction of adherence → human rights awareness is inferred but not proven.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal tension**: While the supported admission group (SAG) showed a higher correlation coefficient (r = 0.7) between adherence and human rights awareness at baseline, this finding was not marked as statistically significant in the results (no asterisk or p-value), whereas the independent admission group’s lower correlation (r = 0.4) was reported as significant (p < 0.05). This discrepancy raises questions about statistical power, variance within SAG, or selective reporting, suggesting the SAG correlation may be less reliable despite its higher magnitude.
- **Caveat**: The study acknowledges that baseline differences in insight and emotional needs could be influenced by florid psychopathology, though the persistence of emotional deficits at the 6-week follow-up suggests systemic issues—such as lack of familial or emotional support in supported care settings—rather than transient clinical states alone.

## Open Questions
- Does improving medication adherence *cause* increased human rights awareness, or do both outcomes stem from shared underlying factors such as insight, social support, or clinical improvement?
- How do psychosocial interventions (e.g., family therapy, peer support, community integration) affect human rights awareness in the supported admission group, particularly in addressing the persistent deficit in emotional needs?
- Are the emotional needs deficits in supported admission patients attributable to systemic issues like stigma, family disengagement, or institutional practices, or are they an inherent consequence of the supported admission process under MHCA 2017?
