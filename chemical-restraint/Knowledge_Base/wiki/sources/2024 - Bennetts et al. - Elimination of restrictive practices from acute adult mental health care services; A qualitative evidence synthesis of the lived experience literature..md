# Elimination of restrictive practices from acute adult mental health care services; A qualitative evidence synthesis of the lived experience literature

## Summary
This study conducted a qualitative evidence synthesis (QES) of 44 qualitative studies published between 1995 and 2023 to synthesize the lived experiences of mental health service users and practitioners regarding restrictive practices in adult acute mental health care. Using PRISMA 2020 guidelines and the Covidence platform for study selection, the research applied inductive thematic synthesis via NVivo to analyze first-order participant quotes and second-order themes from the included studies. Study quality was assessed using the CASP Qualitative Checklist, with most articles rated as high or moderate quality. The synthesis generated five overarching themes: (1) Meaning and emotional experience, (2) Re-traumatisation and dehumanisation, (3) Professional competencies and varying experiences, (4) Balancing safety versus care, and (5) Alternatives to restrictive practices. The findings highlight that restrictive practices are frequently experienced as traumatic, dehumanizing, and emotionally distressing, often triggering past trauma and evoking fear, shame, and powerlessness. Despite this, some staff perceive them as necessary for safety, reflecting a tension between custodial control and therapeutic care. The study concludes that elimination requires addressing staff attitudes, improving communication and de-escalation skills, implementing evidence-based alternatives (e.g., Safewards, Six Core Strategies), and centering lived experience in reform efforts.

## Key Concepts
- **Qualitative Evidence Synthesis (QES)** — A systematic review method for integrating findings from multiple qualitative studies to develop new interpretations; used here to analyze lived experience across 44 international studies.
- **Inductive Thematic Synthesis** — An analytical approach where themes emerge from data coding without predefined categories; applied to derive the five core themes from participant narratives.
- **Restrictive Practices** — Interventions that restrict movement or liberty, including physical/mechanical restraint and seclusion (chemical restraint excluded); the central phenomenon under investigation.
- **Lived Experience** — First-person accounts of individuals with direct experience of mental health services and restrictive practices; prioritized to ensure authentic insight into impact and meaning.
- **Re-traumatisation** — The process whereby restrictive practices trigger or re-experience past traumatic events; identified as a key harmful effect in service user narratives.
- **Dehumanisation** — The experience of being treated as less than human during restrictive interventions; a core theme highlighting ethical violations and loss of dignity.
- **Balancing Safety versus Care** — The tension between maintaining safety and providing therapeutic, person-centered care; a central dilemma influencing staff decisions to use or avoid restrictive practices.
- **Alternatives to Restrictive Practices** — Non-coercive strategies to manage distress and prevent harm (e.g., Safewards, Six Core Strategies); presented as viable pathways toward elimination.
- **Professional Competencies** — The skills, attitudes, and training of staff that influence their use, perception, and confidence in managing distress without restrictive practices.
- **Emotional Experience** — The affective responses (fear, anger, shame, distress) of individuals undergoing restrictive practices; a foundational theme capturing the human impact.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Restrictive practices are experienced as harmful, traumatic, and dehumanizing by service users, yet persist due to perceived safety needs, lack of alternatives, and systemic challenges.
  - **Evidence**: Derived from inductive thematic synthesis of 44 qualitative studies (1995–2023) across 14 countries, encompassing ~339 service users and ~439 practitioners. Themes were validated through team consensus, reflexive discussion, and quality appraisal using CASP.
  - **Strength**: Strong. The study uses rigorous QES methodology (PRISMA-guided), includes diverse international perspectives, assesses methodological quality, and integrates both service user and practitioner views. Limitations include exclusion of chemical restraint and non-English studies, but these are justified and acknowledged.
- **Core harm**: Re-traumatisation and dehumanisation are pervasive, with service users reporting that restrictive practices evoke past trauma, shame, powerlessness, and a sense of being punished rather than cared for.
  - **Evidence**: Repeated across multiple studies (e.g., Beresford, 2000; Brophy et al., 2016; Menezes et al., 2014), where participants described feeling "like an animal," "punished," or "reliving abuse."
  - **Strength**: Well-supported by consistent qualitative evidence; limited only by the exclusion of chemical restraint, which may involve similar dynamics.
- **Core tension**: Staff often describe restrictive practices as necessary for safety, while service users experience them as non-therapeutic and harmful, creating an ethical conflict in care delivery.
  - **Evidence**: Captured in Theme 4 and supported by studies like Muir-Cochrane et al. (2018) and van Dorn et al. (2010), where fear of violence and lack of alternatives drive continued use despite misgivings.
  - **Strength**: Strongly supported; reflects a well-documented dilemma in mental health care, though some studies note that de-escalation and training can reduce reliance.
- **Core solution**: Alternatives such as empathic communication, peer support, and structured models (e.g., Safewards) are effective and do not increase violence when implemented with leadership and training.
  - **Evidence**: Cited in Theme 5 and supported by trials like Fletcher et al. (2017) and Zemore et al. (2018), showing reduced restraint use without increased harm.
  - **Strength**: Moderate to strong; effectiveness depends on implementation fidelity, organizational commitment, and sustained resourcing.

## Open Questions
- How do cultural differences in mental health care affect the acceptability and implementation of alternatives to restrictive practices in low-resource or non-Western settings?
- What specific organizational changes (e.g., leadership models, staffing ratios, environmental design) are most effective in sustaining elimination efforts beyond initial training?
- How can peer support workers and individuals with lived experience be formally integrated into acute mental health teams to reduce reliance on restrictive practices through relational interventions?
- What are the long-term economic and systemic impacts of eliminating restrictive practices, including effects on staff well-being, readmission rates, and community integration?
