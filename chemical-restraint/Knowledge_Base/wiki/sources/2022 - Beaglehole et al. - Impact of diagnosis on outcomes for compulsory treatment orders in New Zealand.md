# Impact of diagnosis on outcomes for compulsory treatment orders in New Zealand

## Summary
This study examined whether the impact of Community Treatment Orders (CTOs) varies by psychiatric diagnosis using national linked health data from New Zealand spanning 2009–2018. Researchers analyzed data from 14,726 individuals placed under CTOs, drawing on the Programme for the Integration of Mental Health Data (PRIMHD) for demographic and service use information and the Pharmaceutical Collection for medication dispensing records. Diagnoses were hierarchically categorized using DSM-IV codes into groups including psychotic disorders, bipolar disorders, major depressive disorder, dementia, personality disorders, and others. The primary outcomes were psychiatric admission frequency and duration per year, compared during versus outside CTO periods, analyzed via rate ratios (RRs) using Poisson regression. The study found that CTOs reduced admission frequency and duration only for individuals with psychotic disorders (RR = 0.82 for admissions, RR = 0.81 for bed days), while increasing these metrics across all other diagnostic groups. Notably, depot antipsychotic dispensing rose significantly during CTOs for all groups, with the highest relative increase seen in major depressive disorder (RR = 8.34). Community contacts also increased across all diagnostic groups during CTO periods. These results suggest that CTOs do not have uniform effects but instead produce diagnosis-specific outcome patterns, challenging uniform assessments of their effectiveness.

## Key Entities
- **Programme for the Integration of Mental Health Data (PRIMHD)** (type: dataset) — National mental health information system providing demographic, diagnostic, and service use data for individuals under CTOs in New Zealand.
- **Pharmaceutical Collection** (type: dataset) — National database supplying psychiatric medication dispensing records, linked to CTO recipients via unique identifiers from PRIMHD.
- **Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992** (type: law/policy) — Legislation governing compulsory mental health treatment in New Zealand, defining eligibility for CTOs based on being "mentally disordered."
- **Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988** (type: law/policy) — Alternative legislation permitting compulsory treatment, particularly relevant for dementia patients based on impaired capacity rather than mental disorder.
- **SPSS version 27 for Windows** (type: tool) — Statistical software used to conduct Poisson regression and calculate rate ratios comparing outcomes during and outside CTO periods.

## Key Concepts
- **Community Treatment Order (CTO)** — A legal mechanism enabling compulsory psychiatric treatment in the community without patient consent, aimed at reducing hospital readmissions for severe mental illness.
- **Outcome Signature** — The distinct pattern of clinical outcomes (e.g., admission frequency, duration, medication use) associated with CTOs that varies by diagnostic group, indicating differential effectiveness.
- **Rate Ratio (RR)** — A statistical measure comparing the incidence of an outcome during CTO periods to periods without CTOs; values below 1 indicate reduced likelihood on CTO, above 1 indicate increased likelihood.
- **Depot Antipsychotic Medication** — Long-acting injectable antipsychotics administered intermittently (e.g., every 2–4 weeks), whose dispensing increased markedly during CTOs across all diagnostic groups.
- **Revolving Door Admissions** — Cycles of repeated psychiatric hospitalization and discharge due to inadequate community treatment adherence or effectiveness; reduced in psychotic disorder patients under CTOs.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Community Treatment Orders produce diagnosis-specific outcome patterns, reducing psychiatric admissions only for psychotic disorders while increasing admissions and medication use across all other diagnostic groups.
  - **Evidence**: Analysis of 14,726 individuals using national linked data (PRIMHD and Pharmaceutical Collection) showed psychotic disorder patients had 18% fewer admissions (RR = 0.82) and 19% fewer bed days (RR = 0.81) on CTOs. In contrast, major depressive disorder patients had over double the admissions (RR = 2.02) and bed days (RR = 2.21). Depot antipsychotic dispensing increased across all groups, reaching 8.34 times higher in major depressive disorder.
  - **Strength**: Strong due to large, population-based sample and use of routinely collected national data minimizing selection bias; within-person comparisons (on vs. off CTO) help control for time-invariant confounding. Limited by observational design (no randomization), potential confounding by indication (CTOs initiated during high-risk periods), and lack of functional or symptom outcomes to assess clinical meaningfulness beyond utilization metrics.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- While CTOs reduce admissions for psychotic disorders (supporting their goal of preventing revolving door admissions), they increase admissions for non-psychotic groups despite higher medication and service use, raising questions about whether increased intervention reflects clinical need or potential harm.
- Contrasts with some RCTs (e.g., OCTET) showing no clear CTO benefit, but aligns with cohort studies finding reduced admissions in psychotic populations; differences may stem from RCT exclusions of high-risk patients and enrichment with extra services.
- Ethical tension arises from increased depot antipsychotic use in non-psychotic disorders (e.g., major depressive disorder) where such medications are not first-line, suggesting potential overmedicalization under coercion.
- The substantial "no diagnosis" group (21.4%) may reflect data reporting gaps in PRIMHD rather than true absence of illness, possibly biasing results if these individuals actually have undiagnosed conditions.

## Open Questions
- Does longer duration of CTO exposure eventually reduce admissions in non-psychotic disorder groups, or does risk merely accumulate over time?
- To what extent are increased admissions during CTOs reflective of true clinical deterioration versus detection bias (i.e., more contact leading to greater identification of need)?
- How do ethnic disparities (e.g., overrepresentation of Māori in psychotic disorder CTOs) interact with diagnostic patterns and outcomes?
- Can functional or recovery-oriented outcomes (beyond utilization metrics like admissions and medication use) be integrated into future studies to better assess CTO impact?
