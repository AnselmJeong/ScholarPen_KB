# Alternatives to Coercion in Mental Health Settings

## Summary
This chapter examines alternatives to coercion in mental health care across inpatient, community-based, and treatment-wide settings. It defines coercion as the use of force (e.g., restraint, seclusion, involuntary medication) that overrides consent and undermines autonomy, dignity, and recovery. The authors argue that coercion is often linked to trauma and erodes therapeutic trust, necessitating alternatives grounded in patient autonomy, therapeutic alliance, and trauma sensitivity. The chapter evaluates evidence for interventions including sensory rooms, open doors policies, advance directives, patient-controlled admissions, recovery colleges, peer support services, crisis intervention teams (CITs), Soteria/respite houses, shared decision-making, Open Dialogue teams, and trauma-informed care (TIC). Evidence shows these alternatives can reduce coercive practices, improve patient satisfaction, and strengthen therapeutic relationships, though implementation varies by context and resource availability.

## Key Entities
- **Recovery colleges** (organization): Provide education and co-production programs led by individuals with lived experience to promote empowerment and reduce reliance on coercive interventions.
- **Peer support services** (organization): Deliver support through individuals with lived experience of mental health challenges, fostering connection, reducing isolation, and decreasing coercion via mutual aid.
- **Crisis Intervention Teams (CITs)** (organization): Police-mental health partnerships trained in de-escalation to divert individuals from the criminal justice system and reduce use of force during mental health crises.
- **Soteria and respite houses** (organization): Non-medical, home-like residential settings offering peer-supported care for individuals in acute distress, aiming to avoid hospitalization and coercion.
- **Sensory rooms** (tool/environment): Therapeutic spaces with adjustable sensory stimuli (light, sound, touch) designed to reduce distress and prevent crisis escalation without coercion.
- **Open doors policy** (policy/tool): Facility policy maintaining unlocked doors to promote freedom of movement, transparency, and trust, reducing reliance on seclusion and restraint.
- **Advance Directives (ADs)** (tool): Legal documents allowing individuals to specify future treatment preferences, supporting autonomy during periods of impaired decision-making.
- **Patient-controlled admissions (PCAs)** (tool): Framework allowing patients to initiate their own hospital admission, enhancing autonomy and preventing crises through self-knowledge.
- **Shared and supported decision-making** (framework/tool): Collaborative process where patients and providers jointly develop treatment plans, respecting patient expertise and improving adherence.
- **Open Dialogue (OD) teams** (organization/tool): Network-based, dialogical approach involving immediate response, social network inclusion, and flexible meeting locations to foster trust and reduce coercion.
- **Trauma-informed care (TIC)** (framework): Organizational approach integrating trauma awareness to prevent re-traumatization, promote safety, and support empowerment in care delivery.

## Key Concepts
- **Patient autonomy**: The capacity to make informed, voluntary decisions about one’s care and life; central to all alternatives as coercion directly undermines this capacity.
- **Therapeutic alliance**: The collaborative, trusting relationship between patient and provider; consistently weakened by coercion and strengthened by alternatives like Open Dialogue and shared decision-making.
- **Trauma-informed care**: A framework recognizing trauma’s widespread impact and avoiding re-traumatization through safety, trust, and empowerment; positioned as a foundational enabler for other alternatives.
- **Coercion**: The application of force (physical, pharmacological, or procedural) to override consent in mental health care; linked to worsened outcomes, trauma, and eroded trust.
- **Recovery-oriented approach**: A model emphasizing hope, agency, and personal goals beyond symptom reduction; underpins recovery colleges, peer support, and alternatives focused on empowerment.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion harms patient autonomy, trust, and recovery; alternatives prioritizing autonomy, relationships, and trauma sensitivity reduce coercion and improve outcomes across settings.
- **Evidence**: 
  - Sensory rooms reduced distress and aggression (Björkdahl et al., 2016), with staff reporting less coercion (Haig & Hallett, 2023).
  - Open doors policies decreased seclusion and restraint (Kowalinski et al., 2019) and improved staff collaboration (Steinert et al., 2019).
  - Advance directives improved satisfaction and therapeutic alliance (Barbui et al., 2021; Braun et al., 2023).
  - Peer support reduced coercion and enhanced empowerment (Johnson & Rogers, 2020).
  - CITs decreased police use of force and increased service access (Marcus & Stergiopoulos, 2022).
  - Soteria/respite houses lowered coercion and improved outcomes (Stupak & Dobroczyński, 2019).
  - Shared decision-making led to better adherence and less coercion (Penzenstadler et al., 2020; Stone et al., 2020).
  - Open Dialogue reduced coercion and strengthened alliances (Sunthararajah et al., 2022).
  - Trauma-informed care reduced seclusion/restraint and improved engagement (Norman, 2022).
- **Strength**: Evidence is consistent in showing reduced coercion and improved relational outcomes, though support varies—strong for peer support and advance directives, emerging for Soteria and PCAs, with most requiring context-specific implementation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Resource and training dependencies**: Alternatives such as CITs, Soteria houses, and Open Dialogue require significant investment in training and systemic change, which may limit accessibility in under-resourced settings.
- **Variable applicability**: No single alternative suits all patients or contexts—for example, open doors may not be safe for individuals at high risk of elopement, and advance directives may be less useful during rapid symptom fluctuations.
- **Evidence heterogeneity**: While some interventions (e.g., peer support, advance directives) have robust support, others (e.g., Soteria, OD) rely on smaller or emerging studies, requiring cautious generalization.

## Open Questions
- How can resource-constrained mental health systems equitably implement intensive alternatives like Open Dialogue or Soteria models without exacerbating access disparities?
- What is the optimal sequencing or combination of alternatives (e.g., should trauma-informed training precede Open Dialogue implementation) for maximum impact on reducing coercion?
