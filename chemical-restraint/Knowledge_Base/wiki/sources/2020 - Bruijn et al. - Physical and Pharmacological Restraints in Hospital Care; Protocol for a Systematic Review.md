# Physical and Pharmacological Restraints in Hospital Care: Protocol for a Systematic Review

## Summary
This document presents a protocol for a systematic review investigating the beneficial and adverse effects of physical and pharmacological restraints in hospital settings. Physical restraints are defined as measures limiting freedom of movement (e.g., bed rails, abdominal restraints), while pharmacological restraints involve drugs used to control behavior without treating a medical condition (e.g., benzodiazepines, antipsychotics for sedation). The authors note increasing concern about restraint use in hospitals despite a lack of consensus on guidelines and limited evidence from non-hospital settings (e.g., nursing homes) that may not generalize. The review aims to fill this gap by synthesizing evidence on outcomes such as length of hospital stay, complication rates, and behavioral measures to inform clinical practice and guideline development.

The methodology follows PRISMA-P guidelines, with searches in MEDLINE, Embase, and PsycINFO, supplemented by reference scanning and VOS analysis for search refinement. Eligible studies include observational designs and, if available, RCTs conducted in general hospital wards (excluding ICU and psychiatric settings). Data will be extracted on patient characteristics, interventions, comparators, and predefined outcomes. Risk of bias will be assessed using the Cochrane Collaboration Tool (for RCTs) and Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (for observational studies). If data permit, quantitative synthesis via meta-analysis using Comprehensive Meta-analysis software will be attempted, with GRADE applied to assess confidence in cumulative evidence. The protocol acknowledges limitations, including likely scarcity of RCTs, challenges in distinguishing therapeutic from restrictive drug use, and the exclusion of ethical analysis despite its relevance to the topic.

## Key Concepts
- **Physical restraints** — Interventions that restrict a person's freedom of movement through physical means, such as bed rails, waist belts, or enclosed beds, used to manage behavior deemed unsafe
- **Pharmacological (chemical) restraints** — Use of sedative drugs (e.g., antipsychotics, benzodiazepines) to control behavior or restrict movement when not primarily intended to treat an underlying medical or psychiatric condition
- **Systematic review** — A structured, reproducible method for identifying, selecting, appraising, and synthesizing all relevant evidence on a specific question, following predefined protocols to minimize bias
- **Meta-analysis** — A statistical technique for combining quantitative results from multiple independent studies to estimate a pooled effect, planned here if data homogeneity and sufficiency criteria are met
- **Risk of bias** — The potential for systematic flaws in study design or conduct to distort findings, assessed here using domain-specific tools depending on study design (RCT vs. observational)
- **GRADE** — An explicit framework for rating confidence in evidence (high, moderate, low, very low) based on study limitations, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias
- **Outcome reporting bias** — A form of bias where study results are selectively reported based on direction or significance, planned to be assessed via comparison of trial protocols (in ICTRP) with published outcomes
- **Publication bias** — The tendency for studies with significant or positive results to be more likely published, assessed through funnel plot analysis if sufficient studies are included in meta-analysis
- **Indirectness** — A consideration in GRADE assessing how closely the evidence addresses the specific population, intervention, comparator, and outcome of interest (e.g., whether nursing home data apply to hospitals)
- **Confounding** — A concern in observational studies where external factors influence both exposure and outcome, noted as a likely limitation due to expected scarcity of randomized trials

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: There is currently no comprehensive literature overview on the beneficial and/or adverse effects of physical and pharmacological restraints in hospital settings, creating an evidence gap for clinical decision-making.
  - **Evidence**: The authors cite clinical guidelines showing low adherence (31% of Dutch nurses, 26.9% of Belgian physicians), lack of national/international consensus, and existing evidence limited to non-hospital settings (nursing homes, psychiatry) with uncertain generalizability.
  - **Strength**: Well-supported by cited literature and observational data on practice gaps; however, as a protocol paper, it presents no new empirical findings, so the claim rests on secondary synthesis and expert concern rather than primary data.
- **Core claim**: The proposed systematic review will generate actionable evidence to guide healthcare professionals in balancing risks and benefits of restraint use.
  - **Evidence**: The protocol outlines a rigorous, predefined methodology (PRISMA-P, duplicate screening, predefined outcomes, bias assessment) designed to minimize bias and produce reliable synthesis.
  - **Strength**: Strong methodological foundation increases credibility of future findings; limited by anticipated scarcity of high-quality evidence (e.g., few RCTs), which may restrict causal inferences and increase reliance on observational data prone to confounding.
- **Core claim**: Distinguishing pharmacological treatment from restraint use (e.g., antipsychotics for psychosis vs. agitation) is conceptually challenging and may introduce misclassification.
  - **Evidence**: Explicitly acknowledged in the text: when treating psychosis with agitation, sedative effects may serve both therapeutic and restrictive purposes, making intent-based classification difficult.
  - **Strength**: Acknowledged as a key limitation; no proposed resolution beyond sensitivity analysis, indicating awareness but not elimination of this threat to validity.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The review excludes ethical analysis despite identifying ethical concerns (e.g., autonomy, dignity) as a motivating factor in the introduction, creating a tension between practical focus and normative dimensions of restraint use.
- Reliance on evidence from nursing homes and psychiatric settings is noted as potentially non-generalizable to hospitals due to differences in patient acuity, staffing, and legal frameworks, yet such data may constitute the majority of available evidence.
- The acknowledgment that drug effects (e.g., sedation from antipsychotics) may serve both therapeutic and restrictive purposes introduces uncertainty in exposure classification that is not fully resolved by the proposed methods.

## Open Questions
- How can research reliably distinguish between the therapeutic use of psychotropic medications and their use as restraints when clinical intent is often dual or ambiguous?
- What strategies might improve guideline adherence for restraint use in hospitals, given observed low compliance even when guidelines exist locally?
- To what extent do cultural, legal, and organizational differences (e.g., legal thresholds for coercion, staff training) influence the prevalence and outcomes of restraint use across healthcare systems?
