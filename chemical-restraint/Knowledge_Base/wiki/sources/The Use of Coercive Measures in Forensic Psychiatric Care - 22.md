# The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care

## Summary
This chapter examines the use of mechanical restraint in forensic psychiatric settings, focusing on legal, ethical, and clinical considerations. It analyzes how mechanical restraint—defined as the application of devices limiting patient movement for behavioral control—can be justified in high-security environments like Rampton Hospital when managing life-threatening self-injury or extreme violence. The authors draw on their experience developing a policy for restricted use, emphasizing compliance with legal frameworks such as the Mental Health Act, Mental Capacity Act, Human Rights Act, and Criminal Law Act (via the "reasonable force" and PLAN-B principles). Ethically, they apply the "four principles plus scope" framework, weighing autonomy against beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice, while acknowledging tensions with patient autonomy and potential trauma. Clinically, they note that restraint is primarily used for self-injury rather than violence in their setting, with monitoring and staff training aimed at reducing misuse, though staff discomfort persists. The chapter concludes that while evidence of efficacy is limited due to ethical barriers to research, mechanical restraint may be necessary as a last resort when governed by strict policies involving oversight, patient involvement (e.g., advance statements), and regular review.

## Key Entities

- **Mental Health Act (MHA) Code of Practice** (tool/guideline) — Legal framework guiding the use of restrictive interventions, emphasizing least restrictive practice, de-escalation, and advance care planning.
- **Positive and Proactive initiative** (tool/guideline) — Department of Health guidance aimed at reducing restrictive interventions, which acknowledges exceptional use of mechanical restraint in high-secure settings for severe self-injury.

## Key Concepts
- **Mechanical restraint** — The application of mechanical devices (e.g., belts, cuffs) to restrict patient movement for the purpose of preventing injury or managing behavior, particularly in cases of self-injury or violence.
- **Least restrictive principle** — An ethical and legal requirement to use the minimum level of restriction necessary to ensure safety, central to justifying intervention choices in mental health care.
- **Proportionate force** — The use of force that is reasonable and necessary in context, grounded in the Criminal Law Act 1967, to ensure legal and ethical compliance.
- **Best interests principle** — A legal standard under the Mental Capacity Act 2005 requiring that actions taken for individuals lacking capacity must benefit them, especially when advance directives are absent.
- **Four principles plus scope** — An ethical framework (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) extended to consider impacts on others, used to analyze the moral implications of restraint in forensic contexts.
- **Coercion Experiences Scale (CES)** — A questionnaire assessing the psychological impact and human rights restrictions of coercive measures, used to evaluate patient trauma and inform care decisions.
- **Last resort** — The principle that mechanical restraint should only be used after all less restrictive interventions have been attempted and failed, a key safeguard in policy and guidance.
- **Advance statements** — Patient-made expressions of future care preferences, used to involve individuals in decision-making about restraint despite potential fluctuations in capacity.
- **Proportionality** — The requirement that any restriction of liberty must be balanced against the risk of harm, ensuring responses are not excessive in relation to the threat.
- **De-escalation** — The use of verbal and environmental strategies to reduce agitation and avoid the need for physical or mechanical restraint, emphasized in policy as a prerequisite.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Mechanical restraint can be ethically and clinically justified in high-secure forensic psychiatric settings as a last resort for managing life-threatening self-injury or extreme violence when embedded in a comprehensive legal, ethical, and governance framework.
  - **Evidence**: Legal compliance is supported by alignment with the Mental Health Act, Mental Capacity Act, Human Rights Act, and Criminal Law Act (via reasonable force and PLAN-B). Ethically, the four principles framework shows potential alignment with beneficence (preventing harm) and justice (protecting others), though autonomy concerns remain; patient involvement via advance statements can mitigate ethical tensions. Clinically, policy implementation at Rampton Hospital shows primary use for life-threatening self-injury, with monitoring reducing misuse, and anecdotal reports indicating some patients feel supported when staff express concern post-restraint.
  - **Strength**: Moderate. The argument relies on policy experience, ethical reasoning, and limited clinical data (e.g., small patient surveys, single case reports), acknowledging the lack of robust evidence due to ethical barriers to randomized trials. The strongest support comes from organizational adherence to guidelines and structured implementation rather than empirical efficacy data.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The source acknowledges that mechanical restraint can cause psychological trauma (e.g., flashbacks, PTSD-like symptoms) and physical harm, yet argues it may be necessary to preserve life in extreme cases, creating an internal tension between beneficence and non-maleficence.
- It notes that while restraint is framed as a last resort, staff discomfort with its use persists even after training and policy implementation, indicating ongoing ethical unease among practitioners.
- There is a tension between the goal of minimizing restriction and the reality that, for some patients, mechanical restraint may be less restrictive than alternatives like long-term seclusion, challenging assumptions about its relative intrusiveness.

## Open Questions
- How do outcomes such as injury rates, mortality, and psychological trauma compare between mechanical restraint and alternatives like seclusion or chemical restraint in high-risk forensic psychiatric populations?
- Can advance statements reliably reflect the care preferences of patients with fluctuating decision-making capacity in forensic settings, particularly during periods of acute psychosis?
- What specific components of staff training (e.g., de-escalation techniques, trauma-informed care, post-restraint debriefing) most effectively reduce adverse psychological effects and improve patient experiences following mechanical restraint?
