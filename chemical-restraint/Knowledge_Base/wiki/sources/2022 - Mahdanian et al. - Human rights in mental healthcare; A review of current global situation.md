# Human rights in mental healthcare; A review of current global situation

## Summary
This review analyzes the global state of human rights in mental healthcare by synthesizing 26 studies published from 2008 onward. It examines tools for assessing compliance (such as the Analysis Instrument for Mental Health and the Beliefs and Attitudes towards Mental Health Service Users’ Rights Scale), evaluates current implementation status across countries, and critiques coercive practices like involuntary treatment and Community Treatment Orders. The authors find that while international frameworks like the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and the WHO QualityRights initiative have driven policy reforms, substantial gaps persist between legal standards and everyday practice. Persistent issues include widespread stigma, coercive interventions experienced as rights-violating, and the continued dominance of substitute decision-making over the CRPD-preferred supported decision-making model. The review emphasizes that legal reforms alone are insufficient without addressing attitudinal barriers, involving people with lived experience, and ensuring accountability through independent monitoring mechanisms.

## Key Entities
- **World Health Organization (WHO)** (org): The WHO's QualityRights initiative is discussed as a central tool for assessing and improving human rights compliance in mental health services globally.
- **United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (org): The foundational international treaty guiding the review, establishing rights such as legal capacity, accessibility, and non-discrimination for persons with psychosocial disabilities.
- **WHO QualityRights Initiative** (tool): A global program launched in 2012 to align mental health services with human rights standards through assessment, training, policy reform, and inclusion of people with lived experience.
- **Analysis Instrument for Mental Health (AIM)** (tool): A 2018 tool designed to evaluate national mental health laws’ compliance with Article 12 of the CRPD on legal capacity and supported decision-making.
- **Beliefs and Attitudes towards Mental Health Service Users’ Rights Scale (BAMHS)** (tool): A 2019 scale measuring mental health professionals’ attitudes toward service users’ rights across dimensions like autonomy vs. coercion and empowerment vs. paternalism.

## Key Concepts
- **Human rights-based approach**: A framework that centers dignity, autonomy, non-discrimination, and participation in mental health policy and service delivery, prioritizing the will and preferences of individuals over paternalistic interventions.
- **Supported decision-making**: A model where individuals receive assistance to make their own choices, aligned with CRPD Article 12, as opposed to having decisions made by others; presented as the rights-respecting alternative to substitute decision-making.
- **Substitute decision-making**: A legal mechanism where a third party makes decisions for someone deemed incapable, often criticized in the review for overriding autonomy and being experienced as coercive when not strictly limited to genuine incapacity.
- **Stigma**: Negative societal attitudes and beliefs toward people with mental health conditions that lead to discrimination, social exclusion, and barriers to care, identified as a persistent obstacle to human rights implementation.
- **Community Treatment Orders (CTO)**: Legal mechanisms allowing compulsory treatment in the community; discussed as frequently experienced by service users as disempowering and rights-violating, despite their intended purpose of reducing hospitalization.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Despite progress in mental health policy and the adoption of frameworks like the CRPD and WHO QualityRights, significant gaps remain in the real-world implementation of human rights in mental health services globally. Persistent issues include stigma, coercive practices, and misalignment between laws and practice (e.g., continued use of substitute decision-making instead of supported decision-making).
  - **Evidence**: The review synthesizes 26 articles from 2008 onward, covering tools (e.g., AIM, BAMHS, QualityRights), status assessments (e.g., studies in Tunisia, Chile, Afghanistan), and critiques of coercive mechanisms (e.g., CTOs). Findings include: incomplete compliance with human rights in community healthcare facilities (Rekhis et al., 2017); gaps between policy and practice in South African prisons (Bantjes et al., 2017); and the limited impact of CRPD ratification without active implementation (Hoffman et al., 2016; Szmukler, 2014).
  - **Strength**: Moderate. The review employs a transparent, systematic search process (608 initial articles, 26 included) and clearly outlines inclusion/exclusion criteria. However, it acknowledges the limited evidence base due to sparse research in this area and relies heavily on qualitative and policy-focused studies rather than large-scale epidemiological data, which affects the generalizability of some conclusions.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The wiki currently treats [[concepts/substitute-decision-making]] and [[concepts/substituted-decision-making]] as neutral or potentially acceptable concepts, but this review argues that substitute decision-making is often experienced as rights-violating and inconsistent with CRPD principles when it overrides autonomy, particularly when used beyond cases of genuine incapacity. This suggests a need to reassess how these concepts are framed in the wiki to reflect critical perspectives from disability rights and human rights frameworks.
- The review notes that even well-intentioned reforms like "fusion legislation" (a single law applying to everyone regardless of disability) may not fully align with the CRPD if they fail to prioritize autonomy and dignity, indicating that structural changes alone are insufficient without concurrent efforts to address stigma, power imbalances, and the inclusion of people with lived experience in decision-making.

## Open Questions
- How can mental health systems effectively scale the transition from substitute decision-making to supported decision-making while ensuring safeguards against genuine risks of harm?
- What are the most effective, evidence-based strategies for reducing stigma among policymakers, clinicians, and the general public in the context of mental health care?
- Is there longitudinal evidence that tools like the WHO QualityRights initiative lead to sustained, long-term reductions in coercive practices and improvements in human rights compliance, beyond short-term gains following implementation?
