# What Is Coercion and Can Its Use Be Justified in Mental Healthcare? An Ethical Analysis

## Summary
This chapter analyzes the ethical justification of coercion in mental healthcare by distinguishing between formal coercion (legally regulated interventions using physical force without consent, such as involuntary admission or restraint) and informal coercion (non-legal psychological pressure like persuasion, threats, or inducements that compromise voluntariness). It examines two primary justificatory frameworks: the harm principle (justifying coercion to prevent harm to others) and soft paternalism (justifying coercion to promote welfare when decision-making capacity is impaired), both requiring conditions of suitability, necessity, proportionality, and procedural justice. The chapter also considers the absolutist position that coercion can never be justified, often grounded in interpretations of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), and explores self-binding directives (SBDs)—a form of psychiatric advance directive where individuals pre-consent to future involuntary treatment under specified conditions—as a potential tool to reconcile autonomy and safety in cases of fluctuating decision-making capacity.

## Key Entities
- **World Psychiatric Association** (org): Issued the 2020 Code of Ethics for Psychiatry, which emphasizes restoring patient autonomy and welfare as the objective of involuntary interventions.
- **World Medical Association** (org): Issued the 2017 Declaration of Geneva, stating physicians’ duty to prioritize patient health and well-being while respecting autonomy and dignity.
- **United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (doc): International human rights treaty whose Committee interprets Article 12 as requiring respect for decisions of persons with disabilities at all times, including in crisis, challenging justifications for substitute decision-making and involuntary intervention.
- **Psychiatric advance interventions** (tool): Includes psychiatric advance directives and self-binding directives (SBDs), discussed as tools to enhance autonomy by expressing treatment preferences in advance, particularly for those with episodic disorders.
- **Szmukler and Appelbaum (2008)** (framework): Proposed a hierarchy of psychological pressure (persuasion, interpersonal leverage, inducements, threats), foundational for analyzing informal coercion and informing ethical evaluation of psychological pressure in clinical practice.
- **World Health Organization (WHO)** (org): Cited for its 1946 definition of health as a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, relevant to defining harm in paternalistic justifications.

## Key Concepts
- **Formal coercion** — Legally regulated interventions involving physical force without consent (e.g., involuntary admission, restraint, seclusion), infringing on autonomy and bodily integrity.
- **Informal coercion** — Non-legal psychological pressure (e.g., persuasion, threats, inducements) that influences decision-making and compromises voluntariness, where context determines whether pressure becomes coercive.
- **Harm principle** — Mill’s principle that power over an individual is only justified to prevent harm to others; requires suitability, necessity, and proportionality for ethical justification.
- **Soft paternalism** — Justifying coercion when a person lacks decision-making capacity to promote their welfare, distinct from hard paternalism which overrides autonomous choices and is generally not justified.
- **Decision-making capacity** — The ability to make informed, voluntary medical decisions; determined functionally for specific decisions at specific times, not as a global trait.
- **Procedural justice** — Fairness in how coercive measures are administered (transparency, respect, opportunity to appeal), which reduces perceived coercion independent of legality.
- **Self-binding directives (SBDs)** — Psychiatric advance directives where individuals pre-consent to future involuntary treatment under self-defined conditions, aiming to reconcile autonomy and safety through advance consent.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion in mental healthcare includes both formal (legally regulated, physical force) and informal (psychological pressure) forms, and its justification depends on context—either via the harm principle (protecting others) or soft paternalism (restoring welfare when capacity is impaired)—but must meet strict conditions (suitability, necessity, proportionality, procedural justice). Some argue coercion can never be justified due to human rights violations (per CRPD interpretations).
  - **Evidence**: Philosophical analysis (Mill, Wertheimer, Nozick); legal/ethical frameworks (WPA, WMA, CRPD); empirical studies on psychological pressure (Szmukler & Appelbaum, Hempeler et al.); clinical observations on procedural justice (Lidz, Katsakou); critiques of CRPD interpretation (Scholten, Gather); evidence on SBDs reducing involuntary admissions (Tinland, de Jong).
  - **Strength**: Strong theoretical grounding and integration of legal, ethical, and empirical perspectives. Acknowledges counterarguments (e.g., CRPD absolutism) and contextual nuances (e.g., SBDs as compromise). Less emphasis on quantitative efficacy data but robust in normative reasoning.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension**: Between paternalist justifications (soft paternalism) and CRPD-based absolutist views (no coercion ever justified), especially regarding decision-making capacity and advance directives.
  - *Example*: CRPD Committee’s view that advance refusals must always be honored (even in crisis) vs. clinicians’ view that honoring them may undermine long-term autonomy and recovery.
- **Internal caveat**: Authors note that even justified coercion risks harm (trauma, eroded trust) and must be minimized; justification does not eliminate ethical cost.
- **Tension in SBDs**: While promoted to enhance autonomy, their effectiveness depends on legal recognition (rare outside Netherlands) and accurate anticipation of future states.
