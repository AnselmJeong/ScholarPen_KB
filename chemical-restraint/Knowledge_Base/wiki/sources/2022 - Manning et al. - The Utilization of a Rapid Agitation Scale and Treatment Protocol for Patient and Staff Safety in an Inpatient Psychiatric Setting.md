# The Utilization of a Rapid Agitation Scale and Treatment Protocol for Patient and Staff Safety in an Inpatient Psychiatric Setting

## Summary
This study evaluated the implementation of the Modified Agitation Severity Scale (MASS) and its associated treatment protocol in an adult inpatient psychiatric setting to assess impact on patient and staff safety. The MASS was developed by modifying an existing agitation severity scale through item reorganization, binary scoring, and clinical weighting to enable rapid (<1 minute) assessment, then integrated into the electronic medical record (EMR) as a physician-ordered protocol guiding behavioral and pharmacological interventions based on score ranges. Over a three-month intervention phase compared to baseline, researchers analyzed EMR data from 742 patients along with staff and patient surveys. The MASS demonstrated strong convergent validity with the Clinical Global Impressions-Aggression Scale (CGI-A, r=0.75), showed no significant increase in nurse burnout despite added twice-daily scoring, and revealed a 44% reduction in average restraint duration per incident (from 18 to 10 minutes) over 18 months post-implementation, though seclusion events did not significantly change. Notably, physicians continued to order the MASS protocol for 55% of patients after the study ended, indicating sustained clinical utility.

## Key Entities
- **University of Oklahoma** (org): Affiliated institution where researchers from the School of Community Medicine and Health Promotion Research Center conducted the study, providing academic and methodological support.
- **Saint Francis Health System** (org): Healthcare system whose Institutional Review Board approved the study, ensuring ethical compliance for human subjects research.
- **Electronic Medical Record (EMR)** (tool): Central system used to embed the MASS assessment and protocol as a clinician order, enabling real-time scoring, intervention tracking, and data collection for outcomes analysis.
- **Oldenburg Burnout Inventory** (tool): Validated instrument used to measure nurse burnout across emotional exhaustion and disengagement domains before and after intervention.
- **Clinical Global Impressions-Aggression Scale (CGI-A)** (tool): Established brief agitation measure used to establish convergent validity of the MASS through correlation analysis.

## Key Concepts
- **Modified Agitation Severity Scale (MASS)**: A revised agitation assessment tool derived from the Agitation Severity Scale, featuring severity-based item organization, binary (present/absent) scoring, and clinical weighting (1–10) to enable rapid (<1 min) evaluation tied to treatment decisions.
- **Convergent Validity**: The degree to which the MASS correlates with another established measure of the same construct (agitation), demonstrated here by a strong correlation (r=0.75) with the CGI-A, supporting that MASS accurately captures agitation severity.
- **EMR Workflow Integration**: The process of embedding clinical tools like assessment scales and treatment protocols directly into electronic health records to streamline use, ensure documentation, and enable real-time clinical decision support.
- **Least Restrictive Intervention**: A clinical and ethical principle prioritizing the use of minimal coercion necessary for safety (e.g., medication over restraints), which underpinned the MASS protocol design to prevent escalation through early intervention.
- **Psychiatric Quality Improvement**: Systematic efforts to enhance safety, reduce harm, and improve care processes in mental health settings, framing this study’s aim to reduce restraint use via protocolized agitation assessment.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The MASS demonstrates strong reliability and convergent validity with established agitation measures.
  - Evidence: Strong correlation with CGI-A (r=0.75, p<0.001); internal consistency implied by scale use and development process.
  - Strength: Strong — supported by large correlation coefficient and p-value; limitation: Cronbach’s alpha not explicitly reported for MASS.
- **Core claim**: Implementation of the MASS protocol reduced restraint use without increasing nurse burnout.
  - Evidence: 44% significant reduction in average restraint duration per incident (t=2.00, p=0.047, d=0.41); no significant change in Oldenburg Burnout Inventory scores pre/post (t=0.03, p=0.980).
  - Strength: Moderate — duration reduction shows statistical significance with medium effect size; limitation: restraint incident count reduction was not statistically significant, and burnout assessment used cross-sectional (not longitudinal) design due to staff turnover.
- **Core claim**: The MASS protocol demonstrated sustained clinical adoption after the study period.
  - Evidence: EMR review showed physicians continued to order the MASS protocol for 55% of patients over 20 months post-study.
  - Strength: Moderate — based on real-world usage data; limitation: no causal link established between continued ordering and patient outcomes.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Despite reduction in restraint duration, there was no significant change in seclusion events, creating tension with the expectation that early agitation intervention would reduce all coercive measures; authors suggest low baseline seclusion frequency may have required larger sample to detect change.
- Nurse survey data showed positive trends in safety perception post-intervention that were not statistically significant, highlighting a tension between qualitative improvement and quantitative underpowering due to limited sample size.
- The protocol added twice-daily scoring workload for nurses, yet no burnout increase was observed, suggesting possible efficiency gains (e.g., reduced need for emergent physician consultations) may have offset burden, though this mechanism was not directly measured.

## Open Questions
- Why did the MASS protocol reduce restraint duration but not seclusion frequency, and does this suggest differential mechanisms of action for these coercive interventions?
- How generalizable are these findings to geriatric psychiatric or emergency department settings where agitation etiology and resource constraints differ?
- Does the MASS protocol reduce the use of specific psychotropic medications (e.g., PRN antipsychotics) or accelerate symptom resolution beyond its impact on restraint duration?
