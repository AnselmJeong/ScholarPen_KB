# Key international themes in coercion

## Summary
This 2017 paper examines coercion in mental healthcare across global contexts, arguing that while coercive practices are universal, their forms differ significantly based on economic development, legal frameworks, and cultural norms. The authors synthesize evidence from high-income countries (HICs) and low/middle-income countries (LMICs) to show how coercion ranges from legally sanctioned mechanisms like Community Treatment Orders (CTOs) in the UK, USA, and Australasia to extreme, often hidden practices such as pasung—physical restraint and confinement—in Indonesia and similar settings. The paper emphasizes that coercion is frequently justified as risk management or care facilitation but lacks evidence for improving clinical outcomes. It highlights ethical tensions, particularly around covert medication and the blurring of voluntary versus coerced treatment when service access depends on compliance.

## Key Entities
- **World Association of Social Psychiatry Working Group on Coercion** (org): The authors are affiliated with this international group, which focuses on comparative studies of coercion in mental health systems and produced the edited volume *Coercion in Community Mental Health Care: International Perspectives* (2016).
- **Community Treatment Orders (CTOs)** (tool): Legal mechanisms mandating psychiatric treatment in community settings, discussed as increasingly prevalent in HICs like the UK, New Zealand, and parts of the USA, often justified by improved service access but criticized for enabling coercion under the guise of care.
- **Pasung** (phenomenon): The Indonesian practice of physically restraining or confining individuals with psychosocial disabilities—through chaining, confinement in animal sheds, or similar means—often for extended periods; banned since 1977 but still widespread, highlighted via a 2016 Human Rights Watch report as emblematic of unregulated coercion in LMICs.
- **Human Rights Watch** (org): Cited for its 2016 report *Living in Hell*, which documented approximately 19,000 people in Indonesia subjected to pasung at any given time, providing critical evidence of severe, under-monitored coercion in low-resource settings.

## Key Concepts
- **Coercion in mental healthcare**: Defined as the use of legal, chemical, physical, or informal means to enforce treatment or compliance, often against a person’s will; the paper frames it as a spectrum ranging from formal legal compulsion to embedded social pressures, with manifestations shaped by national resources and cultural attitudes toward mental illness.
- **Pasung**: A culture-specific practice involving prolonged physical restraint (e.g., chaining, confinement) of individuals with psychosocial disabilities in Indonesia and reported in other LMICs; represents an extreme form of informal, unregulated coercion driven by stigma, lack of services, and familial or community-based enforcement.
- **Community Treatment Orders (CTOs)**: Legal instruments requiring adherence to psychiatric treatment in the community, frequently tied to threats of hospitalization; noted for their expanding global use despite limited evidence on effectiveness and ongoing ethical concerns about autonomy and coercion masked as treatment adherence.
- **Covert medication**: The administration of psychiatric medication without a person’s knowledge or consent, often concealed in food or drink; described as legally permitted in some jurisdictions (e.g., UK, USA) under strict conditions but widely used off-label, raising significant ethical concerns about autonomy and deception.
- **Restriction of movement and association**: Includes locked wards, geographic displacement from home, and social isolation; identified as a culturally variable form of coercion, more prevalent in individualist societies (e.g., UK locked wards) but also occurring in collectivist contexts through familial restriction tied to shame or lack of support.
- **Psychiatric restraint**: Encompasses physical and mechanical methods of limiting movement (e.g., straps, cages, chaining); noted as globally used but under-researched, with some trials (e.g., Bergk et al., 2011) examining its effects, yet still prevalent in both institutional and community settings across income levels.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion in mental healthcare is pervasive worldwide but takes distinctly different forms depending on a country’s economic development, legal infrastructure, and cultural context; in HICs, it is increasingly embedded in legal frameworks (e.g., CTOs, secure units) and framed as risk management, while in LMICs, it often manifests as extreme, unregulated practices like pasung or familial restraint due to service gaps and stigma.
- **Evidence**: 
  - HIC trends: Rising use of CTOs in Australasia, the UK, and the USA; growth in secure inpatient facilities; reliance on legal mechanisms to manage perceived risk, with some qualitative evidence suggesting service users perceive CTOs as improving access to care (e.g., in New York).
  - LMIC evidence: Human Rights Watch (2016) report estimating ~19,000 individuals in Indonesia subjected to pasung at any time; references to similar chaining or confinement practices in Africa and Asia (Raboch et al., 2010); reliance on informal coercion due to limited mental health infrastructure and high stigma.
  - Ethical critique: No empirical evidence that coercion improves clinical outcomes; widespread use of covert medication despite ethical prohibitions in many jurisdictions.
- **Strength of evidence**: Strong for descriptive claims about global variation (supported by UN data, HRW reporting, and multi-country surveys like Raboch et al., 2010); weaker for causal claims regarding CTO effectiveness or safety benefits, which the authors acknowledge are contested and lack robust longitudinal validation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension between care and control**: The paper highlights how interventions intended to support recovery (e.g., CTOs promising better service access) can function as coercive tools, creating ethical ambiguity about whether they constitute care or social control.
- **Geographic double standard**: Notes that physically restraining individuals with psychosocial disabilities is condemned in LMICs (via HRW advocacy) but often regulated, normalized, or ignored in HICs despite similar lack of evidence for efficacy or safety benefits.
- **Effectiveness vs. ethics**: Acknowledges perceived benefits of CTOs (e.g., increased service contact in New York) but stresses the absence of proof that coercion improves clinical or social outcomes, questioning the ethical justification for its widespread use.
- **Cultural relativity**: Observes that restrictions on social association may be more socially acceptable in collectivist cultures, posing a challenge to universal human rights frameworks—but does not resolve whether such practices should be tolerated or challenged.

## Open Questions
- How can mental health systems reduce reliance on coercion without compromising perceived safety or increasing risks of harm to self or others?
- What role does poverty play in shaping societal and familial acceptance of coercive practices like pasung or covert medication, particularly in low-resource settings?
- Are there effective, scalable alternatives to CTOs and physical restraint that have been successfully implemented in low/middle-income countries to reduce coercion while maintaining access to care?
