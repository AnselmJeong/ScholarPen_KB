# Variations in definitions used for describing restrictive care practices (seclusion and restraint) in adult mental health inpatient units: a systematic review

## Summary
This systematic review examined how restrictive care practices (RCPs)—physical/mechanical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint—are defined in recent literature from adult mental health inpatient units. The authors searched six databases (Scopus, MEDLINE, PsycINFO, Web of Science, Embase, CINAHL-Plus) for studies published between 2010 and 2023 that included explicit or implicit definitions of these practices. A total of 95 studies were included, with 72 defining physical/mechanical restraint, 65 defining seclusion, and 19 defining chemical restraint. Through inductive content analysis, the review identified six thematic elements that varied across definitions: methods used, reasons or desired outcomes, extent of patient restriction, timing (duration, frequency, time of day), level of patient autonomy, and personnel involved. No universally accepted definition was found for any RCP type. For example, physical restraint ranged from broad definitions like "any physical/manual or mechanical methods" to narrow ones like "holding the person’s arm." Seclusion definitions varied from locked rooms to non-locked isolation with staff supervision. Chemical restraint was least frequently defined and often conflated with treatment (e.g., antipsychotics for agitation). These inconsistencies hinder valid comparisons of prevalence rates and obscure the true extent of RCP use, complicating efforts to evaluate reduction strategies or implement consistent policies. The review concludes that consensus definitions are essential for accurate measurement, ethical practice, and effective policy implementation across jurisdictions.

## Key Concepts
- Restrictive care practices (RCPs) — Interventions like physical/mechanical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint used to manage behavior and reduce perceived danger in mental health settings
- Physical/mechanical restraint — Use of physical force or equipment (e.g., belts, straps) to restrict movement or behavior
- Seclusion — Involuntary confinement of a patient alone in a room or isolated environment from which they cannot leave
- Chemical restraint — Use of medication to control behavior, not primarily for treating medical symptoms
- Conceptual boundaries — The specific criteria (e.g., methods, duration, consent, personnel) used to define what counts as an RCP
- Operational definition — How a concept is measured or applied in practice (explicit or implicit)

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: There is significant variation in how restrictive care practices (RCPs)—physical/mechanical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint—are defined across recent literature, undermining the validity of cross-study comparisons and prevalence reporting.
  - **Evidence**: Analysis of 95 studies (72 defining physical/mechanical restraint, 65 defining seclusion, 19 defining chemical restraint) revealed no universally accepted definitions. Variations occurred across six thematic elements: methods, reasons/outcomes, extent of restriction, timing, patient autonomy, and personnel involved. For example, physical restraint definitions ranged from "any physical/manual or mechanical devices" (broad) to "holding the person’s arm" (narrow), directly affecting measured prevalence. Chemical restraint was least consistently defined, often conflated with treatment (e.g., antipsychotics for agitation).
  - **Strength**: Strong. The review used rigorous systematic methods (PRISMA, PROSPERO-registered, multi-database search, dual screening, thematic content analysis). Findings are directly supported by extracted definition patterns across a large, diverse sample.
