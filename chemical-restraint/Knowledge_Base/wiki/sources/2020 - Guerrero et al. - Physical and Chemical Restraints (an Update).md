# Physical and Chemical Restraints (an Update)

## Summary
This 2020 review article by Guerrero and Mycyk updates clinical guidelines for managing agitation in emergency departments, focusing on the safe and ethical use of physical and chemical restraints. It synthesizes current evidence, regulatory standards from CMS and The Joint Commission, and guidelines from the American College of Emergency Physicians. The authors emphasize de-escalation as the first-line strategy, detail protocols for physical restraint use (including team composition, positioning, and monitoring), and evaluate pharmacological options such as antipsychotics, benzodiazepines, and ketamine for chemical restraint. Special considerations for pediatric and geriatric populations are addressed, highlighting age-related physiological differences and the need for tailored approaches. The paper stresses that restraint should be a last resort, used only when safety is imminently threatened, and must be accompanied by thorough documentation, staff training, and ongoing oversight to prevent harm and ensure accountability.

## Key Entities
- **Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)** (org): Sets federal regulatory standards for restraint use, including training requirements and reporting protocols.
- **The Joint Commission (TJC)** (org): Accreditation body that mandates reporting of restraint-related deaths within 24 hours and evaluates compliance during inspections.
- **American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP)** (org): Provides clinical guidelines for managing agitated patients, including principles for appropriate restraint use.
- **Ketamine** (drug): Dissociative anesthetic with rapid onset (2–3 minutes) used off-label for agitation; noted for efficacy but associated with intubation risk and potential to exacerbate psychosis.
- **Haloperidol** (drug): First-generation antipsychotic commonly used for agitation; carries risks of QT prolongation and extrapyramidal symptoms.
- **Lorazepam** (drug): Benzodiazepine frequently used for agitation; rapid onset via IM route, useful in hepatic impairment, and often combined with haloperidol to reduce EPS.
- **Midazolam** (drug): Short-acting benzodiazepine with rapid onset; used for sedation but may cause hypotension with IV administration.
- **Olanzapine** (drug): Second-generation antipsychotic effective for agitation; associated with hypotension and anticholinergic effects.
- **Project BETA** (initiative): Initiative by the American Association for Emergency Psychiatry promoting best practices in agitation management, including de-escalation and psychopharmacology guidelines.

## Key Concepts
- **De-escalation**: Use of verbal and environmental strategies (e.g., calm communication, reducing stimuli) to manage agitation before resorting to restraints; emphasized as essential first step.
- **Physical Restraints**: Mechanical devices (e.g., leather, cloth) to limit movement; associated with risks like positional asphyxia and injury if misapplied.
- **Chemical Restraints**: Pharmacologic agents (e.g., benzodiazepines, antipsychotics) used to sedate agitated patients; can reduce struggle-related complications when used early.
- **Positional Asphyxia**: Risk of compromised breathing due to body position during restraint (especially prone); a leading cause of restraint-related death.
- **QT Prolongation**: Cardiac electrophysiologic abnormality increasing risk of torsades de pointes; associated with certain antipsychotics like haloperidol.
- **Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)**: Movement disorders from dopamine blockade (e.g., dystonia, akathisia); a common side effect of antipsychotics.
- **Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)**: Rare, life-threatening reaction to antipsychotics featuring rigidity, fever, and autonomic instability.
- **Sentinel Event**: Unexpected event involving death or serious harm; TJC requires reporting of restraint-related deaths within 24 hours.
- **Least Restrictive Principle**: Ethical and legal standard requiring use of only the minimum restraint necessary to ensure safety.
- **Excited Delirium**: Syndrome of extreme agitation, autonomic instability, and sudden death risk, often linked to stimulant use; controversial in medical and legal contexts.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **De-escalation must precede restraint use**
  - *Claim*: Verbal and environmental de-escalation strategies should always be attempted first; restraints are only justified when safety is imminently threatened.
  - *Evidence*: Cites ACEP guidelines, Project BETA consensus, and clinical reasoning that failure to de-escalate increases restraint use and patient distress.
  - *Strength*: Strong — grounded in consensus guidelines, trauma-informed care, and injury prevention principles; aligns with human rights and safety ethics.

- **Physical restraints carry significant risks but may be necessary**
  - *Claim*: While improper application can cause injury or death (e.g., via positional asphyxia or rhabdomyolysis), properly applied restraints prevent greater harm from uncontrolled agitation.
  - *Evidence*: References TJC sentinel event data on restraint-related deaths and injuries, describes mitigation techniques (e.g., lateral positioning, team training, monitoring), and notes abrasions as most common complication.
  - *Strength*: Moderate to strong — mechanistic risks are well-established; prevention strategies are plausible but depend heavily on training and protocol adherence.

- **Chemical restraints can reduce reliance on physical restraints and associated risks**
  - *Claim*: Early sedative use avoids patient struggle against physical restraints, thereby reducing risks like hyperthermia, acidosis, and exhaustion.
  - *Evidence*: Notes that combinations (e.g., haloperidol + lorazepam) improve efficacy and lower EPS incidence; ketamine offers rapid onset but requires airway vigilance.
  - *Strength*: Moderate — based on clinical pharmacology and expert consensus; direct comparative effectiveness data are limited for emerging agents like ketamine.

- **Special populations require tailored approaches**
  - *Claim*: Pediatric and geriatric patients have distinct physiological, pharmacokinetic, and developmental needs necessitating adjusted dosing, monitoring, and non-pharmacologic strategies.
  - *Evidence*: Notes altered metabolism in elderly, communication barriers in pediatrics, and lack of robust safety/efficacy data in these groups; recommends caution and individualized assessment.
  - *Strength*: Moderate — biologically plausible; acknowledges evidence gaps, supporting the need for further research.

- **Documentation, training, and oversight are critical for safety**
  - *Claim*: Thorough records of de-escalation attempts, restraint indication, timing, and monitoring reduce errors and support accountability and regulatory compliance.
  - *Evidence*: References ACEP and TJC requirements for documentation, staff training, and death reporting; links poor compliance to sentinel events.
  - *Strength*: Strong — rooted in regulatory standards and medicolegal best practices; essential for quality improvement and liability prevention.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The guidance upholds restraint as a last resort but acknowledges its use when patients lack decision-making capacity, creating tension with autonomy-respecting models like supported decision-making or advance directives in psychiatric care.
- Ketamine is presented as a promising rapid-onset agent, yet the text notes it may increase intubation needs and exacerbate schizophrenia — contrasting with its growing off-label use and limited long-term safety data in behavioral emergencies.
- While warning against viewing chemical sedation as a "chemical straitjacket," the article does not deeply critique potential for over-sedation or coercive use under the guise of treatment, especially in resource-constrained settings.
- The authors explicitly state that evidence on risks and efficacy of restraints in pediatric and geriatric populations is limited, yet clinical practices are often extrapolated from adult data — creating a tension between clinical necessity and evidence-based caution.

## Open Questions
- What is the real-world impact of structured de-escalation programs (e.g., Project BETA) on reducing restraint use and disparities across patient demographics in emergency departments?
- How can objective agitation assessment tools (e.g., PsychoMotor Agitation Scale, Bates/Akutag) be integrated into clinical workflows to guide more precise and less coercive intervention thresholds?
- What are the long-term psychological and trauma-related outcomes for patients subjected to physical or chemical restraints in emergency settings, particularly in pediatric and geriatric populations?
- Can predictive analytics or early warning systems identify patients at high risk for agitation before escalation, enabling preventive interventions that reduce reliance on restraint?
