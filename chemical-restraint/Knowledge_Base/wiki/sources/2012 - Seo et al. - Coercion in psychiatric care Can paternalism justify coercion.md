# Coercion in psychiatric care: Can paternalism justify coercion?

## Summary
This study examined whether psychiatric coercion can be justified by the three core assumptions of paternalism: patient incompetence, dangerousness to self/others, and illness-related impairment. Researchers surveyed 248 hospitalized psychiatric patients in Korea, measuring coercion through three lenses: legal status (voluntary vs. involuntary admission), subjective experience (perceived coercion via the Korean-adapted Perceived Coercion Scale), and objective experience (frequency of threats, violence, restraint, or forced medication via the Experienced Coercive Measures scale). Patient competence was assessed using the newly developed Korean Assessment Tool of Competence to Consent to Psychiatric Hospitalization (KATOC), dangerousness via the Overt Aggression Scale, and impairment via the Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale and Global Assessment of Functioning Scale. The study found that while incompetence showed some association with all coercion types, over a third of coerced patients were actually competent to consent; dangerousness showed no significant association with any coercion measure; and impairment only correlated with objectively experienced coercive measures, not legal status or subjective perceptions. These findings challenge the blanket use of paternalism to justify psychiatric coercion, particularly undermining the public and clinical belief that dangerousness warrants restrictive interventions.

## Key Entities
- **Korean Assessment Tool of Competence to Consent to Psychiatric Hospitalization (KATOC)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Used to evaluate patient competence to consent; classified 44% of the 248 patients as competent and 56% as incompetent based on subscale cut-offs in understanding, application, reasoning, and expression.
- **Overt Aggression Scale (OAS)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Employed to measure dangerousness across four subscales (verbal aggression, physical aggression against objects, self, and others); showed no significant differences across any coercion groups in this study.
- **Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale (BPRS)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Utilized to assess psychotic symptom severity (0-108 scale); found significantly higher in patients experiencing high levels of objective coercive measures.
- **Global Assessment of Functioning Scale (GAF)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Applied to measure psychosocial functioning (0-100 scale); found significantly lower in patients reporting high frequency of coercive interventions like restraint or forced medication.
- **Perceived Coercion Scale (PCS)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Adapted from the MacArthur Admission Experience Survey for Korean culture; measured subjective feelings of coercion via five items on a 4-point Likert scale (e.g., whether hospitalization aligned with one's will or freedom).
- **Experienced Coercive Measures (ECM)** (tool: Assessment instrument) — Captured objective frequency of coercive actions (threats, physical violence, restraint, forced medication) on a 4-point Likert scale; the only coercion domain where impairment showed a significant association.
- **Legal Status** (variable: Hospitalization classification) — Distinguished voluntary (16.1%) from involuntary (83.9%) admission; used as a proxy for coercion but found poorly aligned with patient experiences of coercion.
- **Korean Mental Health Act** (policy: National legislation) — Cited as the legal basis for involuntary hospitalization in Korea, permitting psychiatrists to proceed with primary caretaker consent if the patient lacks competence; noted for its lack of specific criteria for assessing competence, symptom severity, or dangerousness.

## Key Concepts
- **Paternalism** — Defined as the intentional overriding of a person's preferences or actions by another, justified by benefiting or preventing harm to that person; operates via beneficence and non-maleficence principles. In this study, it is tested through its three assumptions: incompetence, dangerousness, and impairment as justifications for psychiatric coercion.
- **Incompetence** — The assumption that individuals with mental illness lack the capacity to make sound treatment decisions, thereby justifying limits on autonomy for their well-being. The study found this assumption partially supported coercion across all types but was undermined by the finding that 39-44% of coerced patients were competent to consent.
- **Dangerousness** — The presumption that mentally ill individuals pose a risk of harm to themselves or others, warranting coercive intervention for safety. This assumption received no support in the study, showing no significant association with legal status, perceived coercion, or experienced coercive measures.
- **Impairment** — The idea that severe psychiatric symptoms and dysfunctional psychosocial states necessitate coercive intervention to enable effective treatment. This assumption only justified objectively measured coercive experiences (e.g., restraint, forced medication), not legal admission status or subjective feelings of coercion.
- **Perceived Coercion (PC)** — The subjective experience of feeling coerced into psychiatric treatment, independent of legal status; measured via patient self-report on feelings of voluntariness, choice, and freedom during admission.
- **Experienced Coercive Measures (ECM)** — Objective occurrences of coercive actions during hospitalization, including threats, physical violence, physical restraint, and forced medication; the only form of coercion significantly linked to clinical impairment (higher BPRS, lower GAF).
- **Legal Status as Coercion Proxy** — The use of formal admission status (voluntary/involuntary) as a stand-in for assessing whether coercion occurred; found inadequate, as many voluntarily admitted patients reported high perceived or experienced coercion, and many involuntarily admitted patients did not.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The assumption of incompetence incompletely justifies psychiatric coercion, as over a third of coerced patients demonstrated capacity to consent; dangerousness fails to justify any form of coercion; impairment only justifies objectively experienced coercive measures like restraint or forced medication, not legal status or subjective feelings of coercion.
  - **Evidence for incompetence**: Significant associations found via chi-square tests: involuntary hospitalization (60.6% incompetent vs. 39.4% competent; χ²=10.74, p=.001), high perceived coercion group (64.0% incompetent; χ²=9.54, p=.002), and high experienced coercive measures group (66.2% incompetent; χ²=5.43, p=.02).
  - **Evidence against dangerousness**: No significant differences in any Overt Aggression Scale subscale (verbal aggression, aggression against objects, self, or others) across legal status, high/low perceived coercion, or high/low experienced coercive measures groups (all F-tests non-significant, p>.05).
  - **Evidence for impairment in ECM only**: High experienced coercive measures group showed significantly worse psychotic symptoms (BPRS: 30.37 vs. 25.48; F=4.96, p<.05) and poorer psychosocial functioning (GAF: 42.53 vs. 47.61; F=6.84, p<.01); no such differences were found for legal status or perceived coercion groups.
  - **Strength and limitations**: The study uses culturally adapted, reliable instruments (Cronbach’s α .70-.93) and a sufficient sample size (n=248) for subgroup analysis. However, its cross-sectional design limits causal inferences, the hospital-based sample may exclude high-risk patients who refused participation, and staff-rated dangerousness (OAS) may not reflect actual behavioral risk, potentially biasing results toward null findings for dangerousness.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The study finds that experienced coercive measures (ECM) are associated with worse symptom severity (higher BPRS) and lower functioning (lower GAF), which contradicts claims in some literature that coercion reduces long-term readmission (e.g., Pribe et al., 2009) or improves psychosocial adaptation (e.g., Gove & Fain, 1977), instead aligning with research showing coercion exacerbates illness (e.g., Bindman et al., 2005).
- While the assumption of incompetence showed statistical links to all coercion types, the substantial minority (39-44%) of coerced patients who were competent creates an internal tension: it undermines incompetence as a reliable or ethical blanket justification, suggesting that coercion may often violate autonomy even when capacity is present.
- Legal status (83.9% involuntary) poorly matched patient experiences: only 45.1% of involuntarily admitted patients reported low perceived or experienced coercion, meaning a majority either felt coerced despite formal consent or did not experience coercion despite being labeled involuntary — revealing a systemic gap between legal procedures and lived reality.

## Open Questions
- How can dangerousness be objectively and predictively assessed to justify coercion under the paternalist assumption of dangerousness, given that current tools like the Overt Aggression Scale show no association with coercion incidents in this sample?
- What alternative interventions (e.g., enhanced psychosocial interventions, peer support, advanced directives) can reduce reliance on experienced coercive measures like restraint or forced medication while still addressing clinical impairment in acute psychiatric settings?
- In contexts with high rates of involuntary hospitalization (e.g., Korea’s 90.3%), how can systems be redesigned to ensure routine, objective competence assessment — using tools like KATOC — before overriding a patient’s treatment preferences, thereby preventing unjustified applications of the incompetence assumption?
