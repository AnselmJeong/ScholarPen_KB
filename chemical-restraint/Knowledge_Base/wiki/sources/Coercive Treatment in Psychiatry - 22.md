# Coercion and undue influence in decisions to participate in psychiatric research

## Summary
This chapter examines the concept of voluntariness in research consent, particularly within psychiatric research contexts, by analyzing legal, philosophical, and empirical perspectives. The authors argue that voluntariness is compromised only when four conditions are met: an influence must be external to the individual, intentionally applied to determine choice, illegitimate (not falling within expected social or professional roles), and have a causal impact on the decision. The chapter reviews empirical studies, including the authors’ own study of 88 participants across oncology, substance abuse, and other medical research, which found low levels of perceived coercion—only 35% reported receiving offers, 3% reported pressures, none reported threats, and over 90% rated their decisions as completely voluntary on a modified Voluntariness Ladder. The findings suggest that concerns about coercion and undue influence in research may be overstated, though the authors acknowledge limitations such as reliance on self-report and the potential for very high incentives to pose undue influence risks not captured in their study.

## Key Entities
- Institutional Review Board (IRB) (org): US system for reviewing human subjects research; responsible for evaluating protocols for risks of coercion or undue influence.
- Research Ethics Committee (org): International equivalent of IRB; performs similar ethical review functions globally.
- MacArthur Perceived Coercion Scale (PCS) (tool): Measures perceived coercion in psychiatric care via five true/false questions; adapted in the study to assess coercion in research contexts.
- Coercion Ladder (tool): 10-point scale assessing perceived freedom in decision-making; used to evaluate subjective experiences of coercion.
- Voluntariness Ladder (tool): Modified version of the Coercion Ladder used in the study to assess perceived voluntariness of research participation decisions.

## Key Concepts
- Voluntariness of consent: The ability to make a free choice about research participation without external constraints that override personal agency; central to ethical research and informed consent.
- Coercion: External, intentional, illegitimate influence that causally affects a person’s decision; distinguished from mere pressure or incentive by requiring all four conditions.
- Undue influence: Influence that overwhelms other considerations (e.g., excessive payment), potentially impairing voluntariness; context-dependent and not inherent in the offer itself.
- Undue inducement: A subset of undue influence where an offer is so substantial it distorts risk-benefit evaluation; debated in terms of threshold and applicability.
- Objective coercion: Actual external influences that are illegitimate and would be judged coercive by observers, regardless of the subject’s perception.
- Subjective coercion (perceived coercion): The individual’s experience of being coerced, which may differ from objective reality; critical for ethical evaluation of voluntariness.
- Legitimacy of influence: Whether an influence falls within expected social or professional roles (e.g., a doctor recommending treatment); key to distinguishing permissible from coercive influences.
- Causal impact: The requirement that an influence must actually affect the decision outcome to count as coercive; essential for distinguishing incidental pressure from determinative influence.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- Core claim: Voluntariness in research consent is determined by whether influences are external, intentional, illegitimate, and causally effective—not by the mere presence of incentives, pressure, or institutional status.
  - Evidence: Legal analysis of Nuremberg Code and federal regulations, philosophical distinctions on free choice, and empirical data from a study of 88 research participants across multiple conditions.
  - Strength: Moderately strong—supported by convergent findings across self-report measures (offers, pressures, PCS, Voluntariness Ladder) and alignment with prior literature questioning overstated coercion risks; limited by single-site design, reliance on self-report, and lack of behavioral observation, which may underestimate undue influence at very high incentive levels.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Tension with regulatory assumptions: U.S. federal regulations classify “mentally disabled persons” as vulnerable to coercion, but the authors note a lack of empirical support for this in psychiatric populations, suggesting possible overgeneralization from intellectual disability research.
- Internal caveat: The study did not test very high incentives (e.g., life-changing sums), which could still pose undue influence risks, leaving open the possibility of context-specific concerns not captured in the data.
- Methodological tension: The authors critique both purely objective (context-free) and purely subjective measures of coercion, advocating for hybrid approaches that integrate context and experience—yet such methods remain rare due to high resource demands.

## Open Questions
- How do legitimacy and cultural context shape perceptions of coercion in research, particularly regarding the role of trust in researchers or institutions?
- What specific threshold of incentive constitutes undue inducement across different populations and decision-making contexts?
- How can future research balance ecological validity with scalability when measuring voluntariness, given the trade-offs between ethnographic depth and survey-based efficiency?
