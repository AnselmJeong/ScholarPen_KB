# Mandated Psychiatric Treatment in the Community–Forms, Prevalence, Outcomes and Controversies

## Summary
This chapter reframes outpatient commitment as one form of "mandated community treatment" where leverage—applied through housing, financial benefits, or criminal justice systems—encourages treatment adherence in the community. Drawing on a five-site U.S. study of over 1,000 outpatients, it finds that 51% experienced at least one form of leverage in their lifetime, with housing avoidance (32%) and jail avoidance (23%) being most common, while traditional outpatient commitment (to avoid hospitalization) was least prevalent (15%). The chapter synthesizes preliminary findings from the MacArthur Research Network on mandated community treatment, showing that mental health courts reduce recidivism and are preferred by defendants, housing-as-leverage increases perceived coercion but Housing-First models yield comparable satisfaction, and psychiatric advance directives—when facilitated—reduce coercive crisis interventions. It concludes that leverage mechanisms vary in ethical acceptability and effectiveness depending on whether benefits are framed as rights or discretionary privileges, and critiques the overuse of "coercion" as a concept, proposing instead a contract framework to better capture mutual obligation in treatment agreements.

## Key Entities
- **Treatment Advocacy Center** (org): Advocacy group promoting assisted outpatient treatment; cited for positive outcome interpretations in Table 3.1.
- **Bazelon Center** (org): Advocacy group opposing involuntary commitment; cited for skeptical views on outpatient commitment efficacy in Table 3.1.
- **MacArthur Research Network on Mandated Community Treatment** (org): Research initiative providing preliminary empirical findings on outcomes of leverage mechanisms (e.g., financial coercion, housing effects, mental health court effectiveness).
- **Psychiatric advance directives** (tool): Legal mechanism allowing advance statement of treatment preferences; discussed as a means to reduce coercion when facilitated.
- **Outpatient commitment** (tool): Civil court order requiring community psychiatric treatment under threat of hospitalization; one subtype of mandated community treatment.
- **Mental health courts** (tool): Specialized courts offering supervised treatment as an alternative to jail; shown to reduce arrests and increase participant satisfaction.
- **Representative payee** (tool): Mechanism for managing disability benefits via third party; discussed as a source of financial leverage and interpersonal conflict.
- **Housing-First programs** (tool): Housing models not requiring treatment compliance; presented as an alternative to leverage-based housing with comparable satisfaction.

## Key Concepts
- **Leverage** (concept): Use of external contingencies (housing, money, liberty) to encourage treatment adherence; central to the chapter’s reframing of mandated community treatment beyond clinical coercion.
- **Preventive commitment** (concept): Form of outpatient commitment applied to those not currently meeting inpatient criteria but predicted to deteriorate; described as unambiguously coercive due to lack of choice.
- **Mental health court model** (concept): Adaptation of drug court model offering supervised treatment as a jail alternative; associated with reduced coercion perception and better outcomes.
- **Cultural cognition** (concept): Framework (Douglas’ theory) used to explain U.S. public opinion splits on outpatient commitment based on worldview rather than ideology.
- **Financial coercion** (concept): Perceived pressure from representative payee arrangements affecting treatment participation; noted to increase family conflict.
- **Housing as leverage** (concept): Use of housing access contingent on treatment adherence; found to increase perceived coercion but less so in Housing-First models.
- **Contract framing** (concept): Alternative to coercion discourse proposed by Bonnie & Monahan, emphasizing mutual agreement and baseline conditions to determine voluntariness.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Mandated community treatment encompasses diverse leverage mechanisms (housing, finance, justice system) beyond hospitalization-focused models, requiring re-evaluation of coercion frameworks.
  - **Evidence**: Five-site U.S. survey (n>1000) showing 51% lifetime prevalence of any leverage, with housing (32%) and jail avoidance (23%) most common; outpatient commitment least prevalent (15%).
  - **Strength**: Strong epidemiological data; limits include cross-sectional design and reliance on self-report.
- **Core claim**: Mental health courts reduce recidivism and are experienced as less coercive than traditional criminal courts by mentally ill defendants.
  - **Evidence**: Florida county data showing 95% chose mental health court, fewer arrests and jail days post-enrollment, and lower rearrest rates for graduates.
  - **Strength**: Supported by multisite studies; limited by non-randomized selection and potential self-selection bias.
- **Core claim**: Psychiatric advance directives, when facilitated, significantly increase completion rates and reduce coercive crisis interventions.
  - **Evidence**: RCT showing 79% completion with help vs. 6% alone; associated with reduced police transport, involuntary commitment, and emergency medication use.
  - **Strength**: Strong experimental support; limits include single-site trial and need for implementation scaling.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Advocacy group divide**: Bazelon Center sees no benefit beyond service access; Treatment Advocacy Center cites reduced homelessness and arrests—same data, opposite conclusions driven by values.
- **Coercion paradox**: Outpatient commitment reduces hospitalizations and improves medication adherence, yet users report no difference in perceived coercion vs. voluntary treatment, suggesting objective outcomes ≠ subjective experience.
- **Preventive commitment tension**: Labeled "unambiguously coercive" (no choice) yet linked to reduced hospitalizations, challenging pure coercion/benefit dichotomies.
- **Cultural generalizability**: U.S. leverage models (e.g., tying disability payments to treatment) may not apply where benefits are rights-based, limiting external validity in systems like the NHS or social democratic models.

## Open Questions
- How do leverage effectiveness and ethical acceptability differ when benefits are framed as entitlements versus discretionary privileges in mental health systems?
- Can the contract framework (Bonnie & Monahan) resolve ideological stalemates in mental health coercion debates outside the U.S. legal context?
- What specific implementation features (e.g., procedural justice, service availability, clinician training) determine whether leverage reduces or exacerbates perceived coercion and trauma?
