# Safety and Security: A Delicate Balance

## Summary
This chapter examines safety and security in mental health settings through a multidimensional framework that distinguishes between safety (absence of harm) and security (protection against deliberate threats). It argues that traditional approaches focusing narrowly on procedural and environmental controls—such as locked doors, CCTV, and zero-tolerance policies—are insufficient and can undermine therapeutic relationships. Drawing on safety science concepts like Safety-I (reactive, incident reduction) and Safety-II (proactive, learning from success), the authors advocate for a shift toward relational safety and security, emphasizing trust, communication, and mutual respect between service users and staff. The chapter evaluates organizational models—High Intensive Care (HIC), Six Core Strategies, and Safewards—as practical implementations of this relational approach, aiming to reduce reliance on coercive measures while maintaining safety. Ethical and human rights considerations, particularly regarding involuntary admission under the European Convention on Human Rights and the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, are integrated throughout. The analysis concludes that sustainable safety requires aligning procedural safeguards with relational practices that foster engagement, feedback, and shared responsibility.

- **High Intensive Care (HIC) model** (type: tool) — described as an organizational approach combining recovery-oriented and medical models to improve care, minimize coercion, and involve families, with structured components like the High Care Unit and Intensive Home Treatment.
- **Six Core Strategies** (type: tool) — cited as one of three models helping institutions focus on relational safety and security by targeting leadership, data use, workforce development, prevention tools, patient roles, and debriefing.
- **Safewards** (type: tool) — presented as another model promoting relational safety through specific interventions like mutual help, clear boundaries, and de-escalation to reduce conflict and containment in mental health wards.
- **European Convention on Human Rights** (type: tool) — referenced regarding Article 5, which permits detention of "unsound mind" individuals but has been interpreted by the European Court as potentially violated in cases of involuntary admission.
- **Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD)** (type: tool) — cited for its principles opposing involuntary admission by emphasizing autonomy, dignity, and freedom of choice for persons with disabilities.
- **Intensive Care Unit (ICU)** (type: tool) — described as a component of the HIC model providing one-on-one care in a private, unlocked setting near the High Care Unit, used when standard ward care is insufficient for crisis stabilization.

## Key Concepts
- **Safety vs. Security** — Safety is defined as the absence of potential harm (intentional or not); security refers to protection against deliberate external threats. This distinction is foundational to analyzing risks in mental health settings, where both unintentional harms (e.g., falls) and intentional threats (e.g., aggression) must be addressed.
- **Safety-I vs. Safety-II** — Safety-I focuses on minimizing adverse outcomes through reactive risk management; Safety-II emphasizes maximizing successful outcomes by learning from what works. The chapter argues for adopting a Safety-II mindset in mental health care to promote proactive, relationship-based safety.
- **Relational Safety and Security** — Encompasses the quality of interactions between service users and staff, including trust, communication, therapeutic rapport, and mutual respect. Positioned as central to achieving safety for all, not just staff, and as a necessary complement to procedural measures.
- **Procedural Safety and Security** — Refers to policies, procedures, risk management systems, training (e.g., de-escalation), and audits that manage risk. While necessary, the chapter contends these can interfere with relational safety if overemphasized or applied rigidly.
- **APRACE Framework** — A model of social interaction comprising Actor, Partner, Relation, Activities, Context, and Evaluation. Used to analyze interpersonal dynamics in mental health wards, highlighting how power imbalances (e.g., from involuntary admission) and environmental factors affect communication and mutual responsibility.
- **Zero-Tolerance Policy** — Describes strict enforcement against violence with automatic penalties regardless of context. Critiqued in the chapter as potentially harmful because it may erode trust, discourage help-seeking, and undermine therapeutic relationships despite its intent to increase safety.
- **Involuntary Admission** — Admission to psychiatric care against an individual’s will based on perceived danger to self or others. Examined as an ethical dilemma: while it may enhance safety, it violates autonomy and is linked to trauma, with human rights instruments like the CRPD and ECHR questioning its legitimacy.
- **Coercive Measures** — Interventions such as seclusion, restraint, and forced medication used to manage behavior. Critiqued as often harmful and potentially counterproductive, associated with psychological trauma, yet still used as a last resort in crisis situations.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: True safety and security in mental health settings requires balancing procedural, environmental, and—most importantly—relational dimensions, with relational safety being key to mutual well-being of service users and staff.
  - **Evidence**: The chapter integrates theoretical frameworks (Safety-II, APRACE), organizational models (HIC, Six Core Strategies, Safewards), and empirical references showing the limitations of purely procedural approaches (e.g., zero-tolerance policies increasing fear, coercive measures causing trauma). It draws on human rights instruments to frame involuntary admission and coercion as ethically problematic.
  - **Strength**: Strong theoretical grounding in safety science and human rights law. Empirical support from studies on workplace violence, coercion, and service user experiences. However, direct evidence on the long-term effectiveness of relational models like HIC in reducing coercion or improving recovery is less extensive, though presented as promising practice requiring further evaluation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The text acknowledges that procedural and environmental safety measures (e.g., locked doors, CCTV) may increase objective security but can undermine relational trust and feelings of safety, especially for individuals with trauma histories or psychosis, creating a trade-off between protection and therapeutic engagement.
- While advocating for Safety-II and relational approaches as ideal, the chapter still discusses coercive measures and locked environments (e.g., High-Security Room within the HIC model) as sometimes necessary in extreme cases, revealing a tension between aspirational goals and clinical reality in managing acute risk.
- If existing wiki content emphasizes procedural or risk-averse (Safety-I) models as best practice in healthcare, this source challenges that view by arguing such approaches can be counterproductive and that relational and proactive strategies are superior for sustainable safety.
