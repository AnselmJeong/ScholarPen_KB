# Coercive Measures in Psychiatry: A Review of Ethical Arguments

## Summary
This narrative review by Chieze et al. (2021) systematically analyzes ethical arguments for and against coercive measures in psychiatry, focusing on interventions that restrict freedom of movement: seclusion, physical restraint, and involuntary hospitalization. The authors searched multiple databases (PubMed, Embase, PsycINFO, Google Scholar, Cairn.info) using keywords related to coercion, psychiatry, and ethics, ultimately analyzing 99 articles in English, French, and German that addressed ethical aspects of formal coercion in adult psychiatric inpatient settings. Their qualitative analysis identified four main ethical categories: arguments for absolute prohibition (non-negotiable rejection), conditions under which coercion might be acceptable (both supporting and opposing factors), and procedural guidelines for ethical decision-making. The review finds that while some argue coercion always violates inviolable rights like autonomy and dignity, the majority view holds that coercion can be ethically justified in exceptional circumstances when it serves higher-order values—such as preventing harm, enabling long-term recovery of autonomy, or preserving therapeutic relationships—provided that rigorous procedural safeguards (proportionality, necessity, last resort) are followed and a transparent balancing of competing values occurs. The authors emphasize that ethical justification requires more than legal compliance and must involve active consideration of alternatives, with ongoing attention to the impact on the therapeutic relationship and the patient’s subjective experience.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Coercion in psychiatry is ethically permissible only in exceptional circumstances when it serves higher values (e.g., long-term autonomy, safety) after a transparent, balanced evaluation of pros and cons.  
  - **Evidence**: Narrative review of 99 articles identified four ethical categories: non-negotiable rejection, pros/cons under conditions, and procedural safeguards. Consensus exists that coercion requires justification beyond legality, weighing autonomy against safety/beneficence.  
  - **Strength**: Strong. Based on systematic literature search across multiple databases and languages, with transparent categorization. Limitations include narrative (non-systematic) review design and exclusion of informal coercion/medication.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension**: Between autonomy-as-non-negotiable (minority view) vs. autonomy-as-overridable-for-higher-goods (majority view), reflecting deeper divides in bioethics between deontological and consequentialist frameworks.  
- **Internal caveat**: The review acknowledges that subjective patient experiences of coercion (e.g., trauma, loss of dignity) may conflict with objective clinical assessments of necessity, yet no consensus exists on how to measure or integrate these perspectives into decision-making.  
- **Gap in evidence**: Despite frequent justification of coercion on safety grounds, long-term outcome data on whether coercion actually improves safety, recovery, or autonomy remains scarce, creating a tension between procedural ethics and empirical validation.  

## Open Questions
- How can subjective patient experiences of coercion (e.g., trauma, perceived dignity violation) be reliably measured and incorporated into ethical decision-making processes?  
- What empirical evidence exists regarding the long-term impact of coercive measures on patients’ recovery of autonomy, therapeutic alliance, and reintegration into community life?  
- How do cultural and jurisdictional differences in mental health law and ethical norms affect the application of principles like proportionality and relational autonomy in coercion decisions?  
- To what extent can advance directives or shared decision-making protocols reduce the ethical tensions surrounding coercion by aligning interventions with previously expressed patient values?  
- Is there a meaningful ethical distinction between coercion used for immediate crisis containment versus coercion framed as part of a long-term therapeutic strategy aimed at autonomy recovery?
