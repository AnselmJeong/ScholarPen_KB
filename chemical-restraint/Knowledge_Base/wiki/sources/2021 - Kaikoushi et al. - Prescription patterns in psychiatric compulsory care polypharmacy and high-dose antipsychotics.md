# Prescription patterns in psychiatric compulsory care: polypharmacy and high-dose antipsychotics

## Summary
This nationwide study investigated antipsychotic prescribing patterns in 482 patients under compulsory psychiatric care at Athalassa Psychiatric Hospital in Cyprus from 2016–2018. The researchers examined polypharmacy (prescribing more than one antipsychotic) and high-dose antipsychotic use (exceeding guideline-recommended limits), with special attention to pro re nata (PRN, "as required") medication schemas. Results showed that antipsychotic polypharmacy occurred in 33.2% of patients when PRN was included versus only 5.6% without PRN, while high-dose antipsychotics were prescribed to 39.2% with PRN versus 27.2% without. Male gender, psychiatric history, receiving state benefits, and negative substance use history predicted high-dose prescribing, while polypharmacy with PRN was additionally associated with negative family psychiatric history and higher PANSS positive symptom scores. The authors conclude that PRN antipsychotic use—particularly haloperidol combined with anticholinergics—may function as chemical restraint rather than therapeutic treatment, highlighting a need for prescribing practice revisions in compulsory care contexts.

## Key Entities
- **Athalassa Psychiatric Hospital (APH)** (type: org) — Sole inpatient facility for compulsory psychiatric care in Cyprus where all study data were collected
- **Statistical Package for Social Sciences (SPSS)** (type: tool) — Software used for statistical analysis including chi-square tests and logistic regression modeling
- **Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS)** (type: tool) — Assessment instrument used to measure psychotic symptom severity in study participants
- **Maudsley Prescribing Guidelines** (type: tool) — Reference standard used to calculate antipsychotic dose equivalents and define high-dose prescribing thresholds

## Key Concepts
- **Antipsychotic polypharmacy** — Prescribing more than one antipsychotic substance concurrently; found in 33.2% with PRN and 5.6% without PRN in this compulsory care sample
- **High-dose antipsychotics** — Total daily dose exceeding upper limit recommended by clinical guidelines (e.g., EMA or BNF); occurred in 39.2% with PRN and 27.2% without PRN
- **PRN (pro re nata) medication** — Medication administered "as required" rather than on a fixed schedule; prescribed to 98.1% of participants and significantly inflated polypharmacy/high-dose rates when included
- **Chemical restraint** — Use of medication to control behavior without primary therapeutic intent; implied interpretation that PRN antipsychotics may serve this function in compulsory care
- **Compulsory psychiatric treatment** — Involuntary hospitalization based on clinical judgment of danger or incapacity; the legal framework under which all study participants were admitted in Cyprus

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: High frequency of antipsychotic polypharmacy (33.2% with PRN) and high-dose antipsychotic prescribing (39.2% with PRN) occurs in compulsory psychiatric care in Cyprus, exceeding clinical guidelines and suggesting inappropriate use.
  - **Evidence**: Nationwide descriptive correlational study of 482 compulsory admissions; polypharmacy with PRN: 33.2% (n=160); without PRN: 5.6% (n=27); high-dose antipsychotics with PRN: 39.2% (n=189); without PRN: 27.2% (n=131); logistic regression identified male gender, psychiatric history, state benefits, and negative substance use as predictors
  - **Strength**: Moderate — large nationwide sample strengthens generalizability within Cyprus compulsory care context, but cross-sectional design limits causal inferences and lacks long-term outcome data
- **Core claim**: PRN antipsychotic schemas (particularly haloperidol with anticholinergics) may function as chemical restraint rather than therapeutic treatment in compulsory care settings.
  - **Evidence**: PRN was prescribed to 98.1% of participants; polypharmacy/high-dose rates increased dramatically when PRN included (5.6%→33.2% and 27.2%→39.2%); authors note lack of evidence for PRN effectiveness and suggest use targets sedation/behavioral control
  - **Strength**: Moderate — supported by high PRN utilization and comparative rate increases, but indirect as study did not measure intent or direct restraint outcomes

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal tension**: Low BMI was associated with high-dose antipsychotic prescribing, contradicting typical antipsychotic side-effect profiles where higher doses correlate with weight gain
- **With literature**: Cypriot PRN-included polypharmacy rate (33.2%) exceeds general literature ranges (17–41.6%), suggesting potential overuse in compulsory contexts despite similar international trends

## Open Questions
- Whether PRN antipsychotic use in compulsory psychiatric care meets the definition of chemical restraint under international human rights frameworks such as the CRPD
- How Cypriot prescribing patterns in compulsory care compare to other European nations with comparable legal frameworks for involuntary treatment
