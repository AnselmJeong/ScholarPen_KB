# Prevalence and variability in use of physical and chemical restraints in residential aged care facilities; A systematic review and meta-analysis

## Summary
This study conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis to estimate the prevalence and variability of physical and chemical restraint use in residential aged care facilities worldwide. A total of 85 studies were included, with 50 contributing to the analysis of physical restraint and 7 to chemical restraint. The pooled prevalence of any physical restraint was 33% (95% CI: 0.31–0.35) and any chemical restraint was 32% (95% CI: 0.23–0.42), both showing extreme heterogeneity (I² = 99.9%). Bedrails (44%) and benzodiazepines (42%) were the most common specific restraints. Subgroup analysis revealed significant regional variation, with the lowest prevalence in mixed regions (20%) and North America (22%), and the highest in Australia (65%). Measurement approach strongly influenced estimates, with direct observation (49%) and combined methods (42%) yielding higher prevalence than record review (28%), staff interview (27%), or administrative data (20%). Meta-regression indicated that North American studies (lower prevalence), direct observation (higher), and combined methods (higher) independently explained 25.5% of heterogeneity in physical restraint prevalence. For chemical restraint, only dementia-specific care and Asian region showed significant univariate associations, but multivariate modeling was not feasible due to limited studies (n=7). The authors conclude that methodological inconsistencies, particularly in measurement and regional differences, substantially contribute to observed variability, and emphasize the need for standardized, valid, and reliable measurement across regions to disentangle true cultural differences from measurement artifacts.

## Key Concepts
- **Systematic review** — A research method that identifies, evaluates, and synthesizes all relevant studies on a specific question using explicit, reproducible methods
- **Meta-analysis** — A statistical technique for combining results from multiple studies to derive a pooled estimate
- **Random effects model** — A statistical model used in meta-analysis that assumes true effect sizes vary across studies due to both sampling error and real differences in effect
- **Heterogeneity (I²)** — A measure of variability in effect sizes due to true differences rather than chance; values >50% indicate substantial heterogeneity
- **Meta-regression** — An extension of meta-analysis that examines whether study-level characteristics (e.g., region, method) explain heterogeneity in outcomes
- **Prevalence** — The proportion of a population exhibiting a condition or behavior at a given time
- **Subgroup analysis** — Analysis of outcomes within categories of studies (e.g., by region or method) to explore sources of variation
- **Confounding variable** — A variable that influences both the dependent and independent variables, potentially distorting observed relationships
- **Risk of bias** — Methodological flaws in included studies that may distort results
- **Publication bias** — Systematic error due to selective publication of studies based on results

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The prevalence of physical and chemical restraints in residential aged care facilities is substantial (33% and 32%, respectively), but estimates vary widely due to methodological differences (especially measurement approach) and geographical region. These factors explain a meaningful portion of the variability, particularly for physical restraint.
  - **Evidence**: Pooled estimates from 50 studies (physical) and 7 studies (chemical); subgroup analysis by region and method; meta-regression identifying North America, direct observation, and combined methods as significant predictors of physical restraint prevalence; extreme heterogeneity (I² = 99.9%).
  - **Strength**: Moderate-to-strong for physical restraint conclusions due to large sample size (n=50), comprehensive search, and exploration of heterogeneity sources; weak for chemical restraint due to limited data (n=7), high risk of bias, and inability to adjust for multiple confounders.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The review includes studies with widely varying definitions and methods (e.g., some include bedrails as restraint, others do not), yet pools them—acknowledging this limits precision.
- High prevalence estimates from direct observation contrast with low estimates from administrative data, suggesting possible under-reporting in routine systems or over-detection in reactive observation.
- Authors note that cultural differences may explain regional variation, but also admit that methodological differences (e.g., Australia’s use of direct observation vs. North America’s reliance on records) could drive apparent differences—making causal interpretation uncertain.
- Chemical restraint analysis is underpowered (n=7), yet prevalence is reported with same weight as physical restraint, potentially misleading readers about certainty.

## Open Questions
- What is the true prevalence of restraint when measured with consistent, validated methods across regions?
- How do specific policies (e.g., US OBRA ’87, German least-restraint) affect actual use when measurement is standardized?
- Is there evidence of temporal trends in restraint use over the 2000–2020 period covered?
- How do resident-level factors (e.g., dementia severity, agitation) interact with facility-level practices to predict restraint use?
- What are the psychological and physical outcomes associated with the most prevalent forms (bedrails, benzodiazepines) in this population?
