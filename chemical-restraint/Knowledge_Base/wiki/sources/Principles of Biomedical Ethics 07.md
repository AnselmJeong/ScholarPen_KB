# Principles of Biomedical Ethics

## Summary
This chapter from *Principles of Biomedical Ethics* examines theories of justice relevant to biomedical ethics, focusing on distributive justice in health care and public health contexts. It analyzes six major theories—utilitarian, libertarian, communitarian, egalitarian, capability, and well-being—each offering a distinct material principle for determining fair distribution of benefits and burdens. The chapter argues that no single theory adequately addresses all justice issues in biomedicine, and that real-world policies often blend multiple principles. It emphasizes the fair opportunity rule as a key redress mechanism for undeserved disadvantages arising from natural and social lotteries, and highlights how theories may conflict in practice, particularly under scarcity, requiring contextual balancing rather than rigid application.

## Key Entities
- **Principles of Biomedical Ethics** (org): The textbook containing this chapter; serves as a foundational reference in bioethics education and research.
- **Utilitarian theories** (concept): A theory of justice emphasizing maximization of social utility; applied to health policy via cost-benefit analysis.
- **Libertarian theories** (concept): Focuses on liberty rights and fair procedures (e.g., free-market exchanges), with Robert Nozick as a key proponent; views justice in process, not outcomes.
- **Communitarian theories** (concept): Justice derived from community conceptions of the good; prioritizes common good over individual rights, relevant to organ donation and public health traditions.
- **Egalitarian theories** (concept): Emphasizes equal access to valued goods and liberty; influenced by Rawls’s difference principle and fair equality of opportunity; underpins health care as fair opportunity.
- **Capability theories** (concept): Focuses on securing essential human capabilities for flourishing (e.g., health, bodily integrity, practical reason), pioneered by Amartya Sen and developed by Martha Nussbaum; relevant to disability and global health justice.
- **Well-being theories** (concept): Centers on realizing six core dimensions of well-being (health, security, knowledge, respect, attachments, self-determination); devised by Madison Powers and Ruth Faden; structural approach linking justice to human rights and power dynamics.

## Key Concepts
- **Justice** — Fair, equitable, and appropriate treatment based on what is due or owed; the central ethical concept guiding distribution decisions in biomedicine.
- **Distributive justice** — Fair allocation of benefits and burdens via norms structuring social cooperation; scope includes health care access, research participation, and resource allocation.
- **Formal principle of justice** — Aristotelian requirement that equals be treated equally and unequals unequally; lacks substantive content without material principles to define relevant similarities and differences.
- **Material principles of justice** — Substantive criteria (e.g., need, liberty, equality) specifying relevant characteristics for equal treatment; essential for applying justice; theories differ on which principles are valid.
- **Utilitarianism** — Distributes goods according to rules maximizing social utility or welfare; rights and entitlements are contingent on net social benefit.
- **Libertarianism** — Distributes to maximize liberty and property through free-market exchanges; justice lies in fair procedures, not equal outcomes; opposes patterned distributions unless freely chosen.
- **Communitarianism** — Distributes according to conceptions of the good shared by moral communities; emphasizes common good and social traditions over individual rights in cases like organ allocation.
- **Egalitarianism** — Distributes equal liberty and access to valued goods; incorporates Rawls’s difference principle and fair equality of opportunity; supports health care as a means to fair opportunity.
- **Capability theory** — Distributes means for exercising core human capabilities essential for a flourishing life (based on Nussbaum’s ten central health-related capabilities); shifts focus from resources to what people are able to do and be.
- **Well-being theory** — Distributes means for realizing six core elements of well-being (health, security, knowledge, respect, attachments, self-determination); justice requires securing these states, not just capabilities for them.
- **Fair opportunity rule** — Individuals should not gain or lose benefits based on undeserved properties (e.g., gender, race, natural talent); descends from Rawls; requires redress for disadvantages from natural and social lotteries.
- **Natural lottery** — The distribution of genetic traits and talents not earned by individuals; used to argue against merit-based distribution and support compensatory policies.
- **Social lottery** — The distribution of advantages or disadvantages via family, education, government, and social structures; combined with natural lottery to explain systemic inequities requiring justice-based correction.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: No single theory of justice fully captures biomedical ethics contexts; a pluralistic approach balancing multiple material principles (utilitarian, libertarian, communitarian, egalitarian, capability, well-being) is needed for health care distribution, especially under scarcity.
  - **Evidence**: Analysis shows each theory has strengths and limitations; real-world policies mix principles (e.g., public health via utility/need, education via equality/need, jobs via merit); historical shifts (e.g., HIV/AIDS activism changing research justice from harm protection to benefit access) illustrate context-dependence.
  - **Strength**: Strong theoretical synthesis with concrete examples (legal cases like *Auto Workers v. Johnson Controls*, policy trends, philosophical developments). However, the chapter acknowledges theories are not fully practical instruments and may conflict in application (e.g., pursuing equal access may undercut free choice). Evidence is robust for conceptual frameworks but acknowledges gaps in real-world coherence and procedural guidance.

- **Internal tension**: The chapter notes that pursuing one goal of justice (e.g., equal access to health care) may undercut another (e.g., free choice in treatment or social efficiency), highlighting the incommensurability of principles in real-world dilemmas.
