# Effect of coercive measures on mental health status in adult psychiatric populations: a nationwide trial emulation

## Summary
This study investigated the impact of coercive measures—seclusion, mechanical restraint, and forced medication—on mental health outcomes in adult psychiatric inpatients using nationwide Swiss hospital data from 2019 to 2021. Employing a trial emulation approach with inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW), the researchers aimed to mimic a randomized controlled trial to estimate the causal effect of these interventions on mental health status at discharge, as measured by the Health of the Nations Outcome Scale (HoNOS). The analysis included 178,369 hospitalizations after exclusions, with 9.2% involving at least one coercive measure. Results showed that exposure to any coercive measure was associated with a 1.91-point increase in HoNOS score (indicating worse mental health), with the strongest effect observed for mechanical restraint (2.83-point increase), followed by forced medication (1.97-point) and seclusion (1.60-point). All effects were statistically significant (p < .001). The study concludes that coercive measures have a detrimental impact on patients’ mental health, challenging perceptions of therapeutic benefit and underscoring the need for alternatives in clinical practice.

## Key Entities
- **Swiss National Association for Quality Development in Hospitals and Clinics (ANQ)** (organization): Provided the national registry data on coercive measure use and patient outcomes across Swiss psychiatric hospitals, enabling the nationwide retrospective analysis.
- **Health of the Nations Outcome Scale (HoNOS)** (tool/dataset): Served as the primary outcome measure to assess mental health severity at hospital discharge, with higher scores indicating worse symptomatology and social functioning.
- **Inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW)** (tool/method): Applied to adjust for confounding variables (e.g., age, diagnosis, compulsory admission) and emulate random assignment in the observational data, supporting causal inference in the trial emulation framework.

## Key Concepts
- **Trial emulation**: A methodological approach that uses observational data and statistical techniques like IPTW to mimic the design of a randomized controlled trial when RCTs are impractical or unethical, enabling causal inference about interventions.
- **Coercive measures**: Interventions in psychiatric care that restrict patient autonomy, including seclusion (confined isolation), mechanical restraint (physical binding), and forced medication (involuntary pharmacotherapy), used to manage imminent risk of harm.
- **Mental health status (as measured by HoNOS)**: A quantified assessment of psychiatric illness severity and social functioning across 12 domains, where scores range from 0 to 48 and higher values reflect greater impairment.
- **Confounding adjustment**: The process of controlling for extraneous variables that influence both exposure and outcome, here achieved via IPTW to reduce bias in estimating the effect of coercive measures on HoNOS scores.
- **Effect size**: The magnitude of the association between exposure and outcome, here expressed as the mean difference in HoNOS scores between exposed and unexposed groups, interpreted in context of the scale’s range (0–48) and clinical relevance.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Exposure to any coercive measure during psychiatric hospitalization is associated with a statistically significant worsening of mental health at discharge, reflected by a 1.91-point increase in HoNOS score.
  - **Evidence**: Derived from IPTW-adjusted analysis of 178,369 hospitalizations across all Swiss adult psychiatric hospitals (2019–2021), comparing those exposed versus not exposed to coercive measures, with adjustment for key confounders including age, diagnosis, prior hospitalization, and compulsory admission status.
  - **Strength**: Strong due to large nationwide sample, longitudinal design, and use of causal inference methods; however, limited by potential reverse causality (worsening mental health increasing likelihood of coercion), unmeasured time-varying confounders (e.g., symptom fluctuation, treatment adherence), and small absolute effect sizes relative to the HoNOS scale (0–48), though authors argue the effects are not negligible given the non-therapeutic nature of coercion.

## Open Questions
- Do the observed harms persist beyond hospital discharge, or are they transient effects resolved post-discharge?
- How do patient-reported experiences and subjective perceptions of coercion correlate with objective changes in HoNOS scores?
- Are there dose-response relationships, where longer duration or higher frequency of coercive measures lead to progressively worse mental health outcomes?
- How generalizable are these findings to countries with higher baseline use of coercive measures, such as the United States or Japan, where cultural and institutional practices differ significantly?
