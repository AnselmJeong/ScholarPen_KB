# Defining chemical restraint A preliminary step towards measurement and quality assessment

## Summary
This scoping review addresses the persistent lack of consensus on defining chemical restraint in healthcare and forensic settings. Analyzing 33 sources from 2006–2020, the authors identify significant variability in terminology and conceptualization, with some sources excluding chemical restraint from statistics or using proxies like PRN medication without validation. Drawing on Donabedian’s Structure-Process-Outcome framework, the study proposes an operational definition clarifying that chemical restraint involves medication administered to control behavior, prevent harm, avoid physical restraint/seclusion, or without consent. The definition aims to enable reliable measurement, support quality improvement, and align with human rights goals by distinguishing coercive intent from therapeutic use.

## Key Concepts
- Chemical restraint — Use of medication to control behavior or restrict movement, not primarily for treating a medical condition, often without consent; central focus of the study
- Intentionality — Distinction between administering medication for therapeutic purposes versus behavioral control; key criterion in defining chemical restraint
- Consent — Administration without the person’s agreement is a defining component, highlighting ethical and legal dimensions
- PRN (as needed) medication — Commonly used proxy for measuring chemical restraint but criticized for lacking validity without contextual validation of intent
- Donabedian’s S-P-O model — Framework used to structure the definition across structural (who/what), process (how), and intentional/outcome (why) dimensions

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Chemical restraint lacks a consistent definition across international literature, hindering measurement, comparison, and quality improvement efforts.
  - **Evidence**: Systematic scoping review of 33 sources revealed wide variation in terminology; some exclude it from statistics, others use unvalidated proxies like PRN or IM medication.
  - **Strength**: Strong — based on rigorous PRISMA-ScR methodology, multi-stakeholder validation, and thematic synthesis; limited by English/French language restriction and 2020 cutoff.
- **Core claim**: An operational definition integrating structure, process, and intent can resolve ambiguity and support measurement.
  - **Evidence**: The proposed definition specifies emergency (STAT), PRN, regular, or continuous medication with sedative properties administered to control harmful behavior, avoid physical restraint/seclusion, or without consent.
  - **Strength**: Moderate — grounded in empirical literature and stakeholder input, but requires empirical testing for reliability in clinical settings.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Some guidelines (e.g., NICE) frame similar practices like rapid tranquillization as therapeutic, which may conflict with rights-based interpretations in the wiki that view non-consensual behavioral control as a rights violation (see [[concepts/human-rights]], [[concepts/autonomy]]).
- The definition includes cases where medication is given “to avoid physical restraint or seclusion,” which may be clinically motivated but still constitutes coercion under rights-based ethics, highlighting ongoing tension between safety imperatives and liberty protections.
- Literature such as Allison & Moncrieff (2014) rejects the concept of chemical restraint entirely, viewing all such medication as therapeutic—reflecting a fundamental divide between biomedical and human rights paradigms in psychiatry.

## Open Questions
