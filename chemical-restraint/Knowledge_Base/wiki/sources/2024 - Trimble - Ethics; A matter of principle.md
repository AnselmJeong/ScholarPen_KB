# Ethics – A matter of principle?

## Summary
This paper analyzes the four-principle approach (principlism) proposed by Beauchamp and Childress, which has become standard practice in UK bioethics. It traces the historical origins of principlism to post-WWII scientific concerns about eugenics and human genetic engineering, leading to the Belmont Report and the subsequent development of Beauchamp and Childress' textbook. The authors selected four principles—beneficence, nonmaleficence, autonomy, and justice—from a broader common morality theory to create a working framework mediating between high-level moral theory and everyday morality. While principlism is ubiquitous in medical textbooks, recommended by the UK Clinical Ethics Network, and used in medical school interview preparation, it faces significant criticisms: it reflects Anglo-American bias, fails when patients lack autonomy, produces inconsistent applications (e.g., in assisted dying debates), cannot detect logical errors in ethical arguments, and risks reducing moral deliberation to a mere checklist. The author acknowledges principlism's influence but argues it is only a starting point for, not the end point of, moral deliberation, with a follow-up paper promised to address its philosophical difficulties in greater depth.

## Key Concepts
- **Principlism** — An ethical framework based on four principles (beneficence, nonmaleficence, autonomy, justice) proposed by Beauchamp and Childress; mediates between high-level moral theory and common morality.
- **Common morality theory** — A set of general moral norms applicable everywhere in life, from which Beauchamp and Childress selected their four principles.
- **Formal rationality** — Decision-making focused on how best to achieve agreed or assumed ends (goals), favored by the scientific community in historical debates about eugenics and human genetic engineering.
- **Substantial rationality** — Decision-making that includes the appropriateness of the ends in discussions, favored by theologians and philosophers in historical debates.
- **Respect for Persons** — One of the three principles from the Belmont Report, defined as acknowledging autonomy and protecting those with diminished autonomy; precursor to the autonomy principle in principlism.
- **Beneficence** — Obligation to not harm and to maximize benefits while minimizing harms; one of the four principles and also in the Belmont Report.
- **Justice** — Defined as fairness in distribution or what is deserved; one of the four principles and in the Belmont Report.
- **Autonomy** — One of the four principles; respect for autonomy is described by Gillon as "first among equals."
- **Nonmaleficence** — Obligation to not harm; one of the four principles.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Principlism is widely adopted in UK bioethics but faces significant criticisms regarding its theoretical depth, cultural bias, inconsistency in application, and inadequacy as a standalone framework for resolving complex ethical dilemmas.
  - **Evidence**: Historical origins in post-WWII scientific community debates about eugenics and human genetic engineering; ubiquity in general medical textbooks, endorsement by UK Clinical Ethics Network, and use in medical school interview prep; criticisms from scholars like Huxtable (Anglo-American bias, inapplicability without autonomy, inconsistency in assisted dying, inability to detect argument errors), Green (avoidance of deep theoretical engagement), and Clouser and Gert (reduction to superficial "chapter headings").
  - **Strength**: Strong evidence for historical development and widespread adoption; moderate for criticisms (based on scholarly critique but lacks empirical data on prevalence of criticisms). The paper presents a balanced view, acknowledging principlism's influence while detailing its limitations.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The paper notes an internal tension: Beauchamp and Childress resist viewing principlism as merely a method, yet Beauchamp himself describes it as such elsewhere, creating ambiguity about its theoretical status.
- If the wiki assumes principlism is a complete or sufficient ethical framework, this source challenges that by arguing it is "only a starting point for, and not the end point of, moral deliberation" and incapable of detecting errors in argument.
- The author indicates a future paper will address philosophical difficulties in greater depth, suggesting the current analysis is preliminary.
