# A descriptive study on the use of restrictive interventions for potentially or actually violent patients in the emergency department

## Summary
This study examined the prevalence and predictors of restrictive interventions (chemical, physical, or mechanical restraints) used to manage potentially or actually violent patients in an Australian emergency department. Using a retrospective design, researchers analyzed data from the Response to Occupational Violence Emergencies (ROVE) database linked to electronic medical records from October 2020 to March 2021. The sample included 1,276 patients who triggered a ROVE response due to aggression risk or actual violence. The primary finding was that 70% of these patients received some form of restrictive intervention, with chemical restraint being most common (oral: 55.2%, intramuscular/intravenous: 47.4%). Multivariable logistic regression identified three independent predictors: intoxication status (adjusted odds ratio 3.48), highest triage acuity (score 1 vs. 4-5: aOR 2.084), and involuntary presentation under mental health legislation (aOR 1.494). Notably, agitation severity could not be assessed due to missing Sedation Assessment Tool scores, limiting interpretation of clinical versus behavioral drivers. Patients receiving restraints had significantly longer emergency department stays (36.6 minutes median increase) and were more likely to be admitted to short stay units rather than discharged against medical advice. The study emphasizes patient characteristics over clinician factors in predicting restraint use and suggests systemic interventions—such as sobering centers or public health policies targeting intoxication—may reduce reliance on restrictive measures in emergency settings.

## Key Entities
- **Princess Alexandra Hospital Emergency Department** (organization): The study setting—a public metropolitan adult tertiary referral hospital in Brisbane with dedicated mental health and toxicology units, serving over 69,000 annual presentations.
- **Response to Occupational Violence Emergencies (ROVE)** (organization): A nurse-led team operating 24/7 in the emergency department that responds to potential or actual occupational violence incidents, maintains the primary database used for patient identification and intervention tracking, and provides early risk assessment at triage.
- **FirstNet database** (tool): The electronic medical records system linked to the ROVE database to extract clinical variables including arrival mode, triage category, involuntary status under Queensland’s Public Health Act 2005, and emergency department length of stay.
- **IBM SPSS Statistics V23** (tool): The statistical software used for all data analysis, including chi-square tests, Mann-Whitney U tests, and logistic regression modeling.
- **Metro South Human Research Ethics Committee** (organization): The institutional review board that approved the study protocol (HREC/2021/QMS/75412), ensuring ethical compliance with retrospective data use.

## Key Concepts
- **Restrictive interventions**: Defined as chemical, physical, or mechanical restraints used to manage patients perceived as violent or at risk of violence in the emergency department; the central focus of the study, examining prevalence, types, and predictors.
- **Chemical restraint**: The administration of sedative medications (oral, intramuscular, or intravenous) to reduce agitation; found to be the most frequent intervention type, affecting over half of restrained patients and associated with risks of respiratory and cardiac adverse effects.
- **Physical restraint**: The manual restriction of patient movement by staff holding parts of the body; used in 31.9% of restrained patients, posing risks of physical and psychological harm to both patients and staff.
- **Mechanical restraint**: The use of equipment such as handcuffs or straps to limit patient movement; the least common intervention (2.7%), requiring collaboration with treating physicians and often involving de-escalation rooms.
- **Triage score**: Based on the Australian Triage Scale (1=most urgent, 5=least urgent); a score of 1 was associated with over double the odds of restraint use compared to scores 4-5, indicating acuity as a key systemic driver.
- **Involuntary presentation**: Referral to emergency department under Chapter 4A of Queensland’s Public Health Act 2005 due to perceived mental health risk; increased odds of restraint by 1.494 times, reflecting legal and clinical urgency in management.
- **Intoxication status**: Presence of alcohol and/or drug use (self-reported, breathalyzer, or blood test); the strongest predictor, increasing odds of restraint by 3.48 times, highlighting substance use as a critical modifiable risk factor.
- **Emergency department length of stay**: Total time from arrival to discharge; restrained patients stayed a median of 36.6 minutes longer, impacting departmental throughput and resource allocation.
- **Response to Occupational Violence Emergencies (ROVE) model**: A proactive, nurse-led intervention strategy embedded in triage to identify violence risk early and coordinate responses with security and clinical staff, forming the basis of data collection.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Restrictive interventions are frequently used (70% of cases) in response to patient aggression in the emergency department, primarily driven by modifiable patient characteristics—intoxication, high triage acuity, and involuntary status—rather than unmeasured clinical agitation due to missing Sedation Assessment Tool data.
  - **Evidence**: Multivariable logistic regression of 1,276 ROVE-triggered presentations showed significant adjusted odds ratios: intoxication (aOR 3.48, 95% CI 1.675–7.21), triage score 1 (aOR 2.084, 95% CI 1.094–3.96), and involuntary presentation (aOR 1.494, 95% CI 1.105–2.020). Chemical restraint dominated (oral: 55.2%, IM/IV: 47.4%), and restraint was linked to prolonged ED stays.
  - **Strength**: Moderately strong due to large real-world sample, confounder adjustment, and 5% data audit with error correction. Limited by single-site design, retrospective nature (susceptible to documentation bias), absence of clinician-level variables, and unmeasured agitation severity, which prevents ruling out clinical need as a confounding factor.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The study notes that restrictive interventions should be a last resort, yet 70% of violent patients received them—suggesting potential over-reliance on coercive measures despite the availability of verbal de-escalation (used in only 30% of cases), indicating a possible systemic preference for rapid control over skill-intensive alternatives.
- Contrasts with prior research (e.g., Wong et al., 2019) that identified agitation severity as the primary predictor of restraint use; this discrepancy may stem from the current study’s inability to measure agitation due to missing Sedation Assessment Tool scores, leaving open whether behavioral or clinical factors dominate.
- Findings may not generalize to emergency departments without specialized ROVE teams, on-site toxicology/mental health units, or comparable prevalences of intoxication, limiting external validity to similar high-resource, specialized settings.

## Open Questions
- How does the presence of a dedicated ROVE team influence the frequency and appropriateness of restrictive interventions compared to models relying solely on security or psychiatric consultation services?
- Would routine documentation of agitation severity (e.g., via Sedation Assessment Tool) change the predictive power of patient characteristics like intoxication or triage score in determining restraint use?
- Do community-level interventions such as sobering centers or assertive outreach reduce emergency department presentations of intoxicated individuals at risk for violence, thereby decreasing reliance on restrictive interventions?
