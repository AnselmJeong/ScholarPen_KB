# The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care

## Summary
This chapter synthesizes research on patient experiences of coercive measures in psychiatric care, particularly focusing on seclusion and restraint in forensic and general psychiatric settings. Drawing on studies from the 1970s onward, it examines how patients perceive interventions such as forced medication, seclusion, and restraint, highlighting that while these are often viewed as unnecessary or punitive, some patients report positive experiences like safety, privacy, or emotional relief—especially when accompanied by clear communication, respect, and therapeutic relationships. The authors emphasize that perceptions vary by factors including age, gender, trauma history, and clinical context (e.g., forensic vs. general psychiatric wards), and stress that patient involvement in care planning and staff training in de-escalation and trauma-informed care are critical to reducing reliance on coercion. Although certain practices may coincidentally correlate with improved pre-discharge quality of life (possibly due to contrast with impending freedom), the authors caution against interpreting this as therapeutic benefit and advocate for prevention through human-centered care rather than reliance on coercive interventions.

## Key Concepts
- **Coercive measures** — Interventions such as forced medication, seclusion, or restraint applied in psychiatric care to manage behavior, often without full patient consent; central focus of the chapter.
- **Patient experience of coercion** — The subjective interpretation and emotional response of individuals subjected to psychiatric coercion, encompassing perceptions of fairness, dignity, fear, safety, or therapeutic value.
- **Seclusion and restraint** — Specific coercive practices involving isolation (seclusion) or physical restriction (restraint); examined for their varied patient perceptions, including both negative (punishment, fear) and positive (privacy, safety) reports.
- **Therapeutic relationship** — The collaborative, empathetic bond between patient and care provider; identified as a key moderator influencing how coercion is perceived, with strong relationships reducing negative experiences.
- **Service user involvement** — The active participation of patients in shaping mental health services, policy, and practice; noted as officially recognized in Finland but poorly implemented in routine care.
- **Psychiatric advanced directives (PAD)** — Legal documents allowing individuals to outline future treatment preferences during periods of incapacity; suggested as a tool to reduce coercion by honoring autonomy.
- **Perceived vs. legal coercion** — The distinction between what patients subjectively experience as coercive and what is formally defined by law; highlights cases where voluntary patients report coercion, indicating informal or cultural pressures.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Patient experiences of coercive measures are complex and not uniformly negative; while often perceived as punitive or unnecessary, some individuals report feelings of safety, security, privacy, or therapeutic benefit—particularly when accompanied by respectful communication, clear reasoning, and strong therapeutic relationships.
  - **Evidence**: Synthesis of qualitative and quantitative studies including Katsakou & Priebe (2006), Wallsten et al. (2006), Bowers et al. (2010), Eriksson & Westrin (1995), Sørgaard (2007), and Soininen et al. (2013a,b). These show wide variation in perception (e.g., 33–81% finding involuntary admission justified), with some patients describing seclusion as helpful for self-control or privacy, and others reporting improved pre-discharge quality of life after restraint—possibly due to contextual contrast rather than causal benefit.
  - **Strength**: Strong for documenting descriptive patterns of patient experience; moderate for causal inferences (e.g., the quality-of-life finding in Soininen et al. 2013b noted as likely non-causal, possibly influenced by hospitalization duration or anticipation of discharge). Limitations include heterogeneity in study designs, cultural differences, and reliance on self-report, which may be affected by power dynamics or desire to please staff.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal tension**: Some patients report seclusion or restraint as helpful for privacy, self-control, or reduced stimulation, while others describe it as traumatizing, punitive, or retraumatizing—especially those with trauma histories.
- **Contextual discrepancy**: Soininen et al. (2013a,b) found older patients more critical of coercion, whereas Whittington et al. (2009) reported older patients more accepting—possibly due to differing samples (forensic vs. acute, national/cultural variations).
- **Ethical tension**: Even if some patients benefit subjectively, coercion inherently compromises autonomy and dignity; the focus should remain on prevention and alternatives rather than justifying use based on occasional positive reports.

## Open Questions
- How can psychiatric advance directives be effectively implemented in routine psychiatric care to reduce reliance on coercive measures?
- What specific components of staff training (e.g., de-escalation, trauma-informed care, communication skills) most improve patient experiences during unavoidable coercive incidents?
- Under what conditions do patients report positive experiences of seclusion or restraint, and how can these insights inform humane alternatives (e.g., voluntary quiet rooms)?
- How do cultural, legal, and systemic differences (e.g., Finland vs. U.S. vs. U.K.) shape both staff practices and patient perceptions of coercion in psychiatric settings?
