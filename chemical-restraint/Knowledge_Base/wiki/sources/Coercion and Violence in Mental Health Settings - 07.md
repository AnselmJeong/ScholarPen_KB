# Violence Risk Assessment

## Summary
This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of violence risk assessment (VRA) in mental health settings, tracing its evolution from unstructured professional judgment to structured approaches that emphasize prevention over prediction. It examines three primary methodologies: unstructured professional judgment (UPJ), actuarial decision-making, and structured professional judgment (SPJ), comparing their strengths and limitations. The chapter introduces the Early Recognition Method (ERM) as a proactive, client-centered strategy that identifies and manages early warning signs of aggression through four phases, supported by tools like the Forensic Early Signs of Aggression Inventory (FESAI). Special attention is given to the distinction between static and dynamic risk factors, the importance of protective factors, and the significant contributions of staff, interpersonal interactions, and environmental factors to inpatient violence—particularly noting that 40% of incidents involve staff-patient interactions. The chapter also addresses the challenges of applying standard risk assessment tools to special populations such as individuals with intellectual disability and autism spectrum disorder, advocating for tailored approaches. Ultimately, it argues for a paradigm shift toward organizational innovations like the High Intensive Care model, which prioritizes autonomy, low coercion, and therapeutic environments to reduce aggression and the need for restrictive interventions.

## Key Concepts
- **Violence Risk Assessment** — A clinical decision-making process to evaluate the likelihood of future violent behavior and determine preventive actions, extending beyond prediction to include risk and protective factor analysis, management planning, and ongoing monitoring.
- **Unstructured Professional Judgement (UPJ)** — Clinical judgment without formal rules or procedures for violence risk assessment; prone to bias and inconsistency due to reliance on individual intuition.
- **Actuarial Decision-Making** — Uses fixed algorithms and scored risk factors (often static) to predict violence probabilistically; transparent but limited in dynamic contexts and over-reliant on historical factors.
- **Structured Professional Judgement (SPJ)** — Combines scored risk factors with clinical judgment to formulate risk scenarios and guide interventions; balances structure with professional discretion to inform prevention strategies.
- **Early Recognition Method (ERM)** — A step-wise strategy to identify and manage early warning signs of violence through client collaboration, involving introduction, identification, monitoring, and action phases.
- **Forensic Early Signs of Aggression Inventory (FESAI)** — A 44-item tool in 15 categories designed to identify early warning signs of aggression in forensic settings, used within ERM for systematic monitoring.
- **Static Risk Factors** — Historical, unchanging variables (e.g., past violence, early onset); weakly associated with current aggression but frequently overemphasized in assessment tools.
- **Dynamic Risk Factors** — Modifiable, fluctuating variables (e.g., active symptoms, substance use); crucial for prevention but under-researched and harder to measure consistently.
- **Protective Factors** — Individual or environmental characteristics that reduce violence risk (e.g., team functioning, leadership); considered equally important as risk factors in comprehensive assessment.
- **Risk-Needs-Responsivity Model** — Framework where treatment matches the offender’s risk level, criminogenic needs, and learning ability; underpins ERM application in secure forensic settings.
- **High Intensive Care Model** — Focuses on therapeutic environments with autonomy, privacy, low coercion, and strong staff-patient engagement; presented as an organizational innovation to reduce aggression and coercive practices.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Violence risk assessment must evolve beyond prediction to include management and prevention through dynamic, collaborative processes.
  - *Evidence*: The chapter argues that effective VRA integrates risk and protective factors, develops management plans, and enables ongoing monitoring to adjust interventions, citing Whittington et al. (2013) as support for this preventive framing.
  - *Strength*: Strong — grounded in logical progression and cited research, positioning VRA as a therapeutic rather than purely predictive act.
- **Core claim**: Structured Professional Judgement (SPJ) and actuarial methods are comparable in predictive accuracy but differ in utility and applicability.
  - *Evidence*: Both SPJ and actuarial approaches show similar predictive efficacy (Yang et al., 2010; Guy et al., 2015), but SPJ incorporates dynamic factors and clinical judgment, while actuarial models rely on static factors and fixed algorithms.
  - *Strength*: Moderate — based on synthesized evidence, though limited by variability in implementation and generalizability across settings.
- **Core claim**: The Early Recognition Method (ERM) reduces violence through client-centered early warning sign monitoring and collaborative care.
  - *Evidence*: ERM implementation linked to fewer and less serious incidents in forensic settings (Fluttert et al., 2010a) and reduced relapse in ambulatory care (Johansen et al., 2021b, 2022), with staff-client interaction identified as a key mechanism.
  - *Strength*: Moderate to strong — supported by multiple studies, though the precise mechanisms of how discussion alters behavior are not fully causally established.
- **Core claim**: Staff, ward, and interactional factors significantly contribute to inpatient violence, with 40% of incidents involving staff-patient interactions.
  - *Evidence*: Cites Weltens et al. (2021) systematic review identifying patient, staff, and ward risk factors, including poor communication, lack of empathy, high bed occupancy, and limited daily structure.
  - *Strength*: Strong — based on recent empirical review and consistent with broader literature on systemic and environmental influences in healthcare settings.
- **Core claim**: Special populations (intellectual disability, autism) require tailored risk assessment approaches due to limitations in standard tool validity.
  - *Evidence*: Notes that while tools like HCR-20 and VRAG show some validity in intellectual disability, they risk misestimating risk; autism research remains limited and inconsistent, necessitating population-specific adaptations.
  - *Strength*: Moderate — highlights critical gaps in current evidence and calls for more inclusive validation, acknowledging that current tools may not fully capture context-specific risk expressions.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension between standardization and individualization**: Actuarial models emphasize standardization and transparency, while SPJ and ERM prioritize individualized, clinically informed judgment; the chapter acknowledges this tension but presents SPJ as a balanced approach rather than a resolved solution.
- **Underuse of patient involvement**: Despite evidence that client participation in ERM improves outcomes, systematic reviews indicate patient involvement remains rare in evidence-based risk management strategies, revealing a persistent gap between research and clinical practice.
- **Overreliance on static factors**: The field continues to prioritize static risk factors in assessment tools despite their weak association with aggression, creating a mismatch between empirical evidence and clinical instrumentation due to validation challenges.

## Open Questions

- Does the High Intensive Care model demonstrably reduce the use of coercive interventions beyond improving the therapeutic environment, and what evidence supports its scalability?
