# The Legitimisation of Psychiatric Measures Against a Person's Will

## Summary
This chapter traces the historical evolution of justifications for coercive psychiatric practices, from the institution-centered phase focused on social control and public order to the person-centered phase grounded in clinical-ethical-legal frameworks. During the institution-centered era, coercion was justified through doctrines like parens patriae and concerns about family reputation or public nuisance, often without psychiatric input and under inhumane conditions. The shift toward person-centered care from the 1970s/1980s introduced medical and ethical criteria such as diagnosed mental illness, risk of harm, last resort, and least restrictive alternative, as reflected in guidelines from bodies like the German Ethics Council and European surveys. Despite this evolution, the chapter argues that coercion remains ethically contested, with tensions between autonomy and beneficence, and highlights ongoing debates about its efficacy, particularly regarding claims that it fosters insight or recovery. The analysis underscores a persistent "clinical-ethical-legal stalemate," exemplified by the Geneva impasse in WHO/UN forums, where competing human rights frameworks prevent consensus on abolition versus regulation of coercion.

## Key Entities
- **World Health Organisation (WHO)** (org): Developer of the International Classification of Diseases (ICD), which standardizes mental disorder classifications globally and influences diagnostic criteria used in coercion decisions.
- **German Ethics Council** (org): Issued ethical guidelines affirming that coercion can be justified only when individuals express needs that threaten serious harm but lack capacity for responsible decisions, rejecting blanket abolition due to fears of abandoning vulnerable people.
- **International Classification of Diseases (ICD)** (tool/dataset): Diagnostic classification system developed by WHO, used internationally (except in the U.S.) to define mental disorders, serving as a key criterion in modern coercion justifications.
- **Cochrane Initiative** (org): Conducted reviews on patient-centered care interventions, highlighting difficulties in measuring healthcare user outcomes despite evidence that communication skills can be taught to professionals.
- **Lunacy Commission** (org): Historical UK oversight body that reviewed cases like the Lanchester Case, where it rejected the involuntary commitment of Edith Lanchester despite medical and familial endorsement.

## Key Concepts
- **Institutionalization**: Long-term confinement of individuals in asylums or hospitals, historically justified by social control and perceived danger to self or others, often under inhumane conditions with minimal treatment.
- **Person-centered care**: Care model emphasizing autonomy, shared decision-making, and holistic understanding of the individual, originating from Carl Rogers and Michael Balint, later adopted in psychiatry but sometimes co-opted to enforce treatment adherence.
- **Recovery approach**: User-led framework focused on living a meaningful life despite mental illness, not necessarily tied to treatment adherence, originating from service users and challenging clinical dominance in mental health care.
- **Parens patriae**: Legal doctrine where the state acts as a guardian for those deemed incapable (e.g., minors, mentally ill), historically used to justify involuntary commitment in Anglo-Saxon law and mirrored in Swiss "welfare placements."
- **Coercion in psychiatry**: Use of involuntary measures like detention or forced treatment, justified by mental illness and risk to self/others, with evolving rationales from social control to clinical-ethical frameworks.
- **Ethical justification of coercion**: Framework balancing autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice, used to evaluate modern legitimacy of coercion, as analyzed by the German Ethics Council and international scholarship.
- **Least restrictive alternative**: Principle requiring that less invasive options be exhausted before coercion is used, a key component of ethical and legal standards in many jurisdictions.
- **Insight into illness**: Patient recognition of their condition and need for treatment, often cited as a purported benefit of coercion but debated due to lack of strong evidence and concerns about therapeutic relationship damage.
- **Geneva impasse**: Stalemate in UN/WHO discussions between factions advocating for abolition of psychiatric coercion and those defending its use under strict conditions, reflecting unresolved global tensions in mental health law.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The justification of coercion in psychiatry has shifted from social control (e.g., public order, family reputation) to clinical-ethical frameworks (mental illness + risk), but remains ethically and legally contested due to unresolved tensions between autonomy and beneficence.
  - **Evidence**: Historical cases like the Lanchester Case (social/sexual deviance) and Elsa Asenjieff (gender nonconformity) show coercion based on social norms; widespread use of lobotomies and insulin shocks without consent; modern standards from the German Ethics Council and EU surveys require mental illness, risk, last resort, and least restrictive alternative.
  - **Strength**: Strong historical documentation; moderate for modern ethical frameworks (based on policy analysis); weak for efficacy claims (e.g., coercion improving insight), which are noted as lacking robust empirical support.
- **Core claim**: Even in the person-centered era, concepts like person-centeredness and recovery are sometimes co-opted to justify adherence or used inconsistently, creating tension between ideals and practice.
  - **Evidence**: Person-centeredness is invoked to promote medication adherence despite undermining shared decision-making; the German Ethics Council focuses only on harm to the individual, ignoring risks to others, creating inconsistency within its own framework.
  - **Strength**: Moderate, based on textual analysis of policy and advocacy literature; highlights implementation gaps but less direct evidence on widespread clinical practice.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The German Ethics Council rejects blanket abolition of coercion due to fears of abandoning vulnerable people but fails to consider risks to others, contradicting its own stated framework of balancing individual and social interests.
- Person-centered care is sometimes used to justify treatment adherence, undermining its core principle of shared decision-making and recreating paternalistic dynamics.
- The recovery movement’s anti-medical stance conflicts with psychiatry’s increasing biomedical integration, creating uncertainty about whether combining recovery and person-centeredness leads to genuine reform or superficial adaptation.

## Open Questions
- Does coercion ever lead to genuine insight or recovery, or does it primarily enforce compliance without improving long-term outcomes?
- How do structural factors like poverty, racism, and gender bias influence who gets subjected to coercion, beyond individual risk assessments?
- Can recovery-oriented models be implemented in psychiatry without rejecting evidence-based care entirely, or does their adoption risk undermining treatment efficacy?
