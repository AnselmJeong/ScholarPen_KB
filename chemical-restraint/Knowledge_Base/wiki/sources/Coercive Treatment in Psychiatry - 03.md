# Person-centred psychiatry perspectives on coercion and cooperation

## Summary
This chapter outlines the World Psychiatric Association's (WPA) initiative on psychiatry for the person, launched in 2005, as a framework for addressing coercion in mental health care. It traces the historical roots of person-centred care from ancient traditions through 20th-century developments in medicine, psychiatry, and public health policy, highlighting the influence of the WHO, Institute of Medicine, and recovery movements. The chapter emphasizes the 2007 WPA Dresden Conference as a pivotal moment where psychiatric leadership engaged directly with service user and survivor groups—including Mind Freedom International and Dorothea Buck—marking a shift toward dialogue and partnership. Ongoing initiatives such as the Institutional Program on Psychiatry for the Person (IPPP), the International Network for Person-centered Medicine (INPCM), and the development of Person-Centred Integrative Diagnosis are presented as structural pathways for reform. The chapter argues that centering the person, enhancing clinical communication, and fostering collaborative relationships are essential to resolving the tension between social control and personal autonomy in psychiatric practice.

## Key Concepts
- **Person-centred psychiatry/medicine** — Approach placing the person in context at the centre of care, integrating biological, psychological, social, and spiritual dimensions; emphasizes autonomy, dignity, and collaboration as central to clinical and public health practice.
- **Therapeutic alliance** — Clinician-patient relationship grounded in empathy, mutual respect, and shared decision-making, identified as foundational to reducing coercion and fostering cooperation in psychiatric care.
- **Recovery movement** — Mental health service user-led movement emphasizing lived experience, empowerment, and active partnership in care, contrasting with passive reception and informing WPA’s engagement with user groups.
- **Patient autonomy** — Principle of self-determination and decision-making authority in healthcare, central to the ethical tension with social control in coercive psychiatry.
- **Social control vs. personal autonomy** — Ethical tension between society’s need to manage risk (e.g., via involuntary treatment) and individual rights to self-determination, framed as a balance to be achieved through dialogue and partnership.
- **Person-Centred Integrative Diagnosis** — Diagnostic model combining nosological illness assessment with positive health domains (self-awareness, resilience, etc.), using narratives and collaborative interpretation to support shared understanding and reduce coercion.
- **Clinical communication** — Exchange of information and intentions in clinical settings that fosters empathy, trust, and mutual understanding, identified as critical to person-centred care.
- **Institutional reform** — Structural changes in healthcare systems (e.g., policy, training, organizational programs) that support person-centred approaches, exemplified by WPA’s IPPP and INPCM initiatives.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Person-centred psychiatry offers a constructive framework for addressing the tension between coercion and cooperation in mental health care by centering the person, fostering dialogue, and strengthening the clinician-patient relationship grounded in empathy and mutual respect.
  - **Evidence**: Historical and global precedents (WHO, IOM, recovery movements) show a trend toward person-centred care; the 2007 WPA Dresden Conference demonstrated breakthrough dialogue between psychiatry leadership and user/survivor groups, including extended, respectful engagement (e.g., with Dorothea Buck and Mind Freedom International); ongoing initiatives like IPPP, INPCM, and Person-Centred Integrative Diagnosis provide structural pathways; emphasis on clinician-patient relationship as foundational (per Tasman) and training programs in multiple countries support feasibility.
  - **Strength**: Strong for conceptual and institutional trends; moderate for direct causal impact on reducing coercion (more aspirational than outcome-based). The Dresden engagement is a notable indicator of shifting dynamics, though long-term behavioral or policy outcomes are not yet reported.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- The text acknowledges that consensus on coercion is "unlikely" due to deep-seated historical and philosophical divides, yet advocates for dialogue as a path forward — reflecting realism about limitations while maintaining hope for incremental progress.
- While promoting person-centred ideals, the source does not resolve how to handle cases where a person lacks decision-making capacity (e.g., acute psychosis), leaving the coercion-cooperation balance inadequately addressed in high-risk, impaired-capacity scenarios.
- The discussion emphasizes ideals and dialogue but provides minimal detail on legal mechanisms, current involuntary treatment standards, or enforceable safeguards, risking an overemphasis on aspiration over concrete implementation in constrained systems.
