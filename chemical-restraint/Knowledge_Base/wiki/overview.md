- Variations in definitions used for describing restrictive care practices (seclusion and restraint) in adult mental health inpatient units a systematic: There is significant variation in how restrictive care practices are defined across recent literature, undermining the validity of cross-study comparisons and prevalence reporting.
- The Right to Choose and Refuse Mental Health Care; A Human Rights Based Approach to Ending Compulsory Psychiatric Intervention: The article argues that current U.S. mental health law unjustly denies people with psychosocial disabilities the right to refuse treatment and access culturally responsive care, violating constitutional and international human rights protections.
- The Lived Realities of Chemical Restraint; Prioritizing Patient Experience: The ethical analysis of chemical restraints must prioritize patient lived experience to address how psychotropic drugs undermine authentic consent and perpetuate epistemic injustice through drug-induced compliance and systemic biases like sanism.
- The Limitations of Principlism: Principlism is inadequate for ethical decisions on chemical restraints because it overemphasizes individual autonomy, lacks a value theory to resolve conflicts, and ignores the collective impact on the moral community.
- Prevalence and variability of restrictive care practice use (physical restraint, seclusion and chemical restraint) in adult mental health inpatient settings: The pooled prevalence of physical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint in adult mental health inpatient settings is 14.4%, 15.8%, and 25.7%, respectively, with extreme heterogeneity (I² >99%) driven by geographical location and data collection methods.

- On the Relative Intrusiveness of Physical and Chemical Restraints: Consciousness-altering chemical restraints may be more intrusive on basic liberty than physical restraints because they interfere with a broader range of intentions and mental states, not just specific bodily movements.
- Machine Learning for prediction of violent behaviors in schizophrenia spectrum disorders a systematic review: Machine learning models, particularly gradient boosting, show moderate to strong predictive performance for violent behavior in schizophrenia spectrum disorders, though heterogeneity in outcomes and lack of external validation limit clinical applicability.
