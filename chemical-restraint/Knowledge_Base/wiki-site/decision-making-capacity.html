Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical justifications for psychiatric interventions
  • Determines legitimacy of consent and coercion in treatment decisions
  • Intersects with legal standards for autonomy and decision-making rights
  • Influences clinical protocols for assessing patient capacity in crisis

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Capacity-based ethical decision-making Ethical use of coercion requires structured assessment of decision-making capacity alongside benefit-harm analysis 2023 - Steinert - Using coercion in mental disorders or risking the patients death An analysis of the protocols of a clinical ethics committee
Contextual capacity evaluation Decision-making capacity must be assessed within cultural, religious, and socioeconomic contexts, not as a universal standard 2020 - Atakora, Asampong - Ethical Principles in Psychiatric Practice; A Systematic Review
Experience-informed capacity assessment Apparent restoration of decisional capacity may not reflect authentic consent due to psychological effects of psychotropic drugs 2024 - Dougherty et al. - The Lived Realities of Chemical Restraint Prioritizing Patient Experience
Preference-sensitive capacity determination Least restrictive interventions should prioritize individual service users’ expressed preferences over standardized hierarchies 2024 - Hempeler et al. - Preferences of Individual Mental Health Service Users Are Essential in Determining the Least Restrictive Type of Restraint
Exceptional coercion justified by restored capacity Coercion may be ethically permissible only when it serves to restore long-term autonomy or safety after transparent evaluation 2021 - Chieze et al. - Coercive Measures in Psychiatry A Review of Ethical Arguments
Procedural safeguards for capacity assessment Coercion justification depends on necessity, proportionality, and procedural justice, with formal and informal pressures requiring scrutiny Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 08
