Summary

This literature review examines the use of restraint in psychiatric patients by analyzing 20 studies sourced from PubMed, ProQuest, Google Scholar, and general web searches. The authors investigate both the effectiveness of restraint in managing violent behavior and its associated physical and psychological harms. They compare clinical guidelines from major organizations including JCAHO, APNA, the UK Department of Health, and evidence-based guidelines such as Park and Tang (2007), evaluating criteria for use, standard practices, and documentation requirements. The review concludes that while restraint can be effective in reducing aggression, it carries substantial risks including injury, psychological trauma, and even death, necessitating strict adherence to guidelines, regular monitoring, and thorough documentation to ensure patient safety and ethical practice.

Key Entities

  • ProQuest (database): Primary database used for sourcing academic literature in the review.
  • PubMed (database): Key biomedical database searched for relevant studies on restraint in psychiatry.
  • Google Scholar (tool): Academic search engine used to identify grey literature and supplementary sources.
  • Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organization (JCAHO) (organization): U.S.-based accrediting body whose restraint guidelines were analyzed for safety standards and monitoring requirements.
  • American Psychiatric Nurses Association (APNA) (organization): Professional nursing association whose guidelines on restraint use and documentation were evaluated in the review.
  • Department of Health for England and Wales (organization): UK government body whose guidance on restraint in mental health settings was compared with U.S. standards.
  • Park and Tang (2007) (guideline): Evidence-based guideline on physical restraint use in acute care, cited for its structured approach to assessment, consent, and monitoring.
  • Reeves (2011) (tool/guideline): Referenced for providing a comprehensive 19-element framework for documenting restraint use, integrating elements from NICE, BILD, and other sources.

Key Concepts

  • Physical restraint: Use of physical devices (e.g., straps, belts) to restrict patient movement; discussed as effective for immediate violence control but associated with risks like pressure sores, fractures, and asphyxiation.
  • Chemical restraint: Administration of sedative medications to manage behavior; noted as less frequently used than physical restraint but carrying risks such as respiratory depression and over-sedation.
  • Seclusion: Isolation of a patient in a room from which exit is staff-controlled; considered alongside restraint as a behavioral intervention with potential psychological impacts like fear and isolation.
  • Violent behavior in psychiatric patients: Aggressive or dangerous actions prompting intervention; central to justifying restraint use but also a focus of harm when restraint is misapplied.
  • Risk-benefit assessment: Evaluation of whether restraint’s harms are justified by the need to prevent imminent harm; emphasized across guidelines as a prerequisite for use.
  • Least restrictive intervention: Principle requiring use of minimal necessary restriction; mandates attempting verbal or environmental strategies before resorting to restraint.
  • Monitoring and reassessment: Regular checks (e.g., every 15 minutes) on restrained patients’ physical and psychological status; highlighted as critical for preventing harm and mandated by JCAHO and APNA.
  • Documentation standards: Required elements for recording restraint use (e.g., rationale, alternatives tried, patient response, injuries); underscored as vital for accountability, with Reeves (2011) noted for its 19-element framework.
  • Informed consent: Process of obtaining agreement from patient or caregiver before restraint, where feasible; stressed in Park and Tang (2007) and Reeves (2011) as ethically and legally important.
  • JCAHO guidelines: U.S. accreditation standards specifying time limits by age, face-to-face evaluation within one hour, and discontinuation at earliest possible time.
  • APNA guidelines: Nursing-focused standards emphasizing individualized risk-benefit analysis, 15-minute monitoring, and release upon behavioral criteria being met.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Restraint is effective in reducing violent behavior in psychiatric patients but carries significant physical and psychological risks, necessitating strict adherence to evidence-based guidelines and thorough documentation.
    • Evidence: Effectiveness supported by Saseno & Kriswoyo (2013) and Sulistyowati & Prihantini (2014), showing reduced violence via observational scales; harm documented in Asher et al. (2017), Kalula & Petros (2016), Abrahamse (2001), and Milliken (1998), citing injuries, psychological trauma (fear, humiliation, anger), and fatalities; guideline comparison reveals convergence on core principles (last resort, least restrictive, physician order) but variation in detail (e.g., JCAHO’s age-based time limits vs. UK vagueness); documentation analysis identifies Reeves (2011) as most comprehensive (19 elements), integrating NICE, BILD, and others.
    • Strength: Moderate to strong for effectiveness and harm (based on multiple clinical/qualitative studies); guideline comparisons are descriptive without critical appraisal; no original data—this is a synthesis of 20 selected papers.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Tension between efficacy and safety: While restraint reduces aggression, it causes physical harm (e.g., injuries, deaths) and psychological trauma (fear, humiliation), creating an unresolved ethical dilemma beyond recommending guideline adherence.
  • Inconsistency in guideline detail: JCAHO specifies precise time limits by age (e.g., 1 hour for under 9, 2 hours for 9–17, 4 hours for 18+); UK guidance lacks comparable specificity on timing and frequency of monitoring, creating ambiguity in application.
  • Practical vs. ideal use: Notes clinicians often apply restraint without physician orders in emergencies, contradicting guidelines—indicating a gap between policy and real-world emergency response.
  • Psychological harm underemphasized: Although physical risks are well-addressed in guidelines (e.g., JCAHO monitoring for injury), psychological impacts (trauma, powerlessness) are inconsistently integrated into observation protocols.
  • Lack of detail on alternatives: While “less restrictive interventions” are noted as prerequisites, the review does not specify or evaluate alternatives (e.g., de-escalation, environmental changes), limiting practical guidance for implementation.

Open Questions

  • How frequently are restraint guidelines actually followed in emergency psychiatric settings, particularly regarding physician authorization and monitoring frequency?
  • What is the comparative effectiveness and safety of de-escalation techniques versus restraint in reducing violence and injury in acute psychiatric crises?
  • How do cultural, institutional, and resource-related factors influence the choice between mechanical and chemical restraint across different healthcare systems?
  • Are current monitoring standards (e.g., 15-minute checks) sufficient to detect early signs of psychological distress or trauma during restraint episodes?
