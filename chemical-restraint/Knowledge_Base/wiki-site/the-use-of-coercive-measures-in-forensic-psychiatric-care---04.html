Summary

This chapter examines coercion in psychiatric care through a sociological lens, arguing that coercion extends beyond formal interventions like restraint or seclusion to encompass the inherent power dynamics and structural features of psychiatric institutions. Drawing on Goffman’s concept of “Total Institutions” and Rosenhan’s pseudopatient study, it demonstrates how patients experience coercion through loss of credibility, privacy, and autonomy, even in the absence of overt coercive measures. The analysis integrates empirical data from the multinational EUNOMIA project, which found that nearly half of psychiatric inpatients reported perceived coercion, with variations linked to legal status, gender, symptom severity, and significant regional differences unexplained by resources. Additionally, Bowers’ research on nursing staff attitudes highlights how team dynamics, leadership, and ward atmosphere shape the use of coercive measures, emphasizing micro-social processes over individual pathology or training alone. The chapter concludes that coercion is socially produced through organizational culture, interpersonal interactions, and national traditions, challenging purely biomedical or legalistic explanations.

Key Entities

  • EUNOMIA project (dataset): A multinational study across 11 EU countries measuring perceived coercion and use of coercive measures in psychiatric inpatient facilities; provides core empirical evidence on patient experiences and staff practices.
  • Goffman’s Asylums (1968) (dataset/tool): Foundational ethnographic study defining psychiatric institutions as “Total Institutions” where patients undergo mortification of self and forced role adoption as mentally ill.
  • Rosenhan’s pseudopatient study (1973) (dataset/tool): Classic study revealing diagnostic uncertainty and patient powerlessness, showing how pseudopatients experienced loss of credibility, restricted movement, and minimal privacy despite feigning sanity.
  • Bowers’ research (dataset/tool): Series of studies examining nurses’ attitudes toward coercive measures, team dynamics, leadership, and burnout, linking organizational factors to variations in coercion use across countries.
  • Fiorillo et al. (2012) (dataset): EUNOMIA sub-study analyzing correlates of perceived coercion, identifying higher reports among legally detained, female, and more symptomatic patients.
  • Kalisova et al. (2014) (dataset): EUNOMIA sub-study investigating factors influencing use of coercive measures, highlighting large regional variation unexplained by resources or staffing ratios.
  • MacArthur Scale of Perceived Coercion (tool): Instrument used in EUNOMIA to assess subjective coercion at hospital admission through self-reported loss of control and autonomy.
  • Cantrill Ladder (tool): Subjective scale used in EUNOMIA to measure perceived pressure during admission, adapted to assess ongoing coercion experiences.

Key Concepts

  • Total Institution (Goffman): A setting where individuals are isolated from society and subject to rigid control, leading to loss of prior identity and forced adoption of an institutional role; central to understanding coercion as structural in psychiatric care.
  • Perceived coercion: The subjective experience of pressure or lack of autonomy during hospitalization, measured via self-report scales; distinguishes lived experience from objective use of coercive interventions.
  • Moral career (Goffman): The process by which patients internalize the “mentally ill” identity through institutional social control, explaining long-term psychological impact beyond immediate coercion.
  • Verstehen vs. Erklären: Contrast between interpretive (understanding meaning) and explanatory (causal) epistemologies; justifies qualitative methods in studying coercion as meaningful human experience.
  • Micro-social processes: Small-scale interpersonal dynamics (e.g., staff-patient interaction, team functioning, leadership) that shape coercion use; emphasized via Bowers’ work on team climate and ward atmosphere.
  • Local and national traditions: Unexplained regional variation in coercion use despite similar resources, pointing to cultural or organizational norms as key drivers of practice differences.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The experience of coercion in psychiatric inpatient settings is embedded in institutional structure and social processes, not limited to specific interventions like restraint or seclusion; patients experience coercion through loss of autonomy, credibility, and privacy due to power imbalances and organizational culture.
    • Evidence: Goffman’s Asylums describes how institutions strip prior identity and impose a patient role; Rosenhan’s study shows persistent disempowerment and minimal privacy even when feigning sanity; EUNOMIA data reveal nearly half of patients reported perceived coercion, higher among legally detained, females, and more symptomatic individuals, with reduction over time linked more to adaptation than symptom improvement; Bowers’ research shows staff attitudes and coercion use vary by team dynamics, leadership, and burnout, not just training.
    • Strength: Strong for structural and subjective arguments (supported by historical ethnography and large-scale multinational data); moderate for causal claims (e.g., team climate directly causing coercion variation), as evidence is largely correlational but strengthened by theoretical triangulation across qualitative and quantitative sources.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Tension exists between medical and sociological epistemologies in psychiatry, where sociological views are sometimes dismissed as “anti-psychiatry”; the chapter argues for complementarity, not replacement, of perspectives.
  • Internal nuance: While arguing coercion is structural, the chapter acknowledges that specific interventions (e.g., seclusion) intensify experience and are influenced by patient behavior (e.g., agitation), avoiding determinism by noting adaptation over time and the role of staff-patient relationships.

Open Questions

  • How can macro-level policy changes translate into measurable improvements in micro-social interactions on wards to reduce coercion?
  • What specific aspects of team climate (e.g., trust, leadership clarity, psychological safety) most reliably predict lower use of coercive measures in acute psychiatric settings?
  • Can qualitative insights from studies like Rosenhan’s be systematically integrated into quality improvement initiatives in forensic psychiatric care to better address patient experience of coercion?
