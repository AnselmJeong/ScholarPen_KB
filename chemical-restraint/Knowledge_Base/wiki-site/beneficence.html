Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical decisions about coercion in psychiatric practice
  • Balances against autonomy in cases of impaired decision-making capacity
  • Underpins debates on covert medication and involuntary treatment
  • Highlights context-dependence of ethical principles across cultural and clinical settings

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Beneficence justifies coercion when it prevents harm Coercive measures may be ethically justified if they prevent serious harm or death, even if they limit autonomy 2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry
Beneficence requires individualized assessment Ethical use of coercion depends on capacity, benefit-harm analysis, and biopsychosocial context, not abstract principles 2023 - Steinert - Using coercion in mental disorders or risking the patients death An analysis of the protocols of a clinical ethics committee
Beneficence conflicts with autonomy in covert treatment Covert medication may reduce harm (e.g., foreign object ingestion) but fails to treat psychosis and raises ethical concerns due to lack of consent 2024 - Razzak et al. - Ethical Dilemmas in Covert Prescribing A Case Report on Medication Administration for Noncompliant Psychiatric Patients
Beneficence is not universally applicable Interpretations of beneficence vary across cultural, religious, economic, and political contexts, challenging universal ethical frameworks 2020 - Atakora, Asampong - Ethical Principles in Psychiatric Practice; A Systematic Review
