  • Autonomy: The principle of individual self-determination and decision-making capacity; Central to the debate as both a justification for and against seclusion; Not in wiki index
  • Human Dignity: The inherent or contextual value and respect owed to individuals; Central to ethical arguments regarding seclusion’s impact; Not in wiki index
  • Beneficence/Non-maleficence: Ethical principles of doing good and avoiding harm; Central as they conflict with autonomy in paternalistic models; Not in wiki index
  • Relational Autonomy: The concept that autonomy develops through relationships and social context; Important for arguing that coercion may sometimes support autonomy; Not in wiki index

  • Precautionary Principle (“Primum non nocere”): The duty to avoid harm when evidence of benefit is lacking; Used to argue against seclusion due to lack of evidence; Not in wiki index

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core Claim: Respect for autonomy and human dignity alone cannot justify eliminating coercive measures (like seclusion) in psychiatry, as these principles can also support their use in certain contexts (e.g., when seclusion helps restore autonomy or dignity).
  • Evidence: Ethical analysis shows ambiguity in autonomy and dignity concepts; literature reports mixed patient experiences (some benefit, some harm); no controlled trials prove seclusion’s efficacy or safety.
  • Strength of Evidence: Philosophical/ethical argument is strong conceptually; empirical evidence is weak due to absence of controlled trials, making efficacy claims unsubstantiated. The lack of data is itself a key argument against routine use.
