Summary

This chapter examines the evolution of disability models and their implications for interpreting Article 12 of the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which concerns equal recognition before the law and legal capacity. It traces the historical development from charity and medical models to the social model, noting how the CRPD was initially hailed as embodying social model principles. However, the author critiques strict interpretations of the social model for failing to account for the lived experience of impairment, especially in cognitive disability where intrinsic limitations interact with social barriers. The chapter further analyzes the human rights model of disability as promoted in UN General Comments, arguing that while it attempts to address shortcomings of the social model by affirming impairment as part of identity, it contains an internal contradiction by asserting universal legal capacity without adequately addressing how cognitive impairment affects decision-making capacity. Supported decision-making is presented as an evolving alternative to substituted decision-making under CRPD Article 12, aiming to preserve autonomy while providing necessary support.

Key Entities

  • Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (org): The central international treaty governing disability rights, particularly Article 12 on legal capacity and supported decision-making.
  • General Comment No 1 (tool): The 2014 interpretive document by the UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities that asserts Article 12 requires the abolition of substituted decision-making in favor of supported decision-making.
  • Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (org): The UN body responsible for monitoring CRPD implementation and issuing General Comments, including General Comment No 1.
  • Australian Law Reform Commission (ALRC) (org): Referenced for its work on equality, capacity, and disability law in Australia, providing comparative legal analysis.
  • World Health Organization (WHO) (org): Cited for its biopsychosocial model of disability, which frames disability as arising from the interaction between health conditions and contextual factors.

Key Concepts

  • Social Model of Disability: A framework distinguishing impairment (biological or functional variation) from disability (socially created barriers); emphasizes that disability results from inaccessible environments rather than individual deficit.
  • Medical Model of Disability: Views disability as an individual pathology requiring medical correction or rehabilitation; locates the problem within the person rather than society.
  • Human Rights Model of Disability: Extends the social model by affirming impairment as part of human diversity and identity, while asserting universal legal capacity; includes six features such as recognition of layered identity and impairment relevance.
  • Supported Decision-Making: A practice enabling individuals to make decisions with assistance from trusted others, preserving legal capacity; promoted under CRPD Article 12 as an alternative to substituted decision-making.
  • Substituted Decision-Making: A legal mechanism where a third party makes decisions on behalf of someone deemed lacking capacity; contested under CRPD interpretations that seek its abolition.
  • Legal Capacity: The legal recognition of an individual’s ability to hold rights and duties, including the capacity to make legally binding decisions; central to Article 12 debates.
  • Biopsychosocial Model: A framework (from WHO) understanding disability as arising from dynamic interactions between biological, psychological, and social factors; presented as more comprehensive than purely social models.
  • Impairment: Used descriptively to refer to functional variations (cognitive, physical, sensory); the chapter argues for recognizing its residual impact even when social barriers are removed.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The chapter traces how disability models evolved from charity to social, noting the CRPD’s alignment with social principles but highlighting that strict social models fail to account for residual impairment impacts, especially in cognitive disability.
    • Evidence: Analysis of scholarly commentary, UN documents (including General Comment No 1), and disability law discourse shows persistent tension between social model ideals and lived experience of impairment.
    • Strength: Well-supported by authoritative sources; limitations lie in the interpretive, theoretical nature of the argument rather than empirical gap.
  • Core claim: The human rights model of disability, while valuing impairment as part of identity, contains a contradiction in asserting universal legal capacity without addressing how cognitive impairment affects decision-making.
    • Evidence: Close reading of General Comment No 1 and associated commentary reveals that the model’s first feature (universal legal capacity) conflicts with its third and fourth features (impairment matters, layered identity) when applied to cognitive disability.
    • Strength: Strong textual support from UN and scholarly sources; limitation is that it engages with ongoing interpretive debate rather than settled consensus.
  • Core claim: Supported decision-making represents a progressive evolution from paternalistic guardianship, aiming to uphold autonomy for people with cognitive disability while providing necessary support.
    • Evidence: Described as developing since the 1990s, endorsed in CRPD discourse, and positioned as a means to realize Article 12 without violating integrity of the person.
    • Strength: Supported by policy trends and scholarly advocacy; potential limitation in scalability and safeguard implementation remains an open question.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The human rights model asserts universal legal capacity as a first principle while also claiming to value impairment and layered identity, yet fails to explain how cognitive impairment affects decision-making ability, creating an internal tension between equality and recognition of difference.
  • General Comment No 1’s abolition of substituted decision-making contrasts with arguments that such measures may be necessary for some individuals with cognitive disability to exercise rights, revealing a tension between ideal autonomy and practical support needs.
  • The chapter notes that deinstitutionalization, while intended to increase autonomy, did not uniformly improve lived experience, highlighting a gap between policy intent and outcomes in community care.

Open Questions

  • How can legal frameworks like the CRPD adequately protect persons with cognitive disability without either denying the reality of their impairment or undermining their legal agency? This warrants investigation into whether hybrid models or contextual accommodations could reconcile support needs with autonomy rights.
