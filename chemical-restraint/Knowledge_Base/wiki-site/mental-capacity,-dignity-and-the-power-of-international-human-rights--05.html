Summary

This chapter analyzes the role of dignity in the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), arguing that dignity functions as an inherent legal principle rather than a standalone right. It conducts a textual and comparative analysis of the CRPD alongside foundational human rights instruments like the UDHR, ICCPR, and ICESCR, noting the CRPD’s unusually frequent and central references to dignity in its Preamble, Article 1 (Purpose), and Article 3 (General Principles). The chapter contends that dignity in the CRPD must be understood as a guiding principle for interpreting substantive rights, particularly Article 12 on legal capacity, and proposes an original five-dimensional framework to define its meaning: (1) equal worth or value, (2) recognition of autonomy for those capable of it, (3) recognition of humans as interdependent and social beings, (4) recognition of embodied personhood, and (5) upholding the indivisibility of human rights. This framework aims to counter reductionist views that equate dignity solely with autonomy or rationality, which risk excluding persons with significant cognitive disabilities. The chapter supports its argument with philosophical sources (e.g., Kant on inherent value), legal scholarship (e.g., McCrudden on dignity as a legal principle), and jurisprudence (e.g., Pretty v UK on dignity as the essence of the ECHR), while acknowledging critiques and proposing a case-by-case approach to maintain practical applicability.

Key Entities

  • Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (type: treaty) — the central international human rights treaty analyzed for its treatment of dignity, particularly in Articles 1, 3, and 12
  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) (type: treaty) — foundational UN document cited for establishing dignity as inherent and linked to equal worth
  • International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) (type: treaty) — referenced for incorporating the UDHR’s language on inherent dignity
  • International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) (type: treaty) — cited similarly to ICCPR for its recognition of inherent dignity
  • European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR) (type: treaty) — discussed for its lack of explicit dignity reference but judicial affirmation in cases like Pretty v UK
  • Human Rights Act 1998 (UK) (type: legal instrument) — noted for incorporating ECHR into UK law and influencing domestic dignity jurisprudence
  • Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT) (type: legal doctrine) — cited for interpretive principles guiding treaty interpretation in context of object and purpose
  • Pretty v United Kingdom (type: judicial case) — key ECHR case where the Court affirmed that respect for human dignity is the essence of the Convention
  • Airedale NHS Trust v Bland (type: judicial case) — UK case referenced for its consideration of dignity in end-of-life decisions and protection of inherent value
  • Communication No 854/1999 (Manuel Wackenheim v France) (type: judicial case) — UN Human Rights Committee case referenced regarding dwarf-tossing and dignity

Key Concepts

  • Inherent dignity — the idea that dignity is an intrinsic, inalienable quality of all human beings, not dependent on status, function, or achievement; central to CRPD’s framing and distinguished from conditional notions
  • Dignity as a legal principle — the concept that dignity functions as an overarching norm guiding interpretation of rights, not as a standalone right that can be claimed independently
  • Five-dimensional dignity — a proposed framework comprising: (1) equal worth/value, (2) recognition of autonomy for those capable, (3) interdependent/social nature of being human, (4) embodied personhood, and (5) underpinning indivisibility of rights; presented as the author’s original contribution to define dignity in CRPD context
  • Dignity of risk — the idea that allowing individuals to take risks is essential to preserving dignity, particularly in decision-making autonomy; discussed in context of supported decision-making and critique of overprotection
  • Indivisibility of human rights — the principle that civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights are interconnected and equally important; presented as one dimension of dignity requiring holistic rights fulfillment
  • Embodied personhood — the recognition that personhood includes physical, bodily experience, not just cognitive or rational traits; counters overemphasis on rationality in dignity theories
  • Interdependent/relational dignity — the idea that human dignity is realized through relationships and mutual recognition, not isolation or independence; challenges autonomy-only models
  • Dignity as equal worth — the understanding that all humans possess equal, incomparable value simply by being human; rooted in UDHR, Kant, and religious traditions
  • Status dignity vs. condition dignity — distinction between dignity based solely on human status (inherent) and dignity achieved through meeting conditions (e.g., rights fulfillment); used to resolve tension between “inherent” dignity and language of being “robbed” of dignity

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Dignity in the CRPD is an inherent legal principle (not a right) that must guide the interpretation and implementation of all rights, especially Article 12 on legal capacity, and comprises five interrelated dimensions: equal worth, recognition of autonomy for those capable, interdependence, embodiment, and upholding the indivisibility of rights.
    • Evidence: Textual analysis shows dignity appears in CRPD Preamble, Article 1 (Purpose), Article 3 (General Principles), and is linked to substantive rights (e.g., Articles 16, 24, 25); supported by philosophical sources (Kant), legal scholarship (McCrudden, Degener), and jurisprudence (Pretty v UK, Airedale v Bland, Wackenheim v France); consistent with UDHR, ICCPR, ICESCR describing dignity as inherent and tied to worth/equality.
    • Strength: Strong — grounded in primary legal text (CRPD), authoritative commentary, binding jurisprudence, and philosophical tradition; acknowledges counterarguments (e.g., dignity as meaningless, redundant with autonomy) but responds with a nuanced, multidimensional framework that avoids reducing dignity to autonomy alone and includes those with severe cognitive disabilities; limitation noted is the need for case-by-case application to avoid vagueness.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While dignity is described as “inherent” in CRPD and other treaties, language like “robbed of dignity” suggests it can be lost — resolved by distinguishing status dignity (inherent, unchangeable) from condition dignity (context-dependent, e.g., through rights fulfillment or lived experience).
  • Some scholars argue dignity adds nothing beyond autonomy or respect — countered by showing how the five-dimensional model includes autonomy but adds dimensions like embodiment and interdependence that apply to those lacking conventional autonomy.
  • CRPD avoids calling dignity a “right” (to avoid implying it must be earned), yet treats it as normatively loaded — affirmed as correct: dignity is a principle that shapes rights, not a right itself, consistent with legal theory viewing dignity as a foundational norm.
