Summary

This chapter analyzes the evolution of mental health care through three historical phases: Phase 1 (until ~1970s) disregarded human rights with institution-centered care; Phase 2 (1980s-present) acknowledges human rights but permits restriction for medical reasons through person-centered, evidence-based approaches; and proposes Phase 3 where human rights form the basis of support through person-driven, preference-based care. The core argument is that psychosocial support should prioritize service user preferences, with clinical evidence serving only when aligned with those preferences, based on the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) Article 12 on legal capacity. The chapter details how supported decision-making—not shared decision-making—is required by human rights frameworks, necessitating multi-professional teams without professional dominance and peer involvement in leadership. Implementation requires eliminating all coercion (formal, informal, administrative) except self-binding advance directives (Ulysses clauses), basing care settings and treatment modalities on user preference, focusing on individual life goals rather than symptom management, and involving service users as leaders in research and training rather than subjects.

Key Concepts

  • Human Rights-Based Psychosocial Support — Support model prioritizing user preferences, legal capacity, and non-coercive care grounded in UN CRPD; defines the proposed third phase of mental health care evolution
  • Supported Decision-Making — Process where individuals make decisions with support rather than substituted decision-making; emphasizes will and preference over best interests as required by UN CRPD Article 12
  • Person-Driven — Shift from acting on professional judgments of what is best for the person to acting according to the person’s own preferences; contrasts with and evolves beyond person-centered care
  • Legal Capacity — Recognition of persons as bearers of rights before the law; ability to make legal decisions; must be fully granted per UN CRPD Article 12, opposing historical psychiatric restrictions
  • Preference-Based Support — Support guided by individual user preferences rather than standardized protocols or professional judgment; challenges evidence-based paradigms by prioritizing user choice
  • Positive Risk-Taking — Consciously taking risks under certain circumstances to learn and grow rather than merely avoiding harm; represents shift from risk avoidance in Phase 2 to constructive engagement in Phase 3

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Human rights-based psychosocial support requires prioritizing service user preferences over clinical evidence, with evidence only relevant when it aligns with user-preferred options.
    • Evidence: Draws on WHO’s Quality Rights initiative, UN CRPD Article 12, UK Supreme Court rulings on informed consent requiring full disclosure, and historical examples of Soteria and Open Dialogue as implementations.
    • Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in disability rights law and coherent phase-model framework; limitation lies in limited empirical evidence for Phase 3 outcomes, relying more on conceptual critique than outcome data.
  • Core claim: Supported decision-making (not shared decision-making) is required by human rights frameworks, reversing traditional power dynamics in clinical relationships.
    • Evidence: Based on UN CRPD Article 12, British Royal College of Surgeons standards requiring disclosure of all information including no-treatment options, and analyses showing shared decision-making insufficient for CRPD compliance.
    • Strength: Clear legal and ethical foundation; limitation remains in practical implementation pathways within existing mental health systems.
  • Core claim: Full legal capacity must be granted (no guardianship/conservatorship against will) and all coercion eliminated except self-binding advance directives (Ulysses clauses) for respecting human rights in mental health support.
    • Evidence: References UN CRPD requirements, historical use of coercion, and ethical arguments that coercion fundamentally violates human rights regardless of intent.
    • Strength: Strong normative argument grounded in international law; limitation acknowledges global legal systems still commonly restrict legal capacity.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The chapter’s assertion that user preferences should supersede clinical evidence in support decisions creates tension with the established concept of evidence-based medicine, which prioritizes research evidence in clinical decision-making.
  • The requirement for supported decision-making to implement user choices even when clinicians disagree conflicts with traditional notions of informed consent, where disclosure does not obligate adherence to user decisions against medical advice.
  • Internal tension exists in acknowledging that some users may prefer professionally guided decisions rather than full autonomy, suggesting a need for flexibility within the preference-based framework.
  • The chapter notes that user-chosen non-medical approaches (e.g., Open Dialogue, Soteria) may lack strong evidence for relapse prevention, creating a risk-benefit tension between honoring preferences and preventing harm.
  • Practical implementation faces tension due to global legal systems still restricting legal capacity through guardianship or conservatorship, conflicting with the CRPD requirement for full legal capacity.

Open Questions

  • How can mental health systems balance the duty to respect user preferences with the duty of care when those choices involve significant risks of harm (e.g., refusing life-saving treatment)?
  • What empirical evidence exists demonstrating the effectiveness of preference-based models like Open Dialogue or Soteria in promoting recovery, reducing harm, or improving long-term outcomes compared to standard evidence-based care?
  • How should advance directives (Ulysses clauses) be implemented when a person’s current expressed wishes conflict with their previously documented advance directives, particularly in fluctuating mental health states?
