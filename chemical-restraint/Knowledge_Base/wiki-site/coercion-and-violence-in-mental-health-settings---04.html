Summary

This chapter examines the perspectives of service users, staff, and informal caregivers regarding coercion and violence in mental health settings. It synthesizes research on how these groups experience and perceive coercive interventions such as involuntary hospitalization, restraint, seclusion, and community treatment orders. The authors highlight a fundamental misalignment in perspectives: service users predominantly view coercion as a violation of autonomy and human rights, triggering emotions like fear, anger, and powerlessness, while staff often regard it as necessary for safety and sometimes therapeutic, albeit with acknowledged harm to therapeutic relationships. Informal caregivers express mixed views, balancing concerns for safety with feelings of guilt, exclusion, and strain on family relationships. The chapter discusses measurement tools like the MacArthur Perceived Coercion Scale (MPCS), Experienced Coercion Scale (ECS), Coercion Experience Scale (CES), and Staff Attitude to Coercion Scale (SACS), noting the lack of equivalent tools for measuring perceptions of violence prevention. It concludes that despite policy advancements like the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UN-CRPD) promoting autonomy and dignity, coercive practices persist due to systemic barriers, insufficient implementation of shared decision-making, and lack of meaningful involvement of service users and informal caregivers in care design. The authors advocate for centering lived experience and advancing toward supported decision-making models to reduce coercion.

Key Entities

  • McArthur Perceived Coercion Scale (MPCS) (tool: instrument) — Primary tool for measuring service users’ subjective experience of coercion during hospital admission
  • Experienced Coercion Scale (ECS) (tool: instrument) — Measures coercion experiences from service user perspective, capturing behavioral and emotional aspects
  • Coercion Experience Scale (CES) (tool: instrument) — Assesses coercion experiences, including perceived pressure and lack of choice in treatment decisions
  • Staff Attitude to Coercion Scale (SACS) (tool: instrument) — Evaluates staff attitudes toward the use of coercive measures in psychiatric settings

  • Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) (organization: agency) — Referenced for advocating shared decision-making in mental health care

Key Concepts

  • Perceived coercion — The subjective feeling of being pressured or forced into treatment, regardless of objective coercion; central to understanding patient distress and treatment resistance
  • Procedural justice — The perception of fairness and inclusion in decision-making processes; linked to dignity, respect, and willingness to engage in treatment
  • Involuntary hospitalization — Admission to psychiatric care against an individual’s will; a common but contested practice that significantly influences perceptions of coercion
  • Community Treatment Orders (CTO) — Legally mandated treatment in the community; viewed by service users as a threat to autonomy despite potential benefits like access to care
  • Shared decision-making — A collaborative process where clinicians and patients share information and agree on treatment; promoted but inconsistently implemented in practice
  • Supported decision-making — A model where individuals retain final decision-making authority with support from others; presented as an advancement over shared decision-making that better upholds autonomy
  • Informed consent — Voluntary agreement to treatment based on adequate information and decision-making capacity; discussed in the context of capacity assessment and ethical justification
  • Decision making capacity — The cognitive and functional ability to understand, appreciate, reason, and communicate treatment choices; central to debates about when coercion is justified
  • Therapeutic relationship — The interpersonal bond between clinician and patient; described as frequently damaged by coercive interventions due to eroded trust and feelings of dehumanization
  • Moral injury — Psychological harm resulting from actions that violate one’s moral or ethical code; implied through staff expressions of guilt, emotional distancing, and ethical conflict when using coercion

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Service users predominantly experience coercion as a violation of rights and a source of trauma, evoking emotions such as anger, fear, humiliation, and powerlessness, which damage trust and therapeutic relationships.
    • Evidence: Qualitative studies consistently report that service users feel unheard, disrespected, and dehumanized during coercive incidents, with many associating coercion with past trauma and expressing lasting impacts on well-being and willingness to engage with services.
    • Strength: Strongly supported by multiple qualitative syntheses and survivor accounts; limited only by potential underrepresentation of those with acute psychosis or cognitive impairment in research samples.
  • Core claim: Staff often justify coercion as necessary for safety—protecting patients, themselves, and others—but acknowledge its potential to harm therapeutic relationships and cause moral distress, frequently citing systemic pressures like understaffing or lack of alternatives.
    • Evidence: Survey and interview data show staff perceive coercion as a last resort after other interventions fail, yet express guilt, emotional detachment, and ethical conflict, particularly when they recognize the negative impact on patient dignity and autonomy.
    • Strength: Well-supported by cross-sectional surveys and qualitative staff accounts; tempered by possible desensitization over time or institutional normalization of coercive practices.
  • Core claim: Informal caregivers experience conflicted emotions, supporting coercion to relieve caregiving burden or ensure safety, but also feeling guilty, excluded, and anxious, with strained family relationships following coercive events.
    • Evidence: Qualitative interviews with families reveal relief during crises but subsequent guilt, anxiety, and perceptions of damaged trust with the care recipient, especially when they feel excluded from decision-making or lack information.
    • Strength: Moderately supported; limited by sparse research on this group and potential underreporting due to stigma or reluctance to criticize care systems.
  • Core claim: A fundamental mismatch exists in attributing causes of aggression: service users rarely link violence to mental illness, while staff and informal caregivers often do, reinforcing the perceived legitimacy of coercion from a professional standpoint.
    • Evidence: Studies show service users frequently cite staff behavior, environmental factors, or unmet needs as triggers for violence, whereas staff emphasize patient diagnosis and instability, shaping differing views on prevention and intervention.
    • Strength: Consistently found across qualitative and survey research; highlights a key communication and empathy gap in care settings.
  • Core claim: Despite policy advances like the UN-CRPD emphasizing autonomy, dignity, and legal capacity, coercive practices remain widespread due to systemic barriers, lack of accessible alternatives, and insufficient implementation of shared or supported decision-making models.
    • Evidence: Persistent high use of coercion globally, coupled with policy-practice gaps, is documented in epidemiological studies and human rights monitoring; implementation science shows slow adoption of alternatives even when endorsed.
    • Strength: Robustly supported by epidemiological and policy analysis; acknowledged gap between intent and real-world application in mental health systems.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Staff often view coercion as a necessary safety measure, while service users experience it as a harmful violation of rights, creating a core tension between professional justification and patient harm
  • Despite international human rights frameworks like the UN-CRPD endorsing autonomy and legal capacity, coercive practices remain prevalent, revealing a gap between policy ideals and institutional reality
  • Staff may recognize coercion as ethically problematic or damaging to therapeutic relationships yet continue using it due to systemic constraints such as understaffing, lack of training, or absence of alternatives
  • Informal caregivers express support for coercion in crisis situations to ensure safety, but also report guilt, anxiety, and damaged family relationships afterward, reflecting an internal conflict between relief and harm
  • Service users frequently attribute aggression to interpersonal or environmental factors (e.g., disrespectful staff behavior), whereas staff overwhelmingly cite patient illness or dangerousness, creating a divergence in root-cause analysis and prevention strategies
  • The promotion of shared decision-making in policy contrasts with its inconsistent application in routine clinical practice, particularly during perceived high-risk situations where clinicians revert to paternalistic approaches

Open Questions

  • How can supported decision-making be operationalized in acute psychiatric settings where decision-making capacity may fluctuate or be impaired?
  • What specific interventions effectively reduce reliance on coercion without simply transferring the burden of care or risk to informal caregivers or community networks?
  • How do cultural variations in family dynamics, concepts of autonomy, and help-seeking behaviors shape the experience and acceptability of coercion across different stakeholder groups?
  • In what ways can moral injury among staff be measured and addressed to improve ethical climate and reduce desensitization to coercive practices?
  • How can the perspectives of informal caregivers be systematically integrated into care planning and policy development without overburdening them or exploiting their labor?
