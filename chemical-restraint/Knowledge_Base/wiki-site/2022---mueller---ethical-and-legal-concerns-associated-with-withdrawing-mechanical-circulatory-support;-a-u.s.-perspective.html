Summary

This article examines the ethical and legal permissibility of withdrawing mechanical circulatory support (MCS) devices—such as ventricular assist devices (VADs), total artificial hearts (TAHs), and extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO)—in the United States. Drawing on U.S. legal precedents, clinical case series, and bioethical principles, the author argues that withdrawing MCS at the request of an informed patient or surrogate is permissible when the intent is to remove a burdensome life-sustaining treatment, not to cause death. The analysis emphasizes that death following MCS withdrawal results from the underlying cardiovascular disease, not the withdrawal itself, thereby distinguishing it from physician-assisted suicide or euthanasia. Clinical evidence from Mayo Clinic case series indicates that most MCS withdrawals occur after multidisciplinary care conferences, involve surrogate decision-makers (often due to lack of patient decisional capacity), and result in death within a day, consistent with natural disease progression. The article underscores the importance of advance care planning to address MCS-specific wishes, as few current advance directives mention these technologies.

Key Concepts

  • Informed Consent/Refusal — The process where a patient, after understanding diagnosis and treatment options, voluntarily agrees to or refuses treatment based on decisional capacity; foundational for ethically permissible MCS withdrawal.
  • Life-Sustaining Treatment (LST) — Medical interventions that prolong life, where withdrawal would likely result in death (e.g., mechanical ventilation, dialysis, MCS); central to framing MCS withdrawal as ethically analogous to withdrawing other LSTs.
  • Withholding vs. Withdrawing LST — The ethical and legal equivalence between not initiating treatment and discontinuing it once started; supports the argument that MCS withdrawal is not morally distinct from withholding.
  • Medical Ineffectiveness — A clinical judgment that treatment will not achieve patient-centered goals (e.g., restoring consciousness, enabling discharge); justifies clinician recommendations to withdraw MCS when it no longer benefits the patient.
  • Substituted Judgment — A surrogate decision-making standard where choices are based on what the patient would want if competent; critical when advance directives do not address MCS.
  • Best Interests Standard — A surrogate standard based on promoting the patient’s quality of life and well-being when their wishes are unknown; used as a fallback in MCS withdrawal decisions.
  • Rule of Double Effect — An ethical principle permitting actions with both good and bad effects (e.g., pain relief that may hasten death) if the intent is good and the bad effect is not the means to the good end; distinguishes palliative care from euthanasia.
  • Physician-Assisted Suicide vs. Euthanasia — Distinct from MCS withdrawal: the former involves patient self-administration of a lethal prescription, the latter clinician administration; both require intent to cause death, unlike MCS withdrawal where death results from underlying disease.
  • Advance Care Planning — The process of discussing and documenting future care preferences, recommended to address MCS-specific goals and reduce surrogate distress.
  • Palliative Care — Care focused on symptom relief and quality of life during serious illness; advised alongside MCS withdrawal to manage the dying process.
  • Multidisciplinary Care Conference — A meeting involving patient (if able), surrogate, clinicians, nurses, chaplains, etc., to discuss goals of care; identified as best practice for building consensus on MCS withdrawal.
  • Conscientious Objection — A clinician’s refusal to participate in a legal/ethical practice due to moral beliefs; acknowledged but requires patient transfer, not abandonment.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Withdrawing MCS at the request of an informed patient or surrogate is ethically and legally permissible in the U.S. and does not constitute physician-assisted suicide or euthanasia, provided the intent is to remove a burdensome treatment, not to terminate life.
    • Evidence:
      • Legal: U.S. Supreme Court precedents (e.g., Cruzan v. Director, Vacco v. Quill) affirm the right to refuse or withdraw life-sustaining treatment, establish no ethical/legal difference between withholding and withdrawing, and confirm that death following withdrawal stems from underlying disease.
      • Ethical: Grounded in respect for autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice; withdrawal aligns with patient values when burdens outweigh benefits.
      • Clinical: Three Mayo Clinic case series (VAD: 2003–2009; TAH: 2007–2015; ECMO: 2010–2014) show most withdrawals were requested by surrogates, occurred after multidisciplinary conferences, and resulted in death within a day—indicating natural disease progression.
      • Conceptual: MCS is not a “genuine replacement treatment” (like a transplant) because it lacks physiological responsiveness and depends on external power/control; withdrawal is non-invasive and analogous to discontinuing ventilation or dialysis.
    • Strength: Strong—integrates authoritative legal precedent, consistent ethical frameworks, empirical data from multiple clinical series, and clear conceptual distinctions. Limitations: Focuses on U.S. context; does not examine international variation or institutional barriers (e.g., hospital-specific policies) that may affect implementation.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While the article argues MCS withdrawal is ethically equivalent to withdrawing other life-sustaining treatments, it acknowledges a perceived uniqueness due to MCS being described as “continuous and constitutive” (e.g., taking over heart function). This tension is addressed by arguing that MCS lacks key features of genuine replacement therapies (e.g., responsiveness to physiology, independence from external control), making withdrawal non-invasive and analogous to discontinuing ventilator support.
  • A secondary tension arises from variability in state laws: some U.S. states require heightened evidence (e.g., written advance directive) to withdraw artificial nutrition and hydration; though not explicitly applied to MCS, this suggests potential inconsistency in surrogate authority across jurisdictions, creating uncertainty in practice.
