Definition and Measurement of Physical and Chemical Restraint in Long-Term Care; A Systematic Review

Summary

This systematic review analyzed 86 studies on physical restraint (20 of which also addressed chemical restraint) in long-term care settings to examine how restraint is defined and measured. The researchers extracted both explicit definitions (verbatim statements) and implicit definitions (derived from measurement approaches or result reporting) and conducted a thematic content analysis. Seven core themes emerged across definitions: restraint method, setting where restraint occurs, stated intent, resident capacity to remove or control the restraint, caveats and exclusions, duration/frequency/number, and consent and resistance. The review found substantial variability in how these themes were incorporated into explicit definitions, and even greater inconsistency between explicit definitions and how restraint was actually measured or reported. Notably, while many explicit definitions included resident capacity to remove/control and stated intent, measurement tools like the RAI-MDS frequently omitted these elements. Conversely, implicit definitions from data collection often captured duration and setting but missed intent and consent. No studies reported validity of their measurement approaches, and only a minority reported inter-rater reliability. The review concludes that the lack of alignment between definition, measurement, and reporting undermines the validity of restraint research and hinders the ability to compare studies or evaluate interventions effectively.

Key Entities

  • Resident Assessment Instrument–Minimum Data Set (RAI-MDS) (tool): Used in 25 studies for physical restraint and 7 for chemical restraint outcome data collection; a pervasive but limited measurement tool that does not assess environmentally imposed restraint, human force, consent, or intent.
  • Online Survey Certification and Reporting (OSCAR) System (tool): Used in 4 studies for physical restraint and chemical restraint data; a U.S. regulatory dataset with limited sensitivity to nuanced restraint practices.
  • International consensus definition of physical restraint (2016) (definition/tool): Cited as a benchmark definition encompassing restraint method, stated intent, and resident capacity to remove/control, but omitting setting, duration, caveats, and consent/resistance.

Key Concepts

  • Physical restraint — Defined variably across studies as any action preventing free body movement or normal access to one’s body; the review found explicit definitions commonly included restraint method, stated intent, and resident capacity to remove/control, but rarely setting, duration, caveats, or consent.
  • Chemical restraint — Involves medication use to control behavior, but lacks a consensus definition; explicit definitions were rare (only 4 studies) and focused on medication classes and behavioral control intent, with unclear distinctions between therapeutic and restraining use.
  • Thematic elements of restraint definitions — Seven themes identified across all definitions: restraint method, setting, stated intent, resident capacity to remove/control, caveats and exclusions, duration/frequency/number, and consent and resistance; formed the analytical framework for assessing consistency between definition and measurement.
  • Explicit vs. implicit definitions — Explicit definitions are verbatim statements defining restraint; implicit definitions arise from measurement approaches or result reporting when no explicit definition is given; the review found frequent mismatches between the two within and across studies.
  • Inter-rater reliability — Measure of agreement between observers using the same tool; reported in 27 physical restraint studies and 1 chemical restraint study, but never paired with validity data, limiting confidence in measurement accuracy.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Definitions of physical and chemical restraint in long-term care research are highly variable, and measurement approaches often fail to reflect the themes present in those definitions, undermining the validity and comparability of research.
    • Evidence:
      • 86 studies included (all on physical restraint, 20 also on chemical restraint).
      • Seven thematic elements generated from definitions; explicit definitions of physical restraint commonly included restraint method, stated intent, and resident capacity to remove/control, but rarely setting, duration, caveats, or consent.
      • Implicit definitions (from data collection) often captured duration/frequency and setting but missed stated intent and consent/resistance.
      • No study reported validity of measurement approaches; only 27 physical restraint and 1 chemical restraint study reported inter-rater reliability.
      • The 2016 international consensus definition of physical restraint covers restraint method, stated intent, and resident capacity to remove/control but omits setting, duration/frequency, caveats, and consent/resistance.
    • Strength: Strong, based on a systematic review with PRISMA adherence, PROSPERO registration, dual reviewer screening, and inductive team-based thematic analysis; limitations include exclusion of non-English and pre-2000 studies, but these are appropriate for the review’s scope and goals.

Connections

  • physical restraint — This source highlights definitional and measurement inconsistencies in physical restraint research that challenge the validity of findings linked to this concept.
  • chemical restraint — Extends understanding by showing that chemical restraint lacks consensus definition and suffers from even greater measurement ambiguity than physical restraint.
  • consent and resistance — Identifies this as a critical but frequently omitted theme in both explicit definitions and measurement tools, despite its ethical significance.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Internal tension: Many studies defined physical restraint to include resident consent/resistance (e.g., noting resistance during care in interviews) but excluded this from explicit definitions and standard tools like RAI-MDS, creating a gap between theoretical understanding and practical measurement.
  • Tension with consensus definition: The widely cited 2016 international consensus definition of physical restraint omits key domains (setting, duration/frequency, caveats, consent/resistance) that many studies include in their definitions, creating a mismatch between authoritative guidance and actual research practice.
  • Conceptual debate: The review notes that frequency and duration may not be essential to the definition of restraint but could be useful for measuring intensity—suggesting an unresolved tension about whether these elements belong in definitions or are better suited to severity assessment.

Open Questions

  • Should duration and frequency be part of the definition of restraint, or only used to measure its intensity or severity?
  • How can consensus definitions be developed for chemical restraint given the challenge of distinguishing therapeutic intent from restraining intent in medication use?
  • What measurement tools or approaches best capture the full thematic scope of restraint—including consent, resistance, environmental methods, and human force—in long-term care settings?
