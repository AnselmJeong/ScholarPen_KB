Summary

This chapter examines how Article 12 of the CRPD, which guarantees legal capacity “on an equal basis with others,” should be interpreted in light of equality principles. It argues against formal equality models that demand identical treatment, showing how such approaches fail persons with cognitive disabilities like Jess, whose autonomous choice to remain in unsafe conditions leads to neglect. Drawing on the UN Disability Committee’s General Comment No 6 (2018), the chapter proposes that equality under Article 12 requires substantive equality through the four-dimensional framework of inclusive equality: redistributive, recognition, participative, and accommodating dimensions. This framework justifies supported decision-making as the preferred approach and allows substitute decision-making only as a last resort when necessary to prevent grave harm, thereby balancing autonomy with dignity and safety.

Key Entities

  • UN Disability Committee (type: org) — Author of General Comment No 6, providing authoritative interpretation of CRPD equality provisions
  • United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (type: org/framework) — The international human rights treaty establishing rights for persons with disabilities, including Article 12 on legal capacity
  • General Comment No 6 (2018) on equality and non-discrimination (type: org/document) — Authoritative UN interpretation defining inclusive equality and guiding interpretation of Articles 5 and 12
  • Article 5 CRPD (type: org/provision) — Establishes standalone right to equality and non-discrimination, including provisions for reasonable accommodation and specific measures
  • Article 12 CRPD (type: org/provision) — Guarantees legal capacity “on an equal basis with others,” central to chapter’s analysis of decision-making rights
  • Article 2 CRPD (type: org/provision) — Defines discrimination (including direct/indirect) and reasonable accommodation, providing legal framework for equality analysis
  • Reasonable accommodation (type: tool/mechanism) — Individualized adjustments to remove barriers for persons with disabilities, key mechanism for addressing indirect discrimination
  • Specific measures (type: tool/mechanism) — Temporary or permanent preferential actions to address systemic inequality, distinct from individualized accommodations
  • Inclusive equality (type: framework/concept) — Four-dimensional equality model from General Comment No 6 that integrates redistributive, recognition, participative, and accommodating dimensions to achieve substantive equality

Key Concepts

  • Formal equality — Treating likes alike regardless of outcome; ignores structural disadvantage and fails to address disability-related needs, as shown in Jess’s case where non-interference led to harm
  • Substantive equality — Requires equal outcomes, recognizes difference, and demands state action to counteract disadvantage; core to CRPD approach and necessary for meaningful legal capacity
  • Direct discrimination — Less favorable treatment due to disability in similar situations; easier to identify but less relevant to complex cases involving seemingly neutral rules
  • Indirect discrimination — Neutral rules/practices that disproportionately harm people with disability; central to analysis as Jess’s situation shows how “equal treatment” perpetuates harm
  • Reasonable accommodation — Individualized adjustments to remove disability-related barriers without imposing undue burden; essential for enabling Jess to live safely with support
  • Specific measures — Temporary or permanent preferential actions to redress systemic inequality; relevant for addressing historical exclusion of persons with cognitive disabilities
  • Inclusive equality — Four-dimensional model (redressing disadvantage, combating stigma, encouraging participation, accommodating difference) that organizes the chapter’s argument for contextual interpretation of Article 12
  • Dignity (five-dimensional) — Concept valuing embodied personhood, social relations, and impairment impacts; underlies argument that legal capacity must respect dignity beyond mere autonomy
  • Legal capacity — Ability to hold rights and exercise them; must be understood as “on an equal basis with others” through substantive equality, not identical treatment
  • Supported decision-making — Assistance enabling persons to make their own decisions; presented as preferred approach under inclusive equality that respects autonomy while providing necessary support
  • Decision-making by substitutes — Another person making decisions for the individual; argued as permissible last resort under inclusive equality when support fails and grave harm risks exist

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Article 12’s requirement of legal capacity “on an equal basis with others” does not require identical treatment or abolition of all substitute decision-making; instead, it must be interpreted through inclusive equality, allowing supported decision-making as norm and substitute decision-making as last resort to uphold dignity, safety, and substantive equality.
    • Evidence: General Comment No 6’s four dimensions of inclusive equality; analysis of Jess’s case showing how her autonomous choice to remain under the house leads to malnutrition and neglect, demonstrating that formal equality produces substantive inequality; distinction between direct and indirect discrimination where treating Jess “like others” ignores her disability-related needs; CRPD jurisprudence (e.g., HM v Sweden) showing neutral application of rules can violate rights when disability context is ignored.
    • Strength: Strong — argument is grounded in authoritative UN interpretation (General Comment No 6), supported by jurisprudence and concrete case example, and consistently applies CRPD’s own framework to challenge absolutist readings of Article 12.

Connections

  • inclusive equality — This chapter provides the foundational interpretation of inclusive equality under CRPD General Comment No 6, directly informing how this concept should be understood in the context of legal capacity and decision-making rights.
  • legal capacity — This chapter redefines the understanding of legal capacity under Article 12, arguing it must be exercised “on an equal basis with others” through substantive equality rather than formal equality, thus shaping the conceptual framework for this legal right.
  • supported decision-making — This chapter establishes supported decision-making as the preferred approach for exercising legal capacity under Article 12, positioning it as central to achieving inclusive equality while respecting autonomy.
  • substitute decision-making — This chapter defines the narrowly circumscribed conditions under which substitute decision-making may be permissible under Article 12 — only as a last resort when less restrictive means fail and grave harm risks exist — directly informing the limitations and safeguards around this concept.
  • reasonable accommodation — This chapter clarifies reasonable accommodation as an individualized response to disability-related barriers, essential for enabling substantive equality in legal capacity contexts, and distinguishes it from systemic specific measures.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The author acknowledges tension between respecting expressed will (Jess appears to accept her situation under the house) and preventing harm (malnourished, soiled conditions indicating neglect).
  • Caveat: Substitute decision-making must be strictly limited, regularly reviewed, and used only when less restrictive means fail — echoing CRPD safeguards against potential abuse.
  • Tension between autonomy and dignity: The argument posits that dignity (encompassing safety, belonging, and well-being) may sometimes require limiting absolute autonomy, challenging liberal individualist models that prioritize choice above all else.

Open Questions

  • How can legal safeguards effectively prevent abuse or overreach when substitute decision-making is permitted, even as a last resort, to ensure it remains truly exceptional and not a backdoor to paternalism?
  • What distinguishes legitimate, supportive relationships (e.g., familial care motivated by love and duty) from coercive control in complex domestic situations like Jess’s, where apparent consent may mask manipulation or fear?
  • How should the “last resort” threshold for substitute decision-making be operationalized in legal and clinical practice to ensure consistent application that respects both autonomy and safety without eroding legal capacity rights?
