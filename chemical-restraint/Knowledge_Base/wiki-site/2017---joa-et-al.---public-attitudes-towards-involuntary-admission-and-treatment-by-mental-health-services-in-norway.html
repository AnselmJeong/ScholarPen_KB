Summary

This study investigated public attitudes toward involuntary psychiatric admission and treatment in Norway using a nationally representative sample (n=2001) surveyed via Computer Assisted Telephone Interviews (CATI) in 2009 and 2011. Researchers found strong public support for coercive measures, with 87–97% agreeing with involuntary admission/treatment in specific scenarios (e.g., psychosis, suicidal ideation, dementia). A majority (56%) considered current levels acceptable, 34% thought levels were too low, and only 9.9% supported reduction. Lower education correlated with more positive attitudes toward coercion. The study employed univariate and bivariate analyses (cross-tabulation, ANOVA) and used Cohen’s d for effect size measurement. Findings challenge assumptions that media criticism reflects public opinion and suggest cultural factors like trust in the Welfare State shape attitudes toward coercion in mental health care.

Key Entities

  • SPSS (tool): Statistical software used for data analysis (ANOVA, cross-tabulation, Cohen’s d calculation)
  • Computer Assisted Telephone Interviews (CATI) (tool): Data collection method administered by an independent polling company

Key Concepts

  • Involuntary admission: Admission to psychiatric care against a person’s will, legally permitted under Norwegian mental health law when criteria of serious mental disorder and risk of harm or deterioration are met
  • Compulsory treatment: Administration of psychiatric treatment without patient consent, justified under Norwegian law for therapeutic purposes when individuals lack insight into their treatment needs
  • Public attitudes: General population’s views on coercive psychiatric practices, measured via Likert-scale agreement with statements about involuntary admission/treatment in specific scenarios
  • Treatment principle: Justification for involuntary admission based on serious mental disorder and assumption of treatment benefit, even without perceived dangerousness
  • Dangerousness principle: Justification for involuntary admission based on perceived threat of harm to self or others due to mental illness
  • Effect size (Cohen’s d): Statistical measure used to quantify the magnitude of differences in attitudes across demographic groups (e.g., education levels)

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The Norwegian general public largely supports current levels of involuntary admission and compulsory treatment in mental health services.
    • Evidence: Between 87% and 97% agreed with involuntary admission/treatment in specific scenarios; 56% believed current levels are acceptable; 34% thought levels were too low; only 9.9% supported reduction; lower education correlated with more positive attitudes (Cohen’s d = 0.21)
    • Strength: Strongly supported by large, representative national sample (n=2001) across two years; clear statistical analysis; minimized bias via neutral questioning and independent pollster
    • Limitation: No data on non-respondents; questions did not distinguish between admission vs. specific coercive interventions (e.g., medication, restraint), limiting interpretation of support for particular practices

Connections

  • involuntary admission — This source provides empirical data on public attitudes toward involuntary admission in Norway, showing widespread support contrary to media narratives
  • involuntary treatment — Demonstrates public acceptability of compulsory treatment under Norwegian law, particularly for self-care inability and suicide prevention
  • coercion in psychiatry — Challenges assumptions about public opposition to coercion, highlighting the gap between media criticism and actual public opinion
  • public opinion — Illustrates how public opinion on mental health coercion can diverge from media portrayals and professional assumptions
  • dangerousness principle — Finds weaker public support for coercion based on dangerousness (e.g., neighborhood threats) compared to treatment principle (e.g., self-care inability)
  • treatment principle — Shows strongest public support for involuntary admission when individuals cannot care for themselves, reflecting trust in medical benefit
  • education level — Identifies lower education as a demographic factor associated with more positive attitudes toward coercive psychiatric practices

Contradictions & Tensions

  • With media: Norwegian media often critical of coercion, yet public support remains high — suggests media does not represent public opinion and may reflect professional or advocacy biases rather than population views
  • With prior hypotheses: Authors expected public to favor curtailing coercion (based on media and initial assumptions); found instead that only 9.9% supported reduction, contradicting expectations of public opposition
  • Internal tension: Public supports coercion for “treatment principle” (self-care inability) more than “dangerousness principle” (neighborhood violence), contrary to some international studies (e.g., German studies citing violence as key factor) and suggesting cultural differences in justification priorities
  • Caveat: Support may reflect trust in medical profession or Welfare State, not endorsement of coercion per se; questions did not assess willingness to accept less restrictive alternatives (e.g., advance directives, community support)

Open Questions

  • Does public support for involuntary admission and treatment persist if presented with evidence of harm from coercive measures or evidence of effective alternatives (e.g., psychiatric advance directives, peer-supported care)?
  • How do attitudes differ when coercion is explicitly framed as a “last resort” compared to routine practice, and would support decrease if less restrictive options were widely available and known?
  • To what extent does public support stem from trust in the Norwegian Welfare State and medical profession, and would this support change in contexts with lower institutional trust or different healthcare systems?
