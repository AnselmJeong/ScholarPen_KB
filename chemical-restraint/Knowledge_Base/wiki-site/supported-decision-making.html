Why It Matters

  • Supported decision-making is essential for upholding the rights and dignity of persons with disabilities under international human rights law
  • It provides a framework that preserves legal capacity while recognizing the need for support in decision-making
  • It contrasts with substitute decision-making, emphasizing autonomy and avoiding paternalistic interventions
  • It aligns with the indivisibility of human rights, ensuring that support does not compromise safety or personhood

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Supported decision-making as a right Article 12 of the CRPD mandates supported decision-making to uphold legal capacity, dignity, and safety, with substitute decision-making only as a last resort in hard cases Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -01
Balance of social model and embodiment Effective disability rights frameworks must integrate social model insights with recognition of embodied impairment, especially for cognitive disability, to avoid undermining autonomy Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -02
