Summary

This integrative review synthesized evidence from 10 primary studies (2000–2017) across the UK, Australia, Norway, and Germany to explore the physical and psychological harm of physical restraint in mental health inpatient settings. Using Cooper’s (1998) five-stage framework and quality appraisal via CASP and Walsh & Downe’s tools, the review included qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods designs. Eight interconnected themes emerged: trauma/retraumatization, distress, fear, feeling ignored, control, power, calm, and dehumanizing conditions. The review concluded that harm is inherent in physical restraint regardless of staff intentions or framing as therapeutic or last resort, with psychological harm being pervasive and underrecognized compared to physical injury statistics.

Key Concepts

  • Physical restraint — Defined as any occasion where staff physically hold a patient to prevent movement (typically to prevent imminent harm, enable treatment, or initiate containment); excludes equipment/technology-based restraints.
  • Psychological harm — Encompasses trauma/retraumatization, distress, fear, feeling ignored, loss of control, power imbalance, and dehumanization; central outcome explored in the review.
  • Physical harm — Includes pain, injury, and adverse physical effects from restraint (e.g., being left in urine-soaked clothing, joint locks causing pain); directly documented in included studies.
  • Trauma/retraumatization — Psychological harm where restraint triggers memories of past abuse (e.g., childhood sexual abuse, hospital trauma); identified in five studies.
  • Dehumanizing conditions — Experience of being treated as “subhuman” (e.g., left in soiled clothing, verbal abuse, loss of dignity); identified as a key theme across multiple studies.
  • Power and control — Staff misuse of restraint to exert dominance (e.g., excessive force, “bouncer mentality,” punishment); highlighted as a source of relational injustice.
  • Feeling ignored — Service users’ perceptions that their distress or wishes were overlooked by staff, escalating to crisis; linked to overlooked advance statements.
  • Fear — Anticipatory or ongoing anxiety about restraint (during, after, or future episodes); pervasive across staff and service users.
  • Calm — Paradoxical theme where restraint is perceived as soothing (e.g., releasing agitation, promoting reflection); reported in three studies but deemed outweighed by harm.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Physical restraint in mental health inpatient settings inherently causes physical and psychological harm, even when intended as therapeutic or protective, with harm manifesting across eight interconnected themes: trauma/retraumatization, distress, fear, feeling ignored, control, power, calm, and dehumanizing conditions.
    • Evidence: Synthesis of 10 primary studies using Cooper’s integrative review framework; themes identified via constant comparison across qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods designs from UK, Australia, Norway, and Germany. Quality assessed via CASP and Walsh & Downe’s tools (no D-rated papers excluded).
      • Trauma/retraumatization: Supported by 5 studies (e.g., Bonner et al. 2002; Wynn 2004) linking restraint to past abuse memories.
      • Feeling ignored: Evident in Bonner et al. (2002) and Haw et al. (2011) (e.g., advance statements overlooked).
      • Dehumanization: Documented in Bonner et al. (2002) (urine-soaked clothing), Brophy et al. (2016) (“subhuman”), and Wilson et al. (2017) (not “decent human beings”).
      • Distress/fear: Most prevalent themes; reported by service users and staff across multiple studies (e.g., Wilson et al. 2017; Sequeira & Halstead 2002).
      • Power/control: Highlighted in Brophy et al. (2016) (“first, not last resort”), Lee et al. (2003) (“bouncer mentality”), and Knowles et al. (2015) (staff as “powerful perpetrators”).
      • Calm: Reported in 3 studies (Wynn 2004; Haw et al. 2011; Sequeira & Halstead 2002) but deemed outweighed by harm and potentially reflective of maladaptive coping.
    • Strength: Moderate to strong due to multi-country, multi-method synthesis and rigorous quality appraisal. Limitations include small sample size (10 papers) and potential cultural bias (7 UK studies), limiting global generalizability. However, thematic convergence across diverse settings strengthens internal validity.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Internal tension: The emergence of “calm” as a theme contradicts the overarching harm narrative, suggesting restraint may have subjective benefits for some (e.g., agitation release, reflection). However, authors argue this is outweighed by harm and note it may reflect maladaptive coping (e.g., self-induced restraint to regain control).
  • Caveat: Review excludes equipment/technology-based restraints (e.g., belts, chairs), which may have different harm profiles; findings apply only to manual physical restraint.
  • No contradiction with wiki: Existing concepts (e.g., concepts/physical-restraint) acknowledge risks but this review provides granular, service-user-centered evidence of harm that deepens understanding without conflicting with current framing.

Open Questions

  • How do harms differ across restraint types (manual vs. mechanical vs. chemical) in terms of physical and psychological impact?
  • What interventions most effectively mitigate psychological harm (e.g., advance statements, de-escalation, peer support) when restraint is deemed unavoidable?
  • How can organizational culture shift from “bouncer mentality” to therapeutic engagement, given entrenched staff fear and power dynamics in mental health inpatient settings?
