Summary

This paper analyzes the meaning of “rights, will and preferences” under Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), particularly in mental health contexts where a person’s current expressed preference may conflict with their prior advance directive or enduring values. Drawing on philosophical concepts of will (Kant, Bratman), legal precedents from the German Federal Constitutional Court and English Court of Protection, and interpretive frameworks like Davidson’s radical interpretation, the author argues that “will” represents a higher-order reflection of deeply held values and commitments, distinct from transient preferences. When these diverge—such as during acute mental health episodes—respecting the person’s will (e.g., by following an advance directive) may justify overriding current preferences without constituting impermissible substitute decision-making under the CRPD. The paper concludes that legal capacity must be upheld, and decisions should aim to facilitate the expression of enduring will, supported by interpretive efforts and safeguards, rather than defaulting to best interests or blanket prohibitions on intervention.

Key Entities

  • United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (org): The international treaty establishing rights for persons with disabilities, central to the analysis of legal capacity and decision-making support.
  • UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD Committee) (org): The body issuing authoritative interpretations of the CRPD, whose stance on rejecting substitute decision-making and emphasizing will and preferences drives the paper’s inquiry.
  • German Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) (org): Provided legal precedent distinguishing “free will” and “natural will,” supporting the view that prior competent will can override impaired current expressions.
  • English Court of Protection (org): Issued rulings respecting advance directives and deeply held values (will) over current preferences, illustrating practical application of the will-preference distinction.
  • Advance directives (tool): Legal documents expressing future treatment preferences made when capacitated, serving as key evidence of enduring will in mental health crises.

Key Concepts

  • Will — A higher-order function reflecting enduring values, beliefs, and commitments that guide decision-making; distinct from transient preferences and central to interpreting CRPD Article 12 as the authentic expression of autonomy.
  • Preference — A transient liking or inclination for one option over another, which may fluctuate with mental state and not reflect enduring values; crucial for understanding when current expressions should not be followed.
  • Supported decision-making — A process of assisting persons with disabilities to exercise legal capacity through support aligned with their will and preferences, proposed as an alternative to substitute decision-making under the CRPD.
  • Substitute decision-making — Replacement of a person’s decision-making by another based on perceived best interests; considered inconsistent with the CRPD when based on mental capacity impairment, but potentially justified when honoring prior will overrides current preference.
  • Legal capacity — The legal recognition of the ability to hold rights and duties and exercise them, distinct from mental capacity; a core CRPD principle that cannot be denied due to disability.
  • Best interests — A standard for decision-making based on what is objectively beneficial for a person; contrasted with the will-based approach and rejected by the CRPD Committee as a basis for substitute decisions.
  • Advance statement — A document expressing future treatment preferences made when capacitated, illustrating the tension between past will and present preference and serving as a guide for determining authentic autonomy.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The CRPD’s requirement to respect “rights, will and preferences” requires distinguishing between enduring “will” (deep values) and transient “preferences,” where honoring prior will may justify overriding current preferences without constituting impermissible substitute decision-making.
    • Evidence: Philosophical accounts of will (Kant, Bratman) describe it as a higher-order reflective mechanism; legal precedents (German Federal Constitutional Court, English Court of Protection) recognize advance directives as expressions of will; interpretive methods like Davidson’s radical intention support assessing will through patterns of belief and values.
    • Strength: Well-supported by interdisciplinary analysis, but limited by the CRPD Committee’s lack of formal definition and challenges in determining will in cases of severe or congenital cognitive disability.
  • Core claim: An intervention aimed at giving effect to a person’s will—even against a current preference—does not constitute substitute decision-making in an important sense, as it facilitates authentic autonomy rather than imposing external judgment.
    • Evidence: The paper argues that supporting expression of will aligns with the CRPD’s focus on autonomy and legal capacity, contrasting with best interests models that override personal values; case examples show respecting advance directives honors enduring self-governance.
    • Strength: Conceptually coherent and legally plausible, but depends on accurate will interpretation, which may be contested or unclear in practice.

Connections

  • legal-capacity — This source provides a nuanced interpretation of how legal capacity under the CRPD should be exercised through respect for will and preferences, particularly in mental health contexts.
  • supported-decision-making — The paper advances supported decision-making by clarifying that it must be grounded in discerning and respecting enduring will, not merely current expressions of preference.
  • advance-directive — Advance directives are presented as critical tools for expressing and preserving will, especially when current preferences diverge due to mental health crisis.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The CRPD Committee’s apparent blanket rejection of substitute decision-making creates tension with scenarios where overriding a current preference (to honor prior will) may be necessary and consistent with the CRPD, suggesting the Committee’s stance may be overly simplistic.
  • There is a tension between respecting autonomy through will and protecting individuals from harm when impaired judgment affects preference expression—a balance the paper addresses through interpretive safeguards but does not fully resolve.
  • Internally, the author acknowledges limitations in determining will in cases of severe cognitive disability or unconsciousness, noting that fallback to rights-based decisions may be necessary, which introduces uncertainty into the will-centered framework.

Open Questions

  • How should will be determined in cases of congenital or early-onset cognitive disability where lifelong preferences may not reflect stable or identifiable values, and what role should rights or best interests play as fallbacks?
  • What procedural and substantive safeguards are needed to prevent the misuse of “will” as a justification for overriding genuine, contemporaneous objections, especially in coercive psychiatric settings?
  • How does this will-preference framework interact with other legal domains, such as criminal responsibility or civil commitment, where risk-based decisions may conflict with advance directives or expressions of will?
