Summary

This scoping review maps and systematizes the literature on ethical issues in clinical decision-making about involuntary psychiatric treatment. The study analyzed 35 peer-reviewed articles from 14 countries (published between 2008 and 2023) identified through searches of Embase, PsycINFO, CINAHL, Medline, and Scopus. Using inductive content analysis, the review organized findings into five main categories: ethical foundations guiding decisions, criteria for involuntary treatment, gaps/barriers/risks, strategies to improve practice, and evidence-based recommendations. The review highlights persistent tensions between autonomy and beneficence, inconsistent application of legal criteria, and the prevalence of moral uncertainty among clinicians. It emphasizes the need for context-sensitive, participatory approaches grounded in human rights frameworks like the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD).

Key Concepts

  • Ethical foundations — Core principles (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) used to justify or challenge decisions about involuntary psychiatric treatment
  • Involuntary psychiatric treatment — Psychiatric care administered without valid consent, often involving coercive interventions such as medication, seclusion, or restraint
  • Coercive measures — Actions that limit patient autonomy or freedom of movement (e.g., physical restraints, seclusion, forced medication) in psychiatric care settings
  • Relational ethics — Ethical approach emphasizing interpersonal relationships, mutual understanding, and contextual responsiveness in care, contrasted with rule-based models
  • Rights-based ethics — Framework prioritizing individual autonomy and universal human rights as inviolable, frequently in tension with beneficence-driven justifications for coercion
  • Compulsory Community Treatment (CCT) — Mandatory outpatient treatment requiring medication adherence and appointment attendance, also known as assisted outpatient treatment (AOT)
  • Moral doubt — Uncertainty experienced by clinicians about whether coercive actions are morally justified, identified as a barrier to ethical practice
  • Person-centered care (PCC) — Care model recognizing patients as experts in their own experience, emphasizing dignity, shared decision-making, and responsiveness to lived experience
  • Legal capacity — Legal determination of an individual’s ability to make informed decisions about treatment, central to eligibility criteria for involuntary admission
  • Ethical decision-making — Process of weighing competing values (e.g., autonomy vs. safety) when determining whether to initiate or continue involuntary psychiatric treatment

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Ethical decision-making in involuntary psychiatric treatment requires balancing autonomy with beneficence, non-maleficence, and justice, but is consistently undermined by power imbalances, inadequate communication, and inconsistent application of legal and ethical criteria across jurisdictions.
  • Evidence: Derived from inductive content analysis of 35 studies (qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods) from 14 countries; findings were synthesized into five thematic categories reflecting recurring patterns in the literature.
  • Strength: Strong descriptive validity for identifying widespread ethical tensions (e.g., autonomy vs. beneficence conflicts) and procedural shortcomings (e.g., poor consent processes); limited prescriptive strength due to the scoping review’s exclusion of quality appraisal and meta-analysis, which prevents conclusions about intervention effectiveness or hierarchical evidence ranking.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The review identifies an unresolved tension between those who argue coercion should only benefit the patient (not third parties) and those who justify it via public safety (invoking non-maleficence and justice)—a conflict not resolved in the literature.
  • If the wiki previously portrayed involuntary treatment as ethically straightforward or uniformly effective, this source contradicts that by demonstrating widespread ethical uncertainty, contextual variability in justification, and lack of consensus on when coercion is permissible.
  • A noted limitation is the overrepresentation of studies from Western/Northern Hemisphere countries (particularly Nordic and Western Europe), which may limit the global applicability of findings; grey literature was also excluded, potentially omitting relevant policy or practice-based insights.

Open Questions

  • How can relational ethics be operationalized in under-resourced psychiatric settings where staff lack time, training, or structural support for relationship-centered care?
  • What are the long-term effects of Compulsory Community Treatment (CCT) on patient autonomy, trust in mental health systems, and therapeutic relationships?
  • Under what specific conditions (if any) can coercive measures be considered ethically justified without violating the principles of the CRPD, particularly regarding legal capacity and liberty?
