Summary

The Mysore Declaration emerged from a 2013 international symposium in Mysore, India, bringing together experts from the Indian Forensic Mental Health Association and the European Violence in Psychiatry Research Group to address the under-documented issue of coercion in Indian mental health care. The declaration defines coercive measures for the Indian context—including physical, chemical, mechanical, environmental restraint, seclusion, and psychological restraint—and outlines a multi-pronged strategy to reduce their use. This strategy includes raising awareness, developing evidence-based guidelines, implementing staff training, and establishing benchmarking systems using validated tools to monitor and compare practices. Central to the declaration is the affirmation that coercion, particularly when disproportionate or degrading, violates the human rights of persons with mental illness, while acknowledging the complex interplay between patient autonomy, potential benefits of involuntary treatment in restoring functioning, and the significant role of families in treatment decisions within Indian socio-cultural contexts. The declaration identifies legal ambiguities—such as the lack of competence definitions in the Indian Mental Health Act—as key structural barriers, alongside stigma, resource constraints, and inadequate advocacy, and calls for legal reform, institutional accountability, and increased awareness to align practice with human rights standards.

Key Concepts

  • Coercive measures — Actions that compel treatment or restrict liberty, including physical, chemical, mechanical, environmental restraint, seclusion, and psychological restraint; central focus for definition and reduction in the Indian context.
  • Human rights of persons with mental illness — Entitlement to equality, security, liberty, health, integrity, dignity, and protection from discrimination, violence, and degrading treatment; framed as foundational to the declaration’s call for reform.
  • Least restrictive treatment — Principle advocating minimal necessary intervention to balance therapeutic need with patient autonomy; contextualized within family-influenced decision-making in India.
  • Benchmarking — Systematic measurement and comparison of coercive practices using validated tools; proposed as a mechanism for quality improvement and identifying outliers in practice.
  • Capacity assessment — Evaluation of a person’s ability to make informed treatment decisions; implied need due to legal gaps in defining competence under the Indian Mental Health Act.
  • Structural barriers to reform — Encompasses legal ambiguities (e.g., undefined competence), lack of awareness, prejudice, resource scarcity, and weak advocacy; identified as systemic obstacles to reducing coercion.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Coercion in mental health care is a widespread but inadequately documented problem in India requiring urgent, human rights–based reform that accounts for local realities such as family involvement and legal gaps, and the Mysore Declaration provides a context-specific framework to define coercive measures, reduce their use, and protect patient rights.
  • Evidence: The declaration synthesizes existing literature indicating high use of coercion in India, particularly with family involvement in decision-making (Srinivasan & Thara, 2002), and draws on international evidence from high-income countries showing the effectiveness of training, guidelines, and benchmarking in reducing coercion (Abderhalden et al., 2008; Steinert et al., 2010; Lepping, 2013). It emerged from a consensus process involving Indian and European experts via symposium dialogue.
  • Strength of evidence: Strong on normative and conceptual grounds, grounded in established human rights principles and adapted international best practices. Moderate on direct empirical grounding, as it relies on secondary data and expert consensus rather than new Indian-specific prevalence data or outcome studies of proposed interventions. Recommendations are extrapolated from settings with different infrastructural and legal contexts, requiring local validation for effectiveness.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The declaration acknowledges a tension between upholding absolute patient autonomy (particularly the right to refuse medication) and recognizing the potential therapeutic value of involuntary treatment in restoring functioning, especially when families advocate for intervention. While it references the Hawaii Doctrine—which permits compulsory treatment only if in the patient’s best interest—and stresses shared decision-making, it does not fully resolve how to balance these competing claims when capacity is impaired and consensus is unattainable.

Open Questions

  • What specific legal and policy mechanisms—such as advance directives, independent capacity tribunals, or regulated involuntary treatment criteria—would best align with the Indian Constitution while preventing misuse and protecting persons with mental illness?
