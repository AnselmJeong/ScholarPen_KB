Summary

This chapter examines the tension between psychiatric coercion and the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UN CRPD). It traces the historical evolution of psychiatry through three phases: pre-psychiatric containment, rights-ignoring custodial care, and the current era where rights are considered but can be restricted for medical reasons. The author argues that the UN CRPD presents a fundamental challenge to psychiatric coercion by requiring respect for legal capacity, prohibiting non-consensual medical intervention, and mandating supported decision-making—directly contradicting traditional psychiatric justifications for involuntary treatment based on impaired judgment and risk. The analysis draws on UN human rights bodies’ findings that coercion constitutes torture or ill-treatment, national implementation reviews showing persistent use of guardianship despite CRPD ratification, and scholarly critiques that psychiatric concepts of illness unjustifiably override personhood and autonomy. The significance lies in reframing the debate around psychiatric coercion from a medical ethics framework to a human rights framework that prioritizes the will and preferences of persons with psychosocial disabilities.

  • World Health Organization (WHO) (org) — UN agency issuing guidelines that call for avoidance of coercion and respect for human rights in psychiatry
  • World Psychiatric Association (WPA) (org) — Professional organization that has engaged with UN critiques but maintains positions allowing for coercion in certain circumstances
  • American Psychiatric Association (org) — Professional organization involved in exchanges with UN rapporteurs regarding torture and coercion in psychiatric settings

  • QualityRights Initiative (program) — WHO program promoting rights-based approaches in mental health, including training and service transformation

Key Concepts

  • Legal capacity — The right to make legally binding decisions; under the CRPD, this is retained by all persons with disabilities regardless of mental state
  • Mental capacity — Cognitive ability to make specific decisions; distinguished from legal capacity in CRPD interpretation
  • Supported decision-making — Model where individuals receive assistance in exercising legal capacity without losing it, as opposed to substituted decision-making
  • Substituted decision-making — Legal mechanisms (e.g., guardianship) where another person makes decisions for someone deemed incapable
  • Social model of disability — Framework viewing disability as arising from societal barriers rather than individual impairment
  • Biomedical model of mental illness — Dominant paradigm viewing mental disorders as biomedical conditions requiring medical intervention
  • Therapeutic relationship — The alliance between clinician and service user, which coercion may damage or undermine
  • Least restrictive alternative — Ethical/legal principle requiring minimal freedom restriction when intervention is necessary
  • Best interest standard — Justification for intervention based on presumed benefit to the person, often used to override objections
  • Human rights-based approach — Ethical framework prioritizing individual rights, autonomy, and consent in healthcare, grounded in international human rights law

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The UN CRPD presents a fundamental challenge to psychiatric coercion by asserting that persons with disabilities (including psychosocial disabilities) retain legal capacity and must consent to all medical interventions, directly contradicting traditional psychiatric justifications for involuntary treatment based on impaired judgment and risk.
    • Evidence: Historical analysis of three phases in psychiatry; analysis of UN CRPD Articles 12, 15, and 25; UN human rights bodies’ findings that coercion constitutes torture/ill-treatment; national implementation reviews (Germany 2015, Switzerland 2022) showing persistent guardianship and involuntary treatment; scholarly critiques arguing psychiatric concepts of illness unjustifiably override personhood.
    • Strength: Strong historical and legal foundation synthesizing international law, ethics, and empirical critiques. Less focus on empirical data about outcomes of coercion abolition; relies more on principled argument than outcome evidence.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The author acknowledges that abolishing psychiatric coercion could increase risks (suicide, incarceration) while maintaining it violates rights, presenting this as an unresolved dilemma in implementation.
  • There is a conflict with mainstream biomedical ethics, which does not prioritize human rights over virtues, creating tension with the CRPD’s rights-first approach.
  • Even within social psychiatry, there is disagreement about whether service users can reliably judge their own needs, creating a professional divide on capacity assessments.
  • Conceptual tension exists over whether “mental illness” inherently impairs decision-making capacity or is a social construct that unjustifiably removes rights.
  • Implementation gap noted: WHO promotes recovery-oriented care but retains the biomedical illness concept, creating inconsistency in its own guidelines.

Open Questions

  • How can societal safety concerns be balanced with the CRPD’s absolute consent requirements in psychiatric contexts?
  • What evidence exists on outcomes in systems that have significantly reduced coercion (e.g., Soteria, Open Dialogue) regarding both safety and well-being?
  • How can supported decision-making be operationalized in acute crisis scenarios where individuals may appear unable to express preferences?
  • Does the CRPD require abolition of all forms of coercion (including chemical and mechanical restraint) or only non-consensual application, and how is consent defined in crisis situations?
