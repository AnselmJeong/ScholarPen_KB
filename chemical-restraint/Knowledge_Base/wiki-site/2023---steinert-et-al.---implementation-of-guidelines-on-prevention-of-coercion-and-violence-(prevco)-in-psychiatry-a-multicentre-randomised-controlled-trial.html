Summary

This study evaluated the effectiveness of implementing guideline-based recommendations to reduce coercive measures in psychiatric wards across Germany. Fifty-four psychiatric wards were randomized to either an intervention group receiving support from external implementation consultants to adopt three of twelve guideline recommendations, or a waitlist control group. The primary outcome was the number of coercive measures (seclusion, restraint, forced medication) per occupied bed per month during the final three months of the intervention period. Secondary outcomes included duration of coercive measures and assaults. Intervention fidelity was measured using the newly developed PreVCo Rating Tool, which assesses adherence to the selected recommendations on a scale from 0 to 135. After a three-month baseline period, the intervention lasted twelve months, with outcome data collected during the final three months to account for implementation latency.

The intervention successfully improved guideline adherence, with intervention wards showing a significant increase in PreVCo Rating Tool scores (mean difference: 12.4 points, p < 0.001, Cohen’s d = 1.48). However, no statistically significant differences were found between groups in the primary or secondary outcomes related to coercive measures or assaults. Both groups showed reductions over time (intervention: -45%, control: -28%), suggesting possible secular trends or spill-over effects. The study concludes that while external implementation support can effectively change professional behavior and improve guideline adherence, this did not translate into measurable reductions in coercive practices within the study period, highlighting a potential gap between process improvement and clinical outcomes in psychiatric care.

Key Entities

  • German Association for Psychiatry, Psychotherapy, and Psychosomatics (DGPPN) (org): Developed the clinical practice guidelines on prevention of coercion that informed the 12 recommendations implemented in the study.
  • PreVCo Rating Tool (tool): A fidelity scale developed for this study to measure adherence to guideline recommendations; each of 12 items scored 0–9, with multipliers for complex interventions, yielding a total score range of 0–135.
  • German Innovationsfonds beim Gemeinsamen Bundesausschuss (org): Provided funding for the study (project no. 01VSF19037) but had no role in design or data collection.
  • Safewards (tool): A structured intervention model referenced as one of the options ward teams could select for implementation under the guideline recommendations.
  • Six Core Strategies (tool): An evidence-based framework for reducing seclusion and rupture, referenced as an optional intervention choice for participating wards.
  • Weddinger model (tool): A therapeutic approach to psychiatric care mentioned as one of the optional interventions teams could choose to implement.

Key Concepts

  • Guideline adherence: The extent to which clinical teams followed the selected recommendations from the DGPPN guidelines, measured objectively via the PreVCo Rating Tool; central to the study as the primary mechanism of intervention.
  • Coercive measures: Interventions such as seclusion, restraint, or forced medication used in psychiatric settings; the primary clinical outcome the study aimed to reduce.
  • Cluster-randomized RCT: A study design where entire wards (clusters) were randomized to intervention or control to prevent contamination; key for internal validity.
  • Implementation fidelity: The degree to which an intervention is delivered as intended; operationalized here through the PreVCo Rating Tool scores.
  • Fidelity scale: A measurement instrument assessing how closely an intervention is implemented according to protocol; the PreVCo Rating Tool served this purpose in evaluating the success of the implementation strategy.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The PreVCo intervention significantly improved guideline adherence (as measured by the PreVCo Rating Tool) but did not significantly reduce the frequency, duration, or incidence of coercive measures or assaults in psychiatric wards.
    • Evidence: Intervention wards showed a mean increase of 12.4 points on the PreVCo Rating Tool compared to controls (p < 0.001, d = 1.48). No significant between-group differences were found for coercive measures per bed/month, duration, or assaults in the final three months. Both groups improved over time (intervention: -45%, control: -28%).
    • Strength: Strong evidence for implementation success due to large effect size, statistical significance, and pre-specified analysis. Evidence for clinical impact is weak due to non-significant primary outcome, potential underpowering (n=27 wards/group), and concurrent improvements in control group suggesting secular trends or spill-over.

Connections

  • coercive measures — This study directly measures changes in the use of coercive measures as a primary outcome.
  • guideline adherence — The study introduces and validates the PreVCo Rating Tool as a tool to assess adherence to psychiatric guidelines.
  • cluster-randomized trial — The study employs this design to evaluate a complex behavioral intervention in naturalistic clinical settings.
  • psychiatric interventions — The research evaluates a multi-component strategy to reduce coercion through guideline implementation.
  • implementation science — The study contributes to understanding how best to translate clinical guidelines into changed professional behavior in mental health settings.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • A key tension exists between the significant improvement in process (guideline adherence) and the lack of corresponding change in clinical outcomes (coercive use), challenging the assumption that better adherence necessarily improves patient care.
  • This contrasts with prior expectations and some implementation science literature that posits improved adherence as a mediator of better outcomes; here, the causal chain appears broken or delayed.
  • Limitations such as high baseline adherence (ceiling effects), potential contamination between groups, pandemic-related disruptions, and limited statistical power may explain the dissociation.

Open Questions

  • Does improved guideline adherence eventually lead to reduced coercion with longer follow-up, higher fidelity, or more comprehensive implementation of recommendations?
  • Are certain types of recommendations (e.g., those involving direct patient interaction like debriefing or team communication) more likely to influence coercive practices than others?
  • How do organizational and contextual factors (such as leadership stability, staffing levels, or ward culture) moderate the effect of implementation strategies on both adherence and clinical outcomes in psychiatric settings?
