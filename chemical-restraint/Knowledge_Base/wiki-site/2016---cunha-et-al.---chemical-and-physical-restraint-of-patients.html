Summary

This study evaluates the level of knowledge among 156 nurses in Portugal regarding physical and chemical restraint of patients using a validated 240-item knowledge questionnaire (KQPPCR). The cross-sectional descriptive study assessed knowledge across four dimensions: patient safety, legal and ethical practice, scientific knowledge, and quality of care. Findings reveal that while 92.3% of nurses view physical restraint as a protective measure for patient safety and 87.2% support its use for fall risk, significant gaps exist in legal and ethical understanding—only 47.4% correctly identified that restraint is not an autonomous nursing act. Nurses demonstrated strongest knowledge in scientific concepts (mean 60/88) but weakest in legal and ethical practice (mean 25/38). Despite 69.2% not viewing restraint as a quality indicator, 76.9% still considered it good practice when necessary, indicating cognitive dissonance. The study concludes that targeted training addressing legal and ethical dimensions, not just technical skills, is needed to improve practice.

Key Concepts

  • Physical restraint — Restriction of patient movement using mechanical or manual devices (e.g., belts, vests) to prevent harm; central focus of the study’s knowledge assessment
  • Chemical restraint — Use of pharmaceuticals to restrict behavior or movement, requiring medical prescription; evaluated alongside physical restraint for knowledge gaps
  • Patient safety — Protection from harm (e.g., falls, self-injury); 92.3% of nurses viewed restraint as enhancing safety, making it the most strongly endorsed benefit
  • Legal and ethical practice — Compliance with regulations (e.g., DGS Circular) and ethical principles (autonomy, dignity); revealed as the weakest knowledge domain (mean 25/38)
  • Scientific knowledge — Understanding of evidence-based indications, risks, and techniques; strongest domain (mean 60/88), indicating better grasp of clinical application
  • Quality of care — Alignment with dignified, patient-centered practice; 69.2% did not see restraint as a quality indicator, yet 76.9% endorsed it as good practice when necessary
  • Risk assessment — Evaluation of patient behavior (e.g., aggression, fall risk) to determine necessity; highlighted as prerequisite before restraint use in guidelines
  • Informed consent — Requirement to inform patient/family before restraint use; implied in ethical guidelines but not directly measured in the survey
  • Monitoring protocols — Regular assessment of vital signs, circulation, and skin integrity during restraint; 100% correctly identified skin and vital checks as necessary
  • Alternatives to restraint — Non-restrictive interventions (verbal, environmental) to be tried first; 94.2% agreed restraint should be last resort after alternatives fail

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Nurses demonstrate moderate overall knowledge of physical and chemical restraint, with significant gaps in legal/ethical understanding despite strong beliefs in safety benefits.
    • Evidence: Survey of 156 nurses using the KQPPCR; 46.9% scored in the “good knowledge” range, 6.2% “reasonable,” and 46.9% “weak” overall knowledge. Dimension-specific means: Patient Safety (27/36), Legal/Ethical (25/38), Scientific Knowledge (60/88), Quality of Care (48/72).
    • Strength: Well-supported by a validated instrument and adequate sample size; limited by cross-sectional design (no causality) and self-report bias (social desirability may inflate ethical responses).
  • Core claim: Legal and ethical knowledge is the weakest domain, particularly regarding autonomy and nursing scope of practice.
    • Evidence: Only 47.4% knew restraint is not an autonomous nursing act; 76.3% were aware of the DGS Circular, but 88.5% believed restraint promotes dignity—a view ethically contested.
    • Strength: Consistent with literature on ethical tensions; however, the belief in dignity promotion may reflect proper application rather than inherent ethical validity, creating interpretive ambiguity.
  • Core claim: Strong support for alternatives-as-first-step coexists with belief in restraint as effective immobilization.
    • Evidence: 94.2% agreed restraint should follow failed alternatives (verbal/chemical first), yet 71.8% believed physical restraint is the “best way” to immobilize patients.
    • Strength: Highlights cognitive dissonance; suggests training should reinforce that alternatives are not just procedural steps but preferred evidence-based approaches.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Tension: High belief that restraint promotes dignity (88.5%) conflicts with ethical literature arguing it inherently violates autonomy—a concept acknowledged in the paper’s discussion but not reconciled in participant views, suggesting a justification gap between intent and ethical outcome.
  • Contradiction: 76.9% view restraint as “good practice” while 69.2% deny it indicates quality care—indicating cognitive dissonance where nurses may justify restraint situationally (e.g., emergency) rather than as a systemic quality marker.
  • Internal caveat: Authors note that belief in dignity promotion may reflect proper application rather than inherent ethical validity, but this nuance may not be clear to readers, risking misinterpretation of nurses’ ethical justification as endorsement of restraint’s moral permissibility.

Open Questions

  • How do cultural and institutional factors in Portuguese healthcare settings shape nurses’ ethical justifications for restraint, particularly the belief that it promotes dignity?
  • What is the impact of simulation-based training focused on legal and ethical dimensions (not just technical skills) on reducing knowledge gaps in restraint practices?
  • Is the persistent belief that “restraint promotes dignity” a barrier to adopting restraint-free care models, and how can reframing this perception support organizational change?
