Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis synthesized evidence from 77 studies published between January 2010 and August 2022 to estimate the pooled prevalence of restrictive care practices (RCPs) in adult mental health inpatient settings and identify sources of variability in reported estimates. Using PRISMA 2020 guidelines, the study analyzed data from Scopus, MEDLINE, PsycINFO, Web of Science, Embase, and CINAHL databases. The pooled prevalence estimates were 14.4% for physical restraint, 15.8% for seclusion, and 25.7% for chemical restraint. However, the data exhibited extreme heterogeneity, with I² statistics exceeding 99% for all outcomes, indicating substantial inconsistency across studies. Meta-regression revealed that geographical location and data collection methods significantly explained variability in prevalence estimates, with Asian studies reporting higher rates and direct observation yielding higher estimates than medical record review. Study design, definition specificity, and restraint type did not significantly account for variability. The findings underscore the need for standardized definitions and measurement protocols to enable valid cross-regional comparisons and evaluation of reduction initiatives.

Key Concepts

  • Restrictive care practices (RCPs) — Physical/mechanical or pharmacological interventions that restrict freedom of movement, decision-making, autonomy, or choice; includes physical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint
  • Heterogeneity (I² statistic) — Measures the proportion of variance in study estimates due to heterogeneity rather than chance; values >99% indicate considerable inconsistency across studies
  • Meta-regression — Analysis to determine whether explanatory variables significantly impact outcome estimates; used to identify sources of variability in prevalence estimates
  • Adjusted R² — Proportion of variance in outcome explained by model, adjusted for number of variables; used in meta-regression to better estimate explained variance without inflation from study size
  • τ² (tau-squared) — Estimates between-study variance; square root used to assess variability not systematically increasing with study size or number of variables

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The prevalence of restrictive care practices (physical restraint, seclusion, chemical restraint) in adult mental health inpatient settings is highly variable across studies, and this variability is significantly influenced by geographical location and data collection methods.
    • Evidence: Pooled prevalence estimates derived from 77 studies using random-effects meta-analysis; meta-regression identified geographical location (R² = 17.07%) and data collection methods as significant moderators of heterogeneity; I² >99% for all outcomes indicates extreme inconsistency.
    • Strength: Strong methodological rigor with comprehensive search, PRISMA adherence, and advanced statistical modeling; limited by inconsistencies in definitions and measurement across studies, which hinder definitive conclusions about true practice variation.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The review concludes that geographical differences in RCP use exist but are confounded by inconsistent measurement and definitions, undermining the certainty of cross-regional comparisons as reflections of true clinical or cultural differences.
  • While no direct contradiction exists with current wiki content, the study highlights a gap: the wiki contains extensive conceptual and ethical discussions (e.g., human rights, autonomy) but lacks epidemiological synthesis on actual prevalence and variability of RCPs.
  • This challenges assumptions that regional differences in reported RCP use reflect genuine variations in practice, suggesting instead that methodological inconsistencies may drive apparent disparities.

Open Questions

  • How can global consensus on defining and measuring restrictive care practices be achieved to ensure valid cross-national comparisons?
  • What are the real-world implications of the high pooled prevalence of chemical restraint (25.7%) for patient rights, autonomy, and safety in mental health care?
  • How do variations in reporting and measurement affect the evaluation of intervention programs designed to reduce restraint use, and what methodologies can mitigate this issue?
