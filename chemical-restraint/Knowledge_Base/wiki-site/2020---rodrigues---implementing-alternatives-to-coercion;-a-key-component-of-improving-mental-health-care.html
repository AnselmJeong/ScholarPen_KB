Summary

This position statement by the World Psychiatric Association (WPA) examines the overuse of coercive practices in mental health care globally and advocates for the implementation of evidence-based alternatives. Coercion is defined broadly to include involuntary treatment, seclusion, restraint, and threats, and is shown to violate fundamental human rights such as liberty, autonomy, and bodily integrity. The document synthesizes research, ethical guidelines, and real-world examples to argue that reducing coercion is both feasible and essential for improving mental health care quality, particularly through frameworks like the WHO Quality Rights Initiative, Safewards, and the Six Core Strategies. It emphasizes the role of psychiatrists as leaders in driving systemic change toward recovery-oriented, trauma-informed, and rights-based care.

Key Entities

  • World Psychiatric Association (WPA) (type: org) — Issued the position statement and leads advocacy for reducing coercion in mental health care globally.
  • World Health Organization (WHO) (type: org) — Developed the Quality Rights Initiative, a key framework cited for reducing coercion through human rights-based training and assessment.
  • Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Psychiatrists (RANZCP) (type: org) — Collaborated with WPA on producing related resources and supporting implementation efforts.
  • WHO Quality Rights Initiative (type: tool) — A global program providing training, assessment, and guidance to eliminate coercion and promote rights in mental health services; cited as implemented successfully in Gujarat, India.
  • Safewards (type: model) — An evidence-based model designed to reduce conflict and containment in psychiatric wards through staff training and environmental modifications.
  • Six Core Strategies (type: framework) — A structured approach to reducing seclusion and restraint via leadership, data use, workforce development, prevention tools, service user roles, and debriefing.

Key Concepts

  • Coercion in mental health care — Refers to involuntary treatment, seclusion, restraint, and threats used in response to perceived harm; associated with trauma, eroded trust, and deterrence from help-seeking.
  • Human rights violations — Breaches of rights to liberty, autonomy, integrity, and non-discrimination, particularly under the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD).
  • Recovery-oriented systems of care — Model emphasizing hope, agency, and social inclusion; positioned as an alternative to coercion by addressing root causes of distress.
  • Trauma-informed care — Approach recognizing the widespread impact of trauma and avoiding re-traumatization; central to reducing reliance on coercive interventions.
  • UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD) — International human rights treaty establishing legal standards for dignity, autonomy, and inclusion of people with psychosocial disabilities.
  • Evidence-based alternatives to coercion — Interventions such as Safewards, Six Core Strategies, open door policies, and WHO Quality Rights that reduce coercion without compromising safety.
  • Supported decision-making — Framework enabling individuals to make choices with assistance, preserving legal capacity and avoiding substitute decision-making under coercion.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Coercion in mental health care is overused globally and violates fundamental human rights; implementing evidence-based alternatives is essential to improving care quality and upholding dignity.
    • Evidence: Widespread professional and lived-experience consensus on overuse; documented harms include trauma, injury, eroded trust, staff burnout, and help-seeking avoidance; legal grounding in UNCRPD and General Comment on Article 12; successful implementations cited (e.g., Quality Rights Gujarat).
    • Strength: Strong — supported by international human rights law, clinical ethics, peer-reviewed literature, and real-world case studies; acknowledges implementation barriers but affirms feasibility across income levels.
