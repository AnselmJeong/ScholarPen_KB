Ethical dilemmas in contemporary psychiatry: Findings from a survey of National Psychiatric Associations in Europe

Summary

This study surveyed representatives from National Psychiatric Associations (NPAs) across Europe to assess their ethical guidelines, practices, and perspectives on ethical dilemmas in psychiatry. Conducted between 2021 and 2022, the survey achieved a 70.4% response rate, gathering input from 31 NPAs across 30 countries. The research found that in most participating countries, the general mission statement serves as the primary document addressing ethical issues in psychiatry, with only a minority maintaining separate ethical codes. The most frequently addressed ethical topics in internal documents were medical malpractice and discrimination/racism, particularly in professional relationships. While psychiatric expertise is represented in national medical ethics bodies in most countries, only a minority of NPAs have formally adopted international ethical codes such as the Declaration of Helsinki or the WPA Declaration of Madrid. Despite this, a strong majority of respondents endorsed international codes as the primary source of ethical principles and expressed support for sharing national documents to create a European Psychiatric Association (EPA)-supported database of ethical guidelines.

Key Entities

  • European Psychiatric Association (EPA) (type: org) — the overarching organization that coordinates National Psychiatric Associations in Europe and was proposed as the central body to facilitate a shared database of ethical guidelines.
  • National Psychiatric Associations (NPAs) (type: org) — the national-level organizations surveyed in the study, responsible for setting ethical guidelines for psychiatrists in their respective countries.
  • World Psychiatric Association (WPA) (type: org) — the international body whose Declaration of Madrid was among the most frequently adopted international ethical codes by participating NPAs.
  • Survey Monkey (type: tool) — the online platform used to distribute and collect responses from the psychiatric association representatives.

Key Concepts

  • Ethical codes in psychiatry — formal guidelines governing professional conduct, referenced as either nationally developed or internationally adopted frameworks that NPAs use to guide ethical decision-making.
  • Human rights — principles concerning patient autonomy, informed consent, confidentiality, and protection from coercion, identified as a top concern for ethical debate among NPAs.
  • Medical malpractice — professional negligence or harm in clinical practice, reported as the most frequently addressed ethical issue in internal NPA documents.
  • Discrimination/racism — unfair treatment based on identity, ethnicity, or social status, commonly cited in NPA documents regarding patient-member relationships.
  • Workplace bullying — harassment or mistreatment among psychiatric professionals, frequently reported in internal documents concerning inter-professional relationships.
  • Plagiarism/academic fraud — ethical violations in research and academic activities, noted as a recurring issue in several countries’ internal guidelines.
  • Sexual abuse — misconduct in professional or therapeutic relationships, reported by multiple NPAs as an issue addressed in their internal documents.
  • Psychosurgery, euthanasia, pregnancy termination — controversial medical procedures requiring ethical assessment, addressed in the guidelines of a subset of NPAs.
  • Governmental bodies on medical ethics — state-level entities that provide guidance on ethical medical practice, present in most surveyed countries and often include psychiatric representation.
  • Ethics in psychiatric training — the inclusion of ethics education in the professional development of psychiatrists, reported as implemented in a majority of participating countries.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: National Psychiatric Associations in Europe are active in shaping professional ethics but tend to rely on international ethical codes rather than developing comprehensive national guidelines, with a strong preference for collaborative efforts under the EPA to create a shared, accessible database of ethical documents.
    • Evidence: Survey results showed that only 8 out of 31 NPAs have separate ethical codes, while 12 rely on general mission statements as their main ethical document. Medical malpractice (18/31) and discrimination/racism (13/31) were the most common topics in internal documents. Although 28/31 respondents said international codes should be the primary source of ethical principles, only 15 have formally adopted any (e.g., Helsinki, Madrid). Meanwhile, 25/31 supported creating an EPA-backed database of shared ethical documents, and 20 expressed willingness to share their internal materials.
    • Strength: The findings are well-supported by a high response rate, broad geographic coverage, and clear quantification of responses across multiple domains. The study triangulates closed-ended responses with open-ended thematic analysis. Limitations include reliance on NPA representatives (not frontline clinicians), potential variability in how “ethics” is interpreted nationally, and the inability to confirm real-world implementation of guidelines.

Connections

  • medical ethics — this study provides empirical data on how psychiatric associations implement ethical guidelines in practice, complementing theoretical discussions of medical ethics.
  • human rights in healthcare — the frequent identification of human rights as a top debate topic aligns with and extends existing work on patient rights in psychiatric contexts.
  • European Psychiatric Association — the EPA is positioned as a potential facilitator of cross-national collaboration in ethics, directly supported by the study’s findings on interest in resource sharing.
  • World Psychiatric Association — the WPA’s Declaration of Madrid was among the most adopted international codes, linking this study to global psychiatric ethics frameworks.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While 28 out of 31 respondents endorsed international ethical codes as the primary source of guidance, only 15 have formally adopted any such codes (e.g., Helsinki, Madrid), revealing a gap between endorsement and actual implementation.
  • Strong support exists for sharing ethical documents (20/31) and building an EPA-supported database (25/31), yet only 6 NPAs have adopted the existing EPA Code, indicating enthusiasm for collaboration but limited uptake of current pan-European tools.
  • Human rights and clinical practice violations are identified as top concerns for debate, but few countries address controversial procedures like euthanasia (only 5/31) in their internal guidelines, suggesting a mismatch between perceived ethical priorities and documented guidance.

Open Questions

  • Why do so few National Psychiatric Associations adopt the EPA Code despite expressing strong support for collaboration and resource sharing under its auspices?
  • How do national laws governing controversial practices (e.g., euthanasia, consent, involuntary treatment) interact with or constrain the development of psychiatric ethics guidelines?
  • What structural, cultural, or procedural barriers prevent wider adoption of formal ethics codes, even when there is evident interest in sharing and harmonizing guidelines across borders?
