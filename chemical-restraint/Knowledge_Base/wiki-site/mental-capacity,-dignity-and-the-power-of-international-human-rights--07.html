Summary

This chapter concludes the book by synthesizing its central argument about interpreting Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD). The author argues that legal capacity under Article 12 must be understood through the principle of indivisibility of human rights, which holds that civil-political and socio-economic rights are interdependent and equally important. Drawing on disability theory, jurisprudence (such as Grootboom and D v UK), and real-world cases like the NDIS implementation in Australia, the chapter contends that a strict focus on autonomy and supported decision-making fails in “hard cases” involving severe or profound cognitive disability. Instead, the author advocates for a balanced approach that permits substitute decision-making as a last resort when supported decision-making cannot ensure safety, dignity, or rights enjoyment, grounding this in a five-dimensional model of dignity and substantive equality.

Key Entities

  • United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (type: treaty) — the central international human rights treaty governing disability rights, especially Article 12 on legal capacity
  • UN Disability Committee (Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities) (type: organization) — the UN body that provides authoritative interpretations of the CRPD, notably advocating for abolition of substitute decision-making
  • National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) (type: policy/program) — Australia’s disability support scheme used as a case study to illustrate gaps in decision-making support and risks of overemphasizing autonomy
  • South African Constitutional Court (type: judicial body) — referenced for its Grootboom ruling, which linked dignity to socio-economic rights like housing
  • European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) (type: judicial body) — cited in the D v UK case, where dignity was tied to access to healthcare

Key Concepts

  • Principle of indivisibility of human rights — the concept that all human rights are interdependent and equally important, such that civil-political rights (like legal capacity) depend on socio-economic rights (like support)
  • Supported decision-making — a model where individuals receive assistance to make their own decisions, presented as ideal but insufficient for those with severe cognitive impairment
  • Decision-making by substitutes — a legally authorized person making decisions on behalf of someone lacking capacity, argued as necessary as a last resort to protect rights and dignity
  • Five-dimensional dignity — a framework comprising equal worth, valuing autonomy, relational/interdependent self, embodied personhood, and indivisibility of rights, proposed as a richer basis for personhood than autonomy alone
  • Substantive (inclusive) equality — equality requiring differential treatment to address systemic disadvantages and enable real participation, used to justify substitute decision-making in cases where formal equality fails
  • Residual impact of impairment — lasting effects of cognitive disability that persist despite maximal support, affecting decision-making capacity and necessitating substitute approaches in some cases

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Article 12 of the CRPD should be interpreted through the principle of indivisibility and the values of autonomy, dignity, and equality to allow both supported decision-making and decision-making by substitutes as a last resort.
    • Evidence: Theoretical grounding in human rights law and disability studies; doctrinal analysis of CRPD text and General Comment No. 6; illustrative examples from NDIS implementation and cases (Alex, Sesha, Jess); jurisprudence linking dignity to socio-economic rights.
    • Strength: Strong theoretical and doctrinal coherence; the argument integrates legal, ethical, and practical dimensions. However, empirical support is largely qualitative and case-based, lacking broad statistical validation. The reliance on illustrative examples limits generalizability, though the philosophical consistency is robust.

Connections

  • Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities — this source provides a detailed interpretation of Article 12, emphasizing its interdependence with socio-economic rights
  • supported decision-making — this source defines its limits and argues it must be complemented by substitute decision-making in high-risk or low-capacity scenarios
  • substitute decision-making — this source reframes it not as paternalism but as a rights-affirming practice when grounded in dignity, equality, and accountability
  • principle of indivisibility — this source applies it to legal capacity, arguing that legal agency depends on prior fulfillment of socio-economic rights like support access
  • five-dimensional dignity — this source introduces and applies this novel framework to show how dignity supports personhood even when autonomy is impaired

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The source directly challenges interpretations of Article 12 that advocate for the abolition of substitute decision-making in all cases (such as the current stance of the UN Disability Committee), arguing that such views ignore the reality of irreversible cognitive impairment and risk harm by overestimating the reach of supported decision-making.
  • Internal tension exists between preventing historical abuses in substitute systems and recognizing their necessity; the author addresses this by insisting on accountability, rights-based justification, and procedural safeguards to ensure substitute decision-making enhances rather than undermines dignity.

Open Questions

  • How can legal systems implement substitute decision-making with sufficient accountability and transparency to prevent abuse while preserving the dignity and rights of the person involved?
  • What specific procedural safeguards (e.g., regular review, independent oversight, expressed will preferences) are needed to ensure substitute decision-making aligns with the individual’s rights and dignity?
  • To what extent can supported decision-making be scaled and resourced to reduce reliance on substitute decision-making, and where should the line be drawn between the two approaches?
