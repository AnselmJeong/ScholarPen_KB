Summary

This source examines the interpretation of Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which guarantees the right to legal capacity on an equal basis with others. The author argues that Article 12 must be interpreted through the principle of the indivisibility of human rights, requiring that civil-political rights like legal capacity be understood in conjunction with socio-economic rights and other fundamental values such as dignity and equality. The analysis concludes that while supported decision-making is a primary obligation under Article 12(3), there are ‘hard cases’—such as severe cognitive impairment, risk of serious harm, or undue influence—where decision-making by a substitute may be necessary as a last resort to uphold the person’s indivisible human rights, particularly dignity and safety. The argument draws on the CRPD’s preamble and general comments, disability jurisprudence, philosophical perspectives on personhood (e.g., Nussbaum, Kittay), and illustrative case studies (Alex, Sesha, Jess) to challenge the prevailing view that any substitute decision-making violates Article 12. Instead, it posits that dignity, not autonomy, is the true foundation of personhood for those who cannot exercise autonomous decision-making, even with support, and that denying substitute decision-making in extreme cases may itself violate other rights under the CRPD.

Key Concepts

  • Principle of Indivisibility — the concept that all human rights are interdependent, interrelated, and equally important; used to argue that legal capacity under Article 12 must be interpreted in the context of the entire CRPD, including socio-economic rights and values like dignity and safety
  • Supported Decision-Making — a process where individuals with impaired decision-making capacity receive assistance to make their own decisions whenever possible; presented as a primary obligation under Article 12(3) but not sufficient in all cases
  • Substitute Decision-Making — when a legally authorized person makes decisions for someone with impaired capacity, typically as a last resort when support fails or harm is imminent; argued to be necessary and potentially required under Article 12 in hard cases
  • Legal Capacity — the legal ability to make decisions with legal consequences and to be held accountable for them; the central concept of Article 12, examined in relation to cognitive disability and the limits of autonomy
  • Dignity (as basis for personhood) — the idea that human worth and personhood are grounded in inherent dignity rather than rationality or autonomy, especially relevant for those with significant cognitive disabilities; proposed as the foundation of personhood under the CRPD
  • Inclusive Equality — a conception of equality requiring positive, differential treatment (e.g., support or substitute decision-making) to achieve real equality of outcome, going beyond formal non-discrimination
  • Hard Cases — situations where supported decision-making is insufficient or inappropriate, such as severe cognitive impairment, risk of serious harm, or undue influence by supporters; used to justify the need for substitute decision-making as a last resort

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core Claim: Article 12 of the CRPD requires both supported decision-making and, as a last resort, substitute decision-making in hard cases to uphold the indivisible nature of human rights, particularly dignity, safety, and equality.
  • Evidence:
    • Theoretical: The principle of indivisibility (from CRPD Preamble and General Comments) requires interpreting Article 12 in context of the whole treaty, including socio-economic rights.
    • Doctrinal: General Comment No. 6 on equality supports “inclusive equality,” which may justify differential treatment including substitute decision-making.
    • Jurisprudential: National laws like the UK Mental Capacity Act already permit substitute decision-making in extreme cases, suggesting compatibility with human rights frameworks.
    • Philosophical: Arguments that dignity, not autonomy, is the true foundation of personhood for those with profound cognitive disability, drawing on theorists like Nussbaum and Kittay.
    • Case Studies: The cases of Alex (refusing dental treatment due to trauma), Sesha (profound cognitive disability with no communication), and Jess (dementia with neglectful family care) illustrate scenarios where supported decision-making fails or risks harm, necessitating substitute intervention to prevent neglect or abuse.
  • Strength: Strong theoretical and doctrinal grounding in CRPD texts and jurisprudence; philosophical arguments are well-situated in disability studies; case studies are illustrative but based on real-world literature. The argument challenges the dominant interpretation by the UN Committee but builds on existing legal practices and scholarly critiques advocating for a more nuanced, rights-based approach.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The dominant interpretation in current disability rights discourse (especially from the UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities) holds that Article 12 prohibits all substitute decision-making as a violation of autonomy. This source directly contradicts that view by arguing substitute decision-making may be required in hard cases to uphold other rights like safety from neglect or harm.
  • Internal tension exists between promoting autonomy and preventing harm; the resolution offered subordinates autonomy to dignity and safety in extreme cases via the principle of indivisibility, while emphasizing that substitute decision-making must be a strict last resort to prevent abuse.

Open Questions

  • What procedural safeguards are necessary to prevent abuse when substitute decision-making is permitted as a last resort?
  • How can “last resort” be objectively determined in practice across different cognitive disabilities and contexts to ensure consistency and prevent misuse?
  • Does recognizing substitute decision-making in hard cases risk undermining the progressive development of supported decision-making systems? How can both be balanced to advance disability rights without regressing to paternalism?
