Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical debates on coercion in psychiatry, balancing personal freedom with clinical necessity
  • Interpreted variably across cultural, legal, and disability rights frameworks
  • Key to distinguishing between respecting enduring will and transient preferences in decision-making
  • Underpins arguments for supported decision-making and legal capacity reform

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Autonomy insufficient alone Appeals to autonomy and dignity cannot justify eliminating coercive measures; interpretation depends on context 2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry
Will vs. preferences distinction Respect for CRPD requires distinguishing enduring will from transient preferences; honoring prior will may override current preferences without violating rights 2017 - Szmukler - The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities ‘Rights, will and preferences’ in relation to mental health disabilities
Context-dependent ethics Ethical principles in psychiatry vary across cultural, religious, economic, and political settings; no universal application 2020 - Atakora, Asampong - Ethical Principles in Psychiatric Practice; A Systematic Review
Structured decision-making Coercion decisions should follow algorithms based on capacity, benefit-harm, and biopsychosocial context, not principles alone 2023 - Steinert - Using coercion in mental disorders or risking the patients death An analysis of the protocols of a clinical ethics committee
Autonomy as achievement Autonomy under CRPD Article 12 requires internal capacities and external support; realized through indivisibility of civil-political and socio-economic rights Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -04
