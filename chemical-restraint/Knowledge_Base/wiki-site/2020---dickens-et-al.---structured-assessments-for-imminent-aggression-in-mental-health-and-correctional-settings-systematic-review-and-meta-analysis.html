Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated the predictive validity of structured risk assessment tools for imminent aggression (defined as violence likely within 24 hours of assessment) in mental health and correctional settings. The authors identified 32 relevant instruments but found only five with sufficient research: the Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC), Dynamic Appraisal of Situational Aggression (DASA) and its youth/women variants, and the HCR-20 clinical subscale. A narrative review of 50 papers and meta-analysis of 31 studies (282,238 assessments from 2,723 individuals) revealed that only BVC and DASA had adequate evidence for meaningful conclusions. After removing outliers, both tools showed large effect sizes for predicting person-directed aggression (BVC: Hedges’ g = 1.35; DASA: g = 1.04) and any aggression (BVC: g = 1.24; DASA: g = 0.88). Moderator analyses indicated that effect sizes decreased in lower bias-risk studies and increased in forensic versus civil settings. Implementation studies consistently reported both tools as acceptable and feasible for clinical use, though concerns were noted about duplicated data collection and inconsistent real-world application due to insufficient interrater reliability reporting in many studies.

Key Entities

  • Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC) (tool) — A six-item dichotomous tool assessing confusion, irritability, boisterousness, physical threats, verbal threats, and object attack; designed for per-shift administration in clinical settings to predict imminent aggression within 24 hours.
  • Dynamic Appraisal of Situational Aggression (DASA) (tool) — A seven-item tool combining elements from BVC and HCR-20 with additional items (irritability, verbal threats, impulsivity, negative attitudes, sensitivity to provocation, anger when denied, unwillingness to follow directions); assessed per shift with variants for youth (11 items) and women (10 items).
  • HCR-20: Assessing Risk for Violence version 2.0 clinical subscale (tool) — A five-item subset (lack of insight, negative attitudes, active symptoms, impulsivity, non-responsivity) of the broader violence risk assessment tool, sometimes adapted to dichotomous scoring for imminent aggression prediction.
  • DASA Youth Version (tool) — An 11-item adaptation of DASA adding anxious/fearful, low empathy/remorse, peer rejection, and outside stressors for adolescent populations.
  • DASA Women’s Version (tool) — A 10-item adaptation incorporating female-specific items (covert/manipulative behavior, low self-esteem) and a social climate factor from the HCR-20 Female Additional Manual.

Key Concepts

  • Imminent aggression/risk — Violence expected to occur within 24 hours following assessment, representing the temporal focus of this review and the prediction horizon for evaluated tools.
  • Predictive validity — The ability of a risk assessment tool to accurately forecast future aggression, measured here through area under the curve (AUC) values converted to standardized effect sizes (Hedges’ g).
  • Effect size (Hedges’ g) — A standardized measure of predictive accuracy where values of 0.2, 0.5, and 0.8 represent small, medium, and large effects; values exceeding 1.0 indicate strong predictive capacity.
  • Interrater reliability — The consistency of tool application across different raters, identified as a frequently unreported psychometric property that limits confidence in real-world consistency.
  • Forest mortality bias — A method for detecting and removing outlier studies whose effect sizes fall outside the pooled confidence interval, used to strengthen meta-analytic validity.
  • Moderator analysis — Statistical examination of factors influencing effect size heterogeneity, revealing that study bias risk and legal status significantly modify predictive performance.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Only the Brøset Violence Checklist and Dynamic Appraisal of Situational Aggression have sufficient research to support conclusions about predicting imminent (24-hour) aggression.
    • Evidence: Narrative review of 50 papers and meta-analysis of 31 studies showed these were the only tools with adequate data; HCR-20 clinical subscale and DASA variants had only 1-2 studies each.
    • Strength: Strong — based on comprehensive search and exclusion of tools with insufficient evidence.
  • Core claim: Both BVC and DASA demonstrate large predictive effect sizes for aggression prediction after outlier removal.
    • Evidence: Pooled Hedges’ g values of 1.35 (person-directed) and 1.24 (any aggression) for BVC; 1.04 and 0.88 for DASA, derived from AUC conversion in random-effects models.
    • Strength: Strong — supported by large multi-site dataset (282,238 assessments) with consistent effects across studies.
  • Core claim: The predictive value of BVC and DASA is broadly equivalent despite BVC’s slightly higher point estimates.
    • Evidence: Confidence intervals for effect sizes substantially overlap (BVC: 1.15–1.56 vs. DASA: 0.69–1.39 for person-directed aggression), indicating no statistically significant difference.
    • Strength: Moderate — while point estimates differ, overlapping confidence intervals prevent definitive superiority claims.
  • Core claim: Effect sizes are reduced in studies with lower bias risk and increased in forensic versus civil legal settings.
    • Evidence: Moderator analysis showed these variables significantly influenced heterogeneity, suggesting real-world performance varies by study quality and context.
    • Strength: Moderate — based on subgroup analysis but limited by number of studies available for moderator testing.
  • Core claim: Both tools are generally acceptable and feasible for implementation in clinical settings.
    • Evidence: Fourteen implementation studies reported positive feedback on ease of use, usefulness, and willingness to continue, though some noted duplication of existing data or limited awareness improvement.
    • Strength: Moderate — based on qualitative feedback across multiple settings but with noted variability in user experiences.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While BVC shows higher point effect sizes than DASA, the substantial overlap in confidence intervals challenges claims of clear superiority, suggesting performance equivalence in practice.
  • Many studies failed to report interrater reliability — a critical psychometric property — raising concerns about whether tools are applied consistently in real-world settings despite positive feasibility reports.
  • Implementation research reveals mixed results: staff often find tools usable and informative, but some report they duplicate existing documentation or fail to enhance clinical awareness of risk.
  • Effect sizes decrease in higher quality (low bias risk) studies, indicating potential overestimation of predictive accuracy in lower quality research designs.
  • Limited research exists for DASA variants (youth and women), with only 1-2 studies each, creating an evidence gap despite initial promise for specialized populations.

Open Questions

  • What is the minimum training and ongoing supervision required to achieve reliable interrater reliability for these tools across diverse clinical and cultural settings?
  • How do organizational factors (staffing levels, shift patterns, resource availability) affect the consistent implementation and predictive utility of these tools in real-world practice?
  • What is the optimal assessment frequency beyond the standard “per shift” recommendation for maximizing predictive accuracy while minimizing staff burden?
  • How do these tools perform when integrated into electronic health records or clinical decision support systems compared to standalone paper-based use?
  • What is the relationship between routine use of these tools for prediction and actual reductions in observed violence rates, beyond their statistical predictive validity?
