Summary

This study evaluated the Fordham Risk Screening Tool (FRST) for violence risk assessment in adult emergency department patients undergoing psychiatric evaluation. Using a prospective observational design, researchers compared the FRST against the Historical Clinical Risk Management-20, Version 3 (HCR-20 v3) as a criterion standard in 105 patients presenting to Maine Medical Center’s emergency department between June and August 2022. The FRST demonstrated strong diagnostic accuracy with an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.88, sensitivity of 84%, specificity of 93%, positive predictive value of 87%, and negative predictive value of 91%. Psychometric analysis revealed good internal consistency (KR-20 = 0.79) and unidimensional structure supporting construct validity. However, subgroup analysis indicated significantly reduced performance among involuntarily admitted patients (AUC 0.62 vs. 0.94 in voluntary), suggesting potential limitations in legally coerced populations that warrant cautious interpretation due to small subgroup size.

Key Entities

  • Fordham Risk Screening Tool (FRST) (tool): A brief screening tool designed to assess violence risk using readily available information during psychiatric intake, evaluated in this study for emergency department use.
  • Historical Clinical Risk Management-20, Version 3 (HCR-20 V3) (tool): A 20-item structured clinical judgment tool used as the criterion standard for violence risk assessment in this validation study.
  • Maine Medical Center (MMC) (organization): The study site where data were collected from patients presenting to the emergency department for psychiatric evaluation.
  • RedCap (tool): Database used for data collection and management during the study.
  • SPSS for Windows v. 28.0 (tool): Statistical software used for data analysis including calculation of diagnostic metrics and psychometric properties.

Key Concepts

  • Violence risk assessment: The process of evaluating the likelihood of future violent behavior, central to the study as the FRST is evaluated for its utility in emergency department settings.
  • Area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC): A measure of a test’s ability to distinguish between high and low risk individuals; reported as 0.88 for the FRST versus HCR-20 v3, indicating excellent discriminatory ability.
  • Sensitivity: The proportion of actual positives correctly identified; reported as 84% for the FRST, indicating its ability to detect true high-risk cases.
  • Specificity: The proportion of actual negatives correctly identified; reported as 93% for the FRST, indicating its ability to correctly rule out low-risk cases.
  • Positive predictive value (PPV): The probability that individuals with a positive test are truly high-risk; reported as 87% for the FRST.
  • Negative predictive value (NPV): The probability that individuals with a negative test are truly low-risk; reported as 91% for the FRST.
  • Internal consistency reliability: The degree to which items within a test measure the same construct; measured via KR-20 as 0.79 for the FRST, indicating acceptable reliability.
  • Construct validity: The extent to which a test measures the theoretical construct it claims; supported by factor analysis showing a single-component solution for the FRST.
  • Concurrent validity: The degree to which a test correlates with a gold-standard measure administered concurrently; supported by the AUC comparison with HCR-20 v3.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The FRST demonstrates strong diagnostic accuracy as a brief violence risk screening tool in emergency department settings when compared to the HCR-20 v3 criterion standard.
    • Evidence: AUC of 0.88 (95% CI: 0.81–0.96), sensitivity 84% (95% CI: 69%–94%), specificity 93% (95% CI: 83%–98%), PPV 87% (95% CI: 73%–94%), NPV 91% (95% CI: 83%–86%), and strong psychometric properties including internal consistency (KR-20 = 0.79) and unidimensional factor structure (49% variance explained).
    • Strength: Strong. The study used a prospective observational design with adequate sample size (N=105), direct comparison to a validated gold standard, and comprehensive psychometric evaluation. Limitations include single-site design, limited racial diversity, and potential spectrum bias, but findings are robust and clinically meaningful for emergency department application.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The FRST demonstrated significantly lower performance in involuntarily admitted patients (AUC 0.62 vs. 0.94 in voluntary), suggesting the tool may be less reliable in higher-risk or legally coerced populations. This raises concerns about equity and applicability across legal statuses, though authors note this finding should be interpreted cautiously due to small subgroup size (n=19).
