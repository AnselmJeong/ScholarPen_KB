Why It Matters

  • Reflects systemic failures in mental health settings where coercion is normalized despite ethical concerns
  • Highlights the gap between clinical necessity and the actual use of coercive measures driven by resource constraints
  • Undermines patient autonomy and therapeutic relationships, contributing to mistrust and underreporting of abuse
  • Challenges the legitimacy of paternalistic models in psychiatric care when not grounded in evidence or consent

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Coercion as systemic failure Coercive measures are often used due to understaffing and cost-cutting, not clinical need, and are ethically indefensible from a patient perspective The Use of Coercive Measures in Forensic Psychiatric Care - 20
Underreporting and normalization Violence and abuse by staff occur with concerning frequency but are systematically underreported due to power imbalances and stigma Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 02
