Summary

This chapter examines staff attitudes toward seclusion and restraint in forensic psychiatric settings, focusing on how these attitudes influence clinical decision-making. Drawing on cross-national survey data and qualitative studies, it identifies that staff perceptions of aggression—particularly whether it is viewed as intentional and patient-driven—play a stronger role in intervention selection than attitudes toward specific coercive methods. In forensic contexts, high exposure to violence and patient criminal histories contribute to fear and distrust among staff, which erodes therapeutic relationships and increases reliance on coercion. The authors argue that organizational safety culture in forensic settings often prioritizes control over therapeutic engagement, and that continuing education addressing both skill gaps and underlying beliefs about aggression is needed to reduce inappropriate use of restrictive practices.

Key Concepts

  • Coercive measures — Interventions such as seclusion and restraint used to manage agitated or violent patients; considered last-resort, restrictive actions in psychiatric care.
  • Staff attitudes toward aggression — Beliefs about whether aggression stems from patient intent versus environmental factors; found to predict intervention choices more strongly than attitudes toward specific coercive tools.
  • Safety culture in forensic settings — Organizational emphasis on security and control, shaped by fear, distrust, and custodial priorities, which can reduce informal patient-staff interaction and increase reliance on coercion.
  • Triple stigma — The compound stigma faced by forensic psychiatric patients due to mental illness, criminal history, and (in some contexts) race, contributing to marginalization and strained staff-patient relationships.
  • Forensic nursing competence — The skills and attributes needed to balance care and control in secure settings, including conflict resolution and interpersonal abilities, as outlined in models like Bowring-Lossock’s four-dimensional framework.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Staff attitudes toward coercive measures are shaped more by perceived efficacy and familiarity than by objective safety or patient dignity; beliefs about aggression being patient-intent-driven are stronger predictors of intervention use than attitudes toward the measures themselves.
    • Evidence: Cross-national data (Bowers et al. 2007) showing Finland’s high approval of restrictive measures; Laiho et al. (2014) linking aggression attributions to intervention preferences; staff prioritizing effectiveness over dignity in clinical decision-making.
    • Strength: Moderate to strong; supported by multinational survey data and forensic-specific samples, though limited by self-report potential biases and unresolved cultural confounders (national vs. psychiatric culture).
  • Core claim: Fear and distrust in forensic wards—amplified by patient violence histories and custodial priorities—undermine therapeutic relationships by reducing informal staff-patient interaction, thereby increasing reliance on coercion.
    • Evidence: Qualitative reports of staff fear (Jacob & Holmes 2011); surveillance reducing informal contact (Wright et al. 2014); stigma leading to emotional distancing as a coping mechanism.
    • Strength: Strong; triangulated via qualitative studies, observational data, and staff self-reports documenting decreased interpersonal engagement.
  • Core claim: Continuing education that addresses both skill gaps and maladaptive beliefs about aggression (e.g., its modifiability) is essential to improving forensic nursing competence and reducing inappropriate coercion.
    • Evidence: Staff self-reports of inadequate training (Tema et al. 2011); evidence that training boosts confidence and targets changeable aggression (Brennan 2006; Dickens et al. 2013); application of frameworks like Bowring-Lossock’s 4D model.
    • Strength: Moderate; grounded in expert consensus and intervention studies, but lacks long-term outcome data on actual reductions in coercion use.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • If the wiki currently portrays seclusion and restraint as uniformly negative practices, this source introduces tension by documenting contextual acceptance (e.g., 82% of staff experiencing violence; some viewing coercion as necessary or therapeutic), requiring a nuanced framing that distinguishes between maladaptive overuse and situational necessity in high-risk forensic settings.
  • If the wiki emphasizes patient autonomy as an absolute priority, this source highlights the forensic ethics tension where public safety goals (via behavior control) may, per frameworks like Sen et al. (2007), temporarily supersede autonomy in forensic psychiatric care.
  • Internal tension exists in that staff recognize coercion as necessary for safety yet acknowledge it stems from fear and distrust that actively undermine the trust needed for de-escalation, creating a self-reinforcing cycle where safety efforts erode the very relationships that could reduce coercion.

Open Questions

  • Does reducing physical restraint use in forensic settings lead to an increase in psychological coercion (e.g., threats, increased medication) as a substitute mechanism for control?
  • How do specific security protocols in forensic wards (e.g., locked doors, routine searches, surveillance levels) interact with staff attitudes to either amplify or mitigate fear-driven reliance on coercion?
  • Can restorative justice or trauma-informed approaches be effectively implemented in forensic psychiatric settings to reframe staff-patient relationships and reduce dependence on coercive measures?
