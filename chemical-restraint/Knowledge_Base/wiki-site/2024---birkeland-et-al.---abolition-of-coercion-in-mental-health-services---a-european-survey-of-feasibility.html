Abolition of coercion in mental health services – A European survey of feasibility

Summary

This study surveyed members of the FOSTREN (Fostering and Strengthening Approaches to Reducing Coercion in European Mental Health Services) network to assess the perceived feasibility of totally abolishing psychiatric coercion in European mental health services. Using a mixed-methods web-based survey distributed to 167 network members, 76 respondents (45.5% response rate) answered whether complete abolition is achievable and explained their reasoning. Quantitative analysis showed only 31% (22 respondents) believed abolition is achievable, while 69% (49 respondents) did not. Qualitative thematic analysis revealed that skeptics cited the difficulty of treating severe mental illness, risks of violence, and the need to protect individuals from harm as key barriers, while proponents argued that the harms of coercion (trauma, death, injury) outweigh any benefits and that psychiatric advance directives and better training in alternatives like de-escalation could suffice. Younger age (<51 years) and less work experience (<16 years) were significantly associated with belief in achievability. Respondents also expressed greater optimism about eliminating in-patient coercion versus involuntary commitment and ranked mechanical restraint as the top priority for abolition if full abolition is unattainable.

Key Entities

  • FOSTREN (Fostering and Strengthening Approaches to Reducing Coercion in European Mental Health Services) (type: org) — Central organization whose members were surveyed; a European network focused on reducing coercion through knowledge exchange.
  • International Journal of Law and Psychiatry (type: org) — Peer-reviewed journal that published the study; venue for dissemination of findings.
  • European Cooperation on Science and Technology (COST) (type: org) — Funded the FOSTREN network from 2020 to 2024; provided infrastructural support for the research collaboration.
  • Council of Europe (type: org) — Issued Resolution 2291 (2019) urging member states to transition toward abolition of coercive practices; provides the policy context for the study.
  • Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Dignity of the Human Being with regard to the Application of Biology and Medicine (Oviedo Convention) (type: document/tool) — European human rights treaty that permits coercion in specific circumstances; referenced as a legal framework contrasting with abolitionist goals.
  • United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (type: document/tool) — International human rights framework emphasizing autonomy and liberty; cited as a foundation for abolition efforts in mental health.
  • World Health Organization (WHO) QualityRights (type: program/tool) — Initiative promoting CRPD-aligned practices in mental health; referenced as supporting reduction (not abolition) of coercion through training and alternatives.
  • REDCap (Research Electronic Data Capture) (type: tool) — Web-based platform used to administer the survey; enabled secure data collection across participating countries.
  • European FOSTREN network (type: org) — The specific cohort of mental health practitioners and researchers surveyed; represents a self-selected group already engaged in coercion reduction efforts.

Key Concepts

  • Psychiatric coercion — Measures applied against a patient’s will in mental health settings (e.g., restraint, seclusion, forced medication); the central phenomenon under investigation and focus of abolition debate.
  • Total abolition of coercion — Complete elimination of all coercive measures in mental health services; the primary research question assessed for feasibility among experts.
  • Psychiatric advance directives — Legal documents allowing individuals to specify future mental health treatment preferences while competent; cited by pro-abolition respondents as a potential alternative to coercion.
  • De-escalation — Techniques to reduce agitation and prevent crisis without physical intervention; mentioned as an alternative strategy and identified as a training need among respondents.
  • Mechanical restraint — Physical devices used to restrict patient movement (e.g., straps, cuffs); identified as the top priority for abolition if full abolition is unattainable.
  • Seclusion — Isolation of a patient in a room from which they cannot leave freely; the second most cited coercive measure for prioritized abolition.
  • Recovery-oriented philosophy — Approach emphasizing personal recovery, empowerment, and social inclusion over symptom elimination; referenced as necessary in community services to support abolition efforts.
  • Task shifting — Delegation of tasks to less specialized health workers (implied in community prevention models); suggested as part of resource solutions for achieving abolition feasibility.
  • Involuntary commitment — Admission to mental health care against a person’s will; noted as a form of coercion that respondents were less optimistic about eliminating compared to in-patient measures.
  • Human rights in mental health — Framework grounded in instruments like the CRPD and Oviedo Convention that emphasizes liberty, autonomy, and protection from abuse; provides the ethical basis for abolition aspirations.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Among European mental health professionals dedicated to reducing coercion (FOSTREN network), only a minority (31%) believe total abolition of psychiatric coercion is achievable; the majority (69%) do not.
    • Evidence: Survey of 76 respondents (45.5% response rate from 167 invitations). Quantitative data show 22 said “yes” to achievability, 49 said “no”. Qualitative analysis identified themes explaining both positions.
    • Strength: Moderately strong due to focused expert sample, mixed-methods design, and clear statistical associations (age, experience). Limited by self-selection bias, small sample size, lack of patient perspectives, and inability to generalize to all European mental health professionals.
  • Against achievability: Mental health disorders are difficult to treat and may cause violence; need to protect individuals from harm; lack of resources, training, and systemic barriers.
    • Evidence: Qualitative themes from respondents who said “no”; supported by references to untreated illness and safety concerns in discussion.
    • Strength: Well-supported by participant rationale; aligns with common clinical concerns about risk management.
  • For achievability: Harms of coercion (trauma, death, injury) outweigh benefits; psychiatric advance directives and better training (e.g., de-escalation) are sufficient alternatives.
    • Evidence: Qualitative themes from respondents who said “yes”; cited in discussion as counterarguments to coercion’s use.
    • Strength: Plausible but less empirically grounded; authors note lack of strong evidence that alternatives prevent harm in high-risk scenarios.
  • Nuance: Younger age (<51) and less work experience (<16 years) significantly associated with belief in achievability (p < 0.02); no association with gender or profession.
    • Evidence: Chi-square tests reported in results; age and experience as demographic predictors.
    • Strength: Statistically significant but may reflect cohort effects rather than causal insight.
  • Additional findings: Greater optimism about eliminating in-patient coercion vs. involuntary commitment; mechanical restraint ranked as top priority for abolition if full abolition not possible.
    • Evidence: Survey questions Q3–Q6; frequency data on preferred targets for reform.
    • Strength: Highlights nuanced perspectives within the abolition debate; suggests stepwise reform may be more viable than total abolition.

  • de-escalation — Assesses this as a suggested alternative and training need, noting its role in both pro-abolition arguments and identified gaps in current practice.
  • involuntary commitment — Contrasts perceptions of abolishing this specific coercive measure versus broader in-patient practices, showing differential optimism.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • With potential assumptions in the wiki: If the wiki assumes abolition is a widely accepted or imminent goal based on CRPD/Oviedo principles, this source introduces tension by showing that even reform-oriented professionals remain largely skeptical of its feasibility.
  • Internal tensions/caveats:
    • Respondents who support abolition rely on alternatives (advance directives, de-escalation) that the authors note lack strong evidence for preventing harm in high-risk cases involving imminent danger.
    • The study acknowledges that eliminating coercion might require accepting worse outcomes (e.g., lack of treatment), which some argue should be tolerated due to coercion’s harms—a morally contested trade-off not resolved in the data.
    • Discrepancy between optimism about eliminating specific measures (e.g., mechanical restraint) and skepticism about abolishing all coercion suggests that partial reform may be more viable than total abolition, creating a tension between pragmatic and abolitionist approaches.

Open Questions

  • What evidence exists that psychiatric advance directives or de-escalation can prevent harm in cases of imminent danger where individuals refuse care?
  • How do perspectives of individuals with lived experience of coercion compare to those of professionals in this study regarding the feasibility and desirability of abolition?
  • Is there a threshold of risk (e.g., likelihood of serious harm) beyond which most professionals accept some coercion as necessary to prevent violence or suicide?
  • How do national legal frameworks and implementation practices beyond Council of Europe instruments shape perceptions of abolition feasibility across different European countries?
