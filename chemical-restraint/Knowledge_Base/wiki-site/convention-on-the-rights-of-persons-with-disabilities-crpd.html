What It Is

The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) is an international human rights treaty adopted by the United Nations in 2006, establishing a comprehensive framework for the protection and promotion of the rights of persons with disabilities. It is central to contemporary discourse on mental health and human rights, particularly for its emphasis on legal capacity, autonomy, and the rejection of coercive practices in psychiatry.

Role in the Literature

In 2005 - Hunt - Report of the Special Rapporteur on the Right of Everyone to the Enjoyment of the Highest Attainable Standard of Physical and Mental Health, Paul Hunt: provides the legal foundation for redefining mental health rights, focusing on legal capacity, freedom from coercion, and community inclusion. In 2019 - Puras et al. - Mental health and human rights in the 21st century: establishes the human rights model of disability and obligates states to end forced treatment and substitute decision-making in mental health. In 2021 - Every‐Palmer et al. - Applying Human Rights and Reducing Coercion in Psychiatry following Service User-Led Education; A Qualitative Study.: serves as the theoretical foundation for training, especially through Article 12 on legal capacity and supported decision-making. In Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 05: cited as the most recent and critical international treaty relevant to coercion reduction, particularly Article 12 on legal capacity, which challenges substitute decision-making and promotes supported decision-making. In Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 12: referenced for its principles opposing involuntary admission by emphasizing autonomy, dignity, and freedom of choice for persons with disabilities. In Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -03: identified as the primary treaty under analysis, particularly regarding Article 12 on legal capacity. In Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -05: analyzed as the central international human rights treaty for its treatment of dignity, especially in Articles 1, 3, and 12.
