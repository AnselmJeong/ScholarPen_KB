Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical and legal frameworks governing decision-making for individuals deemed to lack capacity
  • Frequently invoked in psychiatric contexts to justify treatment when consent cannot be obtained
  • Subject to critique for potentially undermining autonomy and legal capacity rights
  • Increasingly challenged by human rights instruments like the UN CRPD

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Traditional justificatory stance Best interest standard justifies medical intervention when a person lacks decision-making capacity, particularly in psychiatric settings Psychiatry and the Law - 07
CRPD-based challenge Persons with psychosocial disabilities retain legal capacity and must consent to all interventions; best interest standard is invalid as a justification for coercion Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 09

Open questions

  • How can the best interest standard be reformed to align with the UN CRPD’s recognition of legal capacity for persons with psychosocial disabilities?
  • What safeguards are needed to prevent the best interest standard from being used as a blanket justification for coercive psychiatric practices?
  • In what contexts, if any, does the best interest standard remain ethically defensible when it overrides expressed or presumed will and preference?
  • How do cultural and legal variations influence the application and acceptability of the best interest standard in mental health care globally?
