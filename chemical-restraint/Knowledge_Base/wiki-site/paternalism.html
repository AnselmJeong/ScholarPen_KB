Why It Matters

  • Central to debates on the ethical justification of coercive practices in psychiatry
  • Highlights tension between beneficence and patient autonomy in clinical decision-making
  • Challenges assumptions of patient incompetence underlying paternalistic interventions
  • Raises concerns about trust erosion and human rights violations in coercive care

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Paternalism can justify coercion Paternalism may justify coercive measures when patients are deemed incompetent, though such justification is imperfect and context-dependent 2012 - Seo et al. - Coercion in psychiatric care Can paternalism justify coercion
Paternalism insufficient to eliminate coercion Appeals to autonomy and dignity alone cannot justify eliminating coercion, as these principles can support or oppose coercion depending on interpretation 2008 - Prinsen et al. - Can we justify eliminating coercive measures in psychiatry
Coercion undermines trust and care Coercion in psychiatry is fundamentally incompatible with trust, genuine care, and human rights, creating an ethical contradiction 2015 - Donskoy - Coercion and trust in psychiatry the ultimate contradiction
