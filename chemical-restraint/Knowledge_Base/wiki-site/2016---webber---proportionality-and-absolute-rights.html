Summary

Grégoire Webber’s paper examines the relationship between absolute rights and the principle of proportionality in human rights law. It critiques two dominant views held by proportionality proponents: that proportionality does not apply to absolute rights, or that absolute rights are merely predictable outcomes of proportionality analysis. Webber argues both views stem from a flawed conception of rights as individual interests rather than relational duties. Through comparative analysis of human rights instruments like the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, European Convention on Human Rights, and national bills of rights, he demonstrates that absolute rights (e.g., prohibitions against torture and slavery) are defined by negative injunctions that specify duties of non-action (“no one shall torture”). This relational structure—where rights correlate directly to duties owed by others—grounds their absoluteness in interpersonal obligations, not formal legal features or probabilistic balancing. The paper concludes that proper specification of rights as duties between persons is necessary to realize their normative force and defend them against reductive interest-based theories.

Key Entities

  • Universal Declaration of Human Rights (document) — Provides dual formulations of rights (affirmative and negative) used to illustrate how negative injunctions explicitly tie rights to duties of non-action.
  • European Convention on Human Rights (document) — Analyzed for its negative injunctions (e.g., Article 3 on torture) treated as absolute prohibitions in case law, despite formal mechanisms for limitation in other articles.
  • Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (document) — Cited for Section 12’s explicit formulation of the right not to be subjected to cruel and unusual treatment as a duty of non-action.
  • New Zealand Bill of Rights Act (document) — Referenced for Section 9’s explicit duty-based framing of the right not to be tortured or subjected to cruel treatment.
  • South African Bill of Rights (document) — Cited for Section 12’s explicit prohibition of torture and cruel treatment as duty-based rights.

Key Concepts

  • Absolute Rights — Rights that admit no exceptions, defined by correlative duties of non-action (e.g., “no one shall torture”), whose absoluteness stems from specifying interpersonal duties rather than absence of limitation clauses.
  • Proportionality Principle — A legal principle requiring state interference with rights to be suitable, necessary, and proportionate in the strict sense; critiqued for reducing rights to interests and failing to account for their relational structure.
  • Specification of Rights — The process of defining what a right requires others to do or refrain from doing, grounding it in interpersonal duties rather than abstract interests; presented as necessary for rights to achieve normative determinacy.
  • Duty of Non-Action — An obligation to refrain from specific acts (e.g., torture, enslavement), correlative to a claim-right (e.g., right not to be tortured); central to Webber’s account of absolute rights.
  • Rights as Interests — The view (held by proportionality proponents) that rights are reducible to the interest-holder’s welfare (e.g., autonomy, well-being), with limits justified by balancing competing interests; criticized for ignoring duty-bearers and rendering rights inherently defeasible.
  • Hohfeldian Analysis — The implicit framework distinguishing claim-rights, duties, privileges, etc., especially the correlativity of claim-right and duty; undergirds Webber’s argument that rights like “no torture” impose duties on others, making them relational and capable of being absolute.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Absolute rights are absolute because they specify duties of non-action between persons (e.g., “no one shall torture”), making their correlative duties immune to proportionality balancing—not because they lack limitation clauses or result from predictable balancing outcomes.
    • Evidence: Comparative analysis showing negative formulations in human rights instruments (UDHR, ECHR, national bills) explicitly tie rights to duties; case law treating provisions like Canadian Charter Section 12 as absolute despite formal override mechanisms; critique of proportionality theorists (Barak, Möller, Alexy, Kumm) who either exclude absolute rights from proportionality or reduce them to predictable balancing, both failing to ground rights in interpersonal duties.
    • Strength: Strong doctrinal support from international and comparative human rights law; philosophical critique is coherent but relies on interpreting others’ views (possible risk of mischaracterization, though Webber engages primary texts).

Connections

  • Absolute Rights — This source redefines the concept by grounding absolutism in relational specification (duties of non-action) rather than formal legal features or probabilistic outcomes.
  • Proportionality — This source challenges the theory’s applicability to rights by arguing it misconceptualizes rights as interests, obscuring their duty-based structure.
  • Hohfeldian Analysis — This source relies on and extends the Hohfeldian framework to show how claim-rights correlate to duties, enabling a non-interest-based account of rights.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • If the wiki currently defines absolute rights solely by the absence of derogation or limitation clauses (e.g., ECHR Article 3), this source challenges that as insufficient—Webber shows even rights with potential limitations can function absolutely when properly specified as duties (e.g., Section 12 of the Canadian Charter).
  • If proportionality is presented as a universal test applicable to all rights, Webber’s argument that some rights preclude balancing due to their relational nature creates tension with universalist accounts.
  • (Internal caveat) Webber acknowledges that terms like “torture” require interpretation but insists this occurs within a duty-defined framework, not open-ended interest balancing; he does not fully address how specification avoids regress (who specifies the specifiers?), though he suggests morality and law jointly secure it.

Open Questions

  • How is the specification of rights actually determined in practice? Webber points to morality and law, but what mechanisms (judicial, democratic, deliberative) prevent arbitrary or biased specification of duties?
  • Can Webber’s account handle conflicts between absolute rights (e.g., self-defense vs. non-violence)? He does not address whether specified duties themselves can conflict, leaving open the possibility of irresolvable tensions between absolute claims.
