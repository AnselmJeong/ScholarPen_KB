Why It Matters

  • Addresses ethical concerns by reducing coercive practices in psychiatric care while supporting patient and staff empowerment
  • Highlights gaps in legal protections for mental health rights in low-resource and conflict-affected settings
  • Connects stigma to the cycle of coercion, showing how coercive measures reinforce negative perceptions of psychiatry
  • Informs policy shifts toward eliminating coercion, such as the ‘zero vision’ approach in regional psychiatry

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Supportive of human rights-based approaches in psychiatry A human rights-based approach can reduce coercive measures and foster empowerment in psychiatric care 2020 - Broberg et al. - A Human Rights-Based Approach to Psychiatry; Is It Possible
Critical of current legal frameworks Existing international laws inadequately protect mental health rights in conflict and low-resource settings, requiring new legal balances 2022 - Miller - Mental health and the law What else is needed for particularly vulnerable contexts facing armed conflict and development obstacles
Focus on stigma and coercion cycle Stigma increases acceptability of coercion, and coercion worsens stigma and public perceptions of psychiatry Coercive Treatment in Psychiatry - 04
