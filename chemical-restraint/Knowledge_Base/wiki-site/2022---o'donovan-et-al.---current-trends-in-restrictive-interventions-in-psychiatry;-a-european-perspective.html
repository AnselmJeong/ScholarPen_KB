Summary

This article reviews current evidence on the use of coercive measures—including seclusion, physical, mechanical, and pharmacological restraint—in psychiatric inpatient settings across Europe. It synthesizes data from multiple countries to describe patterns of use, legal and policy frameworks, clinical indications, consequences, and strategies to reduce reliance on such interventions. The review places particular emphasis on mechanical restraint due to its prolonged duration and unique ethical challenges. The authors aim to clarify when restrictive interventions may be necessary, examine their use in specialist settings (e.g., forensic psychiatry, dementia care), and evaluate current European laws and policies governing seclusion and restraint.

The research design is a narrative review of empirical studies, systematic reviews, and policy documents up to 2022. Findings reveal substantial cross-national variation in reported rates: highest in Poland, Greece, and Italy; lowest in Iceland, where seclusion and mechanical restraint were abolished approximately 30 years ago. Within countries like Germany and Denmark, usage differs significantly between forensic and general psychiatry settings, and even among individual hospitals. Mechanical restraint is more common in forensic contexts in Germany (21.2% seclusion vs. 3.2% mechanical restraint), while the reverse is true in general psychiatry. Trends show increasing use in Ireland (2008–2019) and significant reductions in Italy’s Emilia-Romagna region (62% decrease, 2011–2016) following national guidelines. Mechanical restraint is frequently applied to male patients with neurocognitive disorders or those detained under mental health legislation. Consequences include post-traumatic stress disorder (25–47% incidence), physical injury, death, and staff burnout. The article highlights de-escalation techniques, therapeutic milieus (e.g., Safewards, Six Core Strategies), environmental modifications, and staff training as effective alternatives, particularly in dementia care units where use decreased by 50% in German hospitals between 2004 and 2019. However, similar reductions were not observed in general adult psychiatry, suggesting limited generalizability of these interventions across patient populations.

Key Concepts

  • Restrictive interventions — coercive measures including seclusion, physical, mechanical, and pharmacological restraint used to manage acute agitation or risk of harm in psychiatric inpatient settings; central to the article’s analysis of prevalence and regulation.
  • Mechanical restraint — the use of devices (e.g., belts, handcuffs) to restrict bodily movement, often lasting hours; highlighted due to its prolonged duration and distinct ethical and legal considerations compared to brief physical restraint.
  • Seclusion — confinement of a person alone in a locked room; discussed as a commonly used but controversial intervention, with debates over its perceived invasiveness and psychological impact.
  • Pharmacological restraint — administration of medication (e.g., antipsychotics, benzodiazepines) against a patient’s will via coercion; noted as globally common, especially in emergency agitation management.
  • Least restrictive principle — an ethical and legal standard requiring that any coercive measure used must be the minimum necessary to prevent harm; foundational to justifying restraint as a last resort.
  • De-escalation — verbal and non-verbal techniques aimed at preventing aggression without coercion; presented as a key evidence-based alternative supported by training frameworks.
  • Trauma and re-traumatisation — psychological harm resulting from coercive practices, including PTSD (reported in 25–47% of cases); a critical consequence underscoring the need for reduction strategies.
  • Human rights framework (ECHR Article 3) — the provision prohibiting torture and inhuman or degrading treatment; frequently invoked to challenge restraint practices that may violate dignity and safety.
  • Therapeutic milieu — structured environmental and interpersonal approaches (e.g., Safewards, Six Core Strategies) designed to improve ward culture and reduce reliance on coercion; shown effective in specific contexts like dementia care.
  • Organizational strategies — systemic approaches including staff training, risk assessment (e.g., using the Brøset Violence Checklist), incident reporting, and environmental modifications to minimize coercion; cited as effective in reducing use in certain settings.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The use of coercive measures in European psychiatric inpatient settings remains widespread but highly variable across countries and settings, carries significant human rights and safety risks (including PTSD in 25–47% of cases), yet may be considered necessary as a last resort to prevent imminent harm when de-escalation fails.
    • Evidence: Rates vary widely—highest in Poland, Greece, Italy; lowest in Iceland (abolished 30 years prior). In Germany, forensic psychiatry shows higher seclusion (21.2%) than mechanical restraint (3.2%), while general psychiatry shows the reverse. Ireland reported year-on-year increases in physical restraint (2008–2019); Italy’s Emilia-Romagna region reduced mechanical restraint by 62% (2011–2016) post-guideline. Mechanical restraint is often applied to male patients with neurocognitive disorders or those under mental health detention. Consequences include PTSD, physical injury, death, and staff burnout. Alternatives like de-escalation and therapeutic programs (e.g., Safewards) reduced use by 50% in German dementia units (2004–2019).
    • Strength: Evidence on prevalence, trends, and consequences is moderate to strong, drawn from multi-country studies and systematic reviews. Evidence on the effectiveness of alternatives is weaker due to reliance on observational data, lack of RCTs, and inconsistent definitions across studies. Limitations include reporting bias, heterogeneity in service provision, and non-uniform terminology hindering cross-national comparison.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The source acknowledges an internal tension: while it affirms that coercive measures may be necessary as a last resort for safety (e.g., to prevent harm during acute agitation), it simultaneously emphasizes their traumatic potential (PTSD, physical harm) and advocates for minimization to the greatest extent possible, creating a ethical tension between immediate safety and long-term well-being.
  • It notes a contradiction in perception: seclusion is often viewed as less invasive and better tolerated than mechanical restraint, yet both are coercive and regulated under similar human rights frameworks, suggesting a potential misalignment between patient perception and objective impact.
  • The source highlights a contextual contradiction: interventions like staff training and therapeutic milieus (e.g., Safewards) achieved 50% reductions in dementia care units in Germany but showed minimal impact in general adult psychiatry, suggesting that effective strategies may not generalize across diagnostic or functional populations.

Open Questions

  • Why do organizational and therapeutic interventions (e.g., Staff training, Safewards) succeed in reducing coercive use in psychiatric units serving patients with neurocognitive disorders (e.g., dementia) but show limited effectiveness in general adult psychiatry populations?
  • How can inconsistent definitions, reporting practices, and reference sampling across European countries be standardized to enable valid cross-national comparison of coercive practice rates?
  • What are the long-term outcomes for patients subjected to mechanical restraint beyond PTSD—such as effects on trust in mental health services, therapeutic alliance, or readmission rates—and how do these vary by diagnosis and context?
