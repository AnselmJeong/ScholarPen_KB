Summary

This study investigated the feelings and attitudes of mental health professionals toward coercion in psychiatric care through a survey of 130 staff at Lausanne University Hospital. Using exploratory structural equation modeling (ESEM), the researchers identified four underlying dimensions shaping professionals’ experiences: internal pressure (ethical conflict and doubt about usefulness), emotional impact (stress, lasting memories, loneliness), external pressure (organizational and situational drivers like lack of time or legal fears), and relational involvement (empathy, debriefing, considering patient preferences). While 34.6% agreed coercion violates fundamental rights, a substantial minority also endorsed its necessity and benefit—42.3% found it indispensable, 35.4% beneficial, and 59.2% believed it does not hinder recovery. Key associations showed that recent exposure to coercion reduced internal pressure (suggesting habituation), psychiatrists reported higher emotional impact, and social workers experienced less external pressure. Having a relative with mental illness intensified emotional responses. The findings highlight a persistent gap between ethical concerns and continued reliance on coercion, even without strong evidence for its efficacy.

Key Entities

  • Lausanne University Hospital (org) — The study was conducted among mental health professionals in its Department of Psychiatry, serving as the sole research setting.
  • International Journal of Law and Psychiatry (org) — The peer-reviewed journal where the study was published, disseminating findings on law, ethics, and mental health practices.
  • Mplus 8 (tool) — Used for conducting exploratory structural equation modeling (ESEM) to identify latent attitude dimensions.
  • IBM SPSS 26 (tool) — Employed for descriptive statistics and preliminary data analysis alongside Mplus.
  • Exploratory Structural Equation Modeling (ESEM) (tool/method) — The central analytical method used to uncover the four-dimensional structure of attitudes toward coercion.

Key Concepts

  • Coercion in psychiatry — Refers to forced interventions such as involuntary hospitalization, medication, seclusion, or restraint; central to the study as the object of professionals’ attitudes.
  • Internal pressure — A dimension capturing ethical conflict, moral unease, and doubt about coercion’s usefulness and benefit to patients; lower among those recently exposed to coercion.
  • Emotional impact — Reflects stress, lasting memories, loneliness, and cognitive dissonance when using coercion; higher among psychiatrists and those with a relative with mental illness.
  • External pressure — Encompasses organizational and situational drivers such as lack of time, legal fears, third-party pressure, and administrative burden; lower among social workers.
  • Relational involvement — Involves empathy, debriefing with colleagues, discussing therapeutic options, and considering patient preferences during and after coercion.
  • Moral dilemma — The psychological discomfort when actions (using coercion) conflict with beliefs (that it is wrong); discussed in relation to internal pressure and emotional impact.
  • Habituation — The process by which repeated exposure to coercion reduces ethical discomfort (internal pressure), suggesting adaptation over time.
  • Shared decision making — A recommended approach to reduce reliance on coercion by involving patients in care decisions; highlighted in the conclusion as a potential intervention.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Despite widespread recognition that coercion violates patient rights and lacks evidence for efficacy, a significant proportion of mental health professionals still view it as indispensable, beneficial, and therapeutic.
    • Evidence: Survey results showed 34.6% agreed coercion violates rights, yet 42.3% found it indispensable, 35.4% beneficial, 47.7% therapeutic, and 59.2% believed it does not hinder recovery; 56.2% felt they were acting in the patient’s best interest when using it.
    • Strength: Strong descriptive support from survey data; limited by cross-sectional design and single-site sample (17.8% response rate), which may introduce selection bias and limit generalizability.
  • Core claim: Professionals’ feelings and attitudes toward coercion are structured around four latent dimensions: internal pressure, emotional impact, external pressure, and relational involvement.
    • Evidence: ESEM analysis of 37 Likert-scale items supported a four-factor solution with adequate model fit (RMSEA = 0.045, CFI = 0.919, TLI = 0.898) and interpretable factors.
    • Strength: Methodologically robust for uncovering latent structure; however, the measure was not validated as a formal psychometric scale, and findings are context-specific to Lausanne.
  • Core claim: Personal and professional factors differentially predict the four attitude dimensions.
    • Evidence: Internal pressure lower with recent coercion exposure; emotional impact higher among psychiatrists and those with a relative with mental illness; external pressure lower among social workers; no significant predictors found for relational involvement in the multivariate model.
    • Strength: Reveals nuanced influences beyond simple pro/anti attitudes; limited by lack of correction for multiple comparisons and potential confounding variables.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Professionals simultaneously acknowledge coercion as a violation of fundamental rights yet believe it is indispensable and beneficial—revealing cognitive dissonance between ethical beliefs and practical justifications.
  • Despite limited empirical support for coercion’s efficacy in improving outcomes, many professionals maintain confidence in its therapeutic value and necessity, creating a tension between belief and evidence.
  • The finding that recent exposure to coercion reduces internal pressure (ethical discomfort) contrasts with expectations that repeated moral distress would increase sensitivity; instead, it suggests habituation may erode ethical resistance over time.
  • Social workers reported lower external pressure, challenging assumptions that all frontline staff face similar organizational pressures when considering coercion use.

Open Questions

  • How do patients’ actual experiences and perceptions of coercion compare to professionals’ beliefs about its benefit and necessity?
  • Can systemic interventions targeting specific dimensions—such as reducing external pressure through better staffing or legal protections—decrease reliance on coercion?
  • Do longitudinal studies show that the four dimensions (especially internal and emotional) change with cumulative exposure to coercion, or do they stabilize?
  • How do cultural, legal, and institutional differences across countries or care settings shape the expression of these four dimensions?
  • What role does moral courage or institutional support play in sustaining ethical resistance to coercion despite habitual exposure?
