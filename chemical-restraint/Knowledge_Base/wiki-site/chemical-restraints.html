Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical and legal debates in emergency and psychiatric care regarding patient autonomy and safety
  • Highlights tensions between clinical necessity and fundamental rights, especially when consciousness is altered
  • Underscores limitations of dominant ethical frameworks like principlism in addressing collective and relational impacts
  • Drives policy and practice around de-escalation, informed consent, and regulatory compliance

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
De-escalation-first approach Physical and chemical restraints should be used only after de-escalation fails, with strict protocols 2020 - Guerrero et al. - Physical and Chemical Restraints (an Update)
Ethical concern with consciousness-altering restraints Chemical restraints that sedate or undermine consciousness are more ethically problematic due to infringement on basic liberties 2024 - Crutchfield, Redinger - Basic Liberties, Consent, and Chemical Restraints
Critique of principlism Principlism is inadequate for ethical decisions on chemical restraints as it overemphasizes autonomy and ignores communal moral impact 2024 - Mangal, Scheiner - The Limitations of Principlism
