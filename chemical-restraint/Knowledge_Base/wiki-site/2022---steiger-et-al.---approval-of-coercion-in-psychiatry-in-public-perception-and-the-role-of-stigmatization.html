Summary

This study examined public approval of coercive psychiatric measures through a vignette-based survey of 1,112 residents in Basel-Stadt, Switzerland. Participants evaluated scenarios involving individuals with psychiatric conditions (psychosis, borderline personality disorder, alcohol dependence) exhibiting varying levels of dangerous behavior. The researchers measured approval of three coercive interventions—involuntary hospitalization, medication, and seclusion—and assessed their relationship with stigma indicators (perceived dangerousness and desire for social distance), familiarity with mental illness, and perceived treatment usefulness. Results showed that approval was highest when individuals were seen as dangerous, when treatment was viewed as beneficial, and when actual dangerous behavior was present. Perceived dangerousness consistently predicted approval across all coercive types, while familiarity with mental illness reduced approval only for less severe measures (hospitalization), not for medication or seclusion. Desire for social distance predicted approval of medication and seclusion but not hospitalization. The study concludes that public attitudes align with legal frameworks but are significantly shaped by stigmatizing attitudes, especially for more invasive interventions.

Key Concepts

  • Coercive Measures in Psychiatry — Involuntary interventions such as hospitalization, medication, and seclusion used when less restrictive options fail and there is risk of harm.
  • Mental Illness Stigma — Negative attitudes and beliefs leading to social rejection, discrimination, and exclusion of individuals with mental illness.
  • Perceived Dangerousness — The belief that a person with mental illness is likely to cause harm to self or others; a core component of stigma.
  • Desire for Social Distance — Unwillingness to engage in close social relationships with someone with mental illness; used as a behavioral indicator of stigma.
  • Beneficial Coercion — The idea that coercion can be justified when it prevents harm or enables treatment, increasing public acceptance.
  • Modified Labeling Theory — Theoretical framework suggesting that being labeled as mentally ill leads to social rejection, which can be reinforced by experiences like coercion.
  • Familiarity with Mental Illness — Personal or close-contact experience with mental illness (self, family, or friends), associated with reduced stigma.
  • Psychiatric Diagnosis — The type of mental disorder presented in vignettes (psychosis, BPD, alcohol dependence), influencing perceptions of dangerousness and treatability.
  • Public Perception — The collective attitudes of the general population toward psychiatric interventions and individuals with mental illness.
  • Psychiatric Treatment Usefulness — The belief that coercive intervention will lead to positive outcomes for the individual, strongly predicting approval.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Public approval of psychiatric coercion is shaped by both situation-specific factors (actual dangerous behavior, treatment usefulness) and stigmatizing attitudes (perceived dangerousness, social distance), with the latter gaining influence as coercive measures become more severe.
    • Evidence: Logistic regression analysis showed perceived dangerousness predicted approval across all coercive types (OR=1.146 for any coercion), treatment usefulness was the strongest predictor (OR=4.68), and actual dangerous behavior increased approval. Familiarity with mental illness reduced approval for general coercion and hospitalization but not for medication or seclusion. Desire for social distance predicted approval of medication and seclusion only.
    • Strength: Moderate. Models explained 15–20% of variance (Nagelkerke R²), indicating significant unexplained variance. However, findings are statistically significant (p<0.05), based on a large representative sample, and use validated stigma measures. Limitations include dated data (2013–2014), low response rate (22.1%), and potential overrepresentation of women and highly educated individuals.

Connections

  • mental illness stigma — This study provides empirical evidence on how stigma dimensions (dangerousness, social distance) predict public approval of coercive psychiatric measures.
  • beneficial coercion — The study supports the concept that perceived treatment usefulness strongly increases acceptance of coercion, aligning with ethical justifications for intervention.
  • modified labeling theory — Findings align with this theory by showing that coercion approval is linked to stigmatizing attitudes, which may be reinforced by coercive experiences.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Internal tension: While familiarity with mental illness reduced approval of coercion (consistent with anti-stigma expectations), this effect disappeared for medication and seclusion, suggesting that even informed individuals may rely on stigmatizing heuristics when interventions are perceived as more intrusive or less tied to immediate danger.

Open Questions

  • How have public attitudes toward psychiatric coercion evolved since 2014, particularly in light of increased mental health awareness and anti-stigma campaigns?
  • Does the influence of stigma on coercion approval vary across different healthcare systems or cultural contexts?
  • What types of interventions (e.g., contact-based education, narrative interventions) most effectively reduce the impact of stigmatizing attitudes on public support for coercion?
