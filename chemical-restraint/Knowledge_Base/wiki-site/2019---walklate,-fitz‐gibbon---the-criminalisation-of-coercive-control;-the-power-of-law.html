Summary

This article examines whether criminal law has the capacity to effectively respond to coercive control in intimate partner relationships. The authors analyze how coercive control has been incorporated into legal frameworks across jurisdictions (including England and Wales, Scotland, Ireland, Tasmania, and others) and evaluate these legal responses against empirical evidence of coercive control’s nature in everyday life. They argue that criminalizing coercive control fundamentally misunderstands both the nature of coercive control as a “liberty crime” involving patterned behavior rather than discrete incidents, and the coercive potential of legal systems themselves. Despite legislative innovations like the UK’s Serious Crime Act 2015, enforcement remains limited (only ~9,000 recorded offenses versus 2 million domestic abuse incidents annually in England/Wales), and legal interventions produce unintended consequences including legal systems abuse, erasure of victim agency, and the masking of physical violence. The authors conclude that criminalization fails those it aims to protect and advocate for approaches that center victim autonomy and address structural gender inequalities rather than expanding criminal law.

Key Entities

  • Serious Crime Act 2015 (England and Wales) — Legislation that created the specific criminal offense of coercive control, serving as the primary case study for evaluating legal responses
  • Domestic Abuse (Scotland) Act 2018 — Legislation that contrasts with the English approach by recognizing gendered patterns and including ex-partners
  • Office of National Statistics — Organization providing crime statistics showing low prosecution rates for coercive control offenses relative to domestic abuse incidents
  • Women’s Aid — Organization cited regarding the Sally Challen case and advocacy around coercive control legislation
  • Home Office (UK) — Government department providing the official legal definition of coercive control used in England and Wales

Key Concepts

  • Coercive control — A pattern of behavior involving isolation, degradation, exploitation, and control that creates fear and restricts freedom; conceptualized as a “liberty crime” by Stark (2007) rather than mere physical violence
  • Coercive control creep — The uncritical expansion of the coercive control concept across legal and policy contexts without sufficient scrutiny of its limitations or unintended consequences
  • Liberty crime — Stark’s framework positioning coercive control as a violation of fundamental freedoms and autonomy, not just bodily harm
  • Legal systems abuse — The phenomenon where perpetrators exploit legal processes (protection orders, court proceedings) to further control and abuse victims
  • Victim agency — The capacity of individuals to make autonomous choices within relationships; central to critiques of law’s inability to distinguish between consensual constraints and coercion

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The law lacks the capacity to adequately respond to coercive control due to its nature as a pattern of behavior rather than discrete incidents.
    • Evidence: Statistical mismatch between 9,000 coercive control offenses and 2 million domestic abuse incidents annually in England/Wales; court struggles with evidence and interpretation (e.g., Teresa Craig and Sally Challen cases)
    • Strength: Strong — supported by multi-jurisdictional legal analysis, empirical data, and case law; limitation is focus on Anglophone jurisdictions
  • Core claim: Criminalizing coercive control fails victims because it misunderstands the coercive nature of law itself and fails to see victims’ lived experiences holistically.
    • Evidence: Analysis of how law forces yes/no determinations on complex experiences; women’s reluctance to engage with criminal justice due to fear of losing children or economic security; perpetrators using legal systems to exacerbate control
    • Strength: Strong — integrates feminist legal theory, empirical research on victim experiences, and documentation of legal systems abuse
  • Core claim: Legal interventions produce unintended consequences including legal systems abuse and the erasure of victim agency.
    • Evidence: Documentation of perpetrators using protection orders and court processes to extend control; legal determination of what constitutes coercion overriding victim/survivor perspectives
    • Strength: Strong — supported by Douglas (2018) on legal systems abuse and Hanna (2009) on victim experiences with legal intervention
  • Core claim: The concept of “coercive control creep” leads to uncritical adoption of legal solutions without addressing root causes of gender inequality.
    • Evidence: Rapid diffusion of coercive control laws across jurisdictions despite limited evidence of effectiveness; focus on legal reform rather than economic or social interventions
    • Strength: Moderate — based on policy diffusion patterns and critique of carceral feminism, though could benefit from more longitudinal data

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The authors acknowledge that some legal recognition of coercive control has value (e.g., challenging provocation defenses in homicide cases) while arguing that criminalization ultimately fails victims, creating tension between reformist and abolitionist approaches to legal change.
  • The article notes that victims may want abusive behavior to stop but not necessarily through criminal sanctions, highlighting a tension between protective interventions and respect for victim autonomy in defining safety.
  • There is an internal tension between recognizing law’s limitations and acknowledging that legal discourse can still contribute to shifting social understandings of abuse, even when practical enforcement fails.

Open Questions

  • What non-carceral approaches to addressing coercive control show promise in preserving victim autonomy while enhancing safety?
  • How can legal systems better distinguish between consensual constraint within relationships and coercive control that undermines autonomy?
  • What role should community-based, restorative, or transformative justice models play in responding to patterns of coercive control?
  • How do intersections of race, class, disability, indigeneity, and immigration status affect both experiences of coercive control and interactions with legal systems designed to address it?
