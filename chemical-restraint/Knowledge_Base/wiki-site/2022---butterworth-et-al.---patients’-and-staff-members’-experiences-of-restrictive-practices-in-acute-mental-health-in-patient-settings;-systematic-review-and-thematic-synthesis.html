Summary

This source presents a systematic review and thematic synthesis of qualitative research examining the experiences of restrictive practices (such as physical restraint, chemical restraint, and seclusion) in acute mental health inpatient settings from both patient and staff perspectives. The study analyzed 21 qualitative papers published between 1996 and 2019 across 11 countries, using a pre-registered protocol (PROSPERO: CRD42020176859) and following PRISMA guidelines. Study quality was assessed using the Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) tool, and data were synthesized using thematic synthesis via QSR’s NVivo 12 software. Four overarching themes emerged from patient data: psychological effects, staff communication, loss of human rights, and making changes. From staff data, four themes emerged: the need for restrictive practices, psychological impact, decision-making, and making changes. The findings indicate that restrictive practices are experienced as overwhelmingly negative by both groups, causing psychological harm such as fear, anxiety, and re-traumatization, damaging staff–patient relationships, and persisting beyond the event, even when staff view them as a necessary last resort.

Key Entities

  • PROSPERO (org) — International prospective register of systematic reviews where the review protocol was pre-registered (CRD42020176859), ensuring transparency and reducing duplication in research.
  • Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) (tool) — Used to assess the methodological quality of included qualitative studies, ensuring only sufficiently rigorous evidence contributed to the synthesis.
  • QSR’s NVivo 12 software (tool) — Employed for qualitative data analysis, enabling systematic coding, theme development, and organization of textual data from the included studies.
  • PsycInfo, Embase, MEDLINE (dataset) — Primary electronic databases searched to identify relevant studies, covering psychological, biomedical, and nursing literature comprehensively.
  • PRISMA (guideline) — Framework followed for transparent reporting of the systematic review process, enhancing reproducibility and minimizing bias in study selection and synthesis.
  • SPIDER framework (tool) — Utilized to develop search terms tailored to the qualitative nature of the review question, ensuring sensitivity in capturing relevant phenomena.

Key Concepts

  • Restrictive practices — Interventions that restrict movement, behavior, or liberty (e.g., physical/chemical restraint, seclusion) used to manage challenging behavior in psychiatric settings; central to the review’s focus on lived experiences of both patients and staff.
  • Thematic synthesis — Method for synthesizing qualitative research by coding text into descriptive and analytical themes (following Thomas & Harden); the primary analytical approach used to integrate findings across 21 studies.
  • Psychological effects — Emotional and mental health impacts such as fear, anxiety, re-traumatization, and post-traumatic stress following restraint or seclusion; a dominant theme in patient narratives highlighting harm beyond physical restriction.
  • Loss of human rights — Perceived deprivation of autonomy, dignity, and freedom during restrictive interventions, including feelings of imprisonment and dehumanization; a core ethical concern expressed by patients linking practices to systemic injustice.
  • Staff–patient relationship — Interpersonal dynamic between healthcare providers and patients, particularly how communication and trust affect care; repeatedly cited as damaged by restrictive practices, undermining therapeutic engagement.
  • De-escalation techniques — Non-coercive strategies (e.g., verbal communication, environmental modification) to reduce agitation and prevent use of restraint; emphasized by both patients and staff as a preferred alternative to coercive interventions.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Restrictive practices in acute psychiatric inpatient settings are experienced as overwhelmingly negative by both patients and staff, causing psychological harm, damaging relationships, and persisting beyond the event, despite being viewed by staff as sometimes unavoidable.
    • Evidence: Thematic synthesis of 21 qualitative studies (1996–2019, 11 countries) using CASP-assessed data and NVivo analysis. Patient themes included psychological distress, poor communication, loss of autonomy, and desire for change; staff themes included perceived necessity, emotional toll, decision factors (risk, staffing, alternatives), and calls for support.
    • Strength: Strong due to rigorous methodology (pre-registration, comprehensive search, quality appraisal, thematic synthesis), international scope, and consistency across studies. Limitations include single reviewer for screening and heterogeneity across settings/time, but convergent findings support reliability.

Connections

  • involuntary treatment — This review reinforces concerns about coercion and loss of autonomy in psychiatric care, showing how restrictive practices compound harms in non-consensual treatment contexts.
  • trauma-informed care — Findings on re-traumatization during restraint support the need for trauma-sensitive approaches that avoid triggering past abuse in psychiatric populations.
  • therapeutic alliance — Highlights how restrictive practices damage trust and engagement, directly opposing the collaborative foundation of effective therapeutic relationships in mental health care.
  • crisis intervention strategies — Supports shifting focus toward de-escalation and alternatives like Safewards or Six Core Strategies, which align with patient and staff preferences for non-coercive conflict resolution.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Staff acknowledge restrictive practices as a “last resort” yet describe them as “unavoidable,” reflecting systemic gaps in training, resources, and alternatives despite good intentions — a tension between intention and systemic reality.
  • Some patients reported feeling “safe” during restraint (in 7 of 12 studies), indicating complexity—though these were minority views and often context-dependent, suggesting that perceived safety may coexist with distress or stem from extreme agitation requiring containment.

Open Questions

  • How do cultural and systemic factors (e.g., racism, understaffing) influence experiences of restrictive practices, particularly for marginalized groups?
  • What are the most effective, feasible alternatives to restrictive practices in low-resource mental health settings?
  • How can peer support be integrated into post-restraint debriefing processes to support both patients and staff while repairing relational harm?
