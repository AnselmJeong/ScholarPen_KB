What It Is

The European Convention on Human Rights is an international treaty designed to protect fundamental human rights and freedoms across Europe, forming the basis of legal standards interpreted by the European Court of Human Rights. It establishes binding obligations on member states to uphold civil and political rights, including prohibitions on torture, arbitrary detention, and inhuman treatment. In scholarly literature, it serves as a critical framework for analyzing the limits and enforcement of state power, particularly regarding absolute rights and detention practices.

Role in the Literature

  • In 2016 - Webber - Proportionality and Absolute Rights: The Convention’s provisions, particularly Article 3 on torture, are analyzed for being treated as absolute prohibitions in case law despite formal limitation mechanisms elsewhere.
  • In Coercion and Violence in Mental Health Settings - 12: Article 5 is referenced in discussions of detention of individuals deemed “of unsound mind,” with the European Court interpreting such measures as potentially violating the Convention in mental health contexts.
