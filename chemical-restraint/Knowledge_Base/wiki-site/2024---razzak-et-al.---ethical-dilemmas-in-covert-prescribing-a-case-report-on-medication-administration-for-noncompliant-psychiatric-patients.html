Ethical Dilemmas in Covert Prescribing: A Case Report on Medication Administration for Noncompliant Psychiatric Patients

Summary

This case report examines the ethical and clinical challenges of covert antipsychotic administration in a 54-year-old male with schizophrenia who refused medication due to religious delusions following a traumatic injury. The patient, who believed he would be “healed through God,” consistently declined oral medications despite exhibiting psychosis, disorganized thinking, and unsafe behaviors such as ingesting foreign objects and smearing feces. After multidisciplinary discussion and with consent from his mother acting as medical power of attorney, the care team administered risperidone covertly in his food, starting at 1 mg BID and titrating to 3 mg BID. While this intervention led to cessation of foreign object ingestion, the patient continued to experience persistent psychotic symptoms and developed mild extrapyramidal symptoms (tremors in bilateral upper extremities). The case underscores the limited efficacy of covert prescribing in achieving therapeutic goals and raises significant ethical concerns regarding the balance between respecting patient autonomy and acting in the patient’s best interest (beneficence), particularly when the patient retains some decision-making capacity and refuses treatment based on deeply held beliefs. The authors emphasize the lack of psychiatric-specific guidance for covert prescribing compared to dementia care and call for interdisciplinary collaboration and standardized protocols to ethically navigate such complex scenarios.

Key Concepts

  • Covert prescribing: The administration of medication without the patient’s knowledge or consent, often by concealment in food or drink; central to the case as the primary intervention examined and the source of the ethical dilemma.
  • Patient autonomy: The ethical principle upholding an individual’s right to make decisions about their own body and treatment, including the right to refuse care; directly challenged in this case when medication was administered covertly despite the patient’s expressed refusal.
  • Beneficence: The ethical obligation to act in the patient’s best interest and promote well-being; cited as justification for covert prescribing to prevent harm from unsafe behaviors like foreign object ingestion.
  • Non-maleficence: The principle of “do no harm,” requiring avoidance of causation of injury or suffering; relevant in assessing risks such as extrapyramidal symptoms arising from antipsychotic use.
  • Informed consent: The process of obtaining voluntary agreement for treatment after disclosing relevant information, risks, and alternatives; bypassed in covert raising, raising ethical concerns about transparency and respect for patient autonomy.
  • Medical power of attorney: A legal document designating a surrogate to make healthcare decisions when a patient lacks capacity; here, the patient’s mother provided consent for covert medication, raising questions about the limits of surrogate authority when the patient retains some decision-making ability.
  • Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): Movement disorders such as tremors, rigidity, and restlessness that can occur as side effects of antipsychotics; observed in the patient as mild bilateral upper extremity tremors, complicating the risk-benefit assessment of covert treatment.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Covert prescribing in psychiatric patients with partial decision-making capacity presents significant ethical dilemmas, particularly between autonomy and beneficence, and requires standardized guidelines and interdisciplinary collaboration for ethical navigation.
    • Evidence: The case of a 54-year-old man with schizophrenia who refused medication due to religious delusions; risperidone was given covertly in food with maternal (POA) consent. Outcomes included cessation of foreign object ingestion but persistent psychosis and emergence of extrapyramidal symptoms. The authors support their argument with a literature review showing sparse psychiatric-specific guidance on covert prescribing compared to dementia care, and reference to Texas Health and Safety Code requiring consent unless in emergencies or via surrogate.
    • Strength: The evidence is primarily anecdotal (single case), limiting generalizability. The literature review provides contextual support but is not systematic. Ethical and legal references strengthen the argument, but the n=1 design weakens empirical support. The call for guidelines is reasonable but would require broader evidence for standardization.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Internal tension: The intervention reduced foreign object ingestion (a safety behavior) but failed to control core psychotic symptoms, raising questions about the proportionality and therapeutic value of covert prescribing.
  • Ethical tension: Reliance on medical power of attorney to justify covert administration creates conflict between legal authorization and ethical permissibility, especially when the patient retains decision-making capacity and refuses based on deeply held religious beliefs.
  • Literature gap: Covert prescribing is more commonly discussed and ethically tolerated in dementia care (where incapacity is clearer), but its use in psychiatry—where patients may have fluctuating or partial capacity—is less guided and more ethically fraught, creating a tension between extrapolating dementia-derived models and developing psychiatry-specific ethical frameworks.
  • No direct contradiction with existing wiki content was identified, but the case highlights an under-theorized area that current ethical frameworks may not adequately address.

Open Questions

  • Under what specific conditions, if any, is covert prescribing ethically permissible in psychiatric patients who retain partial decision-making capacity?
  • How should clinicians navigate conflicts between legal authority (e.g., via power of attorney) and ethical obligations when the patient’s values (e.g., religious beliefs) directly oppose recommended treatment?
  • Should patients be informed of past covert medication administration if they later regain insight, and how might such disclosure affect the therapeutic alliance and trust?
  • What safeguards, monitoring mechanisms, or oversight processes are needed to prevent misuse or abuse of covert prescribing under the guise of beneficence in psychiatric settings?
