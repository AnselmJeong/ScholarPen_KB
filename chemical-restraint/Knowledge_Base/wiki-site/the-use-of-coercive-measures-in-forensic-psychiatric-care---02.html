Summary

This chapter examines the systemic neglect of forensic psychiatric patients—those involved with the criminal justice system—in human rights discourse and protections, despite their exposure to conditions that routinely violate international law. The author argues that while civil psychiatric patients benefited from post-1970s “civil rights reforms” that improved conditions and enabled advocacy, forensic patients remained largely invisible, with minimal scholarly or advocacy attention to their plight. The ratification of the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and the Convention Against Torture (CAT) presents a pivotal opportunity to address this gap, as these treaties mandate humane treatment, prohibit torture, and require state accountability—yet forensic populations are seldom considered in their application. The author identifies six interconnected factors perpetuating this invisibility: (1) near-total absence of forensic patients in CRPD/CAT scholarship; (2) globally documented “shocking” abusive conditions (e.g., overcrowding, lack of basic services, sexual abuse in Argentina; inadequate care and transfer delays in England; misuse of untrained prisoners as monitors in Hungary; Albanian prison detention without reevaluation); (3) human rights bodies ignoring plaintiffs’ forensic status in landmark cases (e.g., Victor Rosario Congo where the Inter-American Commission overlooked the forensic context); (4) severe scarcity of lawyers and advocates due to burnout, underfunding, and lack of training; (5) near-total omission from psychiatric survivor movement literature due to stigma and fear of further marginalization; and (6) particular neglect of those with intellectual disabilities in forensic settings, who face exacerbated risks due to perceived permanence of disability and unsuitable prison environments. Evidence is drawn from NGO reports (MDRI, Human Rights Watch), WHO statements, court decisions, and academic studies. The chapter concludes that exposing these conditions is essential to reducing stigma and achieving equality, and that the current state violates therapeutic jurisprudence principles by denying voice, validation, and voluntariness.

Key Concepts

  • Therapeutic jurisprudence (TJ): Theory that law should assess its impact on psychological well-being and avoid anti-therapeutic consequences while upholding due process. Matters because the author argues current forensic facility conditions violate TJ principles (e.g., lack of voice, validation) and TJ offers a lens to reform laws/settings. Not in wiki.
  • Social model of disability: Framework viewing disability as arising from societal barriers (not inherent limitations), emphasizing equality and accommodation. Matters because CRPD is said to embody this model, rejecting the medical model and reframing mental health rights as disability rights. Not in wiki.
  • Medical model of disability: Framework viewing disability as an inherent deficit requiring medical intervention/treatment. Matters as a contrast to the social model; the author notes CRPD’s repudiation of this model is key to advancing rights. Not in wiki.
  • Sanism: Irrational prejudice against people with mental disabilities, analogous to racism/sexism, leading to discriminatory attitudes (e.g., assuming dangerousness). Matters as a core driver of forensic patients’ invisibility, stigma, and exclusion from rights protections. Not in wiki.
  • Pretextuality: Tendency of courts to accept or encourage dishonest testimony (especially from expert witnesses) to justify outcomes. Matters as a barrier to justice for forensic patients, enabling misuse of expert evidence to sustain inhumane conditions without scrutiny. Not in wiki.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Forensic psychiatric patients have been systematically excluded from human rights advancements despite conditions that violate international law, and the CRPD and CAT provide a necessary framework to address this neglect.
    • Evidence: Documentation of abusive conditions in Argentina (overcrowding, lack of water/toilets, sexual abuse, long-term detention without treatment), England (lack of treatment facilities, delayed hospital transfers), Hungary (use of untrained prisoners as monitors for high suicide-risk patients), Albania (detention in prison units without reevaluation for years), and the U.S. (prolonged solitary confinement at Tamms Correctional Center); reports from MDRI, Human Rights Watch, WHO; and legal cases like Victor Rosario Congo v. Ecuador where forensic status was ignored despite clear human rights violations.
    • Strength: The evidence is robust in detailing specific, corroborated abuses across multiple jurisdictions and linking them to treaty violations. However, the argument relies on the interpretive claim that CRPD/CAT should apply to forensic settings—a point not universally settled in law—and acknowledges a lack of follow-up scholarship on this specific application, suggesting the contribution is more prophylactic than reactive to established consensus. The strength is tempered by the field’s historical neglect, which may indicate underreporting or insufficient data collection.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The author notes a tension between the CRPD’s silence on explicit mention of forensic facilities and the argument that it must be interpreted to include this population; this is resolved by invoking the CAT and broad disability rights principles, but remains a point of potential legal contestation.
  • Advocacy groups historically avoided focusing on forensic patients because doing so might reinforce the false link between mental illness and dangerousness—a tension between avoiding stigma and addressing urgent rights violations.
  • The survivor movement’s reluctance to speak out (due to fear of further stigma or learned helplessness) creates a tension between self-advocacy and self-preservation, potentially perpetuating invisibility.
  • Even when rights violations are found (e.g., in Congo), the forensic status is ignored, highlighting a tension between legal victories and their incomplete application to this subgroup.

Open Questions

  • What specific mechanisms (e.g., Optional Protocols, national legislation) could effectively enforce CRPD/CAT protections for forensic patients in jurisdictions with weak rule of law?
  • How can legal training and funding be structured to sustain a dedicated advocacy pipeline for this population, given high burnout risks?
  • What community-based alternatives exist for diverting individuals with intellectual disabilities from forensic settings, and what evidence supports their efficacy and safety?
  • To what extent does the intersection of sexism and racial bias compound disparities in forensic mental health outcomes, and how might this be addressed in treaty monitoring?
