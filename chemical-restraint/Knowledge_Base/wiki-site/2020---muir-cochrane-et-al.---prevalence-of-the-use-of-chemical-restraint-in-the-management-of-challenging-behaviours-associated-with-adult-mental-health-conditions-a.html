Summary

This study conducted a meta-synthesis of 48 studies published between 1996 and 2018 to determine the international prevalence of chemical restraint for non-consenting adults with mental health disorders exhibiting severe agitation, aggression, or violent behaviours. The research utilized data from PsycINFO, CINAHL, MEDLINE/PubMed, and Google Scholar, focusing on retrospective audits and record reviews to calculate prevalence rates. After standardizing data to a one-month time frame, the analysis found that 21.2% of all patients experienced some form of restraint (physical or chemical), and among those restrained, 43.1% received chemical restraint. When calculated against all admissions, the prevalence of chemical restraint was 7.4%. The study found no significant differences in prevalence by country or healthcare setting (emergency department vs. ward), but identified a statistically significant decline in restraint use over time, with a 22.6% decrease in chemical restraint as a proportion of admissions between 2006 and 2018. Despite this decline, the authors note substantial heterogeneity across studies (I² > 98%), indicating wide variation in practices, settings, or reporting methods that limits the generalizability of pooled estimates.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Chemical restraint is widely used internationally in emergency and psychiatric settings, with a median prevalence of 7.4% among all admitted patients, 21.2% for any restraint, and 43.1% of restrained individuals receiving chemical restraint.
    • Evidence: Analysis of 48 studies (302,602 patients over 779 months) using meta-synthesis and random-effects models. Pooled estimates: any restraint 21.6% (95% CI: 14.9–29.1), chemical restraint by admission 9.5% (95% CI: 5.8–13.9), chemical restraint among restrained 47.8% (95% CI: 35.6–60.1).
    • Strength: Strong due to large sample size, comprehensive search (1996–2018), use of validated critical appraisal (JBI), and rigorous synthesis methods. Limited by retrospective data, potential bias in record-keeping, and inconsistent definitions/methods across studies.
  • Core claim: There is a significant decline in restraint use over time (2006–2018), with a 22.6% decrease in chemical restraint as a proportion of admissions (p=0.009).
    • Evidence: Chronological analysis showing negative slope and R² value of trendline; statistically significant decrease in both general restraint and chemical restraint specifically.
    • Strength: Moderately strong due to longitudinal design across 22 years of literature, but limited by ecological fallacy risk and potential publication bias.
  • Core claim: There is no statistically significant variation in prevalence by country or healthcare setting (ED vs. ward).
    • Evidence: ANOVA models testing differences between countries and settings showed no significant differences (p = 0.24).
    • Strength: Moderately strong due to international scope, but limited by high heterogeneity potentially masking true differences.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Despite high heterogeneity (I² > 98% indicating minimal shared variance), the authors report pooled prevalence estimates which may misrepresent true variability and overstate generalizability.
  • Although use decreased over time, absolute prevalence remains substantial (e.g., 7.4% of admissions), raising questions about whether current reduction efforts are adequate or merely slowing growth.
  • Reliance on retrospective audits and record reviews may undercount events due to poor documentation, yet the study treats these as reliable sources without adjusting for potential underreporting.
  • Definition variability exists: while all definitions emphasized non-consensual medication use for behavioral control, specifics varied (e.g., some included psychological coercion), creating ambiguity in cross-study comparison.
  • Adverse events to staff were reported in 2–10% of cases but rarely described, suggesting possible underestimation of risks to healthcare workers.

Open Questions

  • What are the long-term physical and psychological effects of chemical restraint on patients, particularly regarding cognitive function and trauma?
  • How do legal frameworks and professional guidelines across countries actually influence practice patterns despite similar reported prevalence rates?
  • What specific alternatives to chemical restraint (e.g., de-escalation techniques, sensory modulation, staff training) show the greatest promise in reducing use without compromising safety in acute settings?
  • How can standardized data collection be implemented globally to enable valid cross-national comparisons given current inconsistencies in definition, measurement, and reporting?
  • What is the relationship between staffing levels, skill mix, and environmental factors in predicting the use of chemical restraint versus alternative interventions?
