Why It Matters

  • Central to ethical and legal debates in psychiatry regarding patient autonomy and human rights
  • Remains a contested practice despite international human rights frameworks rejecting it as a violation
  • Influences clinical practice, policy, and professional attitudes in mental health systems
  • Highlights tensions between beneficence, autonomy, and legal capacity in mental health care

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Human rights perspective Involuntary psychiatric interventions violate international human rights law under the CRPD; mental health must adopt a rights-based model centered on will and preferences to eliminate coercion 2019 - Puras et al. - Mental health and human rights in the 21st century
Professional attitudes Despite acknowledging coercion as a rights violation, many mental health professionals view it as indispensable and beneficial, with attitudes shaped by internal pressure, emotional impact, external pressure, and relational involvement 2021 - Morandi et al. - Mental health professionals feelings and attitudes towards coercion
Legal capacity framework The UN CRPD asserts that legal capacity must not be denied based on mental health status, requiring supported decision-making over substitute decision-making to challenge psychiatric coercion Human Rights in Psychiatry - 05
Ethical justification Coercion in psychiatry has evolved from social control to clinical-ethical frameworks but remains ethically and legally contested due to conflicting values like autonomy versus beneficence Human Rights in Psychiatry - 06
