Why It Matters

  • Central to understanding legal capacity reform under international human rights law
  • Highlights tension between autonomy and protection in mental health and disability contexts
  • Connects civil-political rights with socio-economic rights through indivisibility principle
  • Informs debates on supported vs. substitute decision-making for people with cognitive disabilities

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Supported decision-making as primary model Autonomy under CRPD Article 12 requires both internal capacities and external support, not just absence of impairment Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -04
Substituted decision-making as last resort Substitute decision-making may be permissible only when strictly necessary and as a last resort for those with severe cognitive impairment Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07
Indivisibility of rights Civil-political and socio-economic rights must be interpreted together to realize dignity and autonomy under CRPD Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -04
Dignity as foundational principle Dignity and indivisibility must guide interpretation of CRPD Article 12 to prevent paternalism and uphold personhood Mental Capacity, Dignity and the Power of International Human Rights -07
