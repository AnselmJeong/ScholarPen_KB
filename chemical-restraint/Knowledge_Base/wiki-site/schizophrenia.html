Why It Matters

  • Central focus in ethical and clinical debates about psychiatric treatment, particularly regarding decision-making capacity and involuntary care
  • Represents a key diagnostic category where coercive interventions, such as covert medication and rapid tranquillisation, are frequently considered
  • Highlights tensions between beneficence and autonomy in psychiatric ethics, especially in cases of noncompliance and impaired insight
  • Serves as a focal point for examining the real-world application of ethical frameworks in managing psychosis and behavioral disturbances

Key Positions

Position Core claim Key sources
Ethical justification of covert antipsychotic use Covert administration may reduce harmful behaviors (e.g., foreign object ingestion) in patients with partial decision-making capacity, despite failing to control core psychosis and risking side effects 2024 - Razzak et al. - Ethical Dilemmas in Covert Prescribing A Case Report on Medication Administration for Noncompliant Psychiatric Patients
Historical context of rapid tranquillisation Rapid tranquillisation emerged alongside antipsychotic development as a response to acute agitation, reflecting evolving practices in managing psychiatric emergencies 2014 - Allison, Moncrieff - ‘Rapid tranquillisation’; an historical perspective on its emergence in the context of the development of antipsychotic medications

Open questions

  • Under what conditions, if any, is covert medication ethically permissible in schizophrenia when it addresses behavioral risks but not psychotic symptoms?
  • How should clinicians balance historical practices of rapid tranquillisation with contemporary standards of consent and least restrictive alternatives?
  • What safeguards are needed to prevent misuse of pharmacological interventions in schizophrenia while preserving therapeutic intent?
  • How can decision-making capacity be assessed fairly in schizophrenia when insight into illness is often impaired by the condition itself?
