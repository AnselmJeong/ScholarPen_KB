Summary

This study compared coercive practices across nine high-income countries (Australia, England, Germany, Ireland, Japan, Netherlands, New Zealand, USA, Wales) using data from WHO, OECD, and national sources. Despite significant variations in how countries define and report restrictive practices—such as seclusion, physical, mechanical, and chemical restraint—the researchers standardized metrics by normalizing for population and using duration data to convert between event counts and time-based measures. The analysis revealed 10- to 100-fold differences in key indicators like mechanical restraints per 100,000 population per year and restrictive practice incidents (RPI) per 1 million population per day, with Japan consistently showing the highest rates across most metrics. Involuntary hospital admission showed less variation (median 41%) and was treated separately due to its distinct ethical and legal character. The findings suggest that cross-national comparison is feasible despite reporting inconsistencies, and that cultural or legal norms—not just clinical need—likely drive national differences in coercion use.

Key Entities

  • World Health Organization (WHO) (organization) — Provider of the Mental Health Atlas dataset, which currently only tracks involuntary hospital admissions as a proxy for coercion; the study recommends expanding it to include comprehensive restrictive practice metrics.
  • Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) (organization) — Supplier of benchmark reports and comparative health data used to contextualize coercion rates alongside indicators like mental health spending and workforce density.
  • Mental Health Atlas (dataset) — WHO’s global compilation of psychiatric practice data; identified as insufficient for coercion monitoring because it omits seclusion and restraint, capturing only involuntary admissions.
  • Six Core Strategies (framework) — Referenced as an evidence-based framework for reducing coercion through debriefing, leadership involvement, and data-driven practice change; cited in implications for monitoring and intervention.

Key Concepts

  • Restrictive Practice Incidents (RPI) — A composite measure combining seclusion, physical, mechanical, and chemical restraint (excluding involuntary admission) to enable cross-national comparison despite differing national reporting scopes.
  • Standardization of Measures — The process of converting disparate national metrics (e.g., event counts, bed-days, hours) into comparable units (e.g., per 100,000 population/year) using population normalization and duration assumptions, essential for valid cross-country analysis.
  • Duration Skew — The statistical property where many restraint events are brief but a few are prolonged, causing the mean duration to greatly exceed the median; necessitates using mean (not median) for converting event counts to time-based metrics like hours of restraint per 1000 hours of care.
  • Benchmarking — The use of international data to identify performance gaps and advocate for change, analogous to how educational attainment reports inform policy; positioned as a tool to drive reduction in coercive practices.
  • Cultural/Legal Determinants of Coercion — The hypothesis that national norms (e.g., Japan’s justification of coercion as protective care) explain variation in practice more than clinical demand or reporting artifacts alone.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Valid cross-national comparisons of coercive practices (seclusion, restraint) are achievable through standardization, revealing 10- to 100-fold variations in use among high-income countries, with Japan exhibiting exceptionally high rates.
    • Evidence: Harmonized data from nine OECD countries showed metrics like mechanical restraints per 100,000/year ranged from 0.72 (New Zealand) to 260 (Japan), and RPI per 1 million/day varied from 37 to 2,100. These extremes persisted even when focusing on conservative thresholds (>10x differences), reducing likelihood of artifact from reporting inconsistency.
    • Strength: High—multi-source triangulation (WHO, OECD, national datasets), explicit documentation of assumptions, sensitivity testing (e.g., focusing on large-magnitude differences), and acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., uncertainty in US/Japan estimations).
  • Core claim: Involuntary hospital admission is a relatively stable indicator (41% median rate) but insufficient as a standalone proxy for overall coercion use due to its distinct ethical/legal nature and poor correlation with restrictive practice variation.
    • Evidence: Involuntary admission rates varied only 2–4x across countries (vs. 10–100x for RPI), and Japan showed high RPI despite only reporting two restraint types, suggesting underreporting or substitution effects.
    • Strength: Moderate—consistent with WHO data and prior studies, but limited by inability to disentangle admission practices from broader coercion patterns.

Connections

  • mental-health-atlas — This study directly critiques the WHO Mental Health Atlas for tracking only involuntary admissions and recommends expanding it to include comprehensive restrictive practice metrics (seclusion, restraint) to enable global monitoring.
  • involuntary-hospital-admission — The source treats involuntary admission as distinct from restrictive practice incidents (RPI) in both ethical character and analytical treatment, noting its lower variability and different drivers compared to seclusion and restraint metrics.
  • benchmarking-in-healthcare — The study frames cross-national coercion comparison as analogous to educational or health benchmarking, advocating for its use to identify best practices and drive policy change, consistent with OECD and WHO approaches to health system evaluation.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The study contradicts the assumption that coercion rates are broadly similar across high-income nations, demonstrating instead extreme (10–100x) variation in restrictive practices that cannot be explained by reporting differences alone.
  • It challenges the sufficiency of the WHO Mental Health Atlas for coercion monitoring, showing that relying solely on involuntary admission overlooks the majority of variation in seclusion and restraint use.
  • Internal tension exists in Japan’s data: despite reporting only two restraint types (seclusion, mechanical), it has the highest total RPI, suggesting either undercount in other categories or cultural acceptance of specific forms; additionally, the extreme skew in duration (mean 30.5 days vs. median 2 days for mechanical restraint) complicates interpretation, as mean values are necessary for time-based conversions but may overrepresent typical experience.

Open Questions

  • How do specific legal frameworks (e.g., Japan’s emergency admission and guardianship laws) directly enable or constrain the use of seclusion and mechanical restraint, and how might reform in these areas affect overall coercion rates?
  • Can reductions in one coercion type (e.g., seclusion) lead to compensatory increases in others (e.g., mechanical restraint or medication), and what monitoring systems are needed to detect such substitution?
  • What are the systemic barriers (e.g., legal, infrastructural, cultural) to implementing standardized restrictive practice monitoring in low- and middle-income countries, given that current evidence and methods are derived almost exclusively from high-income contexts?
