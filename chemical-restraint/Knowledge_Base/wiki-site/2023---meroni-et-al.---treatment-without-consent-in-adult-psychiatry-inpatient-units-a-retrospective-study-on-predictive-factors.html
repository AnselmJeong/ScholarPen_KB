Summary

This study investigates predictors of non-emergency forced medication under Swiss Civil Code Article 434 in adult psychiatry inpatient units at Geneva University Hospitals between 2018 and 2021. Researchers conducted a retrospective analysis of 4,326 patients, identifying 71 individuals (1.64%) who received repeated medication without consent for psychiatric treatment outside emergency situations. The study examined demographic and clinical variables including age, gender, admission status, primary diagnosis, and symptom burden measured by the Health of the Nation Outcome Scales (HoNOS). Univariate and multivariate analyses revealed that female gender, psychotic disorder diagnosis, and involuntary admission were significant predictors of Article 434 medication, while neither global HoNOS scores nor aggression-specific items were predictive. The primary justification for forced medication was risk of self-endangerment due to psychosocial deterioration (53.5% of cases), challenging assumptions that aggression drives most psychiatric coercion.

Key Entities

  • Geneva University Hospitals (org): The healthcare institution where the study was conducted, providing the clinical setting and patient data for the retrospective analysis.
  • Health of the Nation Outcome Scales (HoNOS) (tool): A 12-item clinical scale used to assess mental health and social/behavioral functioning, employed in this study to measure symptom burden and aggression levels at admission.

Key Concepts

  • Forced medication under Article 434 — Repeated administration of psychiatric medication without patient consent for treating psychosis in non-emergency situations, subject to potential appeal to civil court under Swiss law.
  • Psychotic disorder — A category of mental illness characterized by psychosis (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar with psychotic features), identified as a strong predictor of non-emergency forced medication in this study.
  • Involuntary admission — Hospital admission without patient consent or legal authorization, found to be a major predictor of subsequent forced medication under Article 434.
  • Psychosocial deterioration — Decline in social and psychological functioning due to illness, cited as the primary justification (53.5%) for forced medication under Article 434, particularly in patients with poor insight from psychosis.
  • HoNOS item 1 — The specific item measuring aggressive behavior within the Health of the Nation Outcome Scales, which showed lower scores in the forced medication group, indicating aggression is not a driver of Article 434 interventions.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Female gender, psychotic disorder diagnosis, and involuntary admission are significant predictors of forced medication under Article 434, while symptom burden and aggression are not predictive.
    • Evidence: Multivariate logistic regression showed females had 2.22 times higher odds (OR 2.22, 95% CI 1.31–3.74), psychotic disorder patients had 7.84 times higher odds (OR 7.84, 95% CI 3.71–16.55), and involuntarily admitted patients had 10.95 times higher odds (OR 10.95, 95% CI 4.64–25.82) of receiving Article 434 medication. HoNOS global score and item 1 (aggression) were non-significant.
    • Strength: Moderately strong due to adequate sample size (n=4,326) and multivariable control for confounders; limited by single-center design, retrospective nature, and low base rate (1.64%) increasing uncertainty in estimates.

Connections

  • psychotic-disorder — This study identifies psychotic disorder as a key predictor (OR 7.84) of non-emergency forced medication, reinforcing its role in coercive psychiatric practices.
  • involuntary-admission — Involuntary admission shows the strongest association with Article 434 medication (OR 10.95), indicating it is both a precursor and risk factor for prolonged coercive treatment.
  • gender-disparity-in-healthcare — Findings reveal elevated risk for women with psychosis, contributing to evidence on gender-specific patterns in psychiatric coercion.
  • patient-autonomy — The study highlights how lack of insight in psychosis undermines consent processes, raising concerns about autonomy in Article 434 applications.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Despite lower aggression scores in the forced medication group, seclusion (typically used for aggression control) was still applied in 35.2% of cases, suggesting overlapping but distinct pathways for different coercive measures.
  • Contrasts with literature linking forced treatment primarily to hetero-aggressive behavior; this study finds self-endangerment (53.5%) as the most frequent justification, especially in women with psychosis, indicating divergent clinical rationales.
  • The high rate of unsuccessful appeals to civil court (84.4% rejected) contrasts with the legal intent of Article 434 to provide patient recourse, suggesting potential gaps in rights protection during psychiatric crises.

Open Questions

  • How do patients experience and perceive forced medication under Article 434, particularly regarding their sense of autonomy and therapeutic alliance?
  • To what extent do advance directives, joint crisis plans, or supported decision-making tools reduce the use of Article 434 medication by aligning treatment with patient preferences?
  • What are the long-term clinical and social outcomes for patients subjected to Article 434 medication after discharge, including relapse rates, recovery, and subjective well-being?
