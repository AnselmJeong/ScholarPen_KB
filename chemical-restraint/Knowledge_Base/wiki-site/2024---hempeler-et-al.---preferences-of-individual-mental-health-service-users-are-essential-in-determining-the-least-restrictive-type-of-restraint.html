Summary

This commentary argues against rigid ethical hierarchies that prioritize chemical restraint over physical restraint in psychiatric settings, asserting that such rankings fail to account for divergent conceptions of basic liberty and individual patient preferences. Drawing on legal precedents from the German Federal Constitutional Court—which prioritizes bodily integrity over freedom of movement, leading to the conclusion that physical restraint may be less restrictive than involuntary medication—the authors demonstrate that ethical evaluations of restraint must be context-sensitive. Empirical evidence shows substantial variability in patient preferences regarding restraint types, with 36% of psychiatric advance directives specifying preferences for coercive measures. The authors advocate for psychiatric advance directives as a key mechanism to honor autonomy when decision-making capacity is impaired, and emphasize substituted judgment and phenomenological experience as critical factors in ethical decision-making, particularly when informed consent is unobtainable during acute psychiatric crises.

Key Concepts

  • Least restrictive alternative — Ethical principle requiring the use of the liberty-infringing intervention that least impedes freedom while achieving clinical goals; contested due to varying conceptions of liberty.
  • Basic liberty — Freedom required for living the life of a free person; interpreted differently across jurisdictions (e.g., bodily integrity vs. freedom of movement), leading to opposing conclusions about restrictiveness of restraint types.
  • Informed consent — Voluntary agreement to intervention after understanding risks/benefits; often unobtainable in acute psychosis, challenging its applicability in crisis interventions.
  • Decision-making capacity — Cognitive ability to understand, appreciate, reason, and communicate choices; frequently compromised by sedating medications used as chemical restraints.
  • Psychiatric advance directives — Documents allowing individuals to state treatment preferences for future mental health crises; presented as a mechanism to uphold autonomy when capacity is impaired, with 36% including preferences on coercive measures.
  • Substituted judgment — Surrogate decision-making based on what the patient would have chosen if competent; proposed as ethically preferable to emergency exemptions when consent cannot be obtained.
  • Proportionality — Ethical approach weighing risks and benefits of options rather than applying rigid hierarchies; suggested as superior to fixed rankings of restraint types.
  • Chemical restraint — Medication used to control acute agitation/aggression (e.g., benzodiazepines, antipsychotics); distinguished by its effect on consciousness level, with implications for ethical evaluation.
  • Physical restraint — Physical or mechanical restriction of movement (holding, straps) but not seclusion; defended by some as less invasive based on bodily integrity concerns.
  • Phenomenology of restraint — Subjective experience of coercive interventions (e.g., trauma from injections); argued as ethically significant but often overlooked in principle-based approaches.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Ethical decisions about restraint in psychiatry must be guided by individual service users’ preferences, not rigid hierarchies (e.g., favoring chemical over physical restraint).
    • Evidence: Conceptual analysis shows that differing conceptions of basic liberty (e.g., German Federal Constitutional Court prioritizing bodily integrity) lead to opposing conclusions about which restraint is less restrictive; empirical data reveal high variability in patient preferences (Georgieva et al., 2012) and that 36% of psychiatric advance directives include preferences on coercive measures (Gaillard et al., 2023).
    • Strength: Strong conceptual and empirical support; acknowledges low completion rates of advance directives but argues they remain valuable decision-making tools despite implementation challenges.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Internal tension: Acknowledges low real-world completion rates of psychiatric advance directives but still advocates for their use, creating a gap between ideal and feasibility.
  • Caveat: The distinction between conscious-state-altering vs. consciousness-impairing chemical restraints, while analytically useful, may not align clearly with clinical classifications or prescribing practices.

Open Questions

  • How can psychiatric advance directive uptake be improved in real-world mental health care settings?
  • How should conflicting treatment preferences (e.g., between patient, family, and clinicians) be resolved in emergency situations?
  • Is there empirical evidence that using substituted judgment in restraint decisions leads to better clinical or ethical outcomes?
  • How do cultural and legal differences in conceptions of basic liberty affect the application of least restrictive standards across jurisdictions?
