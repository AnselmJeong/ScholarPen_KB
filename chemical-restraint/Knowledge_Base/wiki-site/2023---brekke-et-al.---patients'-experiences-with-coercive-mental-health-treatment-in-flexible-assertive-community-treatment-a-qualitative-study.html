Summary

This qualitative study explored how service users experience coercion within Flexible Assertive Community Treatment (FACT) teams in Norway compared to prior treatment contexts. Using a participatory approach, researchers conducted 24 semi-structured interviews with individuals who had experienced coercion and were engaged with five different FACT teams. Thematic analysis revealed three distinct patterns of experience: some participants viewed FACT as a clear improvement offering greater autonomy and less top-down control; others described “making the best of FACT” by complying to avoid worse outcomes like forced medication; and a third group found coercion equally distressing in FACT as before, particularly due to forced medication and systemic distrust. Across narratives, participants identified feeling safe (through accessible crisis support), improved treatment quality (via team collaboration and tailored medication), and increased participation in care planning as key factors that reduced coercive experiences.

Key Entities

  • Flexible Assertive Community Treatment (FACT) (intervention/model): Community-based mental health service model providing flexible, intensive support to individuals with severe mental illness, often including substance use comorbidities, with goals of reducing coercion through recovery-oriented, person-centred care.
  • Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) (intervention/model): Predecessor model to FACT, providing intensive community-based mental health services; referenced for contextual comparison.
  • Community Treatment Order (CTO) (policy/legal mechanism): Legal mechanism for compulsory outpatient treatment in Norway, under which some participants received medication or supervision prior to or alongside FACT engagement.
  • Norwegian Mental Health Care Act (1999) (law/regulation): National legislation governing the use of coercion in Norwegian mental health services, cited as the legal framework for formal coercion.
  • Norwegian National Advisory Unit on Concurrent Substance Abuse and Mental Health Disorders (organization): Affiliation of the corresponding author; provided research context and support.
  • Akershus University Hospital (organization): Affiliation of one author; contributed to researcher expertise and recruitment context.

Key Concepts

  • Coercion (in mental health) — Use of force, threats, or pressure to compel treatment acceptance, including formal (legally sanctioned) and informal (social/relational) mechanisms such as persuasion or withdrawal of benefits.
  • Formal coercion — Legally authorized interventions such as involuntary admission, medication, or seclusion under mental health legislation like the Norwegian Mental Health Care Act.
  • Informal coercion — Non-legal pressures to comply with treatment, including persuasion, emotional pressure, or conditional access to resources, which can erode trust and autonomy.
  • Patient experience — First-person accounts of receiving mental health services, focusing on lived realities of autonomy, dignity, control, and emotional impact, central to the study’s qualitative methodology.
  • Recovery-oriented care — Approach emphasizing personal agency, hope, and meaningful life beyond symptom reduction, positioned as a core principle of FACT that may counteract coercion.
  • Person-centred care — Model prioritizing individual preferences, values, and lived experience in care planning, cited as a component of FACT designed to increase participation and reduce power imbalances.
  • Safety (as a preventive factor) — Perception of emotional and physical security, reliability of support, and accessibility of help during crises, described by participants as reducing fear and preventing crisis escalation that could trigger coercion.
  • Participation (in treatment) — Active involvement in decision-making, treatment planning, and self-management (e.g., crisis planning, psychoeducation), described as enhancing agency and reducing reliance on coercive measures.
  • Trust in providers — Confidence in clinicians’ intentions, competence, and respect for autonomy, described as fragile and easily broken by coercive experiences but potentially rebuilt through consistent, respectful engagement in FACT.
  • Shared decision-making — Collaborative process where clinicians and patients jointly make treatment choices based on evidence and patient preferences, echoed in participants’ descriptions of increased involvement in medication discussions.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Service users’ experiences of coercion in FACT vary, but many report improvement over prior treatment; key mechanisms that reduce coercion are safety, improved quality of treatment, and increased participation.
    • Evidence: Thematic analysis of 24 qualitative interviews revealed three experiential patterns: (1) FACT as clearly better (more autonomy, less control), (2) making the best of FACT (compliance to avoid worse outcomes), and (3) coercion just as bad as before (especially due to forced medication and distrust). Participants cited feeling safe via crisis access, better medication management through team collaboration, and involvement in care planning as protective factors.
    • Strength: Strong within qualitative methodology—rich, diverse accounts from individuals with lived experience across multiple geographic contexts; enhanced by participatory design and researcher with lived experience. Limitations include lack of generalizability, no direct observation of coercion events, and reliance on self-report. Findings are credible and consistent with prior research on ACT and CTOs, suggesting FACT may reduce—but not eliminate—coercive experiences.

Connections

  • concepts/coercion — This study provides empirical evidence on how coercion is experienced in community-based models, reinforcing that coercion remains a harmful phenomenon even in reform-oriented systems.
  • concepts/coercive-treatment — Findings support the view that coercive treatment undermines autonomy and dignity, aligning with critiques of coercion as incompatible with human rights.
  • concepts/community-treatment-order — Participants’ prior experiences with CTOs informed their perceptions; the study positions FACT as an evolution aiming to improve the experience of community-based compulsory care.
  • concepts/recovery-oriented-care and concepts/person-centered-care — FACT’s alignment with these models is presented as reducing coercion by fostering autonomy and engagement.
  • concepts/shared-decision-making and concepts/supported-decision-making — Participation mechanisms described by patients reflect these principles, suggesting they may mitigate coercion when implemented authentically.
  • concepts/patient-experience — The study exemplifies centering patient voices to understand the real-world impact of service design on coercion and recovery.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While FACT is intended to reduce coercion, some users still experienced it as harmful—especially regarding forced medication—indicating that structural and legal factors (e.g., CTOs, medication laws) can persist within flexible models, creating tension between reform goals and enduring coercive practices.
  • Trust is fragile: prior coercive experiences lead to ongoing fear and suspicion, even when current care is supportive, highlighting that rebuilding trust requires sustained, transparent engagement over time.
  • The “making the best of FACT” pattern reveals a tension between autonomy and pragmatism—some users comply not from consent but to avoid worse outcomes like forced medication, raising questions about the voluntariness of consent under conditions of limited alternatives.

Open Questions

  • How can FACT teams better address power imbalances related to medication decisions, especially when psychiatrists are not embedded in the team or when patients distrust psychiatric authority due to past trauma?
  • What specific training, supervision, or organizational supports help FACT staff avoid reverting to biomedical or paternalistic practices that undermine the model’s recovery-oriented intentions?
  • Can FACT reduce reliance on CTOs and forced medication over time, or does it primarily make existing coercive practices more tolerable without addressing root causes of coercion?
