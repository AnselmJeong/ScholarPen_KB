Summary

This systematic review analyzed 311 primary studies published between 1996 and 2018 on the use of chemical restraint (CR)—defined as the non-consenting administration of medication to manage acute aggression, agitation, or violence in adults with mental health conditions. The review employed PRISMA guidelines and was registered in PROSPERO (CRD 42017055258), searching PsycINFO, CINAHL, MEDLINE/PubMed, and Google Scholar for English-language peer-reviewed research. Studies were excluded if they involved minors, dementia-related agitation, forensic settings, or consenting treatment. The majority of research originated from the USA (25.7%), UK (18.6%), and Australia (11.3%), with only 5.8% involving international collaboration. Most studies were conducted in acute psychiatric wards (23.3%), general psychiatric wards (21.6%), and emergency departments (19.1%). The dominant research theme was medication effectiveness and safety (30.2% of studies), followed by patient perspectives (12.2%) and ward policies (11.6%). Despite increasing methodological sophistication over time, core research questions remained largely unchanged, with over 60 distinct outcome measures indicating a lack of standardization. The average publication lag was 3.4 years, longest for qualitative studies.

Key Entities

  • PRISMA framework (tool) — guided the systematic review methodology for study selection, data extraction, and reporting.
  • PROSPERO (tool) — hosted the protocol registration (ID: CRD 42017055258) ensuring transparency and reducing duplication.
  • NHMRC hierarchy of evidence (tool) — used to classify study designs by methodological rigor, ranging from Level I (systematic reviews/RCTs) to Level IV (expert opinion).
  • PsycINFO (tool) — one of the four databases searched for psychological and behavioral literature on chemical restraint.
  • CINAHL (tool) — database searched for nursing and allied health literature relevant to restraint practices in clinical settings.
  • MEDLINE/PubMed (tool) — major biomedical database searched for clinical studies on medication use in psychiatric emergencies.
  • Google Scholar (tool) — supplemented formal database searches to capture grey literature and additional peer-reviewed sources.

Key Concepts

  • Chemical restraint — the forced (non-consenting) use of medications to sedate individuals deemed at risk of harming themselves or others due to acute agitation or violence; synonymous with rapid tranquillisation.
  • Non-consent — administration of medication without patient agreement, often due to impaired decision-making capacity or emergency necessity; a core inclusion criterion for this review.
  • Rapid tranquillisation — urgent pharmacological intervention to achieve rapid sedation in agitated individuals; used interchangeably with chemical restraint in the literature.
  • Hierarchy of evidence — framework ranking research by validity (e.g., RCTs at Level II, expert opinion at Level IV); used to categorize the 311 studies by design rigor.
  • Seclusion — isolation of a patient in a locked room from which they cannot exit; frequently contrasted with or used alongside chemical restraint.
  • Physical restraint — application of physical force or mechanical devices to restrict patient movement; often considered part of restrictive practices with chemical restraint.
  • Aggressive behaviors — actions posing imminent risk of harm to self or others; the primary behavioral trigger for chemical restraint use in clinical settings.
  • Acute psychiatric wards — inpatient hospital units providing short-term, intensive mental health care; the most common setting for chemical restraint research (23.3%).
  • Emergency departments (ED) — hospital units for urgent medical and psychiatric evaluation; a major setting for chemical restraint research (19.1%), reflecting crisis use.
  • Adverse events — harmful physiological or psychological effects resulting from medication (e.g., respiratory depression, injury); a critical outcome in assessing the risk-benefit balance of chemical restraint.
  • Patient perspectives — firsthand accounts and experiences of individuals subjected to chemical restraint; represented in 31 qualitative and 22 other studies, highlighting human rights and dignity concerns.
  • Ward policies — institutional rules governing the use of restraints (e.g., token economies, open vs. closed wards); studied across multiple methodologies showing organizational influence on practice.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The volume of research on chemical restraint is substantial (311 studies over 22 years), but disproportionately focused on medication efficacy and safety rather than ethics, implementation, or alternatives.
    • Evidence: 30.2% of studies (94/311) examined medication effectiveness and safety; only 12.2% focused on patient perspectives and 11.6% on ward policies.
    • Strength: Strongly supported by thematic analysis across evidence hierarchies; limited by potential underrepresentation of qualitative insights due to publication lag and language restrictions.
  • Core claim: Research is geographically concentrated, with the USA, UK, and Australia producing over half of all studies, and minimal international collaboration.
    • Evidence: 25.7% from USA, 18.6% from UK, 11.3% from Australia; only 5.8% of studies involved multi-country collaboration.
    • Strength: Well-supported by country-level publication counts; limited by possible indexing bias toward English-language journals.
  • Core mark: Outcome measures lack standardization, with over 60 distinct measures grouped into eight categories, hindering cross-study comparison and meta-analysis.
    • Evidence: Explicit enumeration of 60+ measures categorized into sedation, aggression scales, adverse events, etc.; noted in Discussion as a barrier to progress.
    • Strength: Directly documented in results; limitation is descriptive rather than prescriptive—no intervention proposed.
  • Core claim: There is a significant delay between data collection and publication (average 3.4 years), longest for qualitative studies, risking obsolescence of findings.
    • Evidence: Reported in Section 3.6 with standard deviation (SD 2.5); Figure 4 shows qualitative studies take longest to publish.
    • Strength: Empirically measured; limitation is that delay may reflect deeper peer-review challenges rather than researcher delay alone.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The literature reveals persistent tension between the perceived efficacy of chemical restraint in rapidly reducing agitation and the risk of serious adverse events, including respiratory compromise and injury, particularly in vulnerable populations.
  • Researchers and clinicians grapple with determining when chemical restraint is clinically necessary versus when de-escalation, environmental modification, or staff training could suffice, reflecting uncertainty in thresholds for use.
  • Ethical tensions endure between upholding patient dignity, autonomy, and freedom from coercion, and the duty of care to prevent harm to self, others, or staff in high-risk behavioral emergencies.
  • These tensions are not contradictions within the study but reflect enduring debates in the field that the research documents rather than resolves.

Open Questions

  • How do legal frameworks and clinical guidelines governing chemical restraint vary across high-income countries (e.g., United States, United Kingdom, Australia, Canada), and to what extent do these differences influence research priorities and clinical practice?
  • What is the comparative effectiveness and safety of non-coercive alternatives (e.g., sensory modulation, peer-led de-escalation, specialized training) versus chemical restraint in reducing aggression and injury in psychiatric emergency settings?
  • To what extent do systemic factors such as institutional racism, gender bias, or socioeconomic status influence disparities in the administration of chemical restraint, particularly in multicultural urban healthcare settings?
