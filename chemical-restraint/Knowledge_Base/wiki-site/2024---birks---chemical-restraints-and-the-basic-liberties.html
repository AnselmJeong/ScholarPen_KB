Summary

This commentary challenges Crutchfield and Redinger’s (2024) hierarchy of chemical restraints, which holds that only interventions rendering a person unconscious violate basic liberty, while those altering conscious states without unconsciousness do not. Drawing on philosophical theories of liberty (particularly Pettit’s concept of “dominating control”), the author argues that intentionally altering another person’s desires or mental states—such as changing their goals or values—may constitute a deeper infringement on basic liberty than mere unconsciousness. The piece uses a thought experiment involving two individuals, Bernie and Claire, to illustrate that having one’s desires manipulated by another agent represents a loss of autonomy more fundamental than temporary loss of consciousness. The author distinguishes between the capacity to control one’s mental states and freedom from external determination of those states, suggesting that even when individuals cannot self-regulate agitation (e.g., due to mental illness), they retain an interest in not having their mental states intentionally altered by others. The commentary calls for further ethical scrutiny of neurointerventions that modify cognition or affect, arguing they may undermine the liberty to live as a free person even without impairing consciousness.

Key Concepts

  • Basic liberty — A liberty required for living the life of a free person; central to the debate on whether certain restraints infringe upon fundamental freedoms
  • Mental self-determination — The ability to determine one’s own mental states without external interference; distinguished from mere capacity to control mental states
  • Dominating control — Control over another’s choices that undermines freedom; referenced from Pettit (2008) as relevant to cases where desires are altered

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Altering a person’s conscious state (e.g., changing desires) may constitute a greater infringement on basic liberty than rendering them unconscious, challenging Crutchfield and Redinger’s hierarchy of restraints.
    • Evidence: Philosophical thought experiment involving “Aaron” altering competitors’ desires vs. rendering unconscious; reference to Pettit’s concept of “dominating control”; distinction between capacity to control mental states and freedom from external determination of those states.
    • Strength: Moderate; relies on philosophical intuition and conceptual distinction rather than empirical data, but builds on established theories of liberty (Pettit, Rawls) and connects to neuroethics literature.
  • Core claim: Even when individuals cannot control their mental states (e.g., due to agitation), they retain an interest in freedom from external determination of those states.
    • Evidence: Argument that lack of self-control does not negate the interest in not being intentionally manipulated by others; distinction between inability to self-determine and illegitimate external interference.
    • Strength: Moderate; grounded in philosophical reasoning about autonomy and liberty, though not empirically validated in clinical contexts.
  • Core claim: The distinction between merely altering consciousness and undermining consciousness may be ethically misleading, as the former could be more problematic in some cases.
    • Evidence: Critique of prevailing views in neuroethics that prioritize consciousness status; reference to the mental integrity objection to neurointerventions.
    • Strength: Moderate; challenges assumptions in neuroethics and neuroethics literature but lacks direct empirical support.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Contradiction with source: Directly opposes Crutchfield and Redinger (2024)'s claim that altering consciousness without unconsciousness does not infringe basic liberty.
  • Internal tension: Acknowledges that people often lack control over their mental states (e.g., agitation not chosen), yet argues we still have an interest in freedom from external determination of those states—reconciling these requires distinguishing between inability to self-determine and illegitimate external interference.
  • Broader tension: Challenges prevailing views in neuroethics that distinguish between “merely” altering consciousness and undermining consciousness, suggesting the former may be more problematic in some cases.

Open Questions

  • How should mental self-determination be balanced against therapeutic necessity in psychiatric emergencies?
  • What empirical evidence exists regarding patient experiences of desire alteration vs. unconsciousness under chemical restraint?
  • How do legal frameworks (e.g., UN CRPD) address non-consciousness-altering neurointerventions?
  • Can the concept of mental self-determination be operationalized for clinical or legal assessment of coercive interventions?
