Summary

This review article examines the frequent conflation of agitation and aggression in clinical and research settings, arguing that these are distinct behavioral syndromes requiring separate assessment. The authors analyze commonly used measurement scales—including the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS), Neuropsychiatric Inventory (NPI), and Cohen-Mansfield Agitation Inventory (CMAI)—and demonstrate how these tools often combine agitation and aggression indicators in single items or subscales (e.g., NPI’s “agitation/aggression” item, PANSS Excited Component). This conflation obscures differential treatment effects, as a medication may effectively target one syndrome but not the other, leading to misleading null results in clinical trials when only combined scores are used. The paper emphasizes that agitation is defined as excessive motor or verbal activity without focus or intent, while aggression involves behavior intended to cause harm, and highlights clinical observations showing that these syndromes can occur independently (e.g., pure agitation in dementia, reactive aggression triggered by resistance to care without agitation). The authors advocate for using scales that clearly separate these constructs (such as those listed in Table 2) and recommend distinct outcome measures for agitation and aggression in treatment studies, particularly for regulatory submissions to bodies like the FDA.

Key Entities

  • Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS) (tool): A widely used psychiatric rating scale whose Excited Component (PANSS–EC) conflates agitation and aggression by combining Excitement, Hostility, Tension, Uncooperativeness, and Poor Impulse Control items, limiting its specificity for measuring pure agitation.
  • Neuropsychiatric Inventory (NPI) (tool): Considered the gold standard for assessing behavioral symptoms in dementia, but its single “agitation/aggression” item primarily measures resistance to care (which may lead to reactive aggression) rather than agitation, reducing its validity for assessing pure agitation.
  • Cohen–Mansfield Agitation Inventory (CMAI) (tool): A dementia-specific scale that factors into physically nonaggressive behavior, verbally agitated behavior, and aggressive behavior subscales, allowing some separation of agitation and aggression components.
  • Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale (BPRS) (tool): An older psychiatric scale that includes distinct items for tension/excitation (agitation-related) and hostility/uncooperativeness (aggression-related), enabling better separation of the two syndromes.
  • Neurobehavioral Rating Scale (NRS) (tool): A scale that separately assesses agitation (motor overactivity) and disinhibition (including aggression), though less commonly used in current research.

Key Concepts

  • Agitation — Defined as excessive motor or verbal activity without any focus or intent, representing non-directed behavioral excess commonly seen in dementia and psychosis.
  • Aggression — Defined as provoked or unprovoked behavior intended to cause harm, encompassing verbal and physical acts directed toward others or objects, often arising from perceived threats (reactive aggression) or goal-directed planning (proactive aggression).
  • Distinction between agitation and aggression — The core conceptual argument that agitation (non-directed activity) and aggression (harm-intended behavior) are separate syndromes with different etiologies, correlates, and treatment responses, necessitating independent measurement in research and clinical practice.
  • Resistance to care — A defensive behavioral reaction (particularly in dementia) that is frequently mischaracterized as agitation or aggression but actually reflects a response to perceived threats during caregiving, often precipitating reactive aggression.
  • Behavioral symptom measurement — The broader challenge of accurately assessing neuropsychiatric symptoms, where scale construction must align with clear conceptual definitions to avoid conflating distinct constructs like agitation and aggression.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Agitation and aggression are two distinct syndromes that are frequently conflated in clinical practice and research due to inconsistent terminology and measurement scales that combine them (e.g., NPI’s “agitation/aggression” item, PANSS–EC, CMAI factors). This conflation hinders accurate assessment of treatment effectiveness, particularly in drug trials, because a medication may treat one syndrome but not the other, leading to misleading or negative trial results when only a combined score is used.
    • Evidence: The authors support this by analyzing definitions of agitation and aggression, reviewing commonly used scales (PANSS, NPI, CMAI) and their item composition, highlighting how scales like PANSS–EC and NPI merge agitation and aggression indicators. They cite clinical observations (e.g., agitation without aggression in dementia, aggression triggered by rejection of care without agitation) and note that agitation may persist across dementia severity while aggression emerges in moderate dementia and increases with communication deficits. They reference studies showing that interventions like meaningful activities reduce agitation, while modifying care procedures (e.g., bed baths) reduce reactive aggression.
    • Strength: Strong. The argument is grounded in clear conceptual definitions, empirical scale analysis, clinical observations, and references to treatment response patterns. The distinction is further supported by differential responses to interventions and the need for separate outcome measures in regulatory trials (FDA).

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The paper acknowledges that agitation and aggression can co-occur or escalate from one to the other (e.g., severe agitation leading to aggression), and share risk factors like depression, creating complexity in real-world clinical presentation despite the conceptual distinction. This tension does not invalidate the need for separate measurement but acknowledges that behavioral syndromes may interact dynamically.

Open Questions

  • How should clinical guidelines and regulatory frameworks evolve to require separate assessment of agitation and aggression in neuropsychiatric treatment trials, particularly for drug approval studies?
  • What are the barriers to adopting distinct measurement scales for agitation and aggression in routine clinical practice despite their demonstrated utility in research settings?
