Summary

This qualitative study evaluated the impact of a service user-led educational program—the World of Difference program—on psychiatry residents at the University of Otago in New Zealand. The intervention, grounded in the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and the World Health Organization’s QualityRights initiative, aimed to reduce coercive practices such as seclusion, restraint, and substitute decision-making by promoting supported decision-making and human rights principles. Using focus groups with 11 residents five months after the intervention, researchers identified four key themes: a paradigm shift in thinking toward recovery-oriented care, strong initial intentions to reduce coercion, pervasive experiences of powerlessness within hierarchical healthcare systems, and frustration at being unable to enact change due to systemic constraints like time pressures, nursing influence, and institutional culture. While residents reported increased knowledge and motivation to adopt less restrictive practices, they consistently described feeling like “pawns” unable to challenge entrenched norms, revealing a significant gap between attitudinal change and behavioral implementation in psychiatric training.

Key Concepts

  • Supported decision-making — A process where individuals are assisted in making decisions based on their own will and preferences, presented as an alternative to substitute decision-making under mental health legislation.
  • Paradigm shift — A fundamental change in underlying assumptions, here referring to residents’ shift from a biomedical model to a human rights- and recovery-oriented understanding of psychiatric practice.
  • Powerlessness — The experience of lacking agency or influence despite formal authority, described by residents as feeling like “pawns” within hierarchical healthcare systems where they cannot challenge established practices.
  • Apprenticeship model — The training structure in which psychiatry residents learn primarily through supervised clinical work, absorbing institutional norms and prioritizing conformity over innovation.
  • Human rights model of disability — A framework that understands disability through the lens of rights and societal barriers rather than individual deficit, forming the theoretical basis of the CRPD-informed training.
  • Psychiatric coercion — Practices such as seclusion, restraint, and forced treatment that compromise autonomy and are criticized for violating human rights, which the training sought to reduce.
  • Service user-led education — An educational approach where individuals with lived experience of mental health conditions design and deliver training, aiming to increase empathy and challenge stigma in clinical education.
  • Occupational therapy — Referenced implicitly through discussions of functional recovery and daily living, though not a direct focus; relevant to broader rehabilitation contexts in mental health.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Service user-led education successfully shifts psychiatry residents’ attitudes and intentions toward human rights-compliant, less coercive practice but fails to produce sustained changes in clinical behavior due to systemic barriers including powerlessness, hierarchical training models, and resource constraints.
    • Evidence: Residents reported enthusiasm and cognitive shifts (“paradigm shift”) post-training, strong intentions to reduce coercion, and increased use of supported decision-making in theory. However, they described repeated failure to implement these intentions in acute clinical settings due to feeling powerless, time pressures, reliance on nursing assessments for seclusion decisions, and pressure to conform to senior staff practices.
    • Strength: Moderately strong. The study uses rigorous qualitative methods (thematic analysis, researcher triangulation, member checks implicit in design) to explore lived experiences. Limitations include reliance on self-reported data, no behavioral observation or longitudinal follow-up, and potential self-selection bias (only those who valued the program may have participated).
  • Core claim: The apprenticeship model of psychiatric training acts as a significant socializing force that perpetuates traditional, coercive practices by prioritizing conformity to senior clinicians and nurses over innovative, rights-based approaches.
    • Evidence: Residents described feeling compelled to defer to experienced nurses who initiate seclusion and to senior psychiatrists who favor paternalistic interventions, noting their trainee status made them reluctant to challenge established norms despite personal convictions.
    • Strength: Strongly supported by repeated participant narratives about hierarchical influence and role strain. This aligns with broader sociological literature on professional socialization but lacks comparative data from alternative training models.

Open Questions

  • What specific organizational or policy changes (beyond individual education) are necessary to sustain reductions in psychiatric coercion following training interventions?
  • How do power dynamics and influence differ between nursing staff and psychiatrists in shaping decisions about seclusion and restraint, particularly regarding trainees’ perceived ability to challenge these decisions?
  • What is the long-term impact of service user-led education when combined with structural interventions such as adequate staffing, time for patient engagement, and leadership commitment to culture change?
