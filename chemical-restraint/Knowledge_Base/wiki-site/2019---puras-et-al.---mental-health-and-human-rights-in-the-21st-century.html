Summary

This commentary argues that involuntary psychiatric interventions conflict with international human rights law, particularly the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which mandates a shift from the medical model to a human rights model of disability. The authors critique the continued use of coercion—such as forced treatment, seclusion, and restraint—despite its lack of efficacy, documented harm, and misallocation of resources toward institutional renovation in wealthy regions like Europe. They reject the idea that capacity assessments can justify coercion as exceptional, arguing such judgments are subjective and risk reinforcing paternalism and discrimination. Instead, they advocate for a fundamental reorientation of psychiatry toward supporting individuals’ will and preferences, with substituted decision-making set aside except in extreme emergencies requiring ongoing dialogue for rights-respecting alternatives. The piece positions the CRPD as a transformative legal instrument that obliges states to end coercive practices and invest in community-based, rights-respecting care, framing this shift as essential for professional legitimacy, social justice, and effective mental health systems.

Key Entities

  • Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) (type: framework) — central legal instrument establishing the human rights model of disability and obligating states to end forced treatment and substitute decision-making in mental health
  • World Health Organization (WHO) QualityRights Framework (type: tool) — presented as a practical model for implementing CRPD principles in mental health services, promoting rights-based care and alternatives to coercion
  • United Nations Special Rapporteur on the Right to Physical and Mental Health (type: org) — the role held by first author Dainius Pūras, lending authority to the critique of coercion and advocacy for rights-based mental health
  • World Psychiatry (type: journal) — the publication venue, lending academic credibility to the argument as a peer-reviewed commentary in a leading psychiatric journal
  • Szmukler’s paper (2019) (type: paper) — referenced as a key counterpoint advocating for capacity assessments as a safeguard to justify limited coercion, which the authors critique as insufficiently protective of autonomy

Key Concepts

  • Human rights model of disability — a framework under the CRPD that locates disability in societal barriers rather than individual impairment, requiring systemic change to ensure equality and autonomy; used to justify ending coercion in mental health
  • Medical model of disability — views disability as an individual defect requiring medical correction; critiqued as outdated, paternalistic, and incompatible with the CRPD’s emphasis on autonomy and social inclusion
  • Will and preferences framework — an approach to decision-making that prioritizes respecting a person’s own choices, using supported decision-making or best interpretation of will when capacity is uncertain; presented as the CRPD-compliant alternative to substitute decision-making
  • Substitute decision-making — a legal mechanism where others make decisions for a person deemed lacking capacity; criticized as undermining autonomy and requiring abolition under the CRPD, except in narrowly defined emergencies
  • Functional assessments of mental capacity — evaluations of decision-making ability based on cognitive or functional criteria; critiqued as subjective and likely to replicate power imbalances, even when intended as safeguards against harm
  • Coercion in mental health — refers to involuntary interventions like forced treatment, seclusion, and restraint; described as rising globally, ineffective, harmful, and a violation of rights under the CRPD unless truly last resort
  • Treatment gap — the proportion of people with mental health conditions who do not receive care; cited to argue that merely increasing funding without rights reform risks worsening outcomes by expanding coercive systems
  • Supported decision-making — assistance that enables individuals to make their own choices rather than having decisions made for them; implied as a core component of the will and preferences framework and a CRPD-aligned practice
  • WHO European Region institutional investment — referenced as evidence of misallocated resources, with funds continuing to flow into renovating large psychiatric institutions instead of community-based care
  • Emergency response exception — a narrow concession that immediate crisis intervention may be necessary, but must be followed by ongoing efforts to develop rights-compliant alternatives and eliminate the need for coercion

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Involuntary psychiatric interventions generally violate international human rights law under the CRPD, and the mental health field must transition to a rights-based model centered on will and preferences to eliminate coercion.
    • Evidence: The CRPD legally obliges states to end forced treatment and substitute decision-making; global rise in coercion lacks efficacy evidence and shows clear harm (e.g., trauma, death from seclusion/restraint); resources are misallocated toward institutional renovation in Europe despite available community alternatives; coercion persists due to systemic power imbalances and legal sanctioning, not resource scarcity; philosophical and neuroscience challenges to coercion’s legitimacy are reinforced by the CRPD’s binding legal status.
    • Strength: Strong legal and normative grounding (CRPD as binding international law); moderate empirical support for harms of coercion and resource misallocation; speculative but reasonable claims about benefits of role shift (e.g., unlocked funds). The argument is coherent and principled, though less detailed on scalable implementation pathways.
  • Counterargument addressed: Some (e.g., Szmukler) argue coercion is justified in exceptional cases via capacity assessments to preserve autonomy or life.
    • Rebuttal: Determining incapacity is inherently subjective; professionals cannot know a person’s true best interest; such assessments risk replicating paternalism and discrimination; even well-intentioned evaluations undermine autonomy.
    • Concession: Emergency responses may be needed in crisis, but society must continue developing justifiable, rights-respecting alternatives to eliminate reliance on coercion.
  • Finding: Shifting psychiatry from a tutelary (paternalistic) to facilitative role would reduce harm, recover misused funds, enhance professional legitimacy, and advance social justice by aligning practice with human rights principles.
    • Evidence: Logical extension of CRPD principles; alignment with WHO QualityRights and peer-led models; historical examples (e.g., Germany) showing innovation follows restriction of coercion.
    • Strength: Strong theoretical coherence; moderate empirical and experiential support; aspirational but grounded in existing rights frameworks and pilot initiatives.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Tension with Szmukler (2019): Direct disagreement over whether functional assessments of mental capacity can justify coercion in exceptional cases; authors argue such assessments are subjective and inherently risk reproducing power imbalances and discrimination, while Szmukler views them as necessary safeguards.
    • Nuance: Authors concede that emergency intervention may be temporarily needed but insist on ongoing reform to eliminate the perceived need for coercion, rejecting it as a permanent solution.
  • Internal tension: While rejecting most coercion, authors acknowledge “emergency responses” may be required—potentially allowing subjective interpretations that could undermine the abolitionist stance.
    • Mitigation: Emphasize that dialogue must continue to find rights-compliant alternatives, framing current acceptance of exceptions as regrettable and temporary, not a justification for the status quo.
  • Potential conflict with existing wiki: If the wiki currently treats capacity assessments or substitute decision-making as neutral or beneficial tools in mental health, this source challenges that view as incompatible with the CRPD’s requirement to prioritize autonomy and end coercion.
    • Resolution: Would require updating relevant pages to reflect the CRPD’s abolitionist stance on coercion and its preference for supported decision-making over substitute models.

Open Questions

  • What specific, rights-compliant alternatives to coercion have demonstrated effectiveness in diverse, including low-resource, settings?
  • How can legal systems reform laws governing substitute decision-making to fully align with CRPD Article 12 on equal recognition before the law?
  • What role should peer-led, user-controlled, and community-based models play in reducing reliance on psychiatric coercion and transforming mental health systems?
  • How can progress toward eliminating coercion be measured and monitored without compromising access to support or incentivizing underreporting of crises?
