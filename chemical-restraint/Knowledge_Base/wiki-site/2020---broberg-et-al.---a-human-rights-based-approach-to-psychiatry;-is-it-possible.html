A Human Rights-Based Approach to Psychiatry; Is It Possible?

Summary

This paper evaluates a pilot project (2012–2015) that applied a human rights-based approach (HRBA) to psychiatric care in Region Västra Götaland, Sweden. The initiative involved three sites—psychiatric services at Sahlgrenska University Hospital, Angered Hospital, and Bohusläns Museum—and aimed to integrate HRBA principles (dignity and empowerment, equality and non-discrimination, participation and inclusion, accountability and transparency) into organizational practice. The psychiatric component focused on reducing coercive measures, particularly belt restraints, through staff training, patient involvement, and organizational changes such as establishing a user council and revising non-therapeutic ward rules. Outcomes included a reduction in belt use from approximately four times per month to four times per year on Ward 242, increased staff capacity to question coercion, improved well-being for patients and staff, and the adoption of a regional “zero vision” for coercive measures in the 2017–2020 human rights action plan. The authors emphasize that success depended on early involvement of rights holders, dedicated time for reflection, leadership commitment, and linking HRBA to existing quality improvement efforts.

  • Swedish Partnership for Mental Health (NSPHiG) (type: org) — Non-governmental user organization providing lived-experience expertise and co-facilitating the psychiatric arm of the pilot.
  • Sahlgrenska University Hospital – Psychosis Care Chain Northeast (type: org) — Psychiatric outpatient and inpatient service that served as the primary healthcare pilot site for implementing HRBA.
  • Angered Hospital (type: org) — Local hospital participating in the pilot to test HRBA in general healthcare settings.
  • Bohusläns Museum (type: org) — Regional cultural institution involved to assess HRBA applicability outside healthcare.
  • United Nations Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD Committee) (type: org) — International treaty body whose criticisms of Sweden’s use of coercive measures provided external legitimacy for the HRBA initiative.
  • United Nations Committee on the Rights of the Child (CRC Committee) (type: org) — UN body that similarly highlighted gaps in Sweden’s compliance with children’s rights in psychiatric contexts.
  • AAAQ framework (type: tool) — Analytical tool (availability, accessibility, acceptability, quality) used to assess whether duty bearers met obligations under the right to health, particularly in linking health and housing rights.
  • “The aim of the meeting” checklist (type: tool) — Meeting preparation and evaluation tool derived from HRBA principles to ensure dignity, equality, participation, and accountability in group interactions.
  • Social Insurance Agency, Employment Service, City of Gothenburg (type: org) — Public sector partners that collaborated with the pilot to address social determinants and expand HRBA beyond clinical settings.

Key Concepts

  • Human Rights-Based Approach (HRBA) — A framework translating international human rights law into practice, centered on dignity, empowerment, equality, non-discrimination, participation, inclusion, accountability, and transparency; used here to guide organizational change in psychiatric care.
  • Dignity and empowerment — Recognition of inherent worth and strengthening of agency; demonstrated by reduced coercion and staff feeling empowered to question unnecessary interventions.
  • Equality and non-discrimination — Ensuring fair treatment and challenging hierarchies; addressed through workshops on power dynamics and inclusive meeting practices.
  • Participation and inclusion — Involving rights-holders in decisions affecting them; realized via user councils, accessible meeting formats, and outreach like “human rights picnics.”
  • Accountability and transparency — Obligation to justify actions and maintain open processes; illustrated by patient voting rights during elections and linking health and housing responsibilities across sectors.
  • Zero vision for coercive measures — Policy goal to eliminate all coercive interventions (e.g., belts, forced medication); adopted into VGR’s strategic action plan as a direct result of the pilot.
  • User council — Institutionalized forum where service users advise on care, located near waiting rooms; emerged from the pilot as a structural mechanism for ongoing participation.
  • Psychiatric coercion — Use of interventions like belts or forced medication without consent; the primary target for reduction in the psychiatric arm of the pilot.
  • Patient empowerment — Process through which service users gain confidence and ability to influence their care; fostered by language shifts (e.g., “experience expert”) and inclusive forums.
  • Organizational learning mode — Emphasis on reflection and adaptation over rigid performance metrics; described as essential for sustaining HRBA in a large bureaucracy.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: A human rights-based approach can be operationalized in psychiatric care to reduce coercive measures while improving staff and patient well-being.
    • Evidence: Reduction in belt use from ~4/month to ~4/year on Ward 242; staff reports of increased ability to question coercion; patient testimonies about dignity gains; adoption of regional policy goals.
    • Strength: Moderate — based on before/after counts, qualitative reflections, and policy uptake; lacks control group or long-term outcome data but demonstrates clear process and short-term change.
  • Core claim: Meaningful participation of individuals with lived experience is essential for legitimate and sustainable HRBA implementation.
    • Evidence: Early inclusion of service users in the working group; formation of user council; use of accessibility supports (transport, timing) to enable involvement.
    • Strength: Moderate — supported by process documentation and participant reflections; highlights ongoing challenges in achieving equity within groups.
  • Core claim: Leadership commitment and cross-sector partnerships are critical for scaling HRBA beyond pilot sites.
    • Evidence: Manager who transferred HRBA to another ward achieved 70% reduction in coercion and 30% drop in sick leave; collaboration with housing, employment, and social services.
    • Strength: Moderate — based on testimonials and observed outcomes; suggests transferability but not generalizability.
  • Core challenge: Balancing empowerment with duty of care — staff expressed concern that excessive autonomy could be perceived as neglect, potentially undermining safety.
    • Evidence: Staff reflections on tension between self-determination and protection; acknowledged as an ongoing ethical deliberation.
    • Strength: Low — acknowledged as a tension rather than a resolved finding; consistent with broader debates on autonomy vs. beneficence.
  • Core challenge: Transforming entrenched professional hierarchies requires sustained effort and reflective practice.
    • Evidence: Difficulty being heard in large groups; need for small working groups to pre-process issues; reliance on enthusiasts to drive change.
    • Strength: Moderate — documented as a persistent organizational barrier requiring active intervention.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Staff expressed concern that increasing patient autonomy through HRBA could be perceived as neglectful, potentially violating the duty to protect — revealing an unresolved tension between empowerment and safety that requires ongoing negotiation.
  • The initiative revealed persistent challenges in transforming professional hierarchies (e.g., doctors, nurses, managers), where egalitarian ideals met resistance from entrenched status differences, indicating that cultural change requires more than policy alone.
  • While Swedish law aligns with international human rights standards on paper, the authors echo UN critiques that application often falls short — reinforcing, rather than contradicting, the widespread implementation gap noted in other sources.
  • No direct contradiction with existing wiki content was found; instead, the source supports the view that coercive practices are amenable to change through rights-based reforms, countering assumptions about their intractability.

Open Questions

  • Can the outcomes of this pilot be scaled sustainably across diverse psychiatric settings, particularly in regions with fewer resources or different governance structures?
  • What are the long-term clinical effects (e.g., relapse rates, readmission, mortality) associated with HRBA-driven reductions in coercion, beyond process measures like restraint frequency?
  • How should services balance the promotion of autonomy and self-determination with the duty to prevent harm in high-risk scenarios (e.g., acute psychosis, suicidal behavior)?
  • What strategies ensure the longevity of user-led governance structures (e.g., user councils) when initial enthusiasm wanes or staff turnover occurs?
  • Which metrics beyond process adherence (e.g., quality of life, recovery indicators) best capture the impact of HRBA on patient well-being in mental health contexts?
