Summary

This cluster randomized controlled trial evaluated the effectiveness of staff de-escalation training in reducing aggression and coercive measures across all psychiatric hospitals in Slovenia. The study compared baseline (5 months in 2018) and intervention (5 months in 2019) periods, with hospitals randomized to either receive de-escalation training (experimental) or continue treatment as usual (control). The intervention consisted of a 16-hour multimodal training program based on the Beta Project of the American Association for Emergency Psychiatry, WHO Quality Rights training, and the Amdur et al. handbook, covering verbal and non-verbal techniques. Primary outcomes included incidence of aggressive incidents (measured via the Revised Staff Observation Aggression Scale, SOAS-R), severe aggression (SOAS-R ≥9), and physical restraint use, particularly aggression-related restraints. The study included 6,401 patient episodes across 6 hospitals, demonstrating robust real-world applicability.

Key findings showed that de-escalation training significantly reduced aggressive incidents by 73% (IRR=0.268), severe aggression by 86% (IRR=0.142), and aggression-related physical restraint use by 70% (IRR=0.304) in the experimental group compared to controls. The intervention also reduced the proportion of patients with recurrent aggression (from 7.6% to 1.9%) and recurrent restraint (4.8% to 2.2%). Notably, the severity of aggression decreased, with the proportion of severe events dropping from 76% in controls to 40% in the experimental group post-intervention. However, no reduction occurred in restraints for non-aggression reasons (e.g., fall prevention), and while total restraint hours decreased, the mean duration per episode did not significantly decline. The effect was specific to aggression-initiated coercion, supporting targeted intervention approaches. Limitations included pre-existing differences in involuntary admission rates and hospitalization length between groups, though these were stable across periods and adjusted for in regression models. The study strengthens evidence for de-escalation as a first-line strategy, addressing prior gaps identified in Cochrane reviews regarding lack of RCTs and inconsistent data.

Key Entities

  • Slovenian psychiatric hospitals (org): Setting of the multicenter cluster RCT, encompassing all public psychiatric hospitals in Slovenia providing inpatient care for two million inhabitants.
  • Revised Staff Observation Aggression Scale (SOAS-R) (tool): Instrument used to measure aggression incidents and severity, with scores ≥9 defining severe aggression; validated in Slovenian context.
  • Physical restraints with belts (PR) (tool): Primary coercive measure in Slovenian psychiatric wards; defined as mechanical restraint using belts, with episodes tracked from initiation to discontinuation.
  • Beta Project of the American Association for Emergency Psychiatry (org): Provided foundational guidelines for the de-escalation training protocol, particularly verbal techniques and safety principles.
  • World Health Organization Quality Rights training (program): Contributed human rights-based principles to the de-escalation training, emphasizing dignity and empowerment in mental health care.
  • Amdur et al. handbook (tool): Referenced as a practical guide for de-escalation skills in hospital settings, informing the training curriculum’s interpersonal techniques.

Key Concepts

  • De-escalation: Verbal and non-verbal techniques aimed at reducing agitation and preventing escalation to aggression or coercion; the central intervention evaluated in the study.
  • Physical restraint (PR): Application of mechanical restraints (belts) to restrict patient movement; primary outcome, with distinction made between aggression-related and non-aggression-related use.
  • Severe aggression incident: Aggressive behavior scoring ≥9 on the SOAS-R scale, indicating high-risk events requiring closer monitoring; used to assess intervention impact on critical outcomes.
  • Incidence rate ratio (IRR): Statistical ratio comparing event rates (e.g., aggressive incidents) between experimental and control groups per 100 treatment days; primary metric for quantifying intervention effect.
  • Cluster randomized trial: Study design where randomization occurs at group level (hospitals) rather than individual to prevent intervention contamination; essential given shared staff across wards.
  • Treatment as usual: Standard care without de-escalation training, provided to control group; serves as baseline for causal inference of the intervention’s specific effect.
  • Staff observation: Systematic monitoring of aggression by trained personnel using validated scales (SOAS-R), ensuring reliable outcome detection across sites.
  • Non-pharmacological intervention: Approach focusing on behavioral and environmental strategies (like de-escalation) rather than medication to manage aggression; highlights preference for least restrictive methods.
  • Recurrent aggression: Repeated aggressive episodes by the same patient; reduction in this metric indicates impact on high-need individuals and long-term patterns.
  • Treatment fidelity: Degree to which the intervention (training) was implemented as intended; though not directly measured, implied via structured protocol and repeated sessions.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: De-escalation training significantly reduces aggression, severe aggression, and aggression-related physical restraint use in acute psychiatric units.

    • Evidence: In the intervention period, aggressive incidents IRR=0.268 (73% reduction, 95% CI [0.221; 0.342]), severe aggression IRR=0.142 (86% reduction, 95% CI [0.107; 0.189]), and aggression-related physical restraints IRR=0.304 (70% reduction, 95% CI [0.238; 0.386]). Recurrent aggression dropped from 7.6% to 1.9% (p=0.000), and severe event proportion fell from 76% (control) to 40% (experimental).
    • Strength: Strong. Supported by cluster RCT design across all Slovenian psychiatric hospitals, large sample (6,401 patients), pre-registration (NCT05166278), multiple analytical methods (IRR, RR, regression), and adjustment for confounders. Limitations include non-random differences in involuntary admission and hospitalization length between groups, though these were stable across periods and controlled for in models.

  • Core claim: The reduction in physical restraint use is specific to aggression-initiated episodes, with no effect on restraints for non-aggression reasons (e.g., fall prevention).

    • Evidence: No significant change in incidence of restraints for non-aggression reasons between groups; total restraint hours decreased but mean duration per episode did not significantly decline, suggesting dilution by longer non-aggression-related episodes.
    • Strength: Moderate to strong. Clearly demonstrated via stratified analysis by restraint reason; aligns with known clinical practices where non-aggression-related restraints (e.g., for delirium or fall prevention) follow different indicators and are less responsive to behavioral interventions. The dissociation between total hours and episode duration provides internal validity.

Connections

  • De-escalation (psychiatry) — This study provides robust empirical support for the effectiveness of de-escalation training in reducing aggression and coercion in real-world inpatient psychiatric settings.
  • Physical restraint in psychiatry — Quantifies reduction in aggression-related physical restraint use, informing debates on minimizing coercive practices through behavioral interventions.
  • Coercive treatment in psychiatry — Offers evidence-based support for reducing specific subtypes of coercion (aggression-initiated) via upstream skill-based training.
  • Cluster randomized trial — Exemplifies rigorous application of this design in multicenter mental health research to evaluate system-level interventions.
  • Severity of aggression — Demonstrates that de-escalation reduces not only frequency but also severity of aggressive events, a critical advancement over prior mixed evidence.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • While total duration of restraint hours decreased significantly (RR=0.176 for restraint hour), mean duration per episode did not decrease and slightly increased in some analyses. This tension arises because non-aggression-related restraints (e.g., for fall prevention) were unaffected by de-escalation and often longer, diluting the per-episode effect in aggregated data. The study resolved this by showing aggression-specific restraint hours did decrease, highlighting the importance of stratifying by indication.
  • Contrasts with Abderhalden et al. (2013), which found no reduction in severity of aggression despite reduced incidence; this study found reductions in both incidence and severity. Possible reasons include differences in intervention (comprehensive de-escalation training vs. brief risk assessment) and measurement tools (SOAS-R severity vs. unspecified methods in prior work).

Open Questions

  • What is the durability of the de-escalation training effect beyond one year? This study only compared baseline to a single intervention year, leaving long-term maintenance and potential decay of skills unclear.
  • Which specific components of the multimodal de-escalation training are most active in reducing aggression? The intervention bundled verbal, non-verbal, theoretical, and role-play elements, making it difficult to isolate key mechanisms.
  • How do implementation fidelity, staff attitudes, and institutional support moderate the effectiveness of de-escalation training in real-world settings? These factors were not measured but likely influence translation of training into sustained practice change.
  • Is the effect of de-escalation training consistent across different psychiatric subtypes (e.g., psychosis, dementia, personality disorders) or primarily driven by certain populations? Subgroup analyses were not reported, limiting generalizability to specific clinical presentations.
