Summary

This chapter examines strategies to reduce coercive measures—such as physical restraint, seclusion, and chemical restraint—in forensic psychiatric settings. It analyzes policy drivers like the UK’s Positive and Proactive Care (2014), Mental Health Act Code of Practice (2015), and NICE guidelines (2015) that mandate restrictive intervention reduction programs. The author emphasizes that violence often stems from systemic issues like staff-patient conflict, disempowerment, and unfair treatment rather than solely patient symptoms. Evidence shows staff trained in verbal interventions had significantly lower assault rates (1 vs. 24 over 24 months) compared to untrained peers. Case studies, such as Nottinghamshire Healthcare’s co-produced de-escalation training videos, demonstrate how staff engagement and organizational culture change—through leadership, training, and person-centered approaches—can reduce reliance on coercive measures while maintaining safety.

Key Entities

  • Nottinghamshire Healthcare NHS Foundation Trust (org): Implemented staff-co-produced de-escalation training using visual media to improve engagement and reduce coercive practices.
  • Positive and Proactive Care (tool): UK Government guidance (2014) mandating reduction of restrictive interventions in healthcare services.
  • Mental Health Act (Code of Practice) (tool): UK legal framework supporting reduction of restrictive interventions and requiring staff training in restraint management.
  • National Institute for Clinical Excellence [NICE] (org): Issued 2015 guidance advocating de-escalation as first response and restraint as last resort in violence management.
  • NHS Protect (org): Recorded NHS violence incident data (68,683 incidents in 2013/2014) and promotes de-escalation and staff engagement strategies.
  • Health Service Advisory Committee (HSAC) (org): Advised (1997) that violence management training should include theory, prevention, and post-incident analysis.
  • Royal College of Psychiatrists (org): Audited violence management practices (1998), emphasizing prevention, negotiation, and de-escalation over confrontation.
  • World Health Organisation (WHO) (org): Highlighted (2011) the substantial economic burden of violence through healthcare costs, law enforcement, and lost productivity.
  • Care Quality Commission (CQC) (org): Audits compliance with de-escalation practices and appropriate use of restraints as last resorts.
  • Kirkpatrick’s model (tool): Four-level training evaluation framework (Reaction, Learning, Behaviour, Results), noted for frequent underuse beyond reaction-level assessment.
  • Force Field Analysis (tool): Kurt Lewin’s model for analyzing organizational change through driving and resisting forces.
  • Public Health Model (tool): Prevention framework distinguishing primary (root cause), secondary (early response), and tertiary (last-resort) violence prevention strategies.
  • Colton’s Best Practice Model (tool): Reduces violence through leadership, training, staffing, environment, and post-event processing.
  • Value Centred Approach (tool): Three-ring model (Caring, Professional, Educational) organizing practices around core values to minimize violence.

Key Concepts

  • Restrictive Intervention Reduction Programme: NHS-required framework where trusts must demonstrate efforts to minimize coercive measures like restraint and seclusion through policy compliance and best practices.
  • De-escalation techniques: Verbal and non-verbal strategies to reduce patient agitation without physical force, supported by training efficacy data showing fewer assaults among trained staff.
  • Primary Prevention: Public health strategy targeting root causes of violence (e.g., frustration, disempowerment, unfair treatment) to prevent incidents before they occur; positioned as most effective approach.
  • Secondary Prevention: Reactive interventions like de-escalation applied after early warning signs of agitation; intermediate strategy in the Public Health Model.
  • Tertiary Prevention: Last-resort physical interventions (restraint, seclusion, chemical restraint); least preferred tier due to risks and ethical concerns.
  • Therapeutic environment: Clinical setting designed to promote safety, engagement, and meaningful activity to reduce triggers for violence and aggression.
  • Staff engagement/ownership: Involving staff in designing changes (e.g., co-creating training materials) to reduce resistance and increase commitment to new practices.
  • Risk assessment (static/dynamic): Evaluation of historical (static) and changeable (dynamic) factors—such as treatment progress—to inform individualized prevention strategies, as required by NICE 2015.
  • Organizational culture change: Shift toward prevention-focused values via leadership, training, and staff involvement, described as essential for sustaining reductions in coercive use.
  • Person-centered care: Tailoring interventions to individual patient history, preferences, and needs; linked to recovery models and foundational to reducing coercion through dignity and autonomy.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Coercive measures should be minimized to the greatest extent possible through preventive strategies and staff training, but remain necessary as last-resort options in extreme, unpredictable violence (e.g., acute psychosis with imminent harm).
    • Evidence: Policy mandates (Positive and Proactive Care 2014, Mental Health Act 2015, NICE 2015) require reduction programs; training studies show staff with verbal intervention training had 1 assault vs. 24 in untrained peers over 24 months (Infantino & Musingo 1985); Nottinghamshire’s success via staff-co-produced training videos, distributed leadership, and individualized care plans.
    • Strength: Moderate to strong. Policy provides structural leverage; comparative training data shows clear outcomes; organizational case illustrates implementation. Limited by reliance on older studies (e.g., 1985) and lack of long-term metrics in cited examples.
  • Core claim: Violence in psychiatric settings frequently arises from systemic factors—staff-patient conflict, environmental frustration, disempowerment, and perceived unfairness—rather than solely from psychiatric symptoms.
    • Evidence: Whittington & Wykes (1996) found immediate antecedents to violence were often interpersonal conflicts; Richter & Whittington (2007) linked violence to restricted environments and disempowerment; policy guidance (DH 2008, NICE 2015) notes professional and public attitudes shape care more than pure evidence.
    • Strength: Strong. Supported by multiple studies and aligned with policy recognition of systemic drivers; reinforces focus on environmental and relational interventions.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Tension with abolitionist perspectives: The text acknowledges coercive measures may be unavoidable in instantaneous, extreme violence (e.g., untreated psychosis), challenging views advocating total elimination of restraint or seclusion.
  • Internal nuance: Notes seclusion may have therapeutic value in specific cases (e.g., reducing agitation, preventing injury per Cotton 1995; Fisher 1994), complicating its portrayal as uniformly harmful despite ethical concerns.
  • Evidence gap: NICE CG 25 (2015) relied on formal consensus methods due to insufficient empirical studies on prevention, indicating some guidelines lack strong evidence base despite policy authority.

Open Questions

  • How can the long-term impact of primary prevention strategies on violence recidivism be reliably measured in forensic populations with complex histories?
  • Which specific de-escalation techniques demonstrate the strongest evidence for reducing agitation in individuals with personality disorders, and how should they be prioritized in training?
