Summary

This qualitative synthesis review analyzed 17 studies from 1996 to 2020 to explore mental health service user and staff experiences of chemical restraint. Using databases including PsycINFO, CINAHL, MEDLINE, Embase, Emcare, Web of Science, and Scopus, the authors applied CASP for critical appraisal and Thomas and Harden’s (2008) Thematic Synthesis for data analysis. Three core themes emerged: “Unjustified versus justified,” “Violence versus necessity,” and “Reflecting back: Positives and negatives.” Service users predominantly described chemical restraint as an unjustified, violent act akin to punishment or assault, often reporting fear, humiliation, and powerlessness. In contrast, staff generally viewed it as a justified, necessary response to ensure safety, citing systemic pressures such as understaffing and lack of alternatives. Some service users retrospectively acknowledged chemical restraint as less distressing than other coercive forms, particularly in emergency department contexts, highlighting experiential nuances.

  • CINAHL (tool): Database searched to capture nursing and allied health literature relevant to mental health restraint practices.
  • MEDLINE (tool): Biomedical database queried to include clinical and psychiatric studies on chemical restraint.
  • Embase (tool): Biomedical and pharmacological database used to ensure broad coverage of chemical restraint research.
  • Emcare (tool): Nursing-focused database included to capture frontline staff perspectives on restraint practices.
  • Web of Science (tool): Multidisciplinary database searched to identify relevant studies across health and social sciences.
  • Scopus (tool): Large abstract and citation database used to support comprehensive literature identification.
  • Qualitative Critical Appraisal Skills Programme (CASP) (tool): Applied to assess methodological quality of included studies, focusing on validity, results, and applicability.
  • Thomas and Harden’s (2008) Thematic Synthesis (tool): Method used to code, develop descriptive themes, and conduct inductive analysis of service user and staff experiences.

Key Concepts

  • Chemical restraint — Defined as the assertive administration of emergency medication to adults with aggressive, agitated, or violent behaviors for quick calming, sedation, symptom reduction, or harm prevention; central phenomenon under review.
  • Service user experiences — Focus on how individuals receiving mental health services perceive and undergo chemical restraint, emphasizing subjective feelings of injustice, violation, and trauma.
  • Staff perspectives — Encompasses views of nurses, physicians, and security guards on justifying and administering chemical restraint, often framed as a safety necessity amid systemic constraints.
  • Unjustified versus justified — Core theme contrasting service users’ perception of restraint as punitive and unwarranted with staff’s view of it as a legitimate response to dangerous behavior.
  • Violence versus necessity — Theme highlighting service users’ experience of restraint as physically and emotionally violent versus staff’s characterization of it as a necessary last resort.
  • Reflecting back: Positives and negatives — Theme on retrospective evaluations, where some service users found chemical restraint less distressing than other coercive methods, while staff noted clinical benefits like stabilization and discharge.
  • Thematic synthesis — Qualitative analytical approach used to integrate findings across studies, enabling identification of convergent and divergent experiences between service users and staff.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Service users and staff hold divergent views of chemical restraint—service users predominantly experience it as unjustified, violent, and traumatic, while staff generally regard it as justified, necessary, and appropriate within systemic limitations such as staffing shortages and limited access to alternatives.
    • Evidence: Thematic synthesis of 17 qualitative studies revealed consistent service user descriptions of feeling “like being raped,” helpless, or punished, and staff reports of feeling “forced” to act due to safety pressures and lack of de-escalation skills or resources. Some service users in emergency department contexts viewed restraint as justified, and a minority retrospectively preferred it over seclusion or physical restraint due to perceived lower distress.
    • Strength: Strongly supported by rigorous methodology, including dual reviewer screening, CASP critical appraisal, and inductive thematic analysis. Findings are triangulated across diverse settings and roles. Limitations include frequent co-occurrence of chemical restraint with physical/mechanical restraint in studies, power imbalances in researcher-participant relationships, and English-language bias, though the consistency of the service-user/staff divide enhances credibility.

Connections

  • chemical restraint — Directly informs the experiential dimensions of this concept, emphasizing the divide between user and staff perspectives.
  • coercion in psychiatry — Expands understanding of how chemical restraint functions as a specific form of coercion shaped by institutional justifications and staff narratives.
  • patient experience — Provides detailed insight into the subjective, often traumatic, lived reality of chemical restraint from the service user standpoint.
  • trauma informed care — Highlights the need for alternatives that avoid retraumatization, given service users’ descriptions of restraint as violent and humiliating.
  • least restrictive alternative — Reflects staff acknowledgment that restraint is often used not as a last resort but due to perceived lack of viable options.
  • recovery model — Connects to retrospective service user views where some acknowledged restraint as potentially therapeutic in hindsight, aligning with recovery-oriented reinterpretation of crisis events.
  • epistemic injustice — Embodies the marginalization of service user knowledge and experience in favor of staff-authorized narratives of necessity and safety.
  • informed consent — Underscores how chemical restraint undermines authentic consent due to power imbalances, altered mental states, and lack of genuine choice.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • Some service users (e.g., in Yap et al., 2017 ED study) viewed chemical restraint as justified and appropriate, contrasting with the majority who saw it as violent and unjustified—indicating that context (e.g., emergency department vs. inpatient) significantly shapes acceptability.
  • While the review isolates chemical restraint experientially, most included studies examined it in conjunction with physical or mechanical restraint, making it difficult to disentangle effects unique to chemical restraint alone.
  • The reliance on English-language studies may exclude culturally diverse perspectives on restraint, potentially limiting the generalizability of findings across global mental health systems.

Open Questions

  • How can future research on chemical restraint mitigate power imbalances between researchers and participants to capture more authentic service user voices, particularly from marginalized or involuntarily treated populations?
  • What specific de-escalation, trauma-informed, or recovery-oriented interventions are most acceptable and effective to service users in reducing reliance on chemical restraint across different settings (e.g., emergency departments, inpatient units)?
  • How do cultural, legal, and institutional factors (e.g., geographic region, legal status, staff training) influence the perceived legitimacy of chemical restraint among both service users and staff?
