Summary

This chapter examines the international use of coercive measures in psychiatric care, identifying four core types: therapy by coercion (e.g., antipsychotics), therapeutic measures without primary therapeutic intent (e.g., chemical restraint), separation (e.g., seclusion), and mechanical restriction (e.g., restraints). It argues that practices are shaped more by tradition and local norms than evidence, with significant variation in what is considered acceptable—such as seclusion being banned in Denmark but preferred in the Netherlands, or mechanical restraint prohibited in the UK but used elsewhere. Drawing on epidemiological data from studies like EUNOMIA and national registries, the chapter shows that coercion affects 3–16% of psychiatric admissions internationally, with wide differences in duration and frequency. Crucially, it finds that banning one coercive method often leads to increased use of others (substitution effect), and that the least restrictive option depends on individual patient preferences and context rather than a fixed hierarchy. Patient-reported data using tools like the Coercion Experience Scale (CES) reveal significant subjective distress and dignity violations, particularly with methods perceived as dehumanizing (e.g., net beds), challenging purely safety- or efficacy-based evaluations and emphasizing the need for ethically grounded, patient-centered approaches.

Key Concepts

  • Least Restrictive Intervention Principle: The ethical and legal standard requiring that coercion only be used when no less restrictive alternative exists, with the least harmful option selected; context- and preference-dependent.
  • Human Rights Framework (UN CRPD): Asserts that persons with psychosocial disabilities retain legal capacity and must consent to medical interventions, challenging coercion justified by impaired judgment.
  • Medical Ethics Principles (Beauchamp & Childress): Beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, and justice used to evaluate trade-offs in coercion (e.g., forced treatment vs. withheld care).
  • Therapeutic vs. Safety-Oriented Interventions: Distinguishes treatments targeting pathology (e.g., antipsychotics for psychosis) from those primarily aimed at containment without therapeutic intent (e.g., sedative-only medication use).
  • Cultural and National Variation in Coercive Practices: Significant differences in preferred/prohibited methods across countries (e.g., seclusion banned in Denmark, used in Netherlands; mechanical restraint restricted in UK).
  • Substitution Effect of Bans: Banning one coercive method (e.g., seclusion) may increase use of others (e.g., mechanical restraint or medication), undermining reform.
  • Subjective Distress and Loss of Dignity: Coercive measures cause psychological harm (helplessness, shame, fear) and dignity violations, especially when patients are treated like animals (e.g., net beds), measured via tools like CES.

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: Coercive practices vary widely by country and are guided more by tradition than evidence, with no universally least restrictive option.
    • Evidence: Tables 6.1–6.2 show national preferences (e.g., seclusion > medication > restraint in Netherlands; mechanical restraint > seclusion > medication in Germany); EUNOMIA data shows 38% coercion rate in EU with country variation (21% in Spain to 59% in Poland); Danish register study found 22.9% exposure to coercion in schizophrenia-spectrum patients.
    • Strength: Strongly supported by multi-source empirical data; consistent across epidemiological studies.
  • Core claim: Banning one coercive measure often leads to increased use of others (substitution effect).
    • Evidence: Described as a key empirical pattern; e.g., seclusion reduction efforts in Netherlands correlated with increased medication use; historical trends suggest compensatory shifts.
    • Strength: Moderately supported; inferred from longitudinal and cross-sectional trends, not direct causation.
  • Core claim: Patient subjective distress and dignity violations are significant and under-measured; tools like CES reveal lasting harm.
    • Evidence: Bergk et al. (2011) found no short-term difference in CES scores between seclusion and mechanical restraint, but 18-month follow-up showed greater distress after mechanical restraint; Georgieva et al. (2012) reported highest distress with combined measures; Dutch preference study showed individualized preferences (men favored seclusion, women medication).
    • Strength: Strong for subjective experience; multiple studies using CES and interviews confirm distress, though limited by sample size and cultural context.
  • Core claim: Ethical evaluation must distinguish therapeutic intent from mere control (e.g., sedating without treating illness may violate beneficence and autonomy).
    • Evidence: Philosophical argument based on medical ethics; if medication is used to immobilize without treating underlying psychosis, it serves others’ interests, not the patient’s.
    • Strength: Strong theoretically; supported by ethical frameworks, though empirical validation limited due to design challenges.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The chapter acknowledges that some coercive measures may have incidental therapeutic benefits (e.g., seclusion reducing stimulation, restraint enabling interaction), challenging a strict safety-vs.-therapy dichotomy.
  • Notes that patient preferences vary significantly (e.g., by gender or culture), making universal guidelines difficult despite ethical consensus on minimizing harm.
  • If the wiki currently presents a rigid hierarchy of restrictiveness (e.g., medication < seclusion < restraint), this complicates it by showing that what is least restrictive depends on individual and contextual factors.
  • No direct contradiction found, but the chapter complicates overly rigid applications of human rights or ethical principles by emphasizing real-world variation and unintended consequences of reform efforts.

Open Questions

  • How can healthcare systems balance safety needs with minimizing harm when all coercive options carry risks of physical injury, psychological trauma, or dignity violation?
  • What alternatives (e.g., de-escalation, advance directives, peer support, trauma-informed care) show promise in reducing reliance on coercion across diverse cultural and resource settings?
  • Is it possible to develop culturally adaptable guidelines that respect both human rights (e.g., UN CRPD) and local realities of risk, staffing, and infrastructure without imposing external standards?
  • Can tools like the Coercion Experience Scale be standardized and integrated into routine clinical monitoring to ensure patient-centered evaluation of coercive practices?
