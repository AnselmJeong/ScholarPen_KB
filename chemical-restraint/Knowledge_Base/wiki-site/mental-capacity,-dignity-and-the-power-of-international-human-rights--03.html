Summary

This chapter examines the principle of indivisibility of human rights and its application to Article 12 of the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD), which concerns the right to legal capacity. It traces the historical development of the indivisibility principle within the United Nations human rights framework, from the split between the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) to its affirmation in instruments like the Vienna Declaration. The author argues that indivisibility comprises two interrelated concepts: the interdependence of rights (where the realization of one depends on others) and the rejection of any hierarchy between civil-political and socio-economic rights. Applying this to disability rights, the chapter contends that the right to legal capacity under Article 12 cannot be understood in isolation from the right to support, as the two are interdependent—legal capacity often depends on access to support services, which constitute socio-economic rights. Consequently, for some individuals with cognitive disability who face socio-economic disadvantages due to the residual impact of impairment, realizing legal capacity may require support measures, including substitute decision-making as a last resort. This challenges interpretations that view Article 12 as requiring the complete abolition of substitute decision-making, such as the UN Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities’ General Comment No 1. Instead, the author advocates for an interpretation that permits substitute decision-making when necessary to prevent harm and promote social inclusion, while maintaining safeguards and recognizing personhood. The analysis emphasizes that ignoring the socio-economic dimension of legal capacity risks privileging civil-political values like autonomy without adequately addressing the lived realities of persons with disabilities.

Key Concepts

  • Principle of indivisibility (human rights) — the concept that human rights are interconnected, interdependent, and of equal importance, rejecting a hierarchy between civil-political and socio-economic rights; central to the argument that Article 12 must account for socio-economic realities like support needs — Yes, likely exists
  • Interdependence of rights — the idea that the enjoyment of one right depends on the enjoyment of others (e.g., legal capacity may depend on access to support); used to show that legal capacity cannot be exercised in isolation for some persons with cognitive disability — Yes, likely exists
  • No hierarchy between civil-political and socio-economic rights — the rejection of the notion that civil-political rights are superior; asserts equal importance, challenging traditional views that prioritize autonomy — Yes, likely exists
  • Supported decision-making — a model where persons with disabilities receive assistance to make their own decisions rather than having decisions made for them; presented as part of the “right to support” under Article 12 — Yes, likely exists
  • Substitute decision-making — a model where another person is legally authorized to make decisions on behalf of a person who may lack capacity; argued to be necessary as a last resort for some to prevent harm and promote inclusion — Yes, likely exists
  • Social model of disability — framework distinguishing impairment (condition) from disability (social barriers); used to argue that residual impairment impact leads to socio-economic disadvantage, making socio-economic rights relevant to legal capacity — Yes, likely exists
  • Legal capacity — the ability to hold rights and duties and to exercise them (e.g., to make legally binding decisions); core of Article 12, with debate over how it should be exercised — Yes, likely exists
  • Universal legal capacity — the principle that all persons possess legal capacity equally regardless of disability; the UN Committee’s interpretation of Article 12, which the author argues overlooks socio-economic realities — Yes, likely exists

Main Arguments & Findings

  • Core claim: The principle of indivisibility demands an interpretation of Article 12 of the CRPD that allows for, and may require, substitute decision-making as a last resort to promote equality and social inclusion for some adults with cognitive disability, while still recognizing personhood.
    • Evidence: The chapter draws on the legislative history of Article 12 (a compromise that remained silent on substitute decision-making), the interdependence between the right to legal capacity (civil-political) and the right to support (socio-economic), the social model of disability acknowledging residual impairment impact, and the CRPD’s strong emphasis on indivisibility compared to other treaties. It contrasts the UN Committee’s General Comment No 1 (abolition-only view) with scholarly support for a last-resort approach.
    • Strength: Well-reasoned and grounded in legal interpretation, treaty provisions, and disability rights scholarship. However, it directly contests the UN Committee’s official stance, so its acceptance is moderate but well-substantiated; limitations include limited detail on safeguards and potential for overemphasizing individual capacity without addressing systemic barriers.
  • Additional finding: Interpretations of Article 12 that require abolition of substitute decision-making privilege civil-political values like autonomy and non-interference, contrary to the indivisibility principle, by ignoring the socio-economic context in which legal capacity is exercised.

Contradictions & Tensions

  • The UN Committee’s General Comment No 1 asserts that Article 12 requires abolition of substitute decision-making, which directly contradicts this chapter’s argument for a last-resort approach.
  • Internal tension exists between autonomy (non-interference) and protection from harm; the author acknowledges that abolitionist arguments prioritize autonomy, while last-resort arguments recognize that autonomy alone may not suffice for some due to socio-economic disadvantage.
  • Caveat: The chapter does not detail specific safeguards for substitute decision-making, leaving open the risk of misuse if not properly regulated; it assumes such measures would accompany any last-resort interpretation.

Open Questions

  • What specific procedural and substantive safeguards are necessary to ensure that substitute decision-making, when used as a last resort, does not lead to abuse or unjust deprivation of rights?
  • How can the threshold for “last resort” be objectively determined to prevent premature or inappropriate use of substitute decision-making?
  • In what ways can addressing socio-economic inequities (e.g., poverty, lack of access to services) reduce the perceived need for substitute decision-making among persons with cognitive disability?
  • How do different legal and cultural contexts influence the acceptability and implementation of supported versus substitute decision-making models under the CRPD?
