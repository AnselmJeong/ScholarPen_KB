# DSM-III

## Why It Matters
- The DSM-III restructured psychiatric diagnosis around symptom-based categories, shifting away from dynamic psychiatry and toward a biomedical model.
- Its institutional and cultural success transformed mental health practice, insurance, and research, despite fundamental scientific flaws.
- The archive uses DSM-III as a case study in how diagnostic systems can conflate normal distress with mental disorder, raising ongoing questions about validity and overdiagnosis.

## Open questions
- How can a diagnostic system balance institutional utility (standardization, reimbursement) with scientific validity (etiological grounding)?
- What criteria should distinguish normal human distress from genuine mental disorder, given the failure of symptom‑only approaches?
- Is the shift away from dynamic psychiatry reversible, or have DSM‑III’s categories become too entrenched in research and policy?
