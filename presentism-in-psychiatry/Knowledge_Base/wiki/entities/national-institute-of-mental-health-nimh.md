# National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)

## What It Is
The National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) is the U.S. federal agency responsible for funding and guiding research on mental disorders. In the literature, it is depicted as a central force that first promoted a psychosocial model, then drove a shift toward biological and disease-based frameworks, funded large epidemiological studies for DSM-III, and later developed the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) as an alternative to the DSM. Its evolving priorities have profoundly shaped American psychiatry, community mental health policy, and the trajectory of diagnostic reform.

## Role in the Literature
- In [[006-6-the-decline-and-fall-of-dynamic-psychiatry]]: depicted as shifting from a psychosocial to a biological focus and promoting Community Mental Health Centers (CMHCs).
- In [[007-7-diagnostic-psychiatry]]: described as the primary funding body that pressured psychiatry toward a biomedical, disease-based model.
- In [[008-8-biology-re-emerges]]: noted as a former major research sponsor that was later replaced by the pharmaceutical industry as the primary funder of drug studies.
- In [[009-9-the-successes-and-failures-of-the-dsm-revolution]]: initially a key promoter of DSM-III through funding large epidemiological studies; later, under Thomas Insel, publicly disavowed the DSM framework.
- In [[010-10-the-past-and-future-of-mental-illness]]: a central actor that both supported the DSM revolution and later rejected its specificity model by developing the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) alternative.
- In [[012-chapter-twelve]]: funded Goffman's Laboratory of Socio-Environmental Studies and his fieldwork at St Elizabeth's.
- In [[2023 - George et al. - Cycles of reform in the history of psychosis treatment in the United States]]: driver of the CMHC program, Community Support Program, and policy shifts across multiple reform cycles.
- In [[A History of Psychiatry - 08]]: Seymour Kety's institutional base as scientific director; shifted research focus from psychoanalysis to basic science.
