# National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)

## What It Is
The National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH) is the principal U.S. federal agency funding research on mental disorders. It has been a decisive force in shaping psychiatric paradigms—driving shifts from psychosocial to biomedical models, promoting community mental health centers, and developing major diagnostic and research frameworks such as the DSM-III and the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC). Its evolving priorities and funding policies have directly influenced the trajectory of mental health research, treatment, and reform over the past century.

## Role in the Literature
- In [[006-6-the-decline-and-fall-of-dynamic-psychiatry]]: Presented as a central actor that shifted psychiatry from a psychosocial to a biological focus and promoted community mental health centers.
- In [[007-7-diagnostic-psychiatry]]: Described as the primary funding body that pressured psychiatry toward a biomedical, disease-based model.
- In [[008-8-biology-re-emerges]]: Noted as a former major sponsor of psychiatric research later eclipsed by the pharmaceutical industry; its former director Steven Hyman is quoted.
- In [[009-9-the-successes-and-failures-of-the-dsm-revolution]]: Initially a key promoter of the DSM-III through large epidemiological studies, later, under Thomas Insel, publicly disavowed the DSM framework.
- In [[010-10-the-past-and-future-of-mental-illness]]: A central actor that both supported the DSM revolution and later rejected its specificity model by developing the Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) alternative.
- In [[012-chapter-twelve]]: Funded Goffman's Laboratory of Socio-Environmental Studies and his fieldwork at St Elizabeth's.
- In [[2023 - George et al. - Cycles of reform in the history of psychosis treatment in the United States]]: Identified as a driver of the CMHC program, Community Support Program, and policy shifts across multiple reform cycles.
- In [[A History of Psychiatry - 09]]: Funded field trials for DSM-III and sponsored population-based depression screening programs.
