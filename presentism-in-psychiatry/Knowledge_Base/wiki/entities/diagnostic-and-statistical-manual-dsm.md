# Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM)

## What It Is
The DSM is the standard psychiatric classification system used in clinical practice and research. In this literature, it is a central nosological tool whose iterative revisions—especially DSM-III—are seen as a turning point toward biomedical description, yet also critiqued for proliferating diagnoses that misrepresent natural disease boundaries.

## Role in the Literature
- In [[001-chapter-one]]: The DSM is presented as a system of “endless iteration and revision,” whose proliferating diagnoses the author critiques as an “elaborately disguised game of make-believe.”
- In [[2014 - Shorter - The History of Psychiatry as Clinical Science]]: The DSM is the primary target of Shorter’s nosological critique; he argues that DSM-5 classification is full of artifacts that misrepresent natural disease boundaries.
- In [[From Madness to Mental Health - 15]]: The DSM is described as the central nosological tool, with DSM-III marking a turning point toward biomedical description.
