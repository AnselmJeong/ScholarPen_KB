# Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM)

## What It Is
The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM) is the standard classification system for mental disorders used by clinicians and researchers in psychiatry. It has undergone numerous revisions, with DSM-III (1980) marking a major shift toward biomedical description. In this literature, the DSM is critiqued as an imperfect, politically influenced tool whose diagnostic categories often misrepresent natural disease boundaries.

## Role in the Literature
- In [[001-chapter-one]]: Referenced as a psychiatric classification system subjected to "endless iteration and revision," whose proliferating diagnoses are critiqued as an "elaborately disguised game of make-believe."
- In [[2014 - Shorter - The History of Psychiatry as Clinical Science]]: Serves as the primary target of Shorter's nosological critique, arguing that DSM-5 classification is full of artifacts that misrepresent natural disease boundaries.
- In [[From Madness to Mental Health - 15]]: Described as the central diagnostic tool, with DSM-III (1980) marking a major turning point toward biomedical description.
- In [[HIstory of Madness - Colin Gordon]]: Implicitly referenced through Gordon's discussion of psychiatry's defensive relationship to its own history and the ongoing question of diagnostic validity.
