# Salpêtrière

## What It Is
The Salpêtrière was a Parisian hospice and one of the largest *Hôpitaux généraux* in France, originally housing a diverse population of women including the poor, elderly, and a small minority of the insane. It became a pivotal site in the history of psychiatry and neurology under directors such as Philippe Pinel, Jean-Étienne Dominique Esquirol, and later Jean-Martin Charcot, who used its large chronic patient population to study neurological disorders and hysteria. Its institutional routines, inherited from leper houses, exemplified the quasi-monastic regimen of early modern French hospitals.

## Role in the Literature
- In [[005-chapter-five]]: Referenced as the largest of the French *Hôpitaux généraux*, housing a diverse population of which only a small minority were insane women.
- In [[A History of Psychiatry - 02]]: Described as a Parisian hospice for women where Pinel became director in 1795 and where Esquirol later administered its psychiatric division.
- In [[A History of Psychiatry - 04]]: Presented as Charcot’s base — a hospice with a large population of aging women, where he studied neurological illness and later hysteria.
- In [[HIstory of Madness - Colin Gordon]]: Cited as a major Parisian hospital whose daily horarium from 1721 exemplifies the quasi-monastic regimen inherited from leper houses.
