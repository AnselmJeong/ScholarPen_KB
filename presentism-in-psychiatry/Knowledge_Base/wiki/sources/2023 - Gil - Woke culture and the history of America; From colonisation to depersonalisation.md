# Woke culture and the history of America: From colonisation to depersonalisation

## Summary

This essay by Sonsoles Madrid Gil, published in *Church, Communication and Culture*, critiques the contemporary "woke" movement as a destructive ideological force that seeks to deconstruct Western history, replace objective truth with competing narratives, and substitute individual identity with group-based tribal identities. The study focuses on the iconoclastic wave of statue removals and cancellations following the George Floyd protests (2020) and traces its roots through the Black Lives Matter movement into a broader cultural revolution aimed at purging America’s foundational sins—especially colonization.

The author adopts a polemical, essayistic approach rather than empirical social science. She assembles anecdotal evidence (statue vandalism and removal across the US and Latin America), quotes conservative and classical liberal commentators (Dershowitz, Mering, Kaiser, Brague), and applies sociological frameworks such as Campbell and Manning’s "culture of victimhood" thesis and Sue’s concept of microaggressions. The method is interpretive and argumentative, aiming to diagnose a moral and historiographical crisis.

The central finding is that woke culture commits the fundamental historiographical error of presentism: judging historical figures (Columbus, Serra, Roosevelt) by contemporary moral standards, thereby denying historical progress and making any figure guilty by modern criteria. The movement is said to replace reason with emotion, dialogue with censorship, and individual dignity with competitive victimhood, ultimately threatening democratic institutions such as free expression and due process.

Significance lies in the article’s explicit application of the presentism critique—familiar from academic historiography of psychiatry—to a broad public-political controversy. It positions itself as a strong anti-presentist intervention, arguing that decontextualized moral judgment destroys cultural identity and historical truth. The essay also revives the "Black Legend" debate, defending Spanish colonization as more complex and less evil than its detractors claim.

## Key Concepts

- **Woke culture** — A contemporary ideological movement that began with racial justice advocacy and expanded into a broad agenda to deconstruct Western institutions, history, and identity through the lens of systemic oppression, privilege, and victimhood.
- **Cancel culture** — The practice of withdrawing public support, threatening economic boycotts, or ostracizing individuals/organizations for unacceptable opinions or behavior, often without due process or proportionality.
- **Culture of victimhood** — A social framework (from Campbell & Manning) where moral status is attained through claiming victimization and suffering, replacing earlier cultures of honor and dignity; leads to hypersensitivity and competitive grievance.
- **Presentism** — The historiographical error of judging past figures and events by contemporary moral standards rather than their own historical context; identified as the core methodological flaw in woke historiography.
- **Black Legend (Leyenda Negra)** — The historiographical tradition of anti-Spanish propaganda that depicts Spanish colonialism as uniquely brutal and exploitative, often ignoring positive aspects and the complexity of the encounter.
- **Stataphobia** — A neologism for the fear and destruction of statues and monuments, describing the iconoclastic campaign against symbols of oppression.
- **Microaggressions** — Brief, commonplace verbal or environmental indignities (Sue 2010) that communicate hostile or derogatory slights toward marginalized groups; used as a mechanism for social control in victimhood culture.
- **Identity politics** — Political mobilization based on group identities (race, gender, sexuality) rather than universal principles or class; the organizing logic of woke culture that fragments persons into categories of oppressor/oppressed.
- **Intersectionality** — The overlapping of multiple forms of discrimination or oppression within one person or group; used to assign moral status by combining victimized identities.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: Woke culture is a destructive ideological movement that deconstructs Western history, replaces objective truth with narrative, and depersonalises individuals into tribal identity groups.**
  - Evidence: Anecdotal catalog of statue removals (Columbus, Serra, Roosevelt, Cervantes, etc.) across the US and Latin America; quotations from Dershowitz, Mering, Campbell & Manning, Brague, and others; historical comparisons to Orwell’s *1984* and Nazi censorship origins of "cancel."
  - Strength: Weak to moderate. The essay is a polemical interpretation, not an empirical study. It relies on conservative/classical liberal commentators, lacks systematic data on cancel culture’s prevalence, and does not engage seriously with critical race theory or decolonial studies. The evidence is illustrative rather than analytical.

- **Core claim: Cancel culture threatens democratic institutions by undermining free expression, due process, meritocracy, and open debate.**
  - Evidence: Examples of public shaming, boycotts, and professional consequences for those who violate woke norms; references to Harper’s Magazine letter, the Journal of Controversial Ideas, and the Freedom Speech Union as counter-responses.
  - Strength: Moderate. The argument is logically coherent but relies on selective anecdotes; the actual impact on democratic institutions is asserted rather than measured.

- **Core claim: Spanish colonization has been unfairly demonized through the revival of the Black Legend, ignoring legal and theological debates (Vitoria, Las Casas) and positive civilizational achievements.**
  - Evidence: References to the "good savage" myth, Aztec human sacrifice, and the apologetic efforts of three popes; citation of Gilley (2018) on anti-colonial thinking’s political origins.
  - Strength: Weak. The defense is selective, emphasizing positive aspects while minimizing negative impacts (forced labor, disease, cultural destruction). The author concedes "great evils and crimes" but does not integrate them into the evaluation.

- **Core claim: Presentism is the fundamental historiographical error of the woke movement; judging the past by present standards denies moral progress and makes all historical figures guilty.**
  - Evidence: Citation of Brague’s three errors (historical reductionism, inadequate hermeneutic, decontextualization); Lamo de Espinosa’s comment on denying moral progress.
  - Strength: Strong in articulation, but the author’s own critique of woke culture is itself grounded in contemporary values, revealing a potential inconsistency.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal contradiction**: The author condemns presentism while engaging in presentist moral judgment of woke culture. Criticizing "wokeness" using contemporary conservative/classical liberal values is itself a presentist evaluation. The defense of Spanish colonization also selectively emphasizes positive aspects (legal protections, mestizaje) while minimizing negative ones, mirroring the selective attention criticized in woke historiography.
- **Tension with Ikkos (2023)** : Ikkos argues that the primary driver of historical inquiry should be discovery of uncomfortable truths, invoking Benjamin’s *Jetztzeit* to justify present-oriented engagement. This article argues the opposite—that presentist judgment distorts history and destroys cultural identity.
- **Tension with Bashkow (2019)** : Bashkow advocates "enlightened presentism" as a way to recover neglected ancestors for current theory. Gil sees presentism as inherently destructive and refuses any positive role for present-oriented historical judgment.
- **Tension with Lepping & Poole (2022)** : They argue psychiatry needs *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* (honest reckoning with its eugenic past). Gil’s essay could be interpreted as resisting such reckoning for Spanish colonization, though she acknowledges abuses occurred.
- **Caveats acknowledged**: The author concedes "great evils and crimes" were committed by colonial powers; notes that the Church has apologized (three popes); acknowledges statue removals are not new (Lenin example); grants that Las Casas’s exaggerations contributed to the Black Legend. These concessions moderate but do not resolve the underlying tension.

## Open Questions

- Is "woke culture" a coherent analytical concept, or an externally imposed label that obscures internal diversity within movements for racial and social justice? The article treats it as monolithic, but critics might argue it conflates distinct phenomena.
- Can presentism ever be justified in historical scholarship, or does it always distort? The article takes a strong anti-presentist position, but other sources in the wiki (Ikkos, Bashkow) argue for conditional or enlightened forms of presentism—this tension remains unresolved.
- Does the author’s defense of Spanish colonization constitute a form of "reverse presentism"—judging the 16th century positively by modern standards (civilization, law, faith) while criticizing woke activists for judging it negatively by modern standards? Both positions are presentist in their evaluative framework; the difference is direction, not method.
- What empirical evidence exists for the claim that cancel culture significantly threatens democratic institutions? The article relies on anecdote and assertion; systematic data on the prevalence, impact, and public perception of cancel culture is lacking.
- How should societies balance the need to acknowledge historical injustices (e.g., colonization, slavery) with the recognition that historical figures cannot be held to contemporary standards? This normative question is raised but not resolved.
