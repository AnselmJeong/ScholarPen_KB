# Mental Illness Becomes Ubiquitous

## Summary

This chapter examines the dramatic expansion of the concept of mental illness in the United States from Freud's death in 1939 through the mid-1960s. It argues that a confluence of forces — internal developments in dynamic psychiatry, the experiences of military psychiatrists during World War II, the creation of new diagnostic manuals (DSM-I and DSM-II), and an activist federal government — combined to blur the boundary between sanity and madness and to portray mental illness as a widespread, near-universal phenomenon. The analysis is based on a synthesis of secondary historical literature (Grob, Hale, Makari, Lunbeck) and primary sources including wartime reports, DSM texts, and writings of key figures like Karl Menninger and Karen Horney.

The chapter is structured around four main drivers of this expansion. First, within psychiatry, the rise of ego psychology and personality disorders shifted focus from severe psychosis to maladaptive character patterns that were common in the general population. The neo-Freudians (Horney, Fromm) popularized the view that neurosis stemmed from social and cultural contradictions, not innate drives. Second, World War II demonstrated that nearly all soldiers broke down under sustained combat stress, leading to the conclusion that "every man has his breaking point." This environmental, situational view of mental illness replaced earlier hereditary and constitutional theories. Third, the DSM-I (1952) and DSM-II (1968) reflected psychodynamic assumptions, used vague "reaction" concepts, and lacked operational criteria — making it easy to classify a wide range of everyday distress as mental disorder. Fourth, new federal programs and the Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry (GAP) promoted community-based mental health for entire populations, not just identified patients.

The chapter's significance lies in its documentation of a pivotal period when psychiatry maximized its cultural authority and diagnostic reach — a reach that would later provoke the anti-psychiatry backlash and the subsequent DSM-III revolution of 1980. It also highlights the temporary dominance of a dimensional, environmental model that has since re-emerged among contemporary researchers (e.g., HiTOP, RDoC). The analysis is well-supported by historical evidence, though it presents a largely top-down narrative focused on professional and institutional actors.

## Key Entities

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (type: organization) — Published DSM-I and DSM-II; received President Truman's letter linking sanity to peace.
- **Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry (GAP)** (type: organization) — Formed by WWII psychiatrists; advocated population-level mental health and community interventions.
- **National Committee on Mental Hygiene** (type: organization) — Co-founded by Adolf Meyer and Clifford Beers; early effort to extend psychiatry to children and families.
- **Statistical Manual for the Use of Hospitals for Mental Diseases** (type: tool) — Predecessor to DSM; used in mental hospitals from 1918 to 1942.
- **DSM-I (1952)** (type: tool) — First postwar diagnostic manual; psychodynamic orientation, reaction concept, functional-organic distinction.
- **DSM-II (1968)** (type: tool) — Maintained DSM-I's orientation; dropped "reaction" term; few operational criteria.
- **U.S. Federal Government** (type: organization) — New role via New Deal, WWII, and postwar community mental health programs.

## Key Concepts

- **Mental hygiene movement** — Early 20th-century effort to identify and treat mental illness in its early stages, targeting children and families; expanded psychiatry beyond asylums.
- **Ego psychology** — Post-Freudian school focusing on the ego's mastery of environment and adaptation; dominated American dynamic psychiatry from the 1940s to 1960s.
- **Personality disorders** — Stable, maladaptive patterns of behavior distinct from classic neuroses; often not distressing to the person; blurred boundary between health and illness.
- **Neo-Freudianism** — School (Horney, Fromm) emphasizing social and cultural causes of neurosis over biological instincts; popularized environmental causation in the 1940s–1950s.
- **Dimensional view of mental illness** — Belief that mental illness exists on a continuum from mild to severe, with no sharp boundary between health and disorder; championed by Karl Menninger.
- **PIE principles** — Proximity, Immediacy, Expectancy; WWII military psychiatry approach that influenced postwar community care and early intervention ideology.
- **Reaction concept** — Meyerian term used in DSM-I/II for functional disorders (e.g., "depressive reaction"), implying they are responses to life events rather than disease entities.
- **Basic anxiety** (Horney) — Fundamental feeling of being small, helpless, endangered in a hostile world; root of neurosis from cultural contradictions.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Between 1939 and the mid-1960s, the concept of mental illness in the U.S. expanded dramatically to cover nearly the entire population.
  - Evidence: Four forces — dynamic psychiatry's focus on personality disorders and neuroses; WWII showing normal people break under stress; creation of DSM-I/II with psychodynamic classification; new federal involvement (GAP, community mental health).
  - Strength: Moderate to strong; based on scholarly historical synthesis with citations to primary and secondary sources. The argument is coherent and well-supported but interpretive.

- **Core claim**: The dominant view during this period was dimensional and environmental, later repudiated after 1980 but re-emerging in contemporary research.
  - Evidence: Karl Menninger's assertion that "there is only one class of mental illness"; DSM-I/II definitions focused on underlying anxiety and life stressors, not symptoms; WWII evidence that situational factors, not predisposition, predicted breakdown.
  - Strength: Well-supported by wartime data (Beebe/Apple: 88 days average breakdown; Swank/Marchand: <2% exception) and DSM texts. The contemporary re-emergence is noted but not cited in the truncated excerpt.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The environmental/dimensional view is portrayed as both a successful expansion of psychiatry's reach and a source of later vulnerability (lack of scientific rigor, overreach), creating an internal tension.
- DSM-I/II's vagueness is described as appropriate for its clinical context (individualized care, no third-party requirements) but also a liability that made the profession vulnerable to criticism.
- The claim that "everyone has a breaking point" (WWII) directly contradicted the earlier hereditarian view; the chapter notes the return of biological approaches after 1980, but the environmental paradigm's decline is left as a historical fact rather than fully explained.
- Neo-Freudians gained popular acclaim (best-selling books) but were expelled from analytic institutes, highlighting a tension between cultural influence and professional legitimacy.
- The chapter implies a sharp break between DSM-II and DSM-III, but other sources may complicate this narrative (e.g., gradual changes in research practices).
