# The Emergence of Psychiatry: 1650–1850

## Summary

This article traces the historical emergence of Western psychiatry as a medical specialty between the late 18th and early 19th centuries. The authors, Kendler, Tabb, and Wright, argue that psychiatry's birth was not inevitable but a contingent outcome of three converging historical developments: the creation of a conceptual space for the disordered mind (separate from both the body and the Christian soul), the growing acceptance of the brain as the organ of mental illness, and the rise of the asylum as a dedicated institution for care and clinical observation. The study focuses on the contributions of five key figures—Descartes, Willis, Locke, Boerhaave, de Sauvages, and Cullen—to illustrate how philosophical concepts of mind and medical theories of the brain jointly shaped proto-psychiatry.

The authors demonstrate that the first specialized therapy developed by the nascent specialty was a psychosocial one: moral therapy, as practiced by Pinel in Paris and the Tukes at the York Retreat. This therapy treated the mind and the social environment, not just the body. Simultaneously, the specialty cemented its medical identity by grounding itself in the brain, requiring all asylum superintendents to be physicians. This dual focus created a persistent tension between mind-oriented and brain-oriented approaches.

The main finding is that psychiatry has never been a purely mind-based or brain-based discipline; it has always been jointly constituted by both projects. The article challenges origin stories that describe a linear progression from psychological to biological models, arguing instead that the tension between them is foundational and productive. The authors advocate for an "integrative pluralistic approach" or "binocularity" that embraces this dual heritage as the unique charge of the discipline, from lab to clinic.

## Key Entities

- **York Retreat** (type: org) — A Quaker institution founded in 1796 near York, England, where moral therapy was developed and practiced by William and Samuel Tuke. It is presented as a key site for the first specialized psychosocial therapy of the young specialty.
- **St. Luke's asylum** (type: org) — A London asylum founded in 1750 as a rival to Bethlem Hospital. Its physician, William Battie, published an early treatise on madness that argued for limited use of traditional medical cures.
- **Bethlem Hospital** (type: org) — A historic London asylum (infamously known as "Bedlam"), serving as a reference point for early psychiatric practice and the work of physician John Haslam.
- **Association of Medical Officers of Asylums and Hospitals for the Insane** (type: org) — The precursor organization to the Royal College of Psychiatrists, founded in 1841. Its name reflects the centrality of the asylum to the emerging profession.
- **Association of Medical Superintendents of American Institutions for the Insane** (type: org) — The precursor organization to the American Psychiatric Association, founded in 1844. Its name likewise highlights the institutional basis of early psychiatry.

## Key Concepts

- **Moral therapy** — The first specialized treatment developed by proto-psychiatry, emerging in the late 18th and early 19th centuries. It is a psychosocial therapy that focuses on the doctor-patient relationship, the therapeutic milieu, and treating the patient as a rational agent, rather than relying on physical restraints or harsh medications. It treats the psyche, not the brain.
- **Nosology** — The systematic classification of diseases. In this source, the development of psychiatric nosologies (e.g., by de Sauvages and Cullen) is seen as critical to the profession's emergence, as it provided physicians with a specialized clinical vocabulary grounded in faculties of the mind.
- **Mind-brain dualism** — The conceptual and professional tension in psychiatry between approaches that focus on the mind (mental faculties, ideas, emotions) and those that focus on the brain (neuroanatomy, physiology, biology). This source argues this dualism is not a modern problem but a foundational, constitutive feature of psychiatry.
- **Therapeutic milieu** — A concept embedded in moral therapy, referring to the creation of a supportive, humane, and structured environment within the asylum that itself is therapeutic.
- **Animal spirits** — An early modern physiological concept referring to a subtle fluid or spirit thought to flow through the nerves and brain, mediating between the soul and the body. Used by Willis and Descartes to explain madness as a disorder of these spirits.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The emergence of Western psychiatry was a contingent historical event, not an inevitable or predestined development, dependent on the co-occurrence of three factors: a conceptual space for the disordered mind, the brain as the organ of madness, and the asylum.
  - **Evidence**: The article traces the historical genealogy of these three developments through the works of Descartes, Willis, Locke, Boerhaave, de Sauvages, and Cullen, showing how each condition was necessary for the profession's formation.
  - **Strength**: Strong. The argument is built on a comprehensive review of primary and secondary historical sources, providing a detailed and nuanced narrative rather than a simplistic claim.

- **Core claim**: Psychiatry has a dual, "bifocal" identity constituted by attention to both the mind and the brain, and this tension is a persistent, foundational feature of the field, not a weakness.
  - **Evidence**: The article shows that the first specialized therapy (moral therapy) was psychosocial, treating the mind, while the profession simultaneously cemented its medical identity by grounding itself in the brain. This dual focus is traced back to the work of Locke (mind) and Willis (brain).
  - **Strength**: Strong. The evidence spans the entire period under review, demonstrating the consistent coexistence of these two perspectives. However, the article acknowledges that the relationship has been one of alternating dominance and tension.

- **Core claim**: Philosophy played a crucial role in the early formation of psychiatry by providing the conceptual tools and vocabulary (e.g., faculties of the mind) necessary for describing and classifying mental disorders.
  - **Evidence**: The influence of Locke's philosophy on Cullen's understanding of madness and the role of Enlightenment thought in the work of Pinel and other alienists is highlighted. The authors note that early psychiatrists saw themselves as *médecins-philosophes*.
  - **Strength**: Strong. The article provides concrete examples of how philosophical concepts were directly integrated into medical theory and practice.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The article itself acknowledges an internal tension in its narrative: while it argues that psychiatry is necessarily a joint project of mind and brain, the historical evidence it presents is one of continual conflict, alternating dominance, and attempts by one side to expel the other. The authors resolve this by arguing this tension is "healthy" and constitutive, but this normative claim is open to debate.
- This source contradicts any historical narrative that presents psychiatry as a simple linear progression from psychological to biological models (or vice versa). It argues that both perspectives have been present and foundational since the profession's origin.
- There is a potential tension with narratives that date the psychological tradition primarily to the 20th century with Freud. This source roots the psychological (mind-based) approach in 18th-century philosophy and moral therapy.
- The source focuses exclusively on Western European and American developments, raising an implicit question about the applicability of its "mind-brain" narrative to non-Western traditions of healing mental illness.

## Open Questions

- Does the "mind-brain dualism" narrative hold for non-Western contexts of psychiatry, or is it a distinctly Western product of Enlightenment philosophy and Christian theology?
- The article advocates for "integrative pluralism" or "binocularity" as a normative ideal. Is this a satisfying resolution to the long-standing tension, or does it simply reframe a fundamental problem? What would a successful integration look like in practice?
- The article traces the influence of philosophy on early psychiatry but notes its decline in the 20th century. How can the alliance between philosophy and psychiatry be effectively revived in contemporary training and research?
- The source argues that the asylum was essential for the creation of a specialized clinical vocabulary. In the current era of deinstitutionalization, what institutional structures are now providing the observational base for psychiatric knowledge?
