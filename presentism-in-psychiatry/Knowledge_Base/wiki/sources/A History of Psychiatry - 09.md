# From Freud to Prozac

## Summary

This chapter (Chapter 8 of Edward Shorter's *A History of Psychiatry*) offers a critical historical analysis of American psychiatry from the 1970s through the 1990s. It argues that despite a turn toward biological thinking and the neurosciences, psychiatry failed to become genuinely science-driven. The main obstacle was the profession's enmeshment in market competition, popular culture, and political lobbying—forces that pushed psychiatry toward expanding the definition of mental illness to include everyday distress and subthreshold symptoms.

Shorter synthesizes a wide range of evidence: statistical data on therapy-seeking (22 million Americans per year by the 1980s), comparative cross-national diagnostic studies, historical accounts of the DSM revision process, and insider testimony from key figures (Robert Wallerstein, Hagop Akiskal, Daniel Freedman). He also traces the rise of nonmedical competitors (clinical psychologists and social workers) who captured much of the psychotherapy market, incentivizing psychiatrists to lower diagnostic thresholds and pathologize normal behaviors such as boyhood exuberance, anxiety from scary movies, and ordinary unhappiness.

The chapter culminates in a detailed examination of the development of DSM-III (1980), the Feighner criteria (1972), and the Research Diagnostic Criteria. Shorter argues that DSM-III, while intended as a science-driven turn away from psychoanalysis, was heavily shaped by political pressures from interest groups (psychoanalysts, gay activists, Vietnam veterans, feminists). The result was a diagnostic system that multiplied disorders (from 180 in DSM-II to 297 in DSM-IV) without a corresponding increase in validity, ultimately leading psychiatry into a "wilderness" of overdiagnosis and professional disorientation.

The significance of this chapter lies in its forceful critique of psychiatric empire-building and the medicalization of normal life. It provides a historical foundation for debates about overdiagnosis, the validity of DSM categories, and the professional identity crisis of psychiatry at the turn of the twenty-first century.

## Key Entities

- **American Psychiatric Association** (org) — Central actor in DSM development and political lobbying for market share.
- **Washington University of St. Louis (Psychiatry Department)** (org) — Seed of the neo-Kraepelinian counterrevolution; home to Robins, Guze, Winokur, Feighner.
- **Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry** (org) — Professional body that lamented psychotherapy's nonmedical dominance.
- **National Comorbidity Survey (NCS)** (dataset) — Provided lifetime prevalence estimate of 48% for psychiatric disorders.
- **Ontario mental health survey (1990)** (dataset) — Found 42% of help-seekers had no mental disorder.
- **Feighner Criteria (1972)** (tool) — First operational criteria for psychiatric diagnosis; revolutionized standardization.
- **Research Diagnostic Criteria (RDC)** (tool) — Spitzer's refinement of the Feighner criteria, used in DSM-III field trials.
- **DSM-I (1952)** (tool) — First APA diagnostic manual, psychoanalytic in orientation.
- **DSM-II (1968)** (tool) — Strengthened psychoanalytic influence; used "neurosis" and "hysteria."
- **DSM-III (1980)** (tool) — Landmark revision operationalizing criteria; 265 disorders; politically contested.
- **DSM-III-R (1987)** (tool) — Revision adding 292 disorders; further political battles (self-defeating personality disorder, late luteal phase dysphoric disorder).
- **DSM-IV (1994)** (tool) — Final edition covered in this chapter; 297 disorders.
- **Statistical Manual for the Use of Institutions for the Insane (1918)** (tool) — First American psychiatric nosology.
- **Standard Classified Nomenclature of Disease (1933)** (tool) — Collaborative national nomenclature; APA contributed psychiatric part.
- **Prozac (fluoxetine)** (tool) — Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor that contributed to increased depression diagnosis.
- **Ritalin (methylphenidate)** (tool) — Amphetamine-like compound prescribed for ADHD; 6 million prescriptions/year by 1995.

## Key Concepts

- **Diagnostic expansion / threshold lowering** — The systematic reduction of criteria required for a mental illness diagnosis, extending pathology into normal behavior and unhappiness. Shorter argues this was driven by economic self-interest and competition.
- **Diagnostic imperialism / empire-building** — Psychiatry's effort to maintain market share against nonmedical competitors by pathologizing ever more conditions (boyhood, stress, personality traits).
- **Psychologizing vs. medicalizing vs. socializing distress** — Three frameworks for understanding personal problems. Post-1970s shift toward psychologizing (nonmedical therapy) reduced psychiatry's market share.
- **Neo-Kraepelinianism** — The St. Louis–based movement that revived Kraepelin's emphasis on descriptive diagnosis, biological causation, and empirical research criteria; intellectual engine behind DSM-III.
- **Operational criteria** — Explicit, measurable symptom checklists (e.g., Feighner criteria, RDC) that replaced vague clinical judgment, enabling reliable diagnosis across clinicians and countries.
- **Subthreshold symptoms** — Symptoms falling below full diagnostic criteria; a frontier for reaching "previously subthreshold patients" and expanding the patient base.
- **Politics of diagnosis** — The recognition that diagnostic categories are shaped by political pressure from interest groups (gay activists, Vietnam veterans, feminists, psychoanalysts) as much as by scientific evidence. Shorter uses homosexuality removal, PTSD inclusion, and the "neurosis" compromise as prime examples.
- **Diagnostic indifference / nihilism** — The psychoanalytic-era attitude that diagnosis was unimportant compared to psychodynamic understanding; the contrast that made DSM-III's emphasis on classification revolutionary.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: Psychiatry failed to become genuinely science-driven despite embracing biology.**
  - Evidence: Diagnostic expansion was driven by economic competition, not scientific evidence; DSM-III was a political document as much as a scientific one (homosexuality removal, neurosis compromise, PTSD inclusion); the number of disorders multiplied without validation.
  - Strength: Strong historical narrative with multiple specific cases and insider quotes; moderate-weak because Shorter does not systematically consider counter-evidence (e.g., genuine scientific advances behind some expansions).
- **Core claim: The threshold of mental illness dropped dramatically, pathologizing normal behavior.**
  - Evidence: Examples—boyhood exuberance → ADHD (Tom Sawyer → brain damage), anxiety from movies → PTSD (Batman), unhappiness → depression; statistical data on Ritalin prescriptions (2.5 million children) and rising depression diagnoses.
  - Strength: Vivid and persuasive examples; weakness—no control for possible genuine increases in prevalence or improved detection.
- **Core claim: DSM-III was a political document shaped by interest groups.**
  - Evidence: Homosexuality removed by referendum; "neurosis" restored under pressure from analysts; PTSD added due to veteran lobbying; self-defeating personality disorder and late luteal phase dysphoric disorder altered by feminist pressure.
  - Strength: Well-documented timeline and decision points; directly shows politics overruling science.
- **Core claim: The decline of psychoanalysis worsened psychiatry's disorientation rather than clearing the path for science.**
  - Evidence: Proliferation of 130+ competing psychotherapies with similar success rates; residency training shifted from 3,000 hours of psychotherapy (1950s) to 200 hours (1990s); loss of a unifying theoretical framework.
  - Strength: Supported by training data and Wallerstein's testimony; plausible but not causally proven.
- **Core claim: Psychiatry lost market share to nonmedical competitors despite expanding diagnoses.**
  - Evidence: 80,000 clinical social workers vs. 32,000 psychiatrists by 1985; 42% of Canadian therapy-seekers had no disorder; NCS found only 21% of those with disorders were in treatment.
  - Strength: Statistical evidence supports the trend; the causal link to diagnostic expansion is inferential.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Science vs. market**: The chapter claims DSM-III was science-driven in intention but politics-driven in execution; these compete as explanatory frames for why psychiatry "lost its way."
- **Biological psychiatry vs. diagnostic expansion**: If biological psychiatry was the "high road," why did psychiatrists abandon it for subthreshold diagnosis? The chapter's answer (economic competition) implies biology alone was insufficient to sustain the profession.
- **Critique of over-diagnosis vs. recognition of genuine illness**: Shorter acknowledges that major depression and schizophrenia are real while arguing their boundaries have been illegitimately expanded; no clear demarcation is offered between legitimate and over-diagnosis.
- **Psychiatry's agency vs. social demand**: The chapter blames both psychiatrists (for empire-building) and patients (for demanding psychotherapy for unhappiness). It is unclear whether psychiatry is driving or responding to the trend of psychologizing distress.

## Open Questions

- Does the available evidence support the claim that diagnostic expansion was *primarily* driven by economic self-interest rather than by genuine clinical need or scientific evolution?
- To what extent were the Feighner criteria and Research Diagnostic Criteria genuinely validated versus merely more reliable (consistent) without being valid?
- Do the specific examples (ADHD, PTSD, depression expansion) hold up under scrutiny, or are they selectively chosen to support the argument? Are there counterexamples where diagnostic narrowing occurred?
- Is there evidence that psychiatry's loss of market share to psychologists/social workers was actually reversed by DSM-III and biological psychiatry, or did the trend continue?
- How does cross-national adoption of DSM-III (mentioned briefly) compare with the American experience in terms of diagnostic expansion and professional competition?
