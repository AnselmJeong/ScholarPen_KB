# A History of Psychiatry - 09

## Summary

This chapter, "From Freud to Prozac," examines the trajectory of American psychiatry from the decline of psychoanalysis through the DSM-III revolution and its aftermath. The central argument is that despite the promise of a science-driven, biologically grounded psychiatry at the end of the twentieth century, the discipline lost its way by pathologizing normal unhappiness to compete with nonmedical psychotherapists. The chapter blends historical narrative, quantitative data, and political analysis to critique how diagnostic expansion—lowering the threshold for mental illness—became a strategy for maintaining professional market share.

The source draws on a rich array of evidence: national comorbidity surveys showing 48% lifetime prevalence but only 21% treatment rates; studies revealing 42% of therapy clients had no disorder; international diagnostic variability (69% vs 2% schizophrenia diagnosis rates between US and UK); and detailed accounts of the political struggles shaping DSM-III (homosexuality, neurosis, PTSD, self-defeating personality disorder). It traces the intellectual roots of the diagnostic counterrevolution to the Neo-Kraepelinians at Washington University, the Feighner criteria, and Robert Spitzer's leadership of the DSM-III task force.

The chapter's significance lies in its unflinching critique of the DSM project as simultaneously scientific and political. It argues that while DSM-III aimed to ground psychiatry in operational criteria and medical model legitimacy, the process was compromised by interest-group lobbying and the profession's anxiety about losing patients to psychologists and social workers. The result was a diagnostic inflation that blurred the line between illness and ordinary distress, leading the author to conclude that psychiatry "lost its way" in the wilderness of diagnostic scientism and market competition.

## Key Entities

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (org) — Central professional body that developed and sponsored the DSM series
- **DSM-I, DSM-II, DSM-III, DSM-III-R, DSM-IV** (tool) — Successive editions of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, each reflecting the dominant theoretical orientations and political compromises of their era
- **National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)** (org) — Funded field trials for DSM-III and sponsored population-based depression screening programs
- **Feighner Criteria / Research Diagnostic Criteria** (tool) — First set of rigorous operational criteria for psychiatric diagnosis, developed by the St. Louis group in 1972
- **Washington University (St. Louis) – Neo-Kraepelinians** (org) — Intellectual birthplace of the diagnostic counterrevolution; home to Eli Robins, George Winokur, Samuel Guze, and John Feighner
- **National Comorbidity Survey (NCS)** (dataset) — Early-1990s random sample providing prevalence and treatment rate data
- **Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry** (org) — Professional group that lamented the nonmedical takeover of psychotherapy
- **National Association of Social Work** (org) — Representative body of social workers competing with psychiatrists
- **American Psychological Association** (org) — Professional organization for psychologists, whose clinical branch grew to compete directly with psychiatry
- **Maudsley Hospital** (org) — Institution where Erwin Stengel landed after fleeing Vienna
- **New York Academy of Medicine** (org) — Initiated standard medical nomenclature in 1927
- **National Committee for Mental Hygiene** (org) — Collaborated on the first US psychiatric nosology (1918)

## Key Concepts

- **Diagnostic Expansion / Threshold Lowering** — The deliberate broadening of illness definitions to capture more patients, seen as psychiatry’s strategy for maintaining market share against nonmedical therapists.
- **Subthreshold Symptoms** — Clinical features below the formal diagnostic cutoff, targeted for inclusion to reach "previously subthreshold patients."
- **Psychologizing vs. Medicalizing** — Framing distress as psychological (amenable to nonmedical therapy) rather than as medical illness; explains why psychiatry lost market share.
- **Neo-Kraepelinianism** — Movement to return to Kraepelin's symptom-based disease entity approach; intellectual foundation of DSM-III.
- **Operational Criteria** — Explicit, fixed symptom lists required for diagnosis, pioneered by the Feighner criteria and refined by Spitzer.
- **Politics of Diagnosis** — The influence of interest groups (veterans, feminists, gay activists, psychoanalysts) on what counts as a mental disorder.
- **Biopsychosocial Model** — Engel's model integrating biological, psychological, and social factors; the source views it as enabling diagnostic mischief by blurring boundaries between illness and normal distress.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Psychiatry's "scientific" turn (DSM-III) was undermined by empire-building; diagnostic boundaries expanded to pathologize normal unhappiness.
  - Evidence: Increase from 180 disorders (DSM-II) to 297 (DSM-IV); 42% of therapy clients had no disorder (Ontario study); 2.5 million children on Ritalin by 1995; NIMH depression screening programs described as empire-building.
  - Strength: Strongly supported by quantitative and historical data, but the interpretation is openly critical and does not engage counterarguments about underdiagnosis.

- **Core claim**: Diagnostic inflation is driven by market competition with nonmedical therapists.
  - Evidence: Psychologists and social workers vastly outnumbered psychiatrists by the 1990s (80,000 clinical social workers vs 32,000 psychiatrists); psychiatrists pushed threshold downward to retain clients.
  - Strength: Plausible but partly circumstantial; patient demand also plays a role, which the author acknowledges but does not fully explore.

- **Core claim**: DSM-III was as much a political document as a scientific one.
  - Evidence: Lobbying by gay activists to remove homosexuality; psychoanalysts to retain "neurosis"; Vietnam veterans to add PTSD; feminists to remove "self-defeating personality disorder."
  - Strength: Well-documented with specific dates, committees, and outcomes; shows direct influence of interest groups on diagnostic categories.

- **Core claim**: The decline of psychoanalysis created disorientation rather than scientific clarity.
  - Evidence: Proliferation of 130+ competing psychotherapies with similar success rates; Hans Eysenck's finding that none improved significantly on chance; collapse of the biopsychosocial model's psychological anchor.
  - Strength: Supported by historical trends and Wallerstein's testimony; the normative judgment that this constituted "losing the way" is an interpretive stance.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The author's stance on science is ambiguous: biological psychiatry (neuroscience) is presented as the "high road," yet DSM's attempt to be science-driven is criticized for producing 297 disorders. The critique seems to fault psychiatry for being simultaneously too scientific (scientism) and not scientific enough (politicized).
- Competing explanations for diagnostic expansion: the source emphasizes doctor-driven lowering of thresholds but also acknowledges patient-driven demand for psychologizing distress, without fully resolving the tension.
- The decline of psychoanalysis is portrayed as both liberating (freeing psychiatry from dogma) and disorienting (creating a vacuum filled by faddish therapies).

## Open Questions

- Is diagnostic expansion primarily a US phenomenon, or does the same pattern hold in other national contexts (UK, France, Germany)?
- How does this critique relate to the presentism/historicism debate in the broader research project? The author is arguably writing presentist history—judging 20th-century psychiatry against an ideal of pure science.
- Does the evidence better support "doctor-driven" or "patient-driven" diagnostic expansion? The NCS data on 48% lifetime prevalence could support demand-side explanations that the source downplays.
- What is the strongest counterargument to the empire-building thesis? The chapter lacks engagement with defenders of DSM expansion who argue that mental illness is genuinely underdiagnosed and threshold-lowering improves public health.
