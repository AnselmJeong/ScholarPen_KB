# Medieval and Early Modern Europe

## Summary
This chapter from *From Madness to Mental Health* presents five primary documents illustrating how madness and melancholy were understood in medieval and early modern Europe (1st–17th centuries). The texts span clinical case histories from Rufus of Ephesus via a 9th-century Arabic compendium, Ibn Sina’s diagnostic method for lovesickness, Julian of Norwich’s first-person account of religious visions, Erasmus’s satirical *Praise of Folly*, and Robert Burton’s encyclopedic *Anatomy of Melancholy*. Together they reveal a remarkably stable humoral framework that persisted for nearly two millennia.

The documents are presented with editorial introductions that frame historiographical questions, particularly the boundary between religious transcendence and madness. The research design is a curated source collection rather than an empirical study; its strength lies in juxtaposing clinical, spiritual, and satirical perspectives. Key findings include the medicalization of ordinary experiences (love, aging, religious devotion) through humoral theory, the existence of sophisticated diagnostic techniques such as pulse-reading for lovesickness, and Burton’s universalizing claim that melancholy is the “character of mortality” from which no human is exempt.

The significance of this source is twofold: it demonstrates the long continuity of humoral biological psychiatry from antiquity to the 17th century, and it poses persistent questions about how societies adjudicate between legitimate spiritual experience and pathological madness—questions that resonate with modern debates about the definition of mental disorder and the limits of medicalization.

## Key Concepts
- **Humoral Theory / Black Bile Theory** — The medical framework, shared by all texts, that melancholia arises from excess or corruption of black bile, one of four bodily humors. Treatments aim to evacuate the corrupted humor and restore temperamental balance.
- **Lovesickness (Ibn Sina)** — A delusional illness resembling melancholia, caused by obsessive fixation on a beloved. Diagnosed by pulse irregularity when the beloved’s name is mentioned; treated by union, distraction, deception, and moisturizing diet.
- **Folly as Cultural Critique (Erasmus)** — The personified goddess Folly claims credit for all human life, pleasure, and social order. Erasmus uses her voice to satirize Stoic wisdom, Church institutions, and the pretense of rationality, showing “madness” as a rhetorical device for social criticism.
- **Melancholy as Universal Human Condition (Burton)** — Melancholy is “the character of mortality” — a transitory sadness that affects all humans, including sages like Socrates. It is a mixed passion of pleasure and pain, inherent to life rather than a pathological exception.
- **Religious Experience vs. Madness Boundary** — The problem of distinguishing legitimate spiritual visions (Julian) from pathological insanity. The editor explicitly invites comparison with Saul, Jesus, Schreber, and Thea H., raising questions about how communities define the line between transcendence and madness.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Melancholia was understood as a physical brain disease caused by humoral imbalance, not a spiritual or moral failing.
  - Evidence: Rufus located melancholia in the brain; treatments focused on evacuating corrupted black bile and moistening the body.
  - Strength: Strong — multiple detailed clinical cases with treatment rationales.
- **Core claim**: Lovesickness was a recognized medical diagnosis with a sophisticated, replicable diagnostic technique.
  - Evidence: Ibn Sina describes pulse-reading while reciting names, claims to have used the method successfully.
  - Strength: Moderate — method relies on subjective pulse interpretation, but described as empirical.
- **Core claim**: Religious visionary experience sits at the boundary of madness and transcendence; the distinction is socially negotiated.
  - Evidence: Julian’s case is presented without resolution; the editor links it to other historical figures.
  - Strength: Moderate — Julian’s account is rich but mediated by theological interpretation.
- **Core claim**: Folly served as a cultural and moral category beyond pathology; a rhetorical tool for social critique.
  - Evidence: Erasmus uses Folly to lampoon Church and state, showing how “madness” could be a vantage point.
  - Strength: Moderate — literary and satirical, not clinical, but culturally significant.
- **Core claim**: Melancholy was universal and existential — no one is exempt, it is inherent to human life.
  - Evidence: Burton lists Socrates, Zeno, Cato as examples; frames melancholy as “the character of mortality.”
  - Strength: Strong — Burton synthesizes centuries of medical and philosophical authority.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal tension in Rufus’s cases**: The first physician’s aggressive evacuation harmed the patient; the second’s moistening and nourishment helped. Rufus credits both, creating ambiguity about which treatment was truly effective.
- **Ibn Sina’s pulse diagnosis**: Claims empirical replicability (“I have tried this method several times”) but the method relies on subjective interpretation of pulse variability — a tension between claimed objectivity and clinical judgment.
- **Julian’s own framing**: She states she desired “no bodily sight nor shewing of God” yet received such a vision — a tension between her stated intentions and actual experience.
- **Erasmus’s performative contradiction**: Folly praises foolishness while the text itself is a profoundly learned intellectual work, undercutting its own argument.
- **Conflict with existing wiki**: The [[concepts/biomedical-model]] page may frame biological psychiatry as emerging in the 19th–20th centuries, but this source shows a thoroughly biological (humoral) model operating from antiquity — challenging a simple narrative of modern invention.
- **Conflict with existing wiki**: [[concepts/moral-treatment]] is often presented as an Enlightenment innovation, but Rufus’s treatment (entertainment, removal of sadness) anticipates it by 1,600 years.

## Open Questions
- Was lovesickness “real” as a disease, or a social construction serving to medicalize romantic obsession? The editor’s question is deliberately left unanswered.
- How should we characterize the relationship between humoral biological psychiatry and modern biological psychiatry — continuity, rupture, or both?
- Does Julian’s case strengthen or weaken the claim that the category of mental illness is historically contingent?
- What does Erasmus’s satirical use of folly reveal about the social functions of calling someone “mad” — who gains power from the label?
