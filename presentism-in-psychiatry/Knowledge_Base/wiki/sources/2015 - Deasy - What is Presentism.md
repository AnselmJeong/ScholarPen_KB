# What is Presentism?

## Summary
Daniel Deasy investigates the logical foundations of the A-theories of time, specifically focusing on the traditional definition of presentism: the claim that "always, everything is present." He argues that this definition is fundamentally obscure because no plausible interpretation of the predicate "is present" successfully distinguishes presentism from its rivals or avoids triviality and absurdity.

Through a systematic "stress test" of seven candidate interpretations of presentness, Deasy demonstrates that such definitions either lead to contradictions (e.g., implying Napoleon exists now), are consistent with the B-theory, or are trivially true. He concludes that the linguistic framework used in most textbooks to define A-theories is broken.

To resolve this, Deasy proposes a shift in focus from "presentness" to the nature of existence over time. He suggests redefining the A-theories based on whether entities begin or cease to exist. This transition moves the debate from a static ontological claim about what is "real" to a dynamic claim about the transience of existence.

Finally, Deasy extends this logical critique to the realm of modal metaphysics, arguing that "actualism" suffers from the same definitional flaws as presentism and should similarly be redefined in terms of contingent existence.

## Key Entities
- **A-theory / B-theory** (tool/conceptual framework) — The primary metaphysical frameworks for debating the nature of time.
- **Presentism** (tool/theory) — A version of A-theory asserting only the present is real.
- **Growing Block Theory** (tool/theory) — A version of A-theory asserting the past and present are real, but the future is not.
- **Moving Spotlight Theory** (tool/theory) — A version of A-theory asserting the past, present, and future are all real, but the present is privileged.
- **Actualism** (tool/theory) — The modal analogue to presentism, asserting only the actual world exists.

## Key Concepts
- **Transientism** — The view that sometimes something begins to exist and sometimes something ceases to exist; proposed as the core of Presentism.
- **Pastism** — The view that sometimes something begins to exist, but nothing ever ceases to exist; proposed as the core of Growing Block Theory.
- **Permanentism** — The view that nothing ever begins or ceases to exist; proposed as the core of Moving Spotlight Theory.
- **Restrictor Principle** — The theory that temporal operators (like "now") act as implicit quantifiers restricting the scope of individuals to those located at that instant.
- **Property Principle** — The metaphysical principle stating that if $x$ has a property, then $x$ exists.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The traditional definitions of A-theories based on the predicate "is present" are logically flawed and should be replaced by definitions based on the beginning and end of existence.
  - Evidence: A systematic analysis of seven interpretations of "is present" showing they are either trivial (Presentness 1), inconsistent with presentist intuitions (Presentness 5/6), or compatible with B-theory (Presentness 5.5).
  - Strength: Strong (Logically). The author uses formal temporal logic to show that no plausible interpretation satisfies the needs of the theory's proponents.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension**: Deasy acknowledges that his new definitions (e.g., Transientism) might exclude some "putative" presentists, such as those who believe in "permanent simples" arranged cat-wise, but argues this is a necessary sacrifice for logical clarity.
- **External Conflict**: Directly challenges the traditional definitions provided by influential scholars such as Sider, Hare, and Callender.
