# What is Presentism?

## Summary

This paper undertakes a systematic critique of the traditional definition of presentism—the thesis that "always, everything is present"—and proposes an alternative definition. The author, Daniel Deasy, works entirely within analytic metaphysics, aiming to clarify the logical space of A-theories of time (theories that posit an absolute, objective present instant). The research method is conceptual analysis: Deasy examines seven plausible interpretations of the predicate "is present" as it appears in the traditional definition and shows that each one fails for independent reasons. The analysis proceeds by elimination, demonstrating that the traditional definition is either trivial, implausible, consistent with rival theories, or carries unwanted consequences.

The main finding is that every plausible interpretation of the traditional definition is untenable. For example, interpreting "is present" as "exists" makes presentism trivial; interpreting it as "instantiates fundamental presentness" leads to a thesis that self-described presentists reject; interpreting it as "exists now" implies that Napoleon exists now, which most presentists deny; and interpreting it as "has a spatial location" has problematic implications for abstract objects. Because the definitions of the other A-theories (growing block, moving spotlight) are parasitic on the same predicate, the problem infects all traditional A-theory definitions.

Deasy then offers a new way to define the A-theories, replacing the obscure predicate "is present" with clear theses about the beginning and end of existence. Specifically, presentism is redefined as the conjunction of the A-theory (there is an absolute, objective present) and transientism (things sometimes begin and sometimes cease to exist). The growing block theory becomes A-theory plus pastism (things sometimes begin but never cease). The moving spotlight theory becomes A-theory plus permanentism (nothing ever begins or ceases). This recasts the debate in terms of substantive, easily understood questions about whether existence has a beginning and an end. The paper also extends the argument to modal actualism, arguing for a parallel redefinition in terms of contingent existence.

## Key Concepts

- **Traditional presentism** — The thesis that "always, everything is present." Deasy argues this definition is obscure because the predicate "is present" has no satisfactory interpretation.
- **Transientism** — The thesis that sometimes something begins to exist and sometimes something ceases to exist. Deasy proposes this as the ontological component of presentism.
- **Pastism** — The thesis that sometimes something begins to exist but nothing ever ceases to exist. Deasy proposes this as the ontological component of the growing block theory.
- **Permanentism** — The thesis that always, everything always exists. Deasy proposes this as the ontological component of the moving spotlight theory.
- **A-theory** — The view that there is an absolute, objective present instant. All three A-theories share this component.
- **B-theory** — The view that there is no absolute, objective present instant. Also called eternalism.
- **Actualism** — The modal analogue of presentism, traditionally defined as "necessarily, everything is actual." Deasy argues it should be redefined in terms of contingent existence.
- **Property principle** — "Always, for all x, always: if x has a property then x exists." Used in the critique to show that certain interpretations of "present" lead to unwanted consequences.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The traditional definition of presentism ("always, everything is present") is unworkable and should be abandoned in favor of a definition as A-theory plus transientism.
  - **Evidence**: Systematic analysis of seven candidate interpretations of "is present" shows each fails: (1) existence interpretation trivializes; (2) fundamental presentness is rejected by presentists; (3) spatial location has implications for abstract objects; (4) conditional spatial location has implications for mental events; (5/5.5) "exists now" either entails Napoleon exists now or is trivial and B-theory-compatible; (6) maximal slim object entails simultaneity of Napoleon with current events; (7) simultaneity is consistent with moving spotlight theory.
  - **Strength**: The argument is logically rigorous and covers the most plausible interpretations. The weakness is that the new definition is stipulative and may exclude some views that self-identify as presentist (e.g., simplicity + permanentism). The paper concedes some vulnerability to counterexamples but argues the gain in clarity is worth the cost.
