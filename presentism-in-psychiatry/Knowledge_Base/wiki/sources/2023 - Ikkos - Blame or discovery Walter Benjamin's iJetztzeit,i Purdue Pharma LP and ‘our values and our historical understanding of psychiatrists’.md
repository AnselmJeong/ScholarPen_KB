# Blame or discovery? Walter Benjamin's Jetztzeit, Purdue Pharma LP and 'our values and our historical understanding of psychiatrists'

## Summary

This commentary by George Ikkos directly responds to Claire Hilton's paper "Our values and our historical understanding of psychiatrists," which had argued for fairness and avoiding unfair blame when evaluating historical figures in psychiatry. Ikkos challenges this framing, proposing instead that the primary purpose of historical research should be **discovery**—uncovering uncomfortable truths—rather than merely praising or blaming historical actors. The article uses Walter Benjamin's philosophy of history, particularly his concept of **Jetztzeit** (now-time), as a methodological alternative to both linear historicism and simple presentism.

Ikkos draws three key historical case examples into a single constellation: Henry Cotton's systematic organ removal to treat mental illness, psychiatrists' participation in the Nazi euthanasia programme, and the contemporary opioid crisis driven by Purdue Pharma and the Sackler family. He argues that these events, though separated by time and moral context, reveal a persistent pattern: clinical hubris combined with professional confidence and interventionist fervour can lead to catastrophic patient harm, regardless of the practitioners' motivations. The article contends that avoiding these insights because of fear, empathy, or cognitive dissonance—including loyalty to the psychiatric profession—is itself a form of historical negligence.

The piece is significant for the presentism/historicism debate because it proposes a third way: rather than choosing between judging the past by present standards (presentism) or isolating it in its own context (historicism), historians should attend to the "flashes" of recognition that occur when past and present form a constellation. Ikkos applies this directly to contemporary psychiatric institutions, noting both the American Psychiatric Association's relationship with Raymond Sackler and the ongoing Sackler Chair at University College London's Institute of Mental Health. The commentary ultimately calls for a more courageous, truth-seeking historiography that prioritises learning over professional self-protection.

## Key Entities

- **Royal College of Psychiatrists (RCPsych)** (org) — The institutional home of both the author (past Chair of its History of Psychiatry Special Interest Group) and Claire Hilton (its Historian in Residence); the article is a direct response to a paper published under its auspices.
- **Purdue Pharma LP** (org) — The central contemporary case; owned by the Sackler family, its criminal marketing of OxyContin caused hundreds of thousands of preventable deaths, used by Ikkos to argue that physician actions matter more than motivations.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (org) — Mentioned as the organisation of which Raymond Sackler was a Life Fellow, demonstrating the profession's historical entanglement with the family.
- **University College London (UCL)** (org) — Its Institute of Mental Health is led by a Sackler Chair (inaugurated 2018), cited as a contemporary institutional link to the Sackler family.
- **Tate Gallery** (org) — Cited as a cultural institution that dropped the Sackler name, illustrating broader societal reckoning.

## Key Concepts

- **Jetztzeit (Now-Time)** — Walter Benjamin's concept of a historical "now-time" in which past events are not merely precursors to the present but flash into recognisability in a moment of dialectical reversal; the historian's task is to attend to these constellations, not to construct linear narratives.
- **Discovery vs. Blame/Praise** — The core argument: historical inquiry should be driven by the pursuit of uncomfortable truths (discovery) rather than primarily motivated by fear of unfair criticism (blame) or defensive idealisation (praise).
- **Historical Image / Constellation** — Benjamin's idea that history "decays into images, not into stories"; truth emerges when a fragment of the past and a moment of the present form a constellation that produces a flash of insight.
- **Cognitive Dissonance** — Psychological discomfort arising from confronting one's own group's past failings; Ikkos warns this can obstruct honest historical inquiry.
- **Professional Identity** — The underlying tension: how psychiatrists' sense of professional continuity with predecessors can generate either protective defensiveness or productive self-critique.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The primary driver of historical research in psychiatry should be **discovery** of uncomfortable truths, not avoidance of unfair blame or pursuit of praise.
  - Evidence: Ikkos invokes Walter Benjamin's philosophy of history as a methodological framework and juxtaposes three cases (Cotton, Nazi euthanasia, Purdue Pharma) to demonstrate how a constellation of past and present yields insights about persistent patterns of clinical hubris.
  - Strength: Moderate. This is a polemical commentary, not an empirical study. Its strength lies in the philosophical grounding and provocative case analogies; its limitation is that it does not systematically test the opposing view or provide new historical data.

- **Core claim**: Benjamin's Jetztzeit offers a way beyond the presentism/historicism binary by attending to moments of "flash" recognition where past and present form a constellation.
  - Evidence: Direct quotation from *The Arcades Project* (N3.1) describing the constellation of "what has been" with "the now"; applied to the Purdue Pharma case as a contemporary trigger for re-evaluating Cotton and Nazi-era psychiatry.
  - Strength: The philosophical apparatus is well-cited but applied only briefly; the practical methodology for operationalising Jetztzeit in historical research is not fully developed.

- **Core claim**: To patients, physicians' actions matter more than their motivations; the distinction between Nazi psychiatrists (evil intentions) and opioid prescribers (unintentional harm) is less relevant to outcomes than the scale of preventable death.
  - Evidence: Citation of the Stanford-Lancet Commission on the opioid crisis showing higher preventable deaths than the Nazi euthanasia programme.
  - Strength: High for the factual claim about death counts; the moral-ethical comparison is deliberately provocative and intended to challenge comfortable distinctions.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Tension with Hilton's argument**: Hilton emphasises fairness and avoiding "unjust blame" as core values; Ikkos counters that discovery of truth, even when uncomfortable or critical of the profession, should take priority. This is the central conflict between the two papers.
- **Internal tension**: Ikkos acknowledges that both praise and blame are "necessary" in history, yet insists the primary driver should be discovery. The boundary between discovery and blame is left blurry: if discovery reveals systemic harm caused by the profession's predecessors, does that constitute a form of blame?
- **Potential conflict with cognitive dissonance concept**: Ikkos uses cognitive dissonance to critique Hilton's protective stance, but Hilton might argue that Ikkos's own position is driven by a different form of dissonance—an anti-professional bias or an uncritical embrace of Benjamin's esoteric framework.

## Open Questions

- How can Benjamin's concept of Jetztzeit be operationalised as a practical methodology for historians of psychiatry, beyond a philosophical stance?
- Where is the boundary between "discovery" and "blame"? Is it possible to discover uncomfortable truths without implicitly blaming historical actors, or is the distinction primarily rhetorical?
- What are the ethical obligations of contemporary psychiatric institutions (e.g., UCL, RCPsych) toward historical wrongs committed by figures associated with their present-day funding or leadership?
- Does Ikkos's framing risk a new form of presentism—one that selects historical fragments solely for their resonance with contemporary crises, thereby distorting historical context?
