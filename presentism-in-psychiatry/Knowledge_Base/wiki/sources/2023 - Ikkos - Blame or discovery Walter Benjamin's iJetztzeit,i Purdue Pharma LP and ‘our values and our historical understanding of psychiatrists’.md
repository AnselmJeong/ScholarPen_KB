# Blame or discovery? Walter Benjamin's Jetztzeit, Purdue Pharma LP and ‘our values and our historical understanding of psychiatrists’

## Summary
This commentary examines the philosophy of historical inquiry within psychiatry, specifically responding to Claire Hilton's work on professional continuity. The author argues against a purely chronological or judgmental approach to history, proposing instead a framework based on Walter Benjamin's concept of *Jetztzeit* (now-time). This approach views historical failures not as distant events to be contextualized or excused, but as immediate "flashes" of consciousness that reveal recurring patterns of professional failure.

The author identifies a dangerous cycle of "interventional fervour"—the pairing of unwarranted clinical optimism with extreme self-confidence—that has led to atrocities ranging from Henry Cotton's organ removals and Nazi euthanasia to the modern opioid crisis driven by Purdue Pharma. By linking the Sackler family's influence on psychiatric institutions to these historical tragedies, Ikkos asserts that the goal of history is the "discovery" of these patterns to prevent the preservation of a harmful status quo.

## Key Concepts
- **Jetztzeit ("Now-time")** — A Benjaminian concept where the past is not a linear sequence but a "flash" of recognisability that strikes the present, creating a dialectical reversal.
- **Dialectical Reversal** — The overturning of the view that the present simply builds on a fixed past; instead, the past becomes an awakening for the present.
- **Interventional Fervour** — The dangerous combination of unwarranted clinical optimism and self-confidence that makes practitioners vulnerable to implementing toxic or flawed therapeutics.
- **Hagiographic Representation** — The tendency to create idealized, "saint-like" biographies of medical figures, which can obscure historical failures.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Historical psychiatric research should prioritize "discovery" (insight/awakening) over the binary of "praise or blame."
  - Evidence: The author links disparate events (Henry Cotton, Nazi psychiatry, and the Sackler/Purdue Pharma era) as a "constellation" of professional hubris.
  - Strength: Theoretically strong as a philosophical framework; empirically supported by the documented harm caused by the mentioned entities.

- **Core claim**: Professional identity and "cognitive dissonance" often prevent psychiatrists from recognizing their continuity with historical failures.
  - Evidence: The contrast between the Tate Gallery's decisive action and the continued presence of Sackler-funded chairs at UCL.
  - Strength: Moderate; relies on institutional observation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Discovery vs. Fairness**: There is a tension between Ikkos's demand for "discovery" (which may be harsh) and Claire Hilton's "legitimate anxiety" regarding the need for fairness and nuanced context to avoid unfair blame.
- **Motivation vs. Outcome**: The author acknowledges that motivations differ (e.g., Nazi evil vs. corporate greed), but argues that for the patient, the outcome (harm/death) is the primary metric.

## Open Questions
- How can psychiatric institutions implement a "now-time" recognition of their failures without falling into the trap of reactionary blame or hagiography?
- To what extent does current "interventional fervour" exist in modern neuropsychiatric treatments?
