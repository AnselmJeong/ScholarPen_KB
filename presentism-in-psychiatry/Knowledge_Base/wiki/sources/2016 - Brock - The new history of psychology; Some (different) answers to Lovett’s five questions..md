# The New History of Psychology: Some (Different) Answers to Lovett’s Five Questions

## Summary
This paper is a direct response to Benjamin Lovett's 2006 critique of the "New History" of psychology. Brock seeks to demonstrate that the transition from "Old History" (traditional textbook accounts) to "New History" (professional historiography) is not superficial, but involves a profound change in the types of sources used and the conceptual frameworks applied to historical analysis.

The author employs textual analysis and comparative historiography to dismantle Lovett's arguments. Brock specifically addresses the misuse of primary sources in traditional texts, the misunderstanding of "professionalization," and the distinction between internalist and externalist explanations of scientific shifts. 

The main finding is that "Old History" often relied on a "Great Man" narrative and internalist logic (intellectual progress as the sole driver), whereas "New History" utilizes a critical, socially informed approach that accounts for political and economic forces. Brock argues that this shift provides significantly better explanatory power for the actual development of the field.

## Key Concepts
- **Internalism** — A historiographical approach focusing on the internal intellectual development of a science, ignoring the social or political context.
- **Externalism** — An approach that analyzes the social, political, and cultural environment to explain shifts in scientific paradigms.
- **Presentism** — The practice of interpreting the past through the lens of current knowledge, often treating historical figures as "wrong" because they lacked modern findings.
- **Whig History** — The view of history as a linear, inevitable story of progress toward the present "truth."
- **Ceremonial History** — Celebratory accounts of "Great Men" that lack a critical focus on the social forces shaping the discipline.
- **Critical History** — A socially informed method that analyzes institutional and ideological forces rather than just personal intentions of historical figures.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "New History" is fundamentally different from the "Old" in its critical focus and evidentiary standards.
  - Evidence: Brock identifies missing sentences in Lovett's citations of Boring, proving Boring explicitly adhered to "Great Man" theory.
  - Strength: High regarding textual accuracy; moderate regarding the anecdotal evidence of textbook "dabblers."
- **Core claim**: Traditional psychology textbooks frequently misrepresent primary source usage.
  - Evidence: The consistent misidentification of Wundt's *Völkerpsychologie* in textbooks despite its inclusion in bibliographies.
  - Strength: Strong; demonstrates a pattern of "bibliography padding" without actual reading of sources.
- **Core claim**: Externalism offers superior explanatory power for shifts like the rise of Behaviorism.
  - Evidence: Contrast between the internalist "imageless thought controversy" and the externalist view of behaviorism as a tool for institutional utility in law/advertising.
  - Strength: Logically persuasive, though dependent on the researcher's preference for social-contextual analysis.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Lovett vs. Brock**: Lovett claims the distinction between old and new history is "a distinction without a distinction"; Brock argues they are different in every significant way.
- **Internal Tension**: Brock notes that "New History" is sometimes simplified for non-specialists, which can lead critics (like Lovett) to mischaracterize it as uncritically endorsing specific trends.

## Open Questions
- To what extent does "Old History" still dominate undergraduate pedagogy despite the "New History" movement?
- Can "Critical History" effectively recover the stories of marginalized groups without falling into the "Great Woman/Great Minority" (ceremonial) trap?
