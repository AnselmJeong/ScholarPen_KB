# Our values and our historical understanding of psychiatrists

## Summary

This essay by Claire Hilton, historian-in-residence at the Royal College of Psychiatrists, examines how psychiatrists today should approach the history of their profession. Hilton argues against using present-day values as a benchmark for judging past psychiatrists, contending that such "presentist" judgment creates an uneven playing field and ignores the complex forces — scientific, societal, legal, and ethical — that shaped clinical practice. The piece is a historiographic commentary rather than an original empirical study, drawing on secondary historical literature and illustrative case studies.

The main case studies are the early 20th-century adoption of focal sepsis theory by Henry Cotton and the widespread embrace of eugenics across several countries. Hilton shows that these harmful practices emerged from plausible scientific theories, well-intentioned desires to help patients, and institutional pressures, rather than simple malice. She also discusses the routine tonsillectomy craze, insulin coma therapy, and the political misuse of diagnosis (e.g., "sluggish schizophrenia" in the USSR) to demonstrate recurring patterns. The essay’s central finding is that "the uncritical continuation of practices long after they have been shown to be detrimental is arguably more unethical than the misinformed introduction of new methods in the context of their times."

Significantly, Hilton extends the argument to the future: unless future psychiatrists adopt a contextualized view of 2020s psychiatry (e.g., remote consultations, diagnostic instability, resource constraints), they may judge current clinicians as harshly as we judge our predecessors. The piece thus calls for methodological humility, honest historical contextualization, and an awareness that present values are neither uniform nor eternal.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism** — Judging the past by present-day values and standards; Hilton argues against using present values as a benchmark for historical judgment, though she allows that the present can help "probe the past" by providing new perspectives and tools.
- **Contextualism** — Investigating the past from within its own framework, using contemporary sources and avoiding hindsight; Hilton's preferred methodology, calling for understanding forebears "in their own setting."
- **Focal sepsis theory** — Early 20th-century biomedical theory claiming that bacteria in one organ can cause disease in another; key case study showing how a plausible scientific theory led to harmful practices (Cotton's surgical removals of teeth and organs).
- **Eugenics** — Movement to "improve biological quality" of a population through selective reproduction; second major case study showing varied national implementations and psychiatric involvement.
- **Vergangenheitsbewältigung** (coming to terms with the past) — Process of critically analyzing and accepting a profession's problematic history; framing concept borrowed from Lepping & Poole (2022).
- **Medical ethics as contextual** — Ethical codes are interpreted variably depending on historical context, including ideologies, science, and other contemporary concerns; supports Hilton's argument against using present ethics to judge past actions.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Using present-day values to judge past psychiatrists creates an "uneven playing field" — our predecessors could not have foreseen our frames of reference.
  - Evidence: Hilton contrasts the ethical frameworks, legal contexts, and scientific knowledge of the early 20th century with those of today, noting that homosexuality was criminal, suicide was a crime, and medical statistics were in their infancy.
  - Strength: Moderate — logically coherent and supported by historical examples, but the essay does not provide quantitative or systematic evidence; it is a historiographic argument rather than an empirical study.

- **Core claim**: Harmful practices (focal sepsis, eugenics) arose from complex forces — plausible scientific theories, societal values, institutional pressures, and lack of rigorous ethics — not from simple "bad doctor" narratives.
  - Evidence: Cotton's focal sepsis surgeries were grounded in accepted microbiology; routine tonsillectomy was widespread and accepted; eugenics had different national implementations (USA, UK, Sweden, Germany) shaped by local legal and social factors.
  - Strength: Moderate — well-referenced to secondary literature (Scull, Roelcke, Dwyer-Hemmings); the argument is illustrative rather than exhaustive, and Hilton acknowledges selection bias.

- **Core claim**: "The uncritical continuation of practices long after they have been shown to be detrimental is arguably more unethical than the misinformed introduction of new methods."
  - Evidence: Insulin coma therapy continued in the UK long after Bourne (1953) and Ackner et al. (1957) showed no benefit and significant harm.
  - Strength: Moderate — relies on cited historical studies; the ethical claim is philosophical rather than empirically proven.

- **Core claim**: Future generations may judge today's psychiatrists negatively unless they adopt a contextualized view of current constraints.
  - Evidence: Hilton lists plausible criticisms of 2020s psychiatry (poor morale, out-of-area admissions, early mortality in schizophrenia, changing diagnostic classifications) and argues these would be unfair without understanding the contextual forces.
  - Strength: Weaker — it is a hypothetical extrapolation, not based on data; intended to provoke reflection rather than establish a factual claim.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension**: Hilton argues against judging past psychiatrists by present values, yet she clearly labels Cotton's actions as showing "profound disregard of humanity." She acknowledges this tension implicitly (some actions were "worse" than others) but does not fully resolve how to balance contextual understanding with moral evaluation.
- **Selection bias acknowledged**: Hilton states her examples are chosen "to provoke thought rather than to be representative," limiting the generalizability of her argument.
- **Paradox of learning from history**: If present values cannot be used to judge the past, how does one identify which practices to learn from? Hilton wants both contextual understanding and the ability to extract lessons; the methodology for this balance is not fully theorized.
- **Conflict with existing wiki content**: The page [[concepts/presentism]] may treat presentism as straightforwardly problematic; Hilton's more nuanced view — using the present to *probe* the past without *judging* it — extends the concept and may require updating the existing page.

## Open Questions

- How can we operationalize "contextualized judgment" — a methodology that allows both historical understanding and ethical evaluation without lapsing into presentism or moral relativism?
- Does Hilton's argument risk excusing historical harm by over-emphasizing contextual forces? She anticipates this objection but does not fully resolve it.
- How does this historiographic stance relate to critical race theory, postcolonial perspectives, and "woke" historical awareness, which Hilton mentions but does not engage with in depth?
- Should the wiki include a dedicated concept page on "historiography of psychiatry" to tie together the many sources (Stocking, Bashkow, Loison, Brock, Watrin, Hilton) that address historical methodology?
- To what extent can the distinction between "probing" and "judging" the past be maintained in practice, given that the choice of research questions itself reflects present concerns?
