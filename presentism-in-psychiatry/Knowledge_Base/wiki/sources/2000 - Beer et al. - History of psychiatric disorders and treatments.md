# History of psychiatric disorders and treatments

## Summary

This source is a review article surveying the psychiatric history literature published in 1999, primarily drawn from the journal *History of Psychiatry*. The authors—Beer, Jones, and Lipsedge—synthesize findings across two domains: the history of psychiatric disorders (nosology, aetiology, and cultural determinants) and the history of psychiatric treatments (pharmacotherapy, ECT, and institutional care). The review covers studies ranging from ancient Peru to late-twentieth-century America, and from conceptual analyses of classification to large-scale quantitative case-record research.

The review's central historiographical claim is that the field has moved beyond the entrenched "Whig versus Revisionist" debate—the simplistic dichotomy between progressive/celebratory and critical/debunking approaches—toward more nuanced accounts produced by both historians and clinicians. Methodologically, the reviewed works employ a variety of approaches: conceptual history (Berrios on classification and moral insanity), meta-analysis (Kohn et al. on affective disorders among Jews), large-scale computer-assisted case record analysis (Dörries and Beddies on 4000 records from Berlin), and first-person experiential accounts (Thuillier on chlorpromazine, Lebensohn on ECT).

Key findings across the reviewed literature include: (1) culture profoundly shapes psychiatric diagnosis, aetiology, and treatment—demonstrated across Tourette's syndrome, anorexia nervosa, hysteria, and post-traumatic neurosis; (2) psychiatric classifications are "cultural products" rather than natural kinds, with biological causes being time-limited in their explanatory power; (3) deinstitutionalization policy goals actively shaped clinical advice about neuroleptic medication, rather than science driving policy; and (4) community care existed continuously before, during, and after the asylum era, challenging standard periodization narratives. The review is significant as a snapshot of the state of psychiatric history at the turn of the millennium and as evidence of the field's maturation toward more sophisticated, empirically grounded scholarship.

## Key Concepts

- **Post-traumatic neurosis** — Unexplained symptoms following railway accidents or industrial injuries; the 1916 Munich conference decision to deny pensions to veterans with this condition illustrates how legal, economic, and social context shapes psychiatric aetiology
- **Moral insanity (Prichard)** — A classification of insanity characterized by changes of affect in the absence of delusion, not (as sometimes misread) an early concept of psychopathic personality; a revision by Berrios correcting received interpretations
- **Haematoma auris** — An effusion of blood in ear cartilage once regarded as a useful organic indicator of psychosis; serves as a warning against fashionable "evidence-based" diagnoses that flourish due to incomplete knowledge or prejudice
- **Psychiatric classification as cultural product** — Berrios's argument that classificatory systems are cultural artifacts, not natural kinds, and that biological causes have explanatory power only for limited time periods; classification is valuable only insofar as it releases additional information about the objects classified
- **Pathology of liberty (Henri Ey)** — The thesis that psychiatry's unique and fundamental core is the pathology of freedom, offering an epistemological delineation of the discipline
- **Moral treatment of idiocy** — The systematic action of a will upon another for improvement, applied to learning disability in the 19th century; Seguin's "physiological education" combined sensory stimulation with a moral dimension (gratitude, discipline, humane care)
- **Deinstitutionalization's impact on pharmacotherapy** — Gelman's argument that from the 1980s, clinical advice retracting warnings about neuroleptic side effects was driven by policy makers' desire to continue deinstitutionalization, not by new scientific evidence
- **Community care continuity** — The finding that community care existed before, during, and after the asylum era, challenging the narrative that deinstitutionalization was an innovation of the post-1960s period

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: The historiography of psychiatry is maturing beyond the Whig/Revisionist dichotomy.**
  - Evidence: High-quality, well-researched books and articles are being produced by both historians and clinicians, moving beyond entrenched celebratory or debunking stances.
  - Strength: Well-supported by the range and quality of works reviewed; represents a meta-observation about the field's development rather than a single empirical finding.

- **Core claim: Culture, law, and economics fundamentally shape psychiatric diagnosis, aetiology, and treatment.**
  - Evidence: Post-traumatic neurosis (pension policy shaped aetiology); Tourette's syndrome (reinterpreted across eras as possession, hysteria, unconscious conflict); anorexia nervosa (culturally determined control/respect seeking); Chinese vs. Western hysteria (different classifications, similar symptoms).
  - Strength: Strong—multiple independent studies across diverse disorders converge on this conclusion, though the mechanism of cultural influence varies by case.

- **Core claim: Psychiatric classifications are cultural products, not natural kinds, and biological causes are time-limited.**
  - Evidence: Berrios's conceptual history argues classifications are cultural products; he states biological causes are "limited to certain time periods" and that genetics will not become the basis for classification.
  - Strength: Moderate—this is a conceptual/philosophical argument rather than an empirical finding; its validity depends on one's epistemology of psychiatry.

- **Core claim: Deinstitutionalization policy goals shaped clinical pharmacotherapy advice, not vice versa.**
  - Evidence: Gelman's analysis of US neuroleptic treatment shows that warnings about long-term side effects were retracted in the 1980s due to policy makers' unwillingness to interrupt deinstitutionalization.
  - Strength: Strong—well-documented historical analysis of a specific policy-clinical feedback loop; limited to the US context.

- **Core claim: Community care existed continuously before, during, and after the asylum era.**
  - Evidence: Bartlett and Wright's edited collection covers community care from 1750–2000, showing "family power" was significant throughout and that surveillance in community care predates modern proposals.
  - Strength: Strong—based on multiple contributors covering diverse British and Irish contexts across 250 years.

- **Core claim: Admissions to 19th-century English asylums were controlled by Poor Law officials and Justices of the Peace, not asylum superintendents.**
  - Evidence: Bartlett's monograph demonstrates legal and administrative control of admissions; raises parallels with contemporary mental health administration where the poor remain disproportionately confined.
  - Strength: Strong—based on detailed archival research; the contemporary parallel is a provocative suggestion rather than an empirical finding.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Berrios vs. biological psychiatry**: Berrios argues classifications are cultural products and biological causes are "limited to certain time periods," yet the source also celebrates biological discoveries (chlorpromazine, lithium) as transformative. The article does not resolve this tension—whether psychiatry can simultaneously be culturally constructed and biomedically progressive remains an open question.
- **Whig/progress narrative vs. critical history**: While claiming to move beyond the Whig/Revisionist dichotomy, the review includes both celebratory accounts (Thuillier on chlorpromazine "put psychiatry back in the mainstream") and critical accounts (Gelman on sociopolitical manipulation of medication advice). The review implicitly endorses both stances without reconciling them.
- **Empirical vs. epistemological psychiatry**: Lantéri-Laura's distinction between empirical (what psychiatrists treat) and theoretical (epistemologically robust delineation) views of mental disorder is presented without resolution; Henri Ey's "pathology of liberty" proposal is mentioned but not evaluated.
- **Bromide/atropine contradiction**: Malcolm's account of morphine withdrawal treatments reveals a bizarre internal contradiction—atropine-like drugs were used both to deliberately induce delirium and as sedatives—without the historical actors acknowledging the tension. This mirrors the broader historiographical problem of making sense of past practices that appear incoherent from a modern perspective.

## Open Questions

- Berrios predicted that genetics "would not become the basis for classification" in psychiatry—is this still valid given advances in psychiatric genetics since 2000, or has the field moved toward genetically-informed taxonomies?
- If community care existed throughout the asylum era, what specifically changed in the post-WWII period that we now call "deinstitutionalization"—scale, ideology, legal framework, or something else?
- Berrios argues that classification is valuable only insofar as it "releases additional information" about the objects classified—how does this criterion apply to contemporary diagnostic systems (DSM-5, ICD-11), and do they meet it?
- Bartlett's question—"Is it not fair to ask the degree to which twentieth-century mental health administration remains a Poor Law of Lunacy?"—demands empirical investigation: are the poor still disproportionately confined in psychiatric facilities, and what legal and economic mechanisms maintain this pattern?
- The review celebrates both Thuillier's passionate account of chlorpromazine's discovery and Gelman's critical account of neuroleptic politics—can these narratives be integrated into a single coherent history of psychopharmacology, or do they represent irreconcilable historiographical positions?
