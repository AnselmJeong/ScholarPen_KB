# History of psychiatric disorders and treatments

## Summary

This review article surveys high-quality books and journal articles published in 1999 on the history of psychiatric disorders, classification, and treatments. The authors, based at the Institute of Psychiatry in London, synthesize recent scholarship from historians and practitioners, arguing that the field was moving beyond the entrenched "Whig versus Revisionist" debate toward more nuanced and evidence-based accounts. The review is structured around two main themes: the history of psychiatric disorders and the history of psychiatric treatments.

The review examines a series of papers exploring how culture determines psychiatric diagnosis, aetiology, and treatment. Key examples include Schmiedebach's analysis of post-traumatic neurosis in Germany, where legal and economic factors shaped aetiological explanations; Berrios' conceptual history of psychiatric classification, which argues that taxonomic systems are cultural products rather than reflections of natural kinds; and studies of Tourette's syndrome, anorexia nervosa, and hysteria that demonstrate how diagnostic categories are reinterpreted across historical periods and cultures. The review also highlights Berrios' revisionist reading of Prichard's "moral insanity" and his critique of "haematoma auris" as a fashionable but evidence-poor diagnosis.

On the treatment side, the review juxtaposes celebratory and critical accounts of psychopharmacology. Thuillier's first-hand memoir describes chlorpromazine's discovery as a triumphant return of psychiatry to mainstream medicine, while Gelman's sociopolitical history divides the neuroleptic era into four periods, linking the retraction of side-effect warnings to policy makers' desire for deinstitutionalization. Other treatments covered include electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), morphine withdrawal techniques, and John Cade's discovery of lithium. The review also covers the history of community care, asylum admissions under the Poor Law, and the application of moral treatment to "idiocy" (learning disability), arguing that historians have wrongly separated the history of learning disability from the history of psychiatry.

## Key Concepts

- **Cultural construction of psychiatric diagnosis** — The idea that the definition, classification, and explanation of mental disorders are significantly shaped by the cultural, legal, economic, and social context of a given time and place. Examples include post-traumatic neurosis (shaped by pension laws), Tourette's (possession vs. hysteria vs. neurology), and hysteria (Chinese vs. Hippocratic classification).
- **Classification as a cultural product** — Berrios' meta-epistemological stance that psychiatric nosologies should not be taken as reflections of natural kinds but analyzed as products of the specific culture that created them. The value of a classification lies in whether it "releases additional information," not in its correspondence to pre-existing natural reality.
- **Social history of treatment** — An approach emphasizing the role of sociopolitical interests, economic pressures, and policy decisions in shaping the adoption and use of therapies, rather than just their scientific merit. Exemplified by Gelman's four-period history of neuroleptics linking drug policy to deinstitutionalization goals.
- **The "Whig vs. Revisionist" debate** — The historiographical tension between a "Whig" view of history as linear progress toward a superior present (often associated with biological psychiatry) and a "Revisionist" view that is critical, contextual, and emphasizes social control. The authors claim the field was moving on from this dichotomy in 1999.
- **Moral treatment of idiocy** — Simpson's argument that "moral treatment" was applied to learning disability ("idiocy") in the 19th century, particularly through Seguin's "physiological education," and that historians have wrongly imposed a later conceptual separation between mental illness and learning disability on readings of the past.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Historical understanding of psychiatry was moving beyond the simplistic "Whig vs. Revisionist" dichotomy toward more nuanced, evidence-based accounts written by both historians and practitioners.
  - Evidence: The review compiles multiple published peer-reviewed books and articles from 1999 that adopt contextual, non-partisan approaches.
  - Strength: Moderate — the authors assert this trend but many cited works (e.g., Harari's critique of Shorter) show the debate remained active.

- **Core claim**: Psychiatric classifications are culturally contingent and do not represent natural kinds.
  - Evidence: Berrios' conceptual history argues that all classificatory attempts should be viewed as cultural products, not stable natural objects.
  - Strength: High — based on extensive conceptual analysis and historical survey of taxonomic attempts.

- **Core claim**: The history of psychiatric treatment is deeply intertwined with sociopolitical and economic forces, not just pure scientific discovery.
  - Evidence: Gelman's account linking neuroleptic side-effect warnings to deinstitutionalization policy; Bartlett's argument that Poor Law officials, not doctors, controlled asylum admissions; the post-traumatic neurosis debate shaped by pension systems.
  - Strength: High — these are substantial historical analyses based on archival research and legal history.

- **Core claim**: Many key psychiatric "disorders" are historically and culturally constructed, not timeless entities.
  - Evidence: Tourette's syndrome reinterpreted across centuries (possession, hysteria, neurology); anorexia nervosa as culturally determined; multiple personality disorder co-created by patient and physician (Sybil case).
  - Strength: High — multiple specific case studies from peer-reviewed sources support this claim.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The source claims the field was "moving on" from the Whig vs. Revisionist dichotomy, but Harari's critical review of Shorter's biological-progress narrative shows the debate was still very much alive in 1999. This creates an internal tension between the authors' normative hope and the evidence they present.
- The source presents both Thuillier's celebratory account of chlorpromazine (as triumphant progress returning psychiatry to mainstream medicine) and Gelman's critical sociopolitical account (linking drug policy to deinstitutionalization goals). These two accounts sit in unresolved tension—is the story of psychopharmacology one of progress or problematic social control?
- Berrios' constructivist argument that classifications are cultural products implicitly challenges the DSM project's claims to validity, yet the review does not explicitly engage with this tension. The source presents Berrios' view approvingly without fully addressing its implications for modern psychiatric practice.

## Open Questions

- How accurate was the source's claim that the field was moving beyond the Whig vs. Revisionist debate? More recent sources in the wiki index (2022–2025) suggest this historiographical tension remains active and unresolved.
- The post-traumatic neurosis debate of 1916 demonstrates how economic and legal factors (pension systems) directly shaped aetiological explanations. Does this historical case provide a model for understanding contemporary debates about PTSD, chronic fatigue syndrome, or long COVID?
- Simpson argues that the history of learning disability is integral to the history of psychiatry. Does the current wiki project reflect this integration, or does it risk replicating the 20th-century separation that Simpson critiques?
- Berrios questions whether classifying is "in our best interest" for hypothetical constructs. What would it mean to take this question seriously in the context of the DSM's continued dominance?
