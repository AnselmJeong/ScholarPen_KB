# History of psychiatric disorders and treatments

## Summary
This paper provides a comprehensive review of literature published around 1999 regarding the history of psychiatric diagnosis, classification, and treatment. The authors synthesize various historical accounts to challenge the "Whig" narrative of psychiatry as a linear progression toward scientific truth, instead presenting it as a field deeply embedded in shifting cultural and sociopolitical frameworks.

The review covers a broad spectrum of topics, including the evolution of specific disorders (e.g., Tourette's, anorexia), the impact of the "Poor Law" on asylum admissions, and the development of somatic treatments like ECT and lithium. It emphasizes the role of external pressures—such as war pensions, poverty laws, and the drive for de-institutionalization—in shaping medical etiology and prescribing patterns.

The significance of this work lies in its promotion of a critical, revisionist approach to psychiatric history. By highlighting how "fashionable conditions" emerge and vanish, the authors argue for a more objective, epistemologically aware practice of psychiatry that recognizes the influence of the observer's culture on the diagnosis.

## Key Concepts
- **Cultural Determinism in Diagnosis** — The theory that psychiatric categories are not stable natural objects but products of the culture that defines them.
- **Moral Treatment** — The systematic application of one will upon another to improve a patient's condition, particularly noted in the treatment of "idiocy."
- **Whig vs. Revisionist History** — The tension between viewing medical history as steady progress (Whig) versus a series of cultural shifts and reconstructions (Revisionist).
- **Post-traumatic Neurosis** — Historical psychiatric distress following trauma, used to demonstrate how legal/economic needs (e.g., war pensions) influence medical aetiology.
- **Moral Insanity** — An obsolete concept involving changes in affect without delusions, used to contrast early classifications with modern "psychopathic personality."

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Psychiatric classifications are cultural products rather than discoveries of stable natural entities.
  - Evidence: The shifting interpretation of Tourette's syndrome (Satanic possession $\rightarrow$ Hysteria $\rightarrow$ Psychoanalysis) and the contrast between Inca "melancholy" and Aztec "anxiety."
  - Strength: High for bibliographic review; synthesizes multiple peer-reviewed historical analyses.
- **Core claim**: Sociopolitical interests often drive the adoption and application of psychiatric treatments.
  - Evidence: Gelman's analysis of US schizophrenia medication, where the policy of de-institutionalization influenced prescribing patterns.
  - Strength: Strong, based on detailed historical and legal analysis.
- **Core claim**: Institutional admission was historically governed by legal and poverty status rather than medical necessity.
  - Evidence: Bartlett's study of the "Poor Law of Lunacy" showing Justices of the Peace controlled asylum entry.
  - Strength: Strong, supported by archival public records.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Biological vs. Psychological**: The source notes a tension between the "biological psychiatrist" view (e.g., Shorter) and the "psychoanalytic/sociocultural" view (e.g., Harari).
- **Empiricism vs. Epistemology**: Lanteâri-Laura identifies a gap between the empirical management of disorders (what works) and the search for a robust theoretical definition of psychiatry.
- **Serendipity vs. Rigor**: The discovery of lithium is framed not as a "lucky accident" but as the result of John Cade's rigorous pursuit of an unexpected finding.
