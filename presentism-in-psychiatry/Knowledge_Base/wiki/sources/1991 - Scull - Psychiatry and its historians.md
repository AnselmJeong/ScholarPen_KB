# Psychiatry and its historians

## Summary

Andrew Scull's 1991 article provides a comprehensive historiographic survey of writing on the history of psychiatry, tracing its evolution from the early twentieth century through the late 1980s. He identifies three broad phases: an early period dominated by amateur psychiatrist-historians who produced Whig progress narratives and harmless antiquarianism; a subsequent wave of anti-psychiatry polemics in the 1960s–70s that offered mirror-image condemnations of psychiatry as repressive social control; and finally a mature "revisionist" historiography characterized by detailed archival research, institutional case studies, patient perspectives, and nuanced attention to social and professional contexts.

The essay is a meta-historiographic review that synthesizes dozens of works across multiple national traditions—British, American, French, and German—and highlights both the achievements and the persistent precariousness of the field. Scull documents a shift from global synthetic treatments to localized monographs, and from a focus on great men and ideas to "history from below" and the study of therapeutics, gender, and non-institutional psychiatry. He argues that anti-psychiatry, despite its excesses, provokes serious scholarly interest and catalyzes the professionalization of the discipline.

Key findings include the uneven institutionalization of psychiatric history: it flourished in Anglo-American contexts with new journals, seminars, and PhD dissertations, but remained marginal in continental Europe where even seminal figures like Castel moved on to other topics. The significance of the article lies in its framing of the field's development as a movement toward greater methodological sophistication and scholarly rigor, while cautioning that institutional support remains fragile.

## Key Concepts

- **Whig history / progress narrative** — Historical writing that interprets the past as a progressive movement toward the present, uncritically celebrating psychiatry's achievements and obscuring failures.
- **Externalist vs. internalist history** — Externalist history emphasizes social, institutional, and professional contexts; internalist history focuses on ideas, discoveries, and great men. Scull traces a shift toward externalism as a key development.
- **Anti-psychiatry historiography** — Polemical revisionist histories (Foucault, Szasz) that portray psychiatry as repressive social control. Scull acknowledges their catalytic role while criticizing their one-sidedness.
- **Revisionist historiography** — The mature, nuanced approach that emerged in the 1970s–80s, rooted in archival research, patient perspectives, and avoidance of both Whig and anti-psychiatry extremes.
- **Institutional history / asylum history** — Detailed reconstruction of single institutions (Bethlem, York Retreat, Ticehurst) as a core methodological shift away from global syntheses.
- **Patient perspective / history from below** — Reconstructing the experiences and voices of inmates, correcting earlier elite/professional focus.
- **Therapeutics history** — Study of actual treatments (surgical, pharmaceutical, psychological) in their social and professional contexts; an emerging subfield Scull identifies as promising.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: Psychiatric history was long written badly by amateurs and administrative historians.**
  - Evidence: Scull critiques works by Alexander & Selesnick, Zilboorg, Bromberg, and Jones as Whig progress narratives or harmless antiquarianism. Administrative historians (e.g., Deutsch) accepted psychiatry's self-image uncritically.
  - Strength: Well-supported by extensive literature review; the critique is widely accepted among professional historians.
- **Core claim: The anti-psychiatry movement (1960s–70s) was valuable but equally one-sided.**
  - Evidence: Foucault, Szasz produced Manichean mirror-images of earlier hagiography. However, this polemic provoked serious scholarly interest and helped persuade a generation of professional historians to study madness.
  - Strength: Strong on catalytic role; moderate on the evaluation of anti-psychiatry's scholarly contribution, as Scull acknowledges its continuing influence in some quarters.
- **Core claim: A mature revisionist historiography has emerged, characterized by archival detail, patient voices, and institutional case studies.**
  - Evidence: Extensive list of scholars and works: MacDonald on Napier, Andrews on Bethlem, Digby on York Retreat, Tomes on Pennsylvania Hospital, etc. Shift from synthetic treatments to localized monographs.
  - Strength: Very strong for the period up to 1991; however, the source is now over 30 years old and does not cover later developments (neuroscience turn, global psychiatry history, digital humanities).
- **Core claim: The field has flourished in Anglo-American contexts but remains institutionally precarious in continental Europe.**
  - Evidence: Documentation of journal, seminars, lectures, PhD dissertations in Britain and North America. In France and Germany, seminal figures moved on, and new work slowed.
  - Strength: Strong for the period; the claim about precariousness may have changed since 1991.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Scull's own narrative of historiographic progress (from amateur → polemical → mature) is itself a kind of Whig story, carrying a teleological implication that his generation has achieved the correct approach. This internal tension is partly acknowledged but not fully resolved.
- The source implicitly contradicts later instrumentalist approaches to psychiatric history (e.g., Shorter 2014, "The History of Psychiatry as Clinical Science") that advocate using history to directly inform current clinical practice. Scull's emphasis on contextual, non-presentist historiography suggests such instrumentalization risks repeating the errors of earlier Whig work.
- Scull's characterization of national traditions (French = Foucauldian, German = critical theory, American = exceptionalist) may oversimplify diverse scholarship and reinforce essentialist categories that later globalization of the field has partly eroded.

## Open Questions

- Has the institutional security of psychiatric history in continental Europe improved since 1991, or has the field remained precarious?
- Has the "revisionist consensus" Scull describes been challenged or superseded by later developments such as the neuroscience turn, global/colonial psychiatry history, or digital and quantitative methods?
- How does Scull's own scholarship (e.g., *Museums of Madness*) fit within his three-phase framework, given his focus on social control and political economy—does it risk reproducing the very bias he criticizes in others?
- Does the national traditions framework (British, French, German, American) still have heuristic value in an era of increasingly transnational scholarly networks?
