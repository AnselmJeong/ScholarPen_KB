# A Latent Historiography? The Case of Psychiatry in Britain, 1500–1820

## Summary
This article provides a comprehensive historiographical review of the study of psychiatry in Britain between 1500 and 1820. The author argues that while the period after 1800 is well-documented, the early modern period suffers from a "latent historiography"—meaning there is a wealth of empirical research but a lack of systematic analysis regarding how these histories have been written and how paradigms have shifted.

The research design involves a chronological mapping of intellectual "roots and routes" since the 1950s. Houston identifies a progression from mid-century optimism and modernization theory, through the "anti-psychiatry" and social control frameworks of the 1960s-80s, toward a contemporary "neo-revisionist" focus on micro-histories and the lived experience of sufferers.

The author emphasizes the extreme disciplinary diversity of the field, which attracts clinician-historians, social historians, literary scholars, and architects. This diversity leads to frequent tensions between teleological views (seeing the past as a prelude to modern science) and constructionist views (seeing madness as a product of social power). The work concludes that the field has moved away from "grand narratives" toward a more nuanced, interdisciplinary understanding of care in local contexts.

## Key Concepts
- **Latent Historiography** — The state of a field where substantial research exists, but the history of that research (the historiography) remains underdeveloped or unacknowledged.
- **Presentism/Teleology** — The error of interpreting historical events as a linear, inevitable progression toward modern "truth" or scientific success.
- **Social Constructionism** — The perspective that categories of "madness" are created by social, political, and cultural forces rather than objective biological markers.
- **The Linguistic Turn** — A methodological shift toward analyzing how language and discourse construct the identity and experience of the mentally ill.
- **Nomothetic vs. Idiographic** — The tension between seeking universal laws/structures (nomothetic, e.g., sociology/geography) and focusing on unique, particular archival cases (idiographic, e.g., history).

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The history of psychiatry is uniquely fragmented due to the divergent goals of its contributors.
  - Evidence: Contrast between clinician-historians (who prioritize medical progress) and social historians (who prioritize power and class).
  - Strength: Strong; supported by a broad review of monographs and journal trajectories.
- **Core claim**: Michel Foucault’s influence is a "provocative catalyst" that remains unavoidable despite empirical shortcomings.
  - Evidence: The persistence of "power-knowledge" and "surveillance" frameworks in academic writing despite historians' rejection of Foucault's lack of archival rigor.
  - Strength: Well-supported through an analysis of the "psychiatry wars" of the 1960s–80s.
- **Core claim**: There is a shift from "Grand Narratives" to "Micro-histories."
  - Evidence: The transition from studying the "Rise of the Asylum" as a systemic event to studying individual households, parishes, and specific sufferers' narratives.
  - Strength: Strong; evidenced by the recent works of scholars like Houston and Suzuki.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Clinicians vs. Academic Historians**: Tension between using history as a tool for medical science versus analyzing psychiatry as a social construct.
- **Renaissance Interpretations**: Conflict between the view of the Renaissance as "hospitable" to madness (MacDonald) versus the view that it was ambivalent or selectively sentimental (Ingram).
- **Theory vs. Archive**: The conflict between the nomothetic theoretical frameworks of Sociology/Foucault and the idiographic archival focus of traditional historians.
