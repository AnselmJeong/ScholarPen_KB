# A LATENT HISTORIOGRAPHY? THE CASE OF PSYCHIATRY IN BRITAIN, 1500–1820

## Summary

This historiographical review surveys the development of writings on early modern British psychiatry (1500–1820) since the mid-twentieth century. Houston argues that the field exhibits a "vastly greater degree of difference" among its practitioners than any comparable historical subdiscipline, drawing on clinicians, social historians, sociologists, literary scholars, art historians, geographers, philosophers, and intellectual historians. The article traces the phases of scholarship from mid-century Fabian social history (Kathleen Jones, George Rosen), through anti-psychiatry revisionism of the 1960s–70s (Foucault, Goffman, Laing, Szasz), to post-revisionist microhistory emphasizing local context and patient experience in the 1980s–90s.

The central concept is "latent historiography"—the unacknowledged intellectual lineages and personal histories (roots and routes) that shape how historians write about madness. Houston contends that understanding these hidden influences clarifies the past, present, and future of the discipline. The article explores European and North American influences on British traditions, noting the persistent but mostly rejected impact of Foucault, the lasting ghost of anti-psychiatry, and the productive tensions between social, cultural, and literary approaches. The period before 1800 remains relatively neglected compared to modern psychiatry, and this review aims to fill that gap by providing an in-depth chronological analysis of the field's evolution.

## Key Concepts

- **Latent historiography** — The unacknowledged intellectual lineages and personal histories ("roots and routes") that shape how historians write about psychiatry; the field's diversity stems from these hidden influences.
- **[[concepts/presentism]]** — Judging past events by present-day standards; here critiqued in triumphalist clinician histories and anti-psychiatry's political presentism.
- **[[concepts/historicism]]** — Understanding the past on its own terms; the article advocates for contextual analysis and critiques Foucault for failing to historicize properly.
- **Modernization theory** — The belief that societies progress toward rational, bureaucratic, and secular forms; shaped mid-century histories (Rosen, Jones) and later contested.
- **Social constructionism** — The view that diagnoses and categories are socially produced; influenced anti-psychiatry and revisionist approaches.
- **[[concepts/anti-psychiatry]]** — A diverse movement (1960s–70s) critiquing psychiatric authority and institutions; its metanarrative persists as "a ghost at the feast."
- **Social control** — The idea that asylums function to manage deviance and maintain order; central to Scull's Weberian-Marxist interpretation.
- **The "psychiatry wars"** — The 1960s–80s ideological battles over psychiatry's legitimacy, diagnosis, and institutional power; shaped post-revisionist historiography.
- **The linguistic/cultural turn** — The shift toward analysing language and representation as constitutive of reality; influenced literary scholars but debated by historians like Roger Cooter.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: The history of early modern British psychiatry is uniquely diverse** — drawing on more disciplinary perspectives than any comparable field (clinicians, sociologists, literary scholars, art historians, geographers, philosophers).
  - Evidence: Extensive citation of key works across disciplines (Bynum, Porter, Scull, MacDonald, Jones, Parry-Jones, Smith, Digby, Murphy, Ingram, Hodgkin, Kromm, Schmidt, Gowland, Philo).
  - Strength: Strong as a historiographical survey – comprehensive and well-referenced; the claim about "vastly greater degree of difference" is attributed to Micale and Porter and asserted rather than systematically proven.

- **Core claim: This diversity is largely unacknowledged ("latent historiography")** — scholars' intellectual roots and personal histories shape their approaches but are rarely made explicit.
  - Evidence: Identification of specific lineages (MacDonald's secularization model from French anti-clericalism; Scull's Marxism from Past and Present; Porter's constructivism from Cambridge HPS).
  - Strength: The concept is evocative but not fully operationalized; the article functions more as a survey than a systematic demonstration of hidden lineages.

- **Core claim: The field moved through three phases** — mid-century Fabian optimism → anti-psychiatry revisionism → post-revisionist microhistory focused on experience and local context.
  - Evidence: Chronological structuring of the article; discussion of key figures and their intellectual contexts.
  - Strength: Well-supported by the narrative; the article's own structure demonstrates these phases.

- **Core claim: Foucault's influence is persistently provocative but mostly rejected by historians** — few engage systematically and constructively (Philo is the exception).
  - Evidence: Citation of critiques by Porter, MacDonald, and others; note that most historians "challenge and refute, even damn" Foucault.
  - Strength: Moderate – relies on anecdotal evidence from consultations and published critiques.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Internal tension: The article critiques "presentist, triumphalist" clinician histories while acknowledging their "sympathy for sufferers"—it does not fully resolve whether all presentism is bad.
- Internal tension: It champions microhistorical, experiential approaches while worrying that the field has fragmented into "story telling" without "conceptualized scholarship"—an unresolved tension.
- Internal tension: The article is itself a form of latent historiography that reveals Houston's own preference for empirically grounded, archive-driven scholarship and skepticism toward high theory (Foucault, anti-psychiatry).
- External tension with existing wiki: The wiki may treat "presentism vs. historicism" as a binary; this article shows more complex entanglements and multiple forms of presentism.
