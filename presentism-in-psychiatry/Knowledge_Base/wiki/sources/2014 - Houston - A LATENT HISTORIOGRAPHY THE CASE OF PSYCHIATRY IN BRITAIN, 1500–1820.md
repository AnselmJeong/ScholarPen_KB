# A Latent Historiography? The Case of Psychiatry in Britain, 1500–1820

## Summary

This article provides an in-depth historiographical review of the history of psychiatry in early modern Britain (1500–1820), a period that has received far less synthetic attention than the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. R. A. Houston argues that the field exhibits a "vastly greater degree of difference" among its scholarly contributions than any other historical sub-discipline. This fragmentation stems from the unusually wide range of disciplinary perspectives that have shaped the topic: social history, sociology, literary studies, art history, philosophy, intellectual history, and the history of science and medicine. The article traces the development of these diverse approaches from the mid-twentieth century to the 2010s.

Houston organises the review chronologically, mapping the main phases of historiographical change. He begins with the early modernisation and Fabian social policy frameworks of George Rosen and Kathleen Jones, then examines the anti-psychiatry movement of the 1960s–1970s (Foucault, Goffman, Laing, Szasz, Scull) which replaced progress narratives with social constructionism and social control theses. The article then focuses on the key figures of Roy Porter and Michael MacDonald, who revitalised the field in the 1980s, before considering the impact of the linguistic and cultural turns from literary studies, feminist scholarship, and art history. Throughout, the article highlights the persistent tensions between clinician-historians writing presentist, triumphalist narratives and academic historians committed to contextual, externalist approaches.

The central finding is that the historiography of early modern British psychiatry is "latent" — rich and varied yet fragmented across disciplines, lacking a unified synthetic tradition. Houston demonstrates that the field's development has been profoundly shaped by the anti-psychiatry movement and by the ongoing, often contentious engagement with Michel Foucault's ideas. The article also reveals a deep internal debate between grand narratives of social control and micro-historical studies emphasising consumer culture, agency, and well-intentioned care. This historiographical mapping is significant because it fills a gap in coverage of the early modern period and provides a framework for understanding the field's past, present, and future trajectory.

## Key Concepts

- **Latent Historiography** — The central thesis of the article. It describes the state of the historiography of early modern psychiatry as rich, diverse, and fragmented across many disciplines, yet rarely synthesised into a coherent tradition. The term "latent" captures the unacknowledged lineages and the absence of a unified scholarly community.
- **Anti-Psychiatry** — A diverse intellectual and social movement from the 1960s–70s that attacked the legitimacy, power, and epistemology of psychiatry. Key figures include Foucault, Goffman, Laing, Szasz, and Scheff. The article argues this movement fundamentally shaped the historiography by creating a "revisionist" metanarrative that later historians had to grapple with.
- **Presentism vs. Historicism** — The tension between judging the past by present standards (presentism) and understanding it on its own terms (historicism). Houston notes that classic histories of psychiatry were presentist, progressivist, and internalist, whereas modern academic historians advocate a contextual, externalist approach.
- **Social Control** — A key analytical lens, popularised by anti-psychiatry and sociological theory, that interprets the rise of the asylum as a mechanism for managing deviant populations in capitalist society. The article shows this model has been challenged by micro-historical studies emphasising consumer culture and humanitarian motives.
- **Foucauldian Analysis** — An approach derived from Michel Foucault, focusing on discourse, power-knowledge, and the "great confinement." The article describes Foucault as a "ghost at the feast" — most historians engage with him by refuting or challenging his claims, yet his work remains a productive provocation.
- **Linguistic Turn / Cultural Turn** — Methodological shifts emphasising the role of language, discourse, and culture in constituting reality. The article discusses how literary scholars have used close reading to analyse the "madness of language" and how the cultural turn has both enriched and complicated social history.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The historiography of early modern British psychiatry is uniquely diverse, fragmented, and "latent," shaped by contributions from an unusually broad range of disciplines that are rarely synthesised.
  - Evidence: The article provides a detailed, chronological literature review covering social history, sociology, literary studies, art history, intellectual history, and the history of medicine. It traces the influence of modernisation theory, anti-psychiatry, social history (Porter, MacDonald), and cultural/literary approaches (Ingram, Hodgkin, Kromm).
  - Strength: Strong. Houston is an established scholar in this field, and the argument is supported by an expert, annotated critical review of the secondary literature. The evidence is historiographical rather than experimental, appropriate for the genre.
- **Core claim**: The anti-psychiatry movement of the 1960s–70s fundamentally reshaped the historiography, creating a revisionist metanarrative centred on social control and labelling.
  - Evidence: The article details how figures like Foucault, Goffman, Laing, Szasz, and Scull challenged earlier progress narratives and how later historians (e.g., Porter, Smith, Murphy) revised this revisionism.
  - Strength: Well-supported. The article shows the enduring impact of anti-psychiatry through specific debates (e.g., the rise of the asylum) and ongoing tensions.
- **Core claim**: There is a persistent tension between clinician-historians (presentist, triumphalist) and academic historians (contextual, externalist), and between grand narratives of social control and micro-historical studies emphasising agency and consumer culture.
  - Evidence: The article contrasts the work of Hunter & Macalpine with that of MacDonald, Porter, and others, and it highlights debates over the meaning of madness and the role of institutions.
  - Strength: Convincing. The article systematically documents these divides, showing how they shape current scholarship.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Internal tension between the "social control"/revisionist narrative (Scull, Foucault) and micro-historical studies (Porter, Smith, Murphy) that emphasise agency, consumer culture, and humanitarian motives. The field has not resolved this tension.
- Persistent tension between clinician-historians (writing presentist, triumphalist, internalist narratives) and academic historians (pursuing contextual, externalist analysis). Houston notes the gap may be narrowing but remains significant.
- Deep tension between the vast influence of Michel Foucault's work and the widespread rejection of his specific claims by most practising historians of early modern Britain. Foucault is described as a "ghost at the feast" — a productive provocation but not an accepted authority.

## Open Questions

- Has the field moved beyond the fragmentation into micro-history and away from grand narratives that Houston diagnoses, or does the "latent" character of the historiography persist?
- Is the gap in approaches between clinicians and academic historians truly narrowing, as Houston tentatively suggests, or has it remained wide due to different professional incentives?
- How does the historiography of early modern British psychiatry compare to that of other national contexts (e.g., France, Germany, the United States), which Houston mentions only briefly? Would a comparative analysis reveal similar patterns of fragmentation?
