# On History for the Present; Revisiting George Stocking's Influential Rejection of “Presentism”

## Summary
Ira Bashkow examines the origin and evolution of the "presentism" vs. "historicism" dichotomy in the history of anthropology. He argues that George Stocking's influential 1965 warning against presentism—evaluating the past by present standards—was not a timeless historical law but a specific reaction to the "naïve positivism" of Robert Watson and the Cold War-era image of science as a linear march of progress.

Through an analysis of Stocking's writings over five decades and the founding of the *Journal of the History of the Behavioral Sciences* (JHBS), Bashkow demonstrates that Stocking repeatedly qualified his original position. He shows that Stocking eventually embraced a "pluralistic" approach, acknowledging that the tension between past and present is inevitable and can be constructively used to recover forgotten ideas.

The author concludes that because the battle against teleological narratives of science has largely been won, historians of anthropology are now free to use the past more instrumentally. He suggests that a moderated presentism can help make the discipline more inclusive and strengthen knowledge claims in an era of public mistrust of expertise.

## Key Entities
- **Journal of the History of the Behavioral Sciences (JHBS)** (tool/publication) — The venue where the debate over presentism was sparked and promulgated.

- **Psychology Press** (org) — The publishing house that funded the early issues of JHBS.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The methodological vice of evaluating historical material according to present-day purposes or standards, often leading to anachronism.
- **Historicism** — The methodological virtue of understanding the past for its own sake, viewing ideas within their specific temporal and cultural context.
- **Scientific Paradigm** — A set of collectively held tacit assumptions and practices within a scientific community (derived from Thomas Kuhn).
- **Teleological Narrative** — A "story of progress" that presents the past as a linear sequence of discoveries leading inevitably to the present.
- **Enlightened Presentism** — A moderated approach where the past is used to identify forgotten problems or hypotheses that remain relevant to current scholarship.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "avoid presentism" rule was a polemical product of a 1960s intellectual battle against positivism.
  - Evidence: Stocking's reaction to Robert Watson's editorial policy in JHBS, which sought to use history to "implement" a unified behavioral science.
  - Strength: Strong; supported by primary archival evidence of editorial disputes and citations of early JHBS issues.
- **Core claim**: George Stocking's own views shifted from a strict rejection of presentism to a more flexible, pluralistic view.
  - Evidence: A chronological review of Stocking's publications from 1965 to his 2010 memoir, showing repeated "second thoughts" and qualifications.
  - Strength: Very strong; based on a direct bibliographic evolution of the subject's own prose.
- **Core claim**: Strict historicism has reached a point of diminishing returns in the current intellectual climate.
  - Evidence: The wide acceptance of Kuhn's ideas and the "triumph" of historicism in the history of science (as noted by Lorraine Daston).
  - Strength: Moderate; relies on the author's synthesis of current academic trends.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension**: The "presentist/historicist" binary is often taught as a rigid rule, yet the creator of that rule (Stocking) spent decades admitting it was too rigid and "rash."
- **Conflict with Tradition**: The source challenges the traditional academic belief that presentism is an absolute error, arguing instead that it can be a constructive tool for disciplinary survival.

## Open Questions
- How can "creative non-fiction" or "novelization" be used in historical research to engage modern readers without falling back into the "bad history" of naive positivism?
- In an era of widespread mistrust of expertise, how can a "presentist" approach to history be used to specifically strengthen current knowledge claims?
