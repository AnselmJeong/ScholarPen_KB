# Madness in Civilization: Chapter Four

## Summary
This chapter explores the conceptualization of madness in Europe from the late 15th to the early 18th century. It examines the tension between supernatural explanations (demonic possession and witchcraft) and naturalistic medical theories (humoralism), noting that these were not binary opposites but overlapping frameworks used by the era's educated elite.

The author analyzes the cultural phenomenon of "melancholia," which served as both a debilitating medical condition and a mark of intellectual genius. Through the study of medical texts like Robert Burton's *The Anatomy of Melancholy* and the patient records of Richard Napier, the text illustrates how practitioners blended alchemy, astrology, and bleeding to treat mental distress.

Furthermore, the chapter discusses the "naturalization" of madness, where religious disputes between Catholics, Puritans, and Anglicans inadvertently pushed society toward secular explanations. This shift is mirrored in the transition of madness in theatre, moving from the stylized "Senecan fury" of revenge tragedies to the complex, psychologically grounded portrayals found in Shakespeare.

## Key Entities
- **College of Physicians** (org) — The professional medical body in London that navigated the boundaries between natural pathology and divine affliction.
- **Bedlam** (org/facility) — A specialized facility for the mad; while rare in actual practice during this era, it became a prominent dramatic trope in Jacobean theatre.
- **Royal Society** (org) — Mentioned in relation to Joseph Glanvill and the rise of the "virtuosi" natural philosophers.
- **The Anatomy of Melancholy** (tool/text) — Robert Burton's comprehensive 17th-century synthesis of knowledge on melancholy.
- **Compendium Maleficarum** (tool/text) — Francesco Maria Guazzo's authoritative text on witches and demonic influence.

## Key Concepts
- **Melancholia** — A fashionable yet debilitating disorder linked to black bile, characterized by fear, sadness, and hallucinations; often associated with creative genius.
- **Humoralism** — The Galenic medical theory that health depends on the balance of four humors; madness is viewed as an imbalance (e.g., excess black bile).
- **Possession vs. Natural Illness** — The conceptual struggle to determine if madness was a spiritual attack (Satanic) or a physical pathology (brain/humoral).
- **Hysteria / "Suffocation of the Mother"** — A diagnosis involving the "wandering womb," used as a naturalistic alternative to witchcraft accusations.
- **Revenge Tragedy** — A theatrical genre utilizing extreme violence and "unhinged" madness (inspired by Seneca) for entertainment.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Science and superstition were not binary opposites in the early modern era.
  - Evidence: Practitioners like Richard Napier simultaneously used astrology, alchemy, and bloodletting to treat "mopish" patients.
  - Strength: Strong; supported by archival notebooks and contemporary medical texts.
- **Core claim**: Religious conflict accelerated the secularization of madness.
  - Evidence: Anglican figures like Samuel Harsnett mocked both Catholic and Puritan exorcisms as "impostures," promoting naturalistic accounts of the symptoms.
  - Strength: Well-supported via the analysis of Harsnett's influence on Shakespeare's *King Lear*.
- **Core claim**: The portrayal of madness in theatre transitioned from spectacle to psychology.
  - Evidence: The shift from the gore of *Titus Andronicus* (Senecan fury) to the gradual psychological collapse of King Lear.
  - Strength: Strong; uses comparative literary analysis.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **The "Genius-Melancholy" Paradox**: A tension where the same symptoms of melancholy could be interpreted either as a pathology requiring a cure or as a sign of intellectual superiority.
- **Reality vs. Drama**: The author notes a contradiction between the historical rarity of madhouses in the 17th century and their overwhelming prevalence as settings in Jacobean plays.
