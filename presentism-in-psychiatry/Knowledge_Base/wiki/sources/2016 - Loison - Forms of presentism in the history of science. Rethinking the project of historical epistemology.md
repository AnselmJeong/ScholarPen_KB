# Forms of presentism in the history of science. Rethinking the project of historical epistemology

## Summary

This theoretical article addresses the resurgence of presentism in the history of science by constructing a systematic typology of its forms. Loison argues that the discipline has moved beyond a simple good/bad dichotomy and now requires precise definitions of how present knowledge is legitimately used to understand the past. The article is structured in three parts: a classification of four distinct forms of presentism, a redefinition of anachronism, Whiggism, and positivist history as misuses of these forms, and a characterization of historical epistemology as a solution that incorporates a fifth, critical form of presentism.

The core research design is conceptual analysis and philosophical argumentation, drawing on the French tradition of historical epistemology, particularly the work of Georges Canguilhem. Loison distinguishes between Bachelard's more directive normative presentism and Canguilhem's regulatory approach, which preserves both the rationality of science (present as yardstick) and its contingency (the past could have been different). The article uses examples from the history of biology — Hugo de Vries' mutation theory, Lamarck's evolutionary theory, and cell theory — to illustrate each form and its legitimate applications.

The main findings are that presentism is not a monolith; that Whiggism and anachronism are specific misuses rather than inevitable consequences of presentist approaches; and that historical epistemology, as practiced by Canguilhem, offers a "critical presentism" that works bidirectionally — from present to past (normative judgment) and from past to present (contingency awareness). This framework escapes both the teleology of positivist history and the relativism of historicism.

## Key Concepts

- **Empirical presentism** — Using present-day scientific knowledge to specify the characteristics of the empirical substratum studied by past scientists, regardless of their interpretations. This form assumes the natural world exists and is historically stable.
- **Descriptive presentism** — Translating or transcribing the structure of past explanations into terms understandable to present-day readers. This is unavoidable due to the didactic constraint of communication.
- **Causal-narrative presentism** — Using knowledge of later events to identify causal historical processes, select relevant past events, and construct narratives. Includes counterfactual history as a radical form.
- **Normative presentism** — Using present-day concepts and explanations to identify the limitations and theoretical shortcomings of past explanations, based on the progressive nature of science.
- **Critical presentism** — The fifth form characterizing historical epistemology. A bidirectional critical relation that simultaneously acknowledges the rationality of science (allowing normative judgment) and its contingency and historicity (recognizing science could have been different). Works from present to past and past to present.
- **Whiggism** — Defined as a twofold misuse of presentism: (1) misuse of causal-narrative presentism by equating causality with finality (teleological history), and (2) misuse of normative presentism by reducing past science to an incomplete version of present truth (precursor-hunting).
- **Anachronism** — Defined specifically as a misuse of descriptive presentism: conflating the actors' own categories with the historian's analytical categories, or assuming continuity of word meanings across time.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: Presentism is not a monolith; there are four distinct forms (empirical, descriptive, causal-narrative, normative), each with different legitimacy conditions, and a fifth form (critical presentism) resolves the Whiggism-relativism impasse.**
  - Evidence: The fourfold typology is constructed from existing literature (Hull, Jardine, Tosh, Alvargonzález) and the author's experience as a historian of biology. The critical presentism is derived from close reading of Canguilhem's 1946 text on cell theory.
  - Strength: Strong conceptual argument with clear logical structure and illustrative examples. Limitations: This is a methodological prescription, not an empirical finding; the Canguilhem interpretation, while careful, is explicitly one reading.

- **Core claim: Whiggism and anachronism are specific misuses of presentism, not inevitable consequences of any presentist approach.**
  - Evidence: Anachronism is a misuse of descriptive presentism (conflating actor categories with historian categories). Whiggism has two forms: misuse of causal-narrative presentism (teleology) and misuse of normative presentism (precursor-hunting/devaluation of past).
  - Strength: The redefinition resolves conflation in existing literature and provides operational clarity. Limitations: The boundary between legitimate and illegitimate use may blur in practice.

- **Core claim: Historical epistemology, as critical presentism, reconciles the rationality of science (normative judgment) with its historicity (contingency), escaping both positivism and relativism.**
  - Evidence: Canguilhem's concept of "scientific ideology" and his methodological stance against both simplistic normative presentism (Bachelard's excess) and radical historicism (Foucault's archaeology).
  - Strength: Offers a normative characterization of historical epistemology as a research program. Limitations: The distinction between Bachelard's "directive" and Canguilhem's "regulatory" normative presentism may be analytically blurry in practice.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The distinction between Bachelard's "directive" and Canguilhem's "regulatory" normative presentism is conceptually clear but practically blurry. When does normative judgment cross from regulatory to directive?
- The tension between rationality and contingency remains analytically unstable. The article gestures toward resolution (Canguilhem's "making possibility necessary") but does not fully operationalize how one simultaneously holds science as both rational/necessary and contingent/could-have-been-different.
- The two postulates underlying empirical presentism (natural world exists; it is stable over time) are less unproblematic for human sciences like psychiatry, where disease entities may change historically (e.g., hysteria disappearing, ADHD emerging). The "same natural world" assumption is weaker for medical/psychiatric categories.
- The article assumes scientific progress is general and measurable (predictive capacity, explanatory power), but this assumption is contested in the human sciences. The framework's applicability to contested fields like psychiatry may be limited.

## Open Questions
- Does Loison's "critical presentism" framework apply to psychiatry differently than to the natural sciences, given psychiatry's less consensus-based definition of "progress" and its socially constructed diagnostic categories?
- Whose present serves as the yardstick for normative presentism in a field with competing paradigms (biomedical, psychosocial, critical)?
- How does this framework handle sources that use presentism to critique historical racism in psychiatry (e.g., Arya 2024)? Is such critique legitimate critical presentism or does it risk falling into Whiggism?
- Would operationalizing "critical presentism" as a methodology for the current project require choosing among competing present-day psychiatric paradigms?
