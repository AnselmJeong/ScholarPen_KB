# Forms of presentism in the history of science. Rethinking the project of historical epistemology

## Summary
This paper addresses the long-standing tension in the history of science between "presentism" (the use of current knowledge to interpret the past) and "historicism" (the insistence on analyzing the past solely within its own context). The author argues that the traditional dichotomy—where presentism is often equated with the "sin" of Whiggism—is insufficient for a professional practice of history.

The research employs a conceptual and argumentative design to decompose presentism into four distinct functional types: empirical, descriptive, causal-narrative, and normative. By defining these categories, Loison clarifies that "Whiggism" is actually a dual failure: a misuse of causal-narrative presentism (treating history as teleological) and a misuse of normative presentism (devaluing the past for not being "true" by modern standards).

The significance of the work lies in its proposal of "Critical Presentism" as the methodological core of [[historical-epistemology]]. This approach allows historians to acknowledge that science is simultaneously contingent (it could have been different) and rational (it produces transhistorical truths), thereby avoiding both the blindness of positivism and the relativism of radical constructivism.

## Key Concepts
- **Empirical Presentism** — Using current knowledge of the natural world (material facts) to understand past observations; considered legitimate as the physical world remains largely stable.
- **Descriptive Presentism** — Translating past concepts into terms understandable to modern readers; necessary for communication but risks anachronism.
- **Causal-Narrative Presentism** — Using hindsight of later events to identify causal processes or run counterfactuals to differentiate history from anthropology.
- **Normative Presentism** — Using present theories to identify the limitations/shortcomings of past theories; essential for identifying "progress" but risky if used as a "police" of the past.
- **Critical Presentism** — A sophisticated synthesis that accounts for both the rationality (necessity) and historicity (contingency) of science.
- **Whiggism / Positivism** — The misuse of presentism: treating the past as a linear, inevitable path to the present or a failed version of it.
- **Anachronism** — A specific failure of descriptive presentism where the historian conflates their own categories with those of past actors.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Presentism is not a binary of "good" vs "bad," but a set of four distinct functional tools.
  - Evidence: Analysis of historiographical practices and critiques of authors like Latour, Kuhn, and Butterfield.
  - Strength: Strong logical taxonomy, though primarily conceptual rather than empirical.
- **Core claim**: Whiggism is the combined misuse of causal-narrative and normative presentism.
  - Evidence: Contrast between "inevitabilist" narratives (causal failure) and "precursitis" (normative failure).
  - Strength: High; provides a more granular definition than the standard "Whig history" label.
- **Core claim**: Historical Epistemology solves the presentism deadlock via "Critical Presentism."
  - Evidence: Examination of the Bachelard-Canguilhem lineage, specifically Canguilhem's balance of "sense of possibility" (contingency) and "demonstration of necessity" (rationality).
  - Strength: Conceptually robust, though operationalization in specific case studies is suggested as a further step.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension**: The author notes a divergence between Bachelard (who prioritizes the normative power of the present and "epistemological breaks") and Canguilhem (who emphasizes contingency and the "sense of possibility").
- **External Conflict**: The author explicitly challenges "radical constructivists" (e.g., Bruno Latour) who argue that historians must never escape the conceptual world of the actors they study.

## Open Questions
- How can "Critical Presentism" be specifically operationalized in a case study to prove it avoids the pitfalls of the other four types?
- Does this framework apply equally to the history of social sciences, where a "rationality/truth" standard may be more contested than in the natural sciences?
