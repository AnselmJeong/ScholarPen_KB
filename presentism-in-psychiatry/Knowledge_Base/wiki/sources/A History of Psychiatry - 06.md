# A History of Psychiatry - 06

## Summary

This chapter from Edward Shorter's *A History of Psychiatry* presents a revisionist account of psychoanalysis, arguing that it was not the culmination of psychiatric evolution but a temporary interruption—a "hiatus"—between two biological phases of the discipline. Shorter contends that psychoanalysis flourished for roughly three decades (1940s–1970s) not because of its scientific validity or therapeutic efficacy, but because it offered psychiatrists a professionally rewarding escape from asylum work into private office practice. The chapter traces this transformation from Freud's early practice in fin-de-siècle Vienna through the establishment of psychoanalytic institutions in Central Europe and ultimately to the movement's deep but temporary capture of American psychiatry.

The narrative is structured as a social and institutional history rather than a history of ideas. Shorter emphasizes the demographic and economic factors that enabled psychoanalysis to take hold: the availability of middle-class Jewish women as a receptive patient population in Vienna, the professional aspirations of younger psychiatrists seeking independence from university clinics, and the appeal of a doctrine that seemed to validate leisurely introspection. He documents the resistance from senior asylum-based professors who correctly perceived that psychoanalysis was diverting psychiatry from psychosis to neurosis, and details the dogmatic, movement-like structure Freud imposed on his followers—a structure that prevented falsification and alienated early dissenters like Adler, Stekel, Jung, and Bleuler.

The chapter's significance lies in its challenge to teleological histories that treat psychoanalysis as a natural endpoint. By framing it as a "pause" in the biological trajectory, Shorter repositions later developments—the rise of psychopharmacology, the DSM-III revolution, and the return to neuroscience—as a resumption of psychiatry's true course rather than a break. The account is deliberately provocative and relies on contemporary critiques from figures like Aschaffenburg, Raimann, and Rieger, as well as on quantitative data (e.g., the rise in APA private-practice attendance from 3.2% in 1910 to 7.3% in 1921) to support its thesis.

## Key Entities

- **American Psychoanalytic Association** (org) — Professional body that initially restricted training to MDs only, enforcing a medical monopoly over psychoanalytic practice in the US.
- **National Committee for Mental Hygiene** (org) — Founded in 1909, it promoted the "mental hygiene" movement and helped expand psychiatry's reach beyond the asylum before psychoanalysis took hold.
- **Clark University** (org) — Hosted Freud's 1909 lectures, giving a major public boost to psychoanalysis in the United States.
- **Manhattan State Hospital** (org) — Birthplace of the first US psychoanalytic society (1911); 10 of 15 founding members were associated with this asylum.
- **Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology** (tool) — Founded by Morton Prince in 1906, served as an early vehicle for disseminating psychoanalytic ideas among American physicians.
- **Burghölzli** (org) — Zurich university clinic under Bleuler and Jung where psychoanalytic seminars were held, influencing a generation of younger psychiatrists.
- **Bellevue Clinic (Kreuzlingen)** (org) — Private nervous clinic under Ludwig Binswanger, an early adopter of psychoanalysis that flourished despite professional boycotts.
- **General Medical Congress on Psychotherapy** (org) — First annual meeting in 1926 (Baden-Baden); heavily psychoanalytic, with 70% of 500 attendees in private practice.

## Key Concepts

- **Psychoanalytic Hiatus** — The central framing: psychoanalysis was not the destination of psychiatric history but a temporary interruption between two biological phases, lasting roughly 1920s–1970s.
- **Sexual Reductionism** — Freud's doctrine that neurosis stems from repressed childhood sexual fantasies or experiences; the chapter treats this as scientifically unsupported and the main target of early critics.
- **Psychoanalysis as a Movement vs. Science** — Freud organized psychoanalysis as a dogmatic movement where dissent was labeled "resistance," preventing empirical falsification and undermining scientific credibility.
- **Shift from Psychosis to Neurosis** — The discipline's center of gravity moved from severe mental illness requiring asylum care to common neurotic complaints treated in office practice, enabled by psychoanalysis.
- **Private Practice as Professional Aspiration** — The key driver for psychiatrists' embrace of analysis: it offered a way out of the asylum into lucrative, independent office-based practice, transforming the profession's identity.
- **Cathartic Therapy** — The early form of psychoanalysis (Breuer & Freud, 1895) using hypnosis and abreaction; later abandoned by Freud for insight-oriented therapy.
- **Resistance** — In psychoanalytic theory, the patient's unconscious opposition to bringing repressed material to consciousness; the chapter notes that Freud used the concept to dismiss scientific critics, harming the movement's credibility.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Psychoanalysis was a historical hiatus—a brief, scientifically unsupported deviation from psychiatry's biological trajectory, not its culmination.
  - Evidence: Historical narrative of Freud's practice, patient demographics (middle-class Jewish women in acculturation), quantitative data on private-practice shifts, contemporary critiques from Aschaffenburg, Raimann, Forel, and others.
  - Strength: Moderate. The argument is coherent and well-documented within a single monograph, drawing on primary and secondary sources, but it is not a systematic review. Some claims (e.g., "scientifically bankrupt") are evaluative and contestable.

- **Core claim**: Psychoanalysis succeeded not through therapeutic efficacy but because it met professional self-interest (private practice) and patient demand (introspection, sentimental care).
  - Evidence: Descriptions of early analysts' economic dependence on Freud, the appeal to middle-class sensibilities, the shift in APA meeting attendance from 3.2% private practice (1910) to 7.3% (1921), and the testimony of Viktor von Weizsäcker and others.
  - Strength: Strong. The evidence is concrete and well-sourced, though it leaves open the possibility that some patients were genuinely helped.

- **Core claim**: Freud's sexual reductionism was unsupported by evidence, and his dogmatic approach (treating dissent as resistance) prevented falsification, making psychoanalysis a movement rather than a science.
  - Evidence: Freud's self-description as "conquistador," not scientist; his admission of therapeutic uncertainty; the accounts of critics like Raimann and Aschaffenburg; the schisms with Adler, Stekel, Jung, and Bleuler.
  - Strength: Strong for the claim of dogmatism; weaker for the claim that no therapeutic benefit existed (the chapter itself notes that patients "craved" this kind of care). The scientific validity question remains debated in other historiography.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Internal tension: The source acknowledges psychoanalysis was "of enormous consequence for psychiatry" (opening private practice) while simultaneously calling it a "pause" that led to "scientific stagnation." This creates a paradox: a movement that transformed the profession was nonetheless scientifically empty. The resolution offered—that demand rather than truth drove adoption—is plausible but not fully argued.
- Internal tension: The account claims psychoanalysis dominated American psychiatry for only "about three decades" (1940s–1970s), which is relatively brief. This may weaken the "hiatus" metaphor if the analytic era was shorter than other periods of biological dominance.
- Potential external contradiction: Sources that treat psychoanalysis as having enduring cultural or therapeutic legacy (not currently in the wiki but common in secondary literature) would conflict with the source's dismissive framing.
