# Madness in the Ancient World (Chapter Two of *Madness in Civilization*)

## Summary

This chapter examines how madness was understood in the ancient Near East and Greco-Roman world, drawing on biblical texts, Greek drama, historiography, and medical writings. The author argues that two fundamentally different explanatory frameworks coexisted: a supernatural model (divine punishment, demonic possession) and a naturalistic, medical model (humoral imbalance). The Hebrew Bible presents madness as a curse from Yahweh, as in the cases of King Saul and Nebuchadnezzar, while prophets such as Isaiah and Jeremiah displayed behaviors that blurred the line between divine inspiration and insanity. In Classical Greece, literary sources — Homer, the tragedians, and Herodotus — show that popular belief attributed madness to the gods, yet a rival tradition emerged among Hippocratic physicians who insisted that all diseases, including epilepsy and mania, had natural bodily causes.

The Hippocratic corpus, particularly the treatise *On the Sacred Disease*, represents the first systematic attempt to explain mental disturbance without recourse to the supernatural. Humoral theory held that imbalances of blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile (the latter causing melancholia) produced specific forms of madness. The brain, not the heart, was identified as the seat of cognition and emotion. Early diagnostic categories — melancholia, mania, hysteria, phrenitis, and the sacred disease — were delineated, though their boundaries remained fluid. The chapter also explores the gendered doctrine of the wandering womb as an explanation for hysteria, and Plato’s alternative conception of madness as divine inspiration, which offered a positive valuation of non‑rational experience.

The significance of this source lies in its demonstration that the tension between supernatural and naturalistic explanations for madness is not a modern phenomenon but was present from the very beginning of recorded history. It also shows that the conceptual foundations of later psychiatric nosology (humoral theory, melancholia, mania, hysteria) were laid in antiquity, and that the medicalization of madness was already underway in Classical Greece. The author is careful to note the limitations of the evidence — literary sources cannot be read as direct reflections of popular belief, and the experiences of the poor and the mad themselves are largely lost — but the overall picture is one of enduring conceptual coexistence rather than simple linear progress.

## Key Concepts

- **Divine punishment / possession model** — Madness inflicted by gods or demons as retribution for impiety, disobedience, or hubris; the dominant explanatory framework in ancient Israel and among the Greek populace.
- **Humoral theory** — The body composed of four fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile); imbalance causes both physical and mental illness; the core of Hippocratic naturalistic medicine.
- **Melancholia** — Madness caused by excess black bile (cold and dry); characterized by depressive states, fear, and despondency.
- **Mania** — Frenzied, agitated madness, often linked to excess yellow bile; differentiated from melancholia as a distinct polarity.
- **Sacred disease** — Epilepsy (and possibly hysteria) interpreted as divine in origin; the Hippocratics argued it was entirely natural, contesting supernatural explanations.
- **Madness as divine inspiration** — Plato’s concept of non‑rational knowing — madness as a path to truth (erotic, prophetic, bacchic); offers a positive alternative to pathological models.
- **Wandering womb** — The Hippocratic and later Galenic notion that hysteria in women results from the uterus moving within the body or emitting vapours; a gendered explanation for mental disturbance.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim 1: Two competing frameworks coexisted.**
  - Evidence: Biblical narratives of Saul and Nebuchadnezzar (supernatural) versus Hippocratic treatises (naturalistic); Greek drama and Herodotus show popular supernatural beliefs even as medical texts advance naturalism.
  - Strength: Strongly supported by direct textual evidence; the author acknowledges the difficulty of inferring popular belief from elite sources.

- **Core claim 2: Hippocratic medicine represents the first systematic naturalistic break.**
  - Evidence: *On the Sacred Disease* explicitly rejects divine causation and argues that epilepsy has a natural cause (blocked phlegm). Humoral theory is extended to mania, melancholia, and hysteria.
  - Strength: The text survives and its arguments are clear; however, the author notes that temple medicine continued to thrive, so the break was not wholesale.

- **Core claim 3: No sharp distinction between mental and physical illness.**
  - Evidence: Hippocratic medicine treats madness as one outcome of humoral imbalance, just like any other disease. Fevers, delirium, and emotional disturbances are all linked to bodily states.
  - Strength: Well-supported by the holistic framework of humoral theory; later Western medicine would often draw sharper lines.

- **Core claim 4: The brain was identified as the seat of madness.**
  - Evidence: Hippocratic text states that joys, sorrows, wisdom, and madness all arise from the brain, countering Aristotle’s heart‑centered view.
  - Strength: Direct quotation provided; this was a minority view in antiquity but influential in later medicine.

- **Core claim 5: Diagnostic categories were emerging (melancholia, mania, hysteria, phrenitis, epilepsy).**
  - Evidence: The chapter lists these categories and notes debates about whether they were distinct or phases of one disorder.
  - Strength: Based on surviving medical texts; boundaries remained fluid, but the categories were used by physicians such as Celsus, Aretaeus, and Soranus.

- **Core claim 6: Literary and historical sources reveal popular supernatural beliefs.**
  - Evidence: Herodotus’ accounts of Cambyses and Cleomenes, where both divine punishment and naturalistic explanations (epilepsy, drunkenness) are discussed.
  - Strength: The author is cautious — drama may distort — but concludes that the prevalence of divine explanations in multiple genres is telling.

- **Core claim 7: Positive valuation of madness coexisted with pathologization.**
  - Evidence: Plato’s concept of divine madness; the Hebrew prophets (inspired vs. mad); echoes in later holy fool tradition.
  - Strength: Well-attested in philosophical and religious texts; the author does not fully resolve how these coexisted practically with medical pathology.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension:** The author warns against taking Greek drama as direct evidence of popular belief, but then relies heavily on dramatic texts (Euripides, Sophocles) to illustrate supernatural frameworks. This methodological caveat is acknowledged but not fully resolved.
- **Class and access bias:** The chapter focuses on elite medical and literary sources, while noting that the poor could not afford physicians. Their beliefs and practices remain largely unknown, creating a gap in the historical picture.
- **Coexistence of divine inspiration and pathology:** Plato’s positive valuation of madness (divine madness) sits awkwardly alongside the dominant pathological view. The author does not explain how these frameworks interacted in practice — e.g., were inspired prophets also seen as ill?
- **Potential conflict with wiki’s [[concepts/biomedical-model]]:** The wiki may imply the biomedical model is a modern development; this chapter shows that naturalistic, brain‑based explanations of mental illness are at least 2,500 years old, complicating any linear progress narrative.
- **Potential conflict with [[concepts/anti-psychiatry]]:** The wiki’s anti‑psychiatry page (if framed as a 20th‑century movement) would need to acknowledge ancient precedents — the divine madness tradition offers a pre‑modern critique of medical authority.
