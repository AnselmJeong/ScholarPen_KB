# Presentism and Diversity in the History of Psychology

## Summary

This paper examines a controversy surrounding the Wikipedia entry on the history of psychology, where an editor using the name “Jagged_85” inserted claims that most major developments in psychology originated in the medieval Arab world. Brock analyzes these claims as a case study of presentism—the imposition of modern epistemic categories on past actors—and connects them to broader debates about how to write a more diverse history of psychology. The paper draws on classic historiographical work by Graham Richards and Roger Smith, who argued that psychology has a “short history” (beginning in the late 19th century) and a “long past” of pre-disciplinary reflexive discourse.

Brock’s main finding is that retroactively labeling medieval scholars like Ibn al-Haytham as “psychologists” is historically inaccurate because they understood their work as part of optics, medicine, or philosophy, not psychology. He shows that these priority claims are often driven by nationalism, religious identity, or modern geopolitical tensions, not by sound historiography. The paper also critiques the attempt to achieve diversity by simply adding non-Western material to standard textbooks without rethinking what counts as the history of psychology.

The significance of this source lies in its proposal for a non-presentist route to diversity: either by including neglected aspects of modern psychology’s actual history (e.g., psychology outside Europe and the US, women, minorities) or by following Roger Smith’s “thoughtful presentism”—tracing how earlier thought influenced the later discipline without calling it “psychology.” Brock endorses indigenisation as a constructive alternative to retro-labeling, where psychologists in non-Western cultures build new approaches informed by their own intellectual traditions.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism** — In its pejorative sense, the imposition of modern epistemic categories and values onto past actors who did not share them. Brock uses this to critique retroactive labeling of medieval scholars as psychologists.
- **Priority claims** — Assertions that a particular figure was the “first” to discover or do something, often driven by nationalism or cultural chauvinism. Examples include al-Haytham vs. Fechner for psychophysics, Marulić vs. Göckel for the term “psychologia,” and James vs. Wundt for the first experimental laboratory.
- **Reflexive discourse** — Graham Richards’ term for pre-disciplinary human self-understanding (philosophy, theology, literature, etc.) that is not yet psychology. Central to distinguishing legitimate intellectual history from presentist retro-labeling.
- **Indigenised psychology** — The movement to adapt Western psychology to local cultural contexts by incorporating indigenous intellectual traditions, as opposed to claiming those traditions were already “psychology.”
- **Thoughtful presentism** — Roger Smith’s concept: asking present-driven questions of history while ensuring the answers come from the past, not the present. Brock endorses this as a legitimate way to study history.
- **Ebbinghaus distinction** — The claim that psychology has a “long past” but a “short history,” with the formal discipline emerging only in the late 19th century. Brock uses this to argue that pre-1850 thought belongs to reflexive discourse, not psychology.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The Wikipedia edits by Jagged_85 attributing modern psychology to medieval Islamic scholars constitute presentism.
  - Evidence: These scholars (al-Haytham, Avicenna, al-Balkhi) worked within frameworks of optics, medicine, and philosophy, not a discipline called “psychology.” Green’s critique in the special issue of *Advances in the History of Psychology* provides the direct case.
  - Strength: Well-supported by historiographical principles from Richards (1987) and Smith (1988). The reasoning is logically consistent: if we reject Aristotle as a psychologist, consistency requires rejecting al-Haytham as well.
  - Limitation: The paper is argumentative rather than empirical; it offers no new archival evidence, only critical analysis of existing claims.

- **Core claim**: Priority claims in the history of psychology are vehicles for nationalism, chauvinism, or modern political conflicts.
  - Evidence: The al-Haytham claims are linked to Arab/Muslim identity and tensions with the West; the James vs. Wundt dispute reflects American nationalism; the Marulić claim is Croatian national pride.
  - Strength: The pattern is clear across multiple examples, and Brock connects it to modern geopolitical tensions (Israel/Palestine, the Crusades, Western military intervention).
  - Limitation: The connection to geopolitics is speculative; Brock does not provide direct evidence that Khaleefa or Jagged_85 were motivated by these factors.

- **Core claim**: The proper history of psychology begins in the late 19th century; earlier thought is reflexive discourse, not psychology.
  - Evidence: Richards (1987) and Smith (1988) argue that if all reflexive discourse were included, the subject would be unmanageable and historically inaccurate. Brock extends this to non-Western thought.
  - Strength: Strong historiographical foundation. The “short history/long past” distinction is widely accepted.
  - Limitation: Danziger (2008) offers a friendly amendment: the degree of continuity may be greater than the sharp break implies (e.g., the storage metaphor from Plato to modern memory research). This complicates the clean separation.

- **Core claim**: Diversity without presentism is possible through indigenisation or by tracing actual historical influences.
  - Evidence: Brock contrasts retro-labeling (Khaleefa, Jagged_85) with constructive indigenisation (e.g., modern Islamic psychology drawing on medieval thought). He endorses Smith’s caveat that earlier thought can be legitimately studied if it influenced the later discipline.
  - Strength: Provides a practical, principled path forward that satisfies both diversity advocates and professional historians.
  - Limitation: The practicality of indigenisation is not fully addressed; Brock acknowledges that Western disciplinary structures are entrenched and that abandoning them is difficult.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Brock endorses “thoughtful presentism” (Smith) while condemning Jagged_85’s presentism, creating a boundary problem: who decides where the line falls? His own criterion (answers from the past) is clear in theory but contested in practice—e.g., Khaleefa might claim he is tracing genuine influences, not imposing categories.
- Danziger’s friendly amendment—that continuity (e.g., memory storage metaphor) may be greater than the “short history/long past” break implies—partially undermines the sharp distinction Brock relies on. If Plato’s metaphor still shapes modern psychology, why not call it part of psychology’s history?
- Brock acknowledges that Staeuble’s call to abandon disciplinary boundaries is impractical, yet he endorses Smith’s similar provocation that “the history of psychology should be abandoned.” This leaves an unresolved tension between theoretical ideals (unified social science) and institutional reality.
- The wiki’s existing source summaries often refer to pre-19th century thinkers as “psychiatrists” or “psychologists” (e.g., Pinel and Esquirol as laying foundations for “modern psychiatric practice”). Brock’s argument would caution against such retro-labeling unless those historical actors understood themselves as doing psychiatry.

## Open Questions

- **Boundary problem**: If “thoughtful presentism” is acceptable, what criteria distinguish legitimate present-driven questions from pejorative retro-labeling? Is there a clear operational rule beyond “answers must come from the past”?
- **Practicality of indigenisation**: Brock recommends indigenisation as the alternative to retro-labeling, but does not address whether non-Western psychologists face institutional pressures (funding, publishing, tenure) to conform to Western disciplinary norms. How realistic is his proposal?
- **Applicability to psychiatry**: Brock’s analysis focuses on psychology, which has a clear founding date (1879). Psychiatry’s institutional origins as asylum medicine date to the 18th–19th centuries, with a more gradual emergence. Does the “short history/long past” distinction apply equally, or does psychiatry require a different historiographical framework?
- **Danziger’s challenge**: If conceptual continuity (e.g., memory storage metaphors) exists across millennia, at what point does the “short history/long past” break become arbitrary? This remains genuinely unresolved in the source.
