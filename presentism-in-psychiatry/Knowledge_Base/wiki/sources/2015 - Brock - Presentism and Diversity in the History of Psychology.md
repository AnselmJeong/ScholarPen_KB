# Presentism and Diversity in the History of Psychology

## Summary
The author examines the tension between the desire for diversity in the history of psychology and the historiographical requirement to avoid presentism. The discussion was triggered by "priority claims" appearing on Wikipedia and in some scholarly articles, which attributed the foundations of experimental psychology and psychophysics to medieval Arab and Muslim scholars, such as Ibn al-Haytham.

The author argues that such claims are presentist because they project modern disciplinary categories (e.g., "psychologist") onto historical figures who did not conceive of their work in those terms. Using the distinction between psychology's "short history" (as a formal discipline starting in the late 19th century) and its "long past" (general human reflection on the mind), Brock suggests that pre-1850 intellectual work should be categorized as "reflexive discourse."

The paper concludes that true diversity is not achieved by retroactively labeling ancient thought as "psychology," but by recognizing how specific ancient traditions have influenced modern "indigenised" psychologies or by tracing direct intellectual lineages that shaped the formal discipline.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The act of projecting modern epistemic categories, values, and disciplinary definitions onto historical figures who did not share them.
- **Reflexive Discourse** — A broad category of human attempt to understand the human condition (including philosophy, theology, and literature), of which scientific psychology is only one modern, specific form.
- **Priority Claims** — Arguments over who was the "first" to discover or found a scientific field, often driven by nationalism or chauvinism.
- **Indigenised Psychology** — Psychology developed by incorporating local cultural traditions and intellectual history rather than simply importing a Western-defined discipline.
- **Long Past vs. Short History** — The distinction that while the study of the mind is ancient (long past), the formal academic discipline of psychology is recent (short history).

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Claims that medieval Arab/Muslim scholars "founded" psychology are presentist.
  - Evidence: Figures like al-Haytham contributed significantly to optics/vision, but did not operate within a "discipline" of psychology.
  - Strength: Strong; aligns with professional historiographical standards.
- **Core claim**: Labeling ancient non-Western thought as "psychology" is a form of "cultural imperialism."
  - Evidence: It forces diverse epistemic systems into a Western filter, distorting the original intent and context of the thinkers.
  - Strength: Strong theoretical argument regarding epistemic violence.
- **Core claim**: Pre-disciplinary work belongs in the history of psychology only if there is a traceable influence.
  - Evidence: Darwin's evolution or Plato's storage metaphor are included because they directly shaped subsequent psychological development.
  - Strength: Moderate/Strong; standard method for managing the "long past."

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension**: The author acknowledges that traditional histories are Eurocentric, but warns that "correcting" this by labeling all ancient mental study as "psychology" is equally distorting.
- **External Tension**: The author's view conflicts with the "naïve" perspective (e.g., "Jagged_85") that any study of mental processes constitutes psychology, regardless of the era or discipline.
