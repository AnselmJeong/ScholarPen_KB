# Presentism and Diversity in the History of Psychology

## Summary

This paper examines the tension between increasing diversity in the history of psychology and maintaining historiographical rigor, triggered by a controversy over the Wikipedia entry for "history of psychology." An editor using the pseudonym "Jagged_85" inserted claims attributing major developments in psychology — including the founding of psychophysics, experimental psychology, and the first psychiatric hospitals — to medieval Arab and Muslim scholars, drawing on published sources such as Omar Khaleefa (1999). Christopher Green, a historian of psychology who had been maintaining the entry, responded by devoting a special issue of his newsletter *Advances in the History of Psychology* to the problem, titled "Presentism in the Service of Diversity?"

Brock argues that these priority claims constitute presentism in the pejorative sense: imposing modern epistemic categories and values on historical actors who did not share them. Al-Haytham, for instance, could not have understood his work as part of a discipline called "psychology" because no such discipline existed in the 11th century. The paper situates this argument within the broader historiographical framework of Graham Richards (1987) and Roger Smith (1988), who contend that psychology is a historically specific Western discipline that emerged in the late 19th century, and that pre-1850 thought about human nature belongs to a broader category they term "reflexive discourse," not to "psychology."

Brock offers a constructive solution for achieving diversity without presentism: non-Western intellectual traditions can legitimately be included in the history of psychology if they have demonstrably influenced modern psychology, as shown by Kurt Danziger's tracing of the memory storage metaphor from Plato to contemporary research. For traditions without such a historical connection — such as medieval Islamic thought in relation to Western psychology — the proper path lies in taking one's own reflexive discourse seriously and developing indigenized psychologies, rather than claiming priority within a Western disciplinary framework. The paper thus advocates for multiple histories of psychology rather than a single, purportedly universal narrative.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism (pejorative sense)** — Projecting present-day categories, values, and epistemic frameworks onto the past instead of understanding historical actors on their own terms. Brock distinguishes this from "thoughtful presentism" (or "history of the present"), where questions are driven by present concerns but answers are sought in the past.
- **Priority claims** — Assertions that a particular historical figure was "first" to discover or found something, often serving nationalist, ethnic, or religious identity agendas. Examples include claims for al-Haytham as founder of psychophysics and for Marulić as first to use the term "psychology."
- **Reflexive discourse** (Graham Richards) — The broad category of human attempts to understand what it is to be human, found across philosophy, theology, literature, and the human sciences. Psychology is only one modern, historically specific form of reflexive discourse.
- **"Long past but short history"** (Ebbinghaus) — The distinction between psychology's millennia-old subject matter (its past) and its recent institutional history as a discipline (post-1879). Brock uses Danziger's refinement of this distinction to allow for legitimate historical continuity.
- **Indigenization** — The process by which non-Western psychologists adapt Western psychology to local cultural contexts, drawing on indigenous intellectual traditions. Brock contrasts this with the misleading term "indigenous psychology," since psychology is not native to most non-Western societies.
- **Cultural imperialism** — The application of Western disciplinary categories (like "psychology") to non-Western intellectual traditions, which distorts their original meaning and significance. Brock warns that claiming non-Western thought as "psychology" may inadvertently reinforce this imperialism.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The priority claims by Jagged_85 and Omar Khaleefa attributing psychology's foundations to medieval Arab/Muslim scholars constitute presentism in the pejorative sense.
    - Evidence: Al-Haytham could not have understood his work as "psychology" since no such discipline existed; Khaleefa's argument relies on speculation about what al-Haytham "may have written" had he lived longer.
    - Strength: Strong. These claims conflict with established historiographical standards and are acknowledged as speculative even by their proponents.

- **Core claim**: Diversity in the history of psychology is valuable but must not come at the cost of historiographical accuracy.
    - Evidence: The special issue title "Presentism in the Service of Diversity?" captures this tension; Brock uses Green's critique and the distinction between thoughtful and pejorative presentism to navigate it.
    - Strength: Well-supported by historiographical theory and the concrete Wikipedia case study; the solution is internally consistent.

- **Core claim**: Psychology is a historically specific Western discipline; pre-modern and non-Western thought about human nature belongs to "reflexive discourse," not to "psychology."
    - Evidence: Richards (1987) and Smith (1988) argue that the term "psychology" acquired its modern meaning only in the late 19th century; applying it retroactively distorts historical understanding.
    - Strength: Strong, though Brock acknowledges this position "may seem unsatisfying" to those making priority claims for identity reasons.

- **Core claim**: Non-Western traditions can be legitimately included in the history of psychology if they have demonstrably influenced modern psychology, following Danziger's method of tracing historical continuities.
    - Evidence: Dessoir (1902) distinguished between "history" (lasting influence) and "past" (no lasting influence); Danziger (2008a) traces the storage metaphor from Plato to modern memory research.
    - Strength: Provides a positive, rigorous method for inclusion that avoids both presentism and cultural imperialism.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The desire for diversity and the commitment to historiographical rigor are in intrinsic tension; Brock's solution (tracing actual historical influences) is intellectually coherent but may disappoint those wanting more immediate inclusion of non-Western thought for political or identity reasons.
- Brock endorses Smith's view that "the history of psychology should be abandoned" yet is writing within the framework of the history of psychology. He resolves this by arguing for multiple histories rather than a single comprehensive narrative, but the tension between deconstructing and practicing the discipline remains.
- The paper's framework challenges any assumption that diagnostic categories like "melancholia" or "hysteria" can be straightforwardly mapped across time and cultures without presentism, which may conflict with wiki concept pages that treat these as transhistorical categories. Brock would likely argue that such mapping requires careful attention to whether the modern category shaped the historical inquiry.

## Open Questions

- How does this framework apply specifically to the history of psychiatry? The source is about psychology, but the wiki focuses on psychiatry. Are the disciplinary boundaries the same, or does "reflexive discourse" about mental affliction predate and differ from psychology's history?
- Does the wiki's existing emphasis on psychiatric classification and nosology implicitly assume transhistorical validity that Brock's framework would challenge? For example, should "hysteria" and "melancholia" be treated as historically specific categories or as transhistorical human experiences?
- How should the wiki handle the concept of "mental illness" — as a historically specific modern category or as a transhistorical human experience? Brock's framework pushes toward the former, which has implications for how historical sources about madness are categorized and interpreted.
