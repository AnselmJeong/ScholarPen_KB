# A History of Psychiatry - 05

## Summary

This chapter, "Nerves," from Edward Shorter's *A History of Psychiatry*, examines the historical phenomenon whereby psychiatric illness was systematically renamed and treated as "nervous" disease throughout the 19th century. The central thesis is that both patients and physicians colluded in a "massive duplicity": doctors knew they were treating brain-based psychiatric disorders, but used the euphemism "nervous" to avoid the stigma of madness, the taint of hereditary degeneration, and the horror of the asylum. Patients, in turn, found the label of a physical nervous disorder far more reassuring than being diagnosed as insane.

The methodology is synthetic historical analysis, drawing on a wide range of primary sources: institutional records (admission statistics, name-change histories), published statements by physicians, patient memoirs, and literary texts. Evidence is presented across multiple European countries and the United States, spanning the 1830s to the early 20th century. Key examples include the renaming of private asylums to "clinics for nervous diseases," the explosive growth of hydrotherapy spas that functioned as de facto psychiatric hospitals, and the international embrace of neurasthenia and the rest cure.

The chapter's significance lies in its demonstration that diagnostic categories and treatment settings are shaped as much by social pressures—stigma, family honor, professional ambition—as by scientific discovery. It argues that the "flight from psychiatry" began not with deinstitutionalization in the 1950s, but in the 1830s, as patients and families sought any alternative to the asylum. This reframes the history of psychiatry as a story of concealment and euphemism, where the "fig leaf of nerves" allowed the profession to expand beyond the asylum walls into lucrative private practice with middle-class patients.

## Key Entities
- **Rest cure** (tool) — Therapy developed by S. Weir Mitchell (1875), combining seclusion, bed rest, overfeeding (milk diet), massage, and electrotherapy. Served as a key institutional model for private nervous clinics.

## Key Concepts
- **Fig leaf of nerves** — The deliberate, century-long euphemism whereby psychiatrists and patients labelled psychiatric illness as "nervous" to avoid the stigma of madness, hereditary taint, and asylum confinement. This deception enabled the growth of office-based private psychiatry.
- **Neurasthenia** — A functional nervous disease characterized by exhaustion of nerve force, coined by George Beard in 1869. Served as a "bridge" diagnosis between organic and psychiatric symptoms, legitimating outpatient care for the middle classes.
- **Rest cure** — A therapy (seclusion, bed rest, milk diet, massage, electrotherapy) developed by S. Weir Mitchell in 1875. Provided an institutional model for private nervous clinics and a precursor to psychological therapies emphasizing isolation and physician authority.
- **Hydrotherapy as psychiatric camouflage** — The use of mineral springs and water-cure clinics as socially acceptable sites for treating psychiatric illness, especially among the middle classes. Demonstrated how institutional settings shaped diagnostic categories and patient experience.
- **Functional nervous disease** — Hypothetically organic conditions with no detectable tissue pathology, including neurasthenia, hysteria, and hypochondria. Central to the epistemological ambiguity that enabled the "nerves" deception.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "nervous illness" label was a deliberate, century-long deception by physicians to avoid the stigma of madness and enable private practice with middle-class patients.
  - Evidence: Institutional name changes (e.g., Erlenmeyer's asylum renamed from "Insane" to "Brain and Nervous Disease"); admissions statistics from Marienberg hydro showing 52% neurosis and 9% psychosis; contemporary statements by physicians like Ewald Hecker that the label was "chosen only euphemistically"; patient testimony like John Perceval being called "nervous" despite psychosis.
  - Strength: Well-supported with multiple primary sources across several countries and decades. Limitation: Relies on selected examples rather than systematic quantitative data; risks overstating conscious intent.
- **Core claim**: Spa therapy and hydros (water-cure clinics) functioned as de facto psychiatric hospitals for the middle classes throughout the 19th century.
  - Evidence: Marienberg statistics (1883-1888) showing 79% of patients had psychiatric diagnoses; the Winternitz hydro in Vienna, run by a former asylum physician; French spas with detailed psychiatric "indications" for specific springs.
  - Strength: Strong institutional evidence. Limitation: Focuses primarily on middle-class patients and European contexts.
- **Core claim**: Neurasthenia and the rest cure were American inventions that internationalized the pattern of treating psychiatric illness outside the asylum.
  - Evidence: Beard's 1869 neurasthenia definition and its immediate German translation; Mitchell's 1875 rest cure and its components; the global spread of both concepts.
  - Strength: Well-documented historical narrative. Limitation: Less quantitative evidence on the actual extent of neurasthenia diagnoses versus other categories.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- Internal tension between agency and structure: The chapter frames the "deception" as a conscious choice by doctors and patients, but it's unclear how much was deliberate strategy versus gradual accommodation to patient preferences.
- Class dimension: The argument focuses on middle-class patients who could afford private clinics and spas. It does not address how this dynamic worked for working-class or poor patients.
- Gender: Hysteria is mentioned primarily for women, neurasthenia for both sexes, but the gendered dimensions of the "nerves" label are underexplored.
- Potential conflict with [[concepts/anti-psychiatry]]: The chapter shows that 19th-century patients engaged in de facto anti-psychiatric resistance (avoiding asylums, demanding nervous diagnoses) long before the organized anti-psychiatry movement, complicing the movement's claimed novelty.

## Open Questions
- How did the "nerves" deception function for non-white, non-middle-class populations? The chapter is silent on race and class beyond the bourgeoisie.
- Were some patients aware of the deception? Did any reject the nervous label in favor of a psychiatric diagnosis?
- Does the "stress" label function similarly today as a euphemism for psychiatric illness? What are the continuities with 19th-century "nerves"?
- How did the "nerves" label interact with gender specifically? Hysteria remained female while neurasthenia was more universal—what does this reveal about the gendering of diagnostic categories?
