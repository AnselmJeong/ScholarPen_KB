# Could Presentism in the Histories of Psychology Actually be Futuristic?

## Summary
The authors challenge the traditional view of historiography in psychology, arguing that the quest for "correct" origins is less about understanding the past and more about regulating the future of the field. They critique the reliance on "origin myths"—such as the 1879 Leipzig laboratory—which serve as institutional tools to prioritize quantitative and experimental methods while marginalizing other reflexive traditions.

The paper proposes a shift toward "open-ended normativity," suggesting that the discipline should be "de-essentialized." By pragmatically including non-Western intellectual traditions (e.g., Arab, Indian, Chinese), psychology can expand its future possibilities for insight rather than remaining trapped in a narrow Western canon. The authors argue that presentism, often viewed as a historian's error, can actually be a futuristic tool for discipline-building.

## Key Concepts
- **Open-ended Normativity** — The framework where historical interpretations are used to regulate and open new avenues for a discipline's future development rather than just recovering past facts.
- **Origin Myth** — Narratives (like Wundt as the "father" of psychology) used to establish legitimacy and institutionalize specific values, often acting as a "razor blade" to exclude non-conforming knowledge.
- **Presentism** — The act of interpreting the past through current concerns; here, it is reframed as a strategic utility for creating future scientific possibilities.
- **Epistemic Market** — The system of institutional value and power that determines which types of knowledge are validated and which are silenced.
- **De-essentialization** — The process of removing the requirement that psychology must stem from a single, specific Western historical origin to be valid.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: History writing in psychology is a normative act that creates the future of the field.
  - Evidence: The "Vygotsky myth," where Vygotsky was selectively re-invented in North America during the 1970s to fit existing norms, proving that the "historical image" is a tool for current practice.
  - Strength: Philosophical/Hermeneutic; based on critical analysis of disciplinary narratives rather than empirical data.
- **Core claim**: Essentializing the "birth" of psychology limits the field's growth.
  - Evidence: Rorty's claim that all philosophy is Western is used as a counter-example; the authors argue that limiting psychology to a Western origin silences valuable non-Western reflexive discourses.
  - Strength: Strong theoretical argument for pragmatic inclusivity.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **The "Scylla and Charybdis" Tension**: The authors acknowledge a tension between "anything goes" (where psychology becomes too broad to be meaningful) and "essentialism" (where it is too narrow). While they propose "open-ended normativity" as the path between them, they do not provide a rigid set of criteria for what constitutes a valid "psychological" discourse.

## Open Questions
- If any tradition of "reflexive discourse" can be included in psychology for the sake of future utility, at what point does the term "psychology" lose its distinct boundary and meaning?
