# History of psychiatry and its institutions

## Summary

This review article surveys recent English-language scholarship on the history of psychiatric institutions, focusing on work published since 2010. The author, Eric J. Engstrom, argues that the dominant carceral narrative—which framed asylums primarily as sites of social control, discipline, and coercion—is losing its paradigmatic status within psychiatric historiography. Rather than signaling the obsolescence of institutional history, this shift opens space for more nuanced, empirically grounded accounts of asylum culture. The article synthesizes findings from a wide range of studies covering suicide prevention, attendant roles, class divisions, material culture, mortuary practices, nutritional deprivation, and colonial psychiatry.

Engstrom's method is that of a narrative review: he selects and discusses key publications to illustrate broader historiographic trends. He contrasts the older carceral approach with newer work that reveals the complexity of institutional life—how staff mediated between policy and practice, how families remained involved in asylum care, and how physical therapies like leucotomy and ECT reshaped both hospital routines and extramural family dynamics. The review also connects institutional history to contemporary topics such as psychopharmacology, deinstitutionalization, and the emergence of the "ADHD-subject." Engstrom concludes that institutional perspectives remain indispensable for understanding psychiatry's past and present, precisely because they foreground historical contingency and local specificity, thereby resisting presentist narratives of scientific progress or pure social control.

## Key Concepts

- **Carceral narrative** — historiographic paradigm that interprets asylums primarily as sites of social control, discipline, and coercion, associated with Foucault, Goffman, and Rothman; Engstrom argues this is losing dominance.
- **Deinstitutionalization** — the shift from institutional to community-based psychiatric care; Engstrom contends it did not end institutional logic but transformed it into new forms of governance (therapism, psychotropic self).
- **Psychotropic self** — a concept from the anthropology of psychopharmacology describing how psychotropic drugs shape identity, subjectivity, and self-management; links institutional control to contemporary subject formation.
- **Therapism** (Dowbiggin) — a culture of emotional welfare, nonjudgmentalism, and dependence on expert advice, where emotional well-being is seen as a right; represents a new form of therapeutic governance that replaced asylum control.
- **Medical pluralism** — recognition that colonial asylums were one of many agents (alongside faith healers, families, metropolitan norms) mediating psychiatric knowledge and practice.
- **Diathesis-stress model** — a framework positing that mental illness arises from the interaction of biological predisposition (diathesis) and environmental stressors; used by Grimsley-Smith to argue that famine biology drove Irish asylum admission rates.
- **Presentism** — judging the past by present standards; Engstrom positions institutional history as a methodological tool for resisting presentist teleologies of scientific progress.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim:** Carceral narratives are losing their paradigmatic status within psychiatric historiography, creating space for more nuanced accounts of asylum culture that move beyond social-control frameworks.
  - Evidence: York (2012) shows asylum attendants mediated suicide prevention, complicating the view of asylums as purely carceral; Comstock (2011) traces the emergence of the ADHD-subject from institutional moral/juridical control to rational self-management via drugs; Long (2011) demonstrates that psychiatric social workers developed extramural expertise in response to physical therapies like leucotomy, revealing institutional roots of community care; Grimsley-Smith (2011) uses a diathesis-stress model to show that famine biology, not just policy, drove Irish asylum rates; Ernst (2011) presents the Ranchi hospital as a site where Indian psychiatrists tested global paradigms, not as a mere colonial imposition.
  - Strength: Moderate to strong. The claims are syntheses of peer-reviewed work, each methodologically sound (archival, qualitative, quantitative). However, the article is a review of reviews and does not present original evidence; the assertion that carceral narratives are "losing paradigmatic status" is asserted rather than systematically demonstrated.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Engstrom's normative stance is ambiguous: he celebrates the distancing from carceral narratives while also approvingly citing Dowbiggin's claim that today's patients are "more enslaved" than their institutionalized forebears. The article does not resolve whether deinstitutionalization represents liberation, new oppression, or both.
- The article argues that institutional history is anti-presentist (by emphasizing contingency), yet Dowbiggin's own narrative is deeply presentist in its moral judgment of contemporary "therapism" as worse than past asylum care. Engstrom does not reconcile this tension.
- Engstrom implicitly challenges the dominance of anti-psychiatry critiques (as represented in the existing wiki concept [[concepts/anti-psychiatry]]), but he does not directly engage with the anti-psychiatry literature to substantiate his claim that carceral narratives are waning.

## Open Questions

- Is the claim that carceral narratives are "losing paradigmatic status" empirically demonstrable through systematic historiography, or is Engstrom advocating for such a shift rather than documenting it?
- How can institutional history claim to be anti-presentist when it is used to mount moral critiques of the present (as Dowbiggin does with "therapism")?
- Can the concept of the "psychotropic self" be reconciled with the dominance of the biomedical model, or does it strengthen biological reductionism by grounding identity in drug response?
- In an era of deinstitutionalization, what counts as an "institution"? If the family has become a site of care and control, should the concept be extended beyond the asylum to encompass all sites of therapeutic governance?
- How does the emphasis on "medical pluralism" in colonial psychiatry avoid romanticizing non-Western healing practices or underestimating the power asymmetries of colonial medicine?
