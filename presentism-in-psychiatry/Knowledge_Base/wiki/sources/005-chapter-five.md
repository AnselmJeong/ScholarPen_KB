# Chapter Five: Madhouses and Mad-Doctors

## Summary
This source examines the institutionalization and cultural representation of madness in 17th and 18th-century Europe, focusing on England, France, and the Netherlands. The author investigates the transition from managing the "idle and disreputable" in general hospitals to the emergence of specialized madhouses. The research utilizes a multidisciplinary approach, combining architectural history (the contrast between Bedlam and St Luke's), legal analysis (*lettres de cachet*), and an examination of artistic works (Hogarth's engravings and Baroque/Classical opera).

The main finding is a direct critique of Michel Foucault's "Great Confinement." The author argues that the scale of systemic incarceration was vastly overstated; in many regions, the burden of care remained with families. Instead, the source highlights the "marketization" of madness in England, where private mad-doctors turned confinement into a profitable business catering to the wealthy.

Furthermore, the text explores how madness was consumed as a cultural commodity. From the voyeuristic visits to Bedlam to the "mad scenes" in operas by Handel and Mozart and the "false confinement" tropes in Gothic novels, madness served as a tool for social commentary and entertainment.

## Key Entities
- **Bedlam (Bethlem Royal Hospital)** (org) — The primary English asylum, evolving from a Restoration palace to a site of public voyeurism.
- **St Luke’s Hospital** (org) — An 18th-century alternative to Bedlam emphasizing "plainness and simplicity."
- **Dolhuizen** (org) — Dutch "madhouses" that pioneered early segregation of the insane from the general poor.
- **Hôpitaux généraux / Dépôts de mendicité** (org) — French general hospitals used for social control of the "idle."
- **The Salpêtrière** (org) — The largest French general hospital; a prison-hospital for women and a minority of lunatics.
- **Maisons de santé** (org) — Expensive private French madhouses.
- **Royal College of Physicians** (org) — Professional body associated with elite mad-doctors like Sir William Battie.
- **Lettres de cachet** (tool) — Royal warrants in France allowing indefinite detention without appeal.
- **Interdiction** (tool) — French legal process to authorize incarceration and protect property.
- **Engravers’ Copyright Act** (tool) — 1735 law that allowed William Hogarth to monetize his prints.
- ***A Rake’s Progress*** (dataset) — Hogarth's series depicting madness as the "wages of sin."
- ***Orlando* & *Idomeneo*** (dataset) — Operas using musical dissonance to represent mental fragmentation.
- ***The Distress’d Orphan*** (dataset) — Gothic novella illustrating the trope of false confinement.

## Key Concepts
- **The Great Confinement (Critique of)** — The refutation of Foucault's claim that the 17th/18th centuries saw a universal systemic locking up of the insane.
- **Trade in Lunacy** — The commercialization of madness in England, where private keepers profited from providing "discretion" to wealthy families.
- **Madness as the "Wages of Sin"** — The 18th-century view that insanity resulted from moral failure, debauchery, or excess.
- **False Confinement** — The use of asylums to silence political dissidents or "dissolute" relatives, often for financial or social gain.
- **Sentimentalism (in Literature)** — A movement emphasizing refined empathy to distinguish the "genteel" from the "vulgar" (e.g., reacting with horror to Bedlam).

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "Great Confinement" was not a universal reality.
  - Evidence: Patient numbers at the Salpêtrière were small relative to the total population, and provincial centers like Montpellier had very few cells.
  - Strength: Strong; based on comparative institutional data.
- **Core claim**: Early confinement was driven by social control and economics, not medicine.
  - Evidence: Use of *lettres de cachet* for family honor; the rise of the "Trade in Lunacy" for profit.
  - Strength: High; supported by legal records and the biographies of wealthy "mad-doctors."
- **Core claim**: Madness was "domesticated" as a cultural spectacle.
  - Evidence: Analysis of Hogarth's *A Rake's Progress* and the specific musical devices (tempo shifts, dissonance) in Handel and Mozart.
  - Strength: Strong; synthesizes art history and musicology.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Foucault vs. Author**: The author explicitly contradicts Foucault's thesis on the scale and nature of the "Great Confinement."
- **Charity vs. Reality**: Tension between the "plain and simple" charitable mission of St Luke's and the "barbarous usage" (chains/mixing sexes) reported in the wards.
- **Privacy vs. Tyranny**: The "Trade in Lunacy" offered families discretion but created a system prone to "False Confinement."
