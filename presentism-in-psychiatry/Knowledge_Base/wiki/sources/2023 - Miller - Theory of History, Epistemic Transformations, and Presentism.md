# Theory of History, Epistemic Transformations, and Presentism

## Summary
This source is a scholarly review by Nicola Miller of Javier Fernández Sebastián's work on the conceptual history of the Iberian Atlantic (1808–1840). Miller examines the intersection of language, time, and revolution, focusing on how the independence movements in the Iberian Americas functioned as "epistemic revolutions" rather than mere political shifts. The text advocates for a transition from treating history as a set of techniques (method) to a rigorous "theory of history" characterized by deep reflexivity.

The review highlights the importance of "elective traditions" in the construction of modernity and warns against the uncritical application of European analytical categories to non-Western contexts. It further explores the tension between the desire to avoid anachronism and the epistemological necessity of reconstructing the past from a present-day vantage point.

## Key Concepts
- **Conceptual History (Begriffsgeschichte)** — The study of how meanings of concepts change over time and shape historical understanding, specifically seeking a path beyond the Bielefeld and Cambridge schools.
- **Epistemic Transformation** — A fundamental shift in how knowledge is produced and the categories used to understand the world, coinciding with political upheaval.
- **Presentism** — The retrospective application of current concepts and values to the past; debated as either a danger to be avoided or an ontological necessity.
- **Elective Traditions** — The theory that traditions in modernity are actively selected by agents, providing a nuanced alternative to the idea of "invented traditions."
- **Ideals of Inquiry** — The shared conceptual common ground (e.g., concepts of nature or land) that makes social and political debate possible within a specific era.
- **Regimes of Historicity** — The evolving relationship between past, present, and future in societal consciousness (e.g., the shift from future-oriented modernism to present-oriented contemporaneity).

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The Iberian independence revolutions were "epistemic revolutions" that altered the very mechanisms of concept production.
  - Evidence: Fernández Sebastián's analysis of the Iberian Atlantic (1808–1840).
  - Strength: High; based on extensive scholarly synthesis, though framed as a review.
- **Core claim**: Modernity is characterized by "multiplying traditions" where agency is expressed through the election of specific traditions.
  - Evidence: Contrast with Hobsbawm's "invented traditions" framework.
  - Strength: Strong theoretical proposition for reintroducing agency into modernity studies.
- **Core claim**: Eurocentric categories of analysis often obscure the "and, and" (non-binary) logic prevalent in Latin American intellectual history.
  - Evidence: Miller's examples of "nature" and "land" as politicized ideals of inquiry in Spanish America.
  - Strength: Moderate to High; serves as a critical methodological warning.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Anachronism vs. Epistemological Presentism**: There is a tension between Fernández Sebastián's rejection of retrospective application of concepts and David Armitage's argument that all historical reconstruction is inherently a presentist act, making "responsible presentism" necessary.
- **Western vs. Global Historicity**: The Western schema of shifting from future-focus to present-focus (Hartog) may not accurately map onto the historical experiences of non-Western regions like China or Latin America.
