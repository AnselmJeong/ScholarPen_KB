# Theory of History, Epistemic Transformations, and Presentism

## Summary

This source is a review essay by Nicola Miller of Javier Fernández Sebastián’s book *Historia conceptual en el Atlántico ibérico: lenguajes, tiempos, revoluciones* (2021). The review engages with the book’s four interwoven strands: a conceptual history of the Iberian independence revolutions (1808–c.1840), a rethinking of modernity as an era of elective traditions, a critical history of conceptual history itself, and a call for a deeper theory of historical practice. Miller uses the review to explore two overarching themes: the nature of analytical categories in historiography and the problem of presentism.

Methodologically, the essay is a theoretical reflection situated within the roundtable "Languages, Times, and Revolutions" in *The Historical Journal*. It does not present new empirical research but synthesizes and extends arguments from conceptual history, the philosophy of history, and postcolonial critique. Miller draws on the Iberconceptos project, David Armitage’s defense of presentism, François Hartog’s regimes of historicity, and Steve Smith’s critique of Eurocentric time-schemas to build its case.

The main findings are threefold. First, presentism is not a monolithic vice: wilful presentism (political abuse of the past) is rightly condemned, but epistemological and ontological presentism—the recognition that historical reconstruction always occurs in the present—may be necessary and responsible. Second, deep epistemic transformations involve not only changes in concepts but changes in how concepts are produced, requiring historians to scrutinize their own categories (e.g., “political,” “modern”) with the same rigor they apply to historical sources. Third, Eurocentric paradigms, especially the linear stage model of history, persist even among those who reject progress, and conceptual history can reveal coexisting temporalities that challenge binary either/or thinking.

The significance of this source lies in its nuanced treatment of presentism and its insistence on reflexivity about historical categories. It provides a methodological bridge between general historiography and the specific challenges of writing histories of psychiatry, where diagnostic categories and treatment paradigms are themselves historically contingent and often retrospectively imposed.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism** — The imposition of present-day categories, values, or concepts onto the past. Miller distinguishes three forms: (1) wilful presentism (political abuse of history), (2) epistemological presentism (knowledge is always situated in the present), and (3) ontological presentism (the past can only be accessed from the present). The latter two may be unavoidable and even ethically responsible.
- **Epistemic transformation** — A deep shift in the production of knowledge that alters not just the content of concepts but the very methods by which new concepts are generated. Fernández Sebastián argues the Iberian revolutions constituted such a transformation.
- **Conceptual history (Begriffsgeschichte)** — The study of how concepts change over time, associated with Reinhart Koselleck. The source examines its methods and limitations, particularly regarding the selection and categorization of “key” concepts.
- **Categories of analysis** — The frameworks (e.g., “political,” “modern,” “scientific”) that historians use to organize their inquiries. Miller argues these are not neutral and must be subjected to the same critical scrutiny as historical concepts.
- **Theory of history** — A reflexive engagement with historical practice beyond mere method, addressing the ontological, epistemological, and ethical foundations of historical writing.
- **Regimes of historicity** — François Hartog’s framework for analyzing how societies relate to past, present, and future. Miller engages with Smith’s critique that this model is Eurocentric and does not fit twentieth-century China.
- **Critical historical consciousness** — A reflexive awareness of how one’s own historical position shapes interpretation; Fernández Sebastián emphasizes its importance for historians.
- **“And, and” epistemology** — A non-binary mode of thinking attributed to Latin American intellectuals, contrasted with the Western “either/or” divide; it allows for the coexistence of multiple temporalities and perspectives.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Presentism is more complex than a simple historiographical vice; it has epistemological and ontological dimensions that may be unavoidable and even ethically necessary.
  - Evidence: Reference to David Armitage’s “In defense of presentism” (2022), which argues that a professional commitment to avoiding anachronism has come at the cost of failing to think about time itself. Miller also cites Smith’s critique of Hartog’s Eurocentric regime of historicity as showing that multiple conceptions of time can coexist.
  - Strength: Moderate. The argument is theoretical and relies on recent historiographical debates rather than empirical data. It is well-reasoned but not definitively settled.
- **Core claim**: Historians must subject their own analytical categories (e.g., “political,” “modern”) to the same critical scrutiny they apply to historical concepts.
  - Evidence: Miller gives the example of nature and land serving as “ideals of inquiry” in post-independence Spanish America, showing that the category “political” had different contents in that context. She also notes that education policy served as a proxy for power debates, complicating retrospective application of present-day categories.
  - Strength: Moderate. The claim is supported by specific historical examples but remains a general methodological recommendation rather than an empirical finding.
- **Core claim**: Eurocentric paradigms, especially the linear stage model of history, persist even among historians who reject progress, and conceptual history can reveal coexisting temporalities that challenge binary either/or thinking.
  - Evidence: Fernández Sebastián’s demonstration that Iberian intellectuals operated with an “and, and” epistemology rather than a Western “either/or” logic. Smith’s critique of Hartog is also cited.
  - Strength: Moderate. The evidence is drawn from historical cases and critiques, but the scope is limited to the Iberian Atlantic and twentieth-century China.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Internal tension between rejecting presentism and accepting its unavoidability. Miller agrees with Fernández Sebastián that retrospective application of present-day concepts is problematic, yet she also entertains Armitage’s defense of presentism as “epistemological and ontological.” This tension is not resolved.
- Potential conflict with anti-presentist historians of psychiatry (e.g., Berrios, Shorter) who argue that presentism distorts historical understanding. Miller’s openness to a qualified defense of presentism could be seen as undermining that position.
- Tension between universal and particular historical frameworks: the source advocates for multiple temporalities and “and, and” epistemology while also calling for a “theory of history” that implies a unifying framework.

## Open Questions

- Miller directly asks Fernández Sebastián: is the concern about the lack of a theory of history primarily ontological, epistemological, or ethical? This question remains open and is relevant to any field using historical methods.
- How do historians determine what constitutes a “key” concept or category (e.g., “political”)? The problem of selection is inherent but under-theorized, and it has direct implications for histories of psychiatric classification.
- How can the “and, and” epistemology, drawn from Latin American thought, be applied to challenge Western binary thinking in psychiatric historiography (e.g., normal/pathological, somatic/psychic)?
- Is presentism ever avoidable at an ontological level? If not, how should historians of psychiatry balance ethical responsibility to the past with their situatedness in the present?
