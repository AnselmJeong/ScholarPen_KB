# The First Biological Psychiatry

## Summary

This chapter (Chapter 3 of *A History of Psychiatry*) examines the rise and fall of the "first biological psychiatry" — the 19th-century movement that sought to ground the understanding and treatment of mental illness in systematic neuroscience research. Moving beyond earlier humoral theories, figures such as Wilhelm Griesinger, Theodor Meynert, Paul Flechsig, Eduard Hitzig, and Carl Wernicke attempted to link mind and brain through postmortem examination, microscopy, cerebral localization studies, and genetic theorizing. The chapter covers the period from approximately 1865 to 1900, with national comparisons across Germany, France, and the Anglo-Saxon world.

The research design is synthetic historiography: the author draws on biographical accounts, institutional histories, journal publications (especially Griesinger's *Archive for Psychiatry and Nervous Diseases*), and contemporary observer reports to construct a comparative narrative. Key case studies include Griesinger's transformation of the Charité Hospital in Berlin, Meynert's neuroanatomical program in Vienna, Wernicke's localization work in Breslau, and parallel French efforts at Sainte-Anne and the Salpêtrière. The account is driven by institutional and structural analysis rather than quantitative data.

The main finding is that the first biological psychiatry achieved genuine scientific advances — identification of neurosyphilis as an organic psychiatric disease (Bayle), mapping of aphasia (Wernicke), and foundational work on cerebral localization (Flechsig, Hitzig) — but ultimately failed because it became too detached from clinical care. The movement slid into therapeutic nihilism (the belief that psychiatric diseases are incurable) and "brain mythology" (Karl Jaspers' term for excessive localization of symptoms without adequate evidence). The degeneration doctrine of Morel and Magnan emerged as a harmful consequence of this biological reductionism.

The chapter's significance lies in its structural explanation of national differences: German dominance is attributed to the state-funded university system and the habilitation mechanism; French failure to extreme centralization, personal rivalries, and the Charcotian hysteria disaster; British backwardness to a decentralized charity-based hospital system with no research funding. This analysis makes the chapter a key resource for understanding how institutional configurations shape psychiatric science and clinical practice.

## Key Entities

- **Charité Hospital (Berlin)** (org) — Griesinger's base; model for the modern university psychiatric clinic focused on teaching and research rather than custodialism
- **Sainte-Anne Asylum (Paris)** (org) — Magnan's base; admissions bureau for Parisian patients; site of the *délire chronique* classification tradition
- **Archive for Psychiatry and Nervous Diseases** (tool) — Flagship journal founded by Griesinger in 1867; symbol of the new neurologically-oriented psychiatry that displaced asylum-based journals
- **Habilitation system** (tool) — German postdoctoral research requirement that created a pipeline of publication and academic advancement unmatched elsewhere
- **Agrégation** (tool) — French academic examination system that prioritized memorization over research, contributing to French psychiatric stagnation
- **Clinical-pathological method** (tool) — Reasoning from autopsy findings to antemortem symptoms; methodological foundation borrowed from general medicine

## Key Concepts

- **First biological psychiatry** — 19th-century movement to ground psychiatry in systematic neuroscience research (genetics, chemistry, brain anatomy) rather than asylum custodialism or purely psychological approaches; defined by Griesinger's 1867 programmatic statement that "mental illnesses are really illnesses of the nerves and brain"
- **Degeneration theory** — The notion that inherited mental illness worsens steadily over generations; associated with Morel and Magnan; presented as a harmful consequence of biological reductionism
- **Brain mythology (Hirnmythologie)** — Karl Jaspers' term for the excessive attempt to localize psychiatric symptoms in specific brain regions without adequate evidence; characterizes the endpoint of Wernicke's program
- **Cerebral localization** — Mapping specific brain regions to specific functions; the core research program of figures like Flechsig, Hitzig, and Wernicke; produced genuine advances but also excesses
- **Therapeutic nihilism** — Belief that psychiatric diseases are incurable, justifying a focus on research rather than treatment; exemplified by Meynert, Flechsig, and Hitzig
- **Longitudinal vs. cross-sectional approach** — Kraepelin's longitudinal focus on illness course versus Wernicke's cross-sectional focus on symptom clusters at a given moment; the victory of Kraepelin's approach marked the end of the first biological psychiatry

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The first biological psychiatry was a movement of ideas (not bricks and mortar) that systematically sought to link mind and brain through research, distinguishing it from earlier humoral theories.
  - Evidence: Griesinger's 1845/1861 textbook, his 1867 programmatic statement in the *Archive*, the founding of multiple university clinics (16 by 1911 in Germany), and the institutional history of the habilitation system.
  - Strength: Strong — supported by detailed biographical, institutional, and textual evidence across multiple national contexts.

- **Core claim**: The movement achieved genuine scientific advances (cerebral localization, identification of neurosyphilis, neurological aphasias) but ultimately failed because it became detached from clinical care, sliding into therapeutic nihilism and "brain mythology."
  - Evidence: Meynert's aloofness from patients, Flechsig's clinical neglect (Schreber's "murderer of my soul"), Hitzig's reputation as "intolerable," and Wernicke's dying words ("autopsychic disorientation") as emblematic of excessive abstraction.
  - Strength: Moderate — the success/failure narrative is compelling but the criteria for "failure" (clinical neglect) are not explicitly justified; no dissenting views are considered.

- **Core claim**: German dominance was structural, not accidental — the habilitation system, state-funded universities, and chair-hopping created a research engine unmatched elsewhere.
  - Evidence: Description of the habilitation process, the number of university clinics and psychiatric physicians (1,400 by 1911), and the mobility of professors between chairs.
  - Strength: Strong — well-supported by comparative institutional data.

- **Core claim**: French failure was political — extreme centralization, personal rivalries, and the Charcotian hysteria disaster prevented university-based biological research comparable to Germany.
  - Evidence: The Bayle case (discovery of neurosyphilis but never given a university post), Babinski's exclusion, the 1877 decree that created chairs but hamstrung them with internal conflicts, and the post-Charcot collapse of "grand hysteria" as an artifact of suggestion.
  - Strength: Strong — multiple case studies demonstrate the pattern, though the dismissal of Charcot's hysteria as a "complete disaster" may oversimplify.

- **Core claim**: Anglo-Saxon backwardness stemmed from decentralized, charity-based hospital systems with no state funding for laboratories or research.
  - Evidence: Clarence Farrar's diary entry quoting Stoddart, Walter Rivington's 1879 comparison of Paris and London systems, and the absence of pathological research at Bethlem.
  - Strength: Moderate — relies on a single observer (Farrar) but is corroborated by structural analysis.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Success vs. failure paradox**: The chapter simultaneously argues that biological psychiatry achieved genuine scientific advances *and* that it "failed" and reached a "dead end." The criterion for failure appears to be clinical neglect and therapeutic nihilism, not scientific incorrectness. This tension is acknowledged but not fully resolved — the chapter states that "the failure of the idea did not necessarily mean the idea itself was faulty" without clarifying what "faulty" would mean.
- **German dominance vs. German dead end**: Germany is presented as the ideal research model, yet German biological psychiatry is also where the dead end was most pronounced (Meynert's alcoholism, Flechsig's clinical incompetence, Wernicke's "brain mythology"). The structural features that enabled German success may have also enabled its excesses.
- **Griesinger as hero vs. Griesinger as symptom**: Griesinger is celebrated as the founder, yet his biological reductionism ("mental illnesses are brain diseases") laid the groundwork for the therapeutic nihilism the chapter criticizes. His career was mostly in internal medicine, raising the question of whether psychiatry's turn toward the neurosciences was a bid for legitimacy through identification with a more prestigious field.
- **Presentist framing**: The chapter's evaluative judgments (that biological psychiatry "failed" because it neglected patients) apply modern therapeutic values to a period when effective treatments did not exist. This is itself an example of the presentism the broader research project interrogates. The chapter does not reflect on its own historiographic position.

## Open Questions

- **Causality question**: Did the first biological psychiatry fail *because* of its biological reductionism, or *despite* its biological ambitions (i.e., did institutional/political factors doom it)? The chapter is ambiguous on this causal direction.
- **Periodization question**: The chapter treats 1865-1900 as the "first biological psychiatry" and implies a clean break after Kraepelin. But Kraepelin himself was highly biological. Is the "second biological psychiatry" (post-1980) really a return to the same research program, or is it fundamentally different in its methods and assumptions?
- **National comparison completeness**: The triumphalist narrative for Germany and dismissive narrative for France/Britain is compelling but one-sided. Were there French or British biological psychiatrists who succeeded despite the system? The chapter does not mention them, leaving the comparative claims potentially overstated.
- **Patient perspective**: The chapter mentions Schreber and Schnitzler's critique of Meynert, but what did patients *as a whole* experience under the first biological psychiatry? The sources are entirely from the physician-researcher perspective, and patient outcomes are not systematically assessed.
- **Presentist bias**: The chapter evaluates 19th-century psychiatry by 20th-century standards. Is this legitimate historical analysis, or does it impose anachronistic criteria? This question connects directly to the project's research question about presentism in psychiatric historiography and is not addressed within the chapter itself.
