# Historicism

## Summary
This entry from the Encyclopedia of Political Thought examines historicism as both a methodological principle and a philosophical movement. It traces the evolution of the concept from 18th-century thinkers like Burke and Vico, through the Romantic era, to 20th-century figures such as Dilthey and Croce. The author explores how historicism shifts the understanding of truth and human nature from universal constants to products of specific historical processes.

The text highlights a critical tension in the definition of historicism: while some (like Meinecke) view it as an emphasis on the unique and irrational, others (like Popper) critique it as a search for universal laws of societal development. Ultimately, the source argues that a radical historicist approach transforms political philosophy into a descriptive "aesthetic contemplation" of competing worldviews, stripping it of the ability to establish universal norms for justice or liberty.

## Key Concepts
- **Historicism** — A methodological approach where events and entities are understood exclusively through the historical process they belong to.
- **Genetic Method of Explanation** — The practice of explaining a phenomenon by viewing it as a specific phase of a larger historical trajectory.
- **Historical Relativism** — The view that truth, norms, and human nature vary across different historical ages and worldviews.
- **Weltanschauung** — A comprehensive "worldview" that filters the perception of truth in a given era.
- **Grundnorm** — The "basic norm" at the apex of a legal system; in historicism, this is viewed as a historical fact rather than a universal truth.
- **Crisis of Historicism** — The intellectual instability and "anarchy of convictions" resulting from the loss of eternal, universal truths.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Historicism reduces political thought to a subset of history, thereby eliminating the possibility of universal political norms.
  - Evidence: The transition from Enlightenment universalism to the focus on *zeitgeist* and the "genetic method" used by thinkers like Dilthey.
  - Strength: Strong synthesis of intellectual history, though it functions as a philosophical overview rather than an empirical study.
- **Core claim**: There is a fundamental contradiction in the term "historicism" regarding the existence of laws.
  - Evidence: The contrast between Meinecke's focus on the unique/irrational and Popper's critique of "laws of history" in Hegel and Marx.
  - Strength: Well-supported by contrasting the primary definitions used by the respective scholars.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **The Relativist Paradox**: The self-referential contradiction where the claim "all truth is relative" is presented as a universal truth.
- **Uniqueness vs. Determinism**: The tension between seeing history as a series of unique, non-repeatable events versus seeing it as a process governed by predictable laws (e.g., Marx).
- **Liberation vs. Disenchantment**: The conflict between the freedom from old dogmas and the resulting "icy darkness" of meaninglessness described by Weber.

## Open Questions
- Can "historicity" (the quality of being historical) coexist with stable, authentic standards of inquiry, as suggested by the references to Habermas and Lonergan?
