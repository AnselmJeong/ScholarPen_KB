# Introduction: Whose Present? Which History?

## Summary
This introductory essay explores the contemporary shift within the history profession—specifically intellectual history—toward "presentism." This "turn to the present" has been accelerated by the post-2016 crisis of democracy, the COVID-19 pandemic, and global racial injustice movements. The author examines the tension between traditional historiographical prohibitions against presentism (which warn against projecting current values onto the past) and the urgent need for the discipline to remain relevant to a public facing immediate crises.

The author argues that while the move toward the present is widespread, it is often executed through a narrow, Eurocentric lens. Historians frequently use "Weimar analogies" or "Cold War" frameworks to explain current events in the US, Europe, and China, effectively treating the West as the sole measure of modernity. By synthesizing various scholarly contributions, the text advocates for a "decentered" global intellectual history that recognizes different cultural and social experiences of time.

The piece distinguishes between two primary methods of engaging the present: a descriptive "contemporary history" (the "history of now") and a critical "genealogical approach." The latter seeks to uncover the contingency of the present by exploring "paths not taken," thereby providing a resource for political action and a way to challenge the perceived inevitability of current structures.

- **University of Cambridge** (org) — Cited as a site where a high percentage of M.Phil. students are focusing on 20th-century subjects, signaling a trend toward the modern.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The practice of interpreting the past through the lens of the present; once a professional taboo, now being reclaimed as a tool for social and political relevance.
- **Regimes of Historicity** — A framework (via François Hartog) describing how different eras perceive time; the current "presentist regime" is characterized by the collapse of the border between past and present.
- **History of the Present** — A methodology split into "Contemporary History" (explaining the present via historical terms) and "Genealogy" (interrogating the instability of the present to find alternatives).
- **Present-Past** — A Du Boisian concept where the past (specifically the trauma of slavery) does not recede but persists and claims the present.
- **Cold War Modernization Theory** — A framework that positions the West as the standard for modernity, treating non-Western trajectories as anomalies or pathologies.
- **Theory Revolt** — A movement calling for the integration of critical theory into history to overcome "methodological fetishism" and pure empiricism.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The current "turn to the present" in history is hampered by provincialism and Eurocentrism.
  - Evidence: The over-reliance on 1930s European fascism to explain modern populism and the use of "New Cold War" rhetoric to frame China's rise.
  - Strength: Strong; supported by a synthesis of multiple scholarly perspectives (Devji, Lanza, Young) and observable trends in academic course offerings.
- **Core claim**: A genealogical approach to history is more liberating than a descriptive "history of now."
  - Evidence: The use of mid-century decolonization struggles to identify "paths not taken" that offer alternatives to the modern nation-state.
  - Strength: Medium; it is presented as a methodological alternative rather than an empirical result, and is contested by some scholars (e.g., Robertson) as still being "presentist."

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Professional Obsolescence vs. Academic Rigor**: A tension exists between those who believe the "turn to the present" is necessary to save history majors from declining (Armitage, McMahon) and those who fear it turns history into "general social studies" (Hunt, Robertson).
- **The Paradox of Critique**: The author notes the contradiction of using Euro-American intellectual terms and frameworks to critique the very phenomenon of Eurocentrism.
- **Methodological Conflict**: The conflict between "contextualism" (treating the past as a separate realm) and the "paths-not-taken" approach (using the past to find tools for current political action).

## Open Questions
- How can history departments integrate "History of the Present" courses without sacrificing the rigorous contextualism that prevents history from becoming mere political commentary?
- Can a truly non-Eurocentric global intellectual history be constructed if the very categories of "present" and "modernity" are rooted in Western temporality?
