# Introduction: Whose Present? Which History?

## Summary

This article serves as the introduction to a forum in *Modern Intellectual History* examining the recent turn toward the present in the history profession. Steinmetz-Jenkins synthesizes a wide-ranging debate among intellectual historians about whether, how, and for whom historians should engage with contemporary crises. The piece is not an empirical study but a scholarly position paper that maps the landscape of competing methodological approaches to "history of the present" in the wake of the post-2016 political upheavals (Brexit, Trump, racial injustice, pandemic).

The introduction identifies a core tension: while the profession has decisively moved away from Lynn Hunt's famous 2002 jeremiad "Against Presentism," this turn is marked by what Steinmetz-Jenkins calls "undeniable provincialism." Historians consistently reach for European and American analogies—1930s fascism, the world wars, 1968 protests—to explain contemporary events, often obscuring more than they reveal. The piece argues that the "present" is not self-evident; it varies across cultures, classes, and nations, and the question "whose present?" must be central to any non-Eurocentric historical practice.

The main findings are diagnostic rather than empirical: there is no methodological consensus on how to do history of the present. The forum showcases competing approaches including critical/genealogical history (inspired by Foucault, Kleinberg, and Scott), the "paths-not-taken" approach (endorsed by Quentin Skinner in a notable revision of his earlier contextualism), and contemporary history proper. The significance lies in its clear articulation of the global and decolonial dimensions often missing from presentism debates, and its demonstration that the crisis of democracy is itself a contested frame that can reproduce Western-centric assumptions.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism (historiographical)** — The use of present concerns to frame historical inquiry; normalized post-2016 as the profession turned toward contemporary crises, but remains contested over methodology and scope.
- **Provincial/Eurocentric presentism** — The tendency of presentist history to rely on Western analogies (1930s fascism, world wars, 1968) that may obscure non-Western realities and reproduce the West as the measure of modernity.
- **Critical/genealogical history of the present** — A Foucault-inspired approach that interrogates taken-for-granted categories (gender, race, nation) as contingent and unstable, revealing "paths not taken" rather than assuming a stable past mirrors a stable present.
- **Paths-not-taken approach** — Historical recovery of abandoned political alternatives to challenge present assumptions; endorsed by Quentin Skinner in a late-career revision of his contextualism.
- **Presentist regime of historicity** — François Hartog's term for an "enlarged present" saturated by the past that abolishes temporal boundaries and traps us in an eternal end of history.
- **Present-past** — W. E. B. Du Bois's concept of a temporality in which the past of slavery persists and claims the present, generating intergenerational obligation; central to African American intellectual history's temporal framework.
- **Denial of coevalness** — Refusing to grant another society simultaneous existence in the same historical time; used to critique Western treatments of China as always "anomalous" or "pathological."
- **Modernization theory** — Cold War framework presuming Western liberal capitalism as the universal measure of modernity; critiqued as haunting contemporary presentist approaches to non-Western contexts.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The post-2016 "crisis of democracy" has normalized presentism in the history profession, but this turn is marked by provincial Eurocentrism.
  - Evidence: Citations of widespread reliance on European analogies (1930s fascism, WWII economies, 1968 protests) by prominent historians making public interventions.
  - Strength: Well-supported discursively through published debates and multiple scholarly examples; weaker as empirical proof since the piece is a synthetic introduction rather than systematic analysis.

- **Core claim**: There is no methodological consensus on how to do history of the present, and "whose present" is at stake remains unresolved.
  - Evidence: The forum itself showcases competing approaches (critical genealogy, paths-not-taken, contemporary history, pedagogical presentism) with significant disagreements among contributors.
  - Strength: Strongly supported by the structure of the forum and the explicit acknowledgment of disagreements; a feature of the argument rather than a weakness.

- **Core claim**: The African American Intellectual History Society (AAIHS) and Black intellectual historians are leading the reclamation of presentism from a non-Eurocentric perspective rooted in Du Bois's "present-past" temporality.
  - Evidence: Citation of Brandon Byrd's work, the institutional rise of AAIHS since 2014, and the global vision of Black intellectual history.
  - Strength: Well-documented but presented as one strand among many; the argument is that this tradition offers an alternative to provincial presentism, not that it has resolved the tensions.

- **Core claim**: Cold War modernization theory continues to structure presentist approaches to non-Western contexts, particularly China and Japan.
  - Evidence: Essays by Fabio Lanza and Louise Young demonstrating how modernization theory's assumptions persist in contemporary scholarship.
  - Strength: Strongly supported by the cited forum contributions; represents a significant critical insight.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The forum explicitly acknowledges that there is no consensus on methodology for the history of the present, nor agreement on whose present is of concern — this is presented as a feature of the field's current state rather than a resolvable contradiction.
- Tension between two crisis frames: the post-2016 "crisis of democracy" (which dominates the forum) versus the longue durée of racial and colonial violence (which drives AAIHS presentism). These imply different temporalities and political projects.
- David Armitage argues presentism can save the profession by speaking to public concerns; John Robertson warns it threatens disciplinary integrity and leads to "narrow thinking" — a direct internal tension within the forum.
- Quentin Skinner's famous contextualism is now revised by Skinner himself, who expresses "regret" for treating the past as a "separate realm" — an acknowledged internal contradiction within a major figure's intellectual trajectory.

## Open Questions

- How can historians do history of the present without falling into provincial Eurocentrism, given that most institutional resources and training remain Western-focused?
- Does the "crisis of democracy" frame itself reproduce Western-centrism by assuming liberal democracy as the universal normative baseline?
- Can the "paths-not-taken" approach avoid simply confirming the historian's own political preferences, as Robertson worries?
- How should the profession navigate the tension between public engagement (which demands presentist relevance) and disciplinary rigor (which values contextualist depth)?
- What would a genuinely non-Eurocentric history of the present look like for regions (China, Africa, Japan) that refuse to fit Western temporal frameworks?
- Does the decline in history majors represent a crisis that presentism can solve, or is presentism itself a symptom of that decline?
