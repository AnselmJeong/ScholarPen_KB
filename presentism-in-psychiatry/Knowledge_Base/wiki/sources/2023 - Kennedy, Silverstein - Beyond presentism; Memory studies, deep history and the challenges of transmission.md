# Beyond presentism; Memory studies, deep history and the challenges of transmission

## Summary
This paper critiques the field of memory studies for its inherent "presentism"—a tendency to focus almost exclusively on the recent past, colonial modernity, and traumatic ruptures (primarily from the 15th century onwards). The authors argue that this narrow focus reinforces Eurocentrism, even within decolonial scholarship, by centering the European encounter as the primary point of rupture.

To rectify this, the authors propose a dialogue between memory studies and "deep history," which utilizes archaeology, genomics, and anthropology to explore humanity's history over vast timescales (e.g., 60,000+ years of Indigenous presence in Australia). The research synthesizes ethnomusicology, art history, and memory theory to challenge the notion that oral transmission is only "accurate" for a few generations.

The central finding is the proposal of the "deep present": a temporal state where the deep past is not a distant memory to be replicated accurately, but is activated and embodied in the current moment through ceremony, performance, and art. By analyzing "soft technologies" (song, dance) and public art installations in Sydney, the authors demonstrate how Indigenous memory persists not as a static archive, but as a living, adaptive process of relational activation.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The systemic bias in memory studies toward the recent past and traumatic events of colonial modernity.
- **Deep History** — An interdisciplinary approach to human history over vast timescales, moving beyond written records to include archaeological and genomic evidence.
- **Deep Present** — A state in which ancestral power and the deep past are evoked and activated in the contemporary moment through performance and practice.
- **Soft Technologies of Memory** — Intangible mnemonic tools (song, dance, storytelling) that encode vital information and identity, allowing for adaptation over millennia.
- **Postmemory** — The transmission of traumatic knowledge to generations who did not experience the original event but inherit its affective force.
- **Geomythology** — The study of myths as records of geological events; critiqued here for prioritizing "mimesis" (accuracy) over cultural practice.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Memory studies is limited by a Eurocentric focus on "rupture" and "recent history"; it must instead embrace "deep history" and the "deep present" to truly decolonize.
  - Evidence: Comparison between Jan Assmann's "communicative memory" (3-4 generation limit) and the proven longevity of Aboriginal songlines and rock art (thousands of years).
  - Strength: Strong theoretical synthesis, though empirical proof of longevity relies on interdisciplinary citations (e.g., Nunn and Reid) rather than new primary data.
- **Core claim**: Transmission should be viewed as "activation" rather than "linear replication."
  - Evidence: Analysis of the "soft technologies" of song and dance, which allow for "combinatorial flexibility" and adaptation while maintaining core ancestral power.
  - Strength: Well-supported by ethnomusicological research (e.g., Barwick) and Indigenous artistic practices.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Mimesis vs. Practice**: There is a tension between "geomythologists" (who seek to prove oral stories are accurate geological maps) and "cultural analysts" (who argue the meaning and performance of the story are more important than its geological precision).
- **Decolonial Paradox**: The authors note that most "decolonial" scholarship still centers the 15th-century European encounter, thereby inadvertently reinforcing the Eurocentric timeline it seeks to dismantle.

## Open Questions
- How can the "deep present" be empirically identified or measured in non-Indigenous contexts, or is it an exclusive feature of cultures with continuous, long-term habitation of specific "Country"?
