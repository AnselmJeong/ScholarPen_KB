# The “Human Subject,” “Vulnerable Populations,” and Medical History; The Problem of Presentism and the Discourse of Bioethics

## Summary
This paper examines the methodological danger of "presentism" within medical history, specifically how the modern discourse of bioethics—developed largely since the 1970s—is often inappropriately projected onto earlier eras. The author argues that applying contemporary concepts such as "informed consent," "vulnerable populations," and "human subjects" to historical figures leads to a "Whiggish" interpretation of history, where the past is viewed merely as a progression toward a morally superior present.

Using a case study of a 1928 attempted sterilization in Newfoundland, the author demonstrates the tension between understanding a physician's actions within his own contemporary moral framework (e.g., a sense of societal duty and goodwill) versus judging him through the lens of the Belmont Report. The paper further critiques recent scholarship on Canadian eugenics, suggesting that some historians have shifted from intellectual history to "forensic" or "activist" history, prioritizing political agendas over historical veracity.

The author concludes that while social justice is important, historians must maintain a distinction between interpreting the past and adjudicating it. He advocates for a disciplined suspension of current moral imperatives to achieve a more nuanced understanding of how historical actors perceived their roles and responsibilities.

## Key Entities
- **Belmont Report** (tool/dataset) — The foundational document for modern bioethical principles used as the benchmark for "presentist" critiques.
- **Declaration of Geneva** (tool/dataset) — A moral code for physicians used to illustrate the modern professional indoctrination of medical students.
- **Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR)** (org) — Funding body for the author's research.
- **Harvard Medical School** (org) — The training ground for the physician in the Newfoundland case study.

## Key Concepts
- **Historical Presentism** — The act of interpreting the past through the lens of current values, beliefs, and terminology.
- **Intellectual Presentism** — Specifically projecting contemporary technical or scientific terminology onto eras before those concepts existed.
- **Bioethics Discourse** — The modern framework emphasizing autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, and social justice.
- **Vulnerable Populations / Human Subject** — Modern "human kinds" (categories) used to identify and protect research participants; argued to be anachronistic when applied to pre-1970s actors.
- **"Made Up People" (Ian Hacking)** — The theory that classifications and the people they describe emerge together, meaning the category "vulnerable subject" creates a new way for people to perceive themselves.
- **Whiggish History** — A teleological approach viewing the past as a steady progression toward a more "correct" or "advanced" present.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The application of modern bioethical categories to historical medical practice creates a distorted, "morally complacent" view of history.
  - Evidence: Contrast between the 1928 physician's "goodwill" (period-appropriate) and the modern label of "unethical" (presentist).
  - Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in historiography; however, the author admits the analogy between chemical discovery (oxygen) and moral rights has limits due to human agency.
- **Core claim**: There is a distinct difference between "intellectual history" and "forensic/activist history."
  - Evidence: Critique of Malacrida and Stote, who use "defensive" language or explicitly "politically motivated" frameworks.
  - Strength: Provides a clear methodological distinction, though it may be viewed as dismissive of the social necessity of advocacy in history.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Objectivity vs. Complicity**: The tension between the historian's goal of objectivity (understanding the actor) and the moral risk of appearing to excuse atrocities.
- **Triple Loyalty**: The conflict a medical historian in a medical school faces between loyalty to the historical field, the institution, and the profession of medicine.
- **Forensic vs. Academic**: The tension between using history to adjudicate current compensation claims (where presentist bioethics are useful) and writing formal academic history (where they are distorting).

## Open Questions
- At what point does the suspension of current morality for the sake of "historical objectivity" cross the line into moral complicity?
- Can a historian successfully integrate a social justice mandate without falling into the trap of presentism?
