# The “Human Subject,” “Vulnerable Populations,” and Medical History; The Problem of Presentism and the Discourse of Bioethics

## Summary

Connor examines the methodological problem of presentism in medical history, specifically the tendency to apply contemporary bioethical frameworks—such as "informed consent," "human subjects," and "vulnerable populations"—to past medical practices. Focusing on eugenics and sterilization in Canada, particularly a 1928 attempted sterilization in Newfoundland, the author argues that this presentist approach distorts historical understanding and fosters moral self-congratulation rather than nuanced analysis. The study is not a systematic historiographic review but a cautionary essay drawing on a detailed primary case and selective engagement with recent scholarship.

The research design combines a microhistorical analysis of archival hospital records with a critical reading of four recent works on Canadian eugenics (by Dyck, Malacrida, Stote, and Baker). Connor uses the 1928 case to illustrate how a physician's actions—deciding to sterilize a woman labeled "mentally deficient" without her consent—can be understood within the historical context of the time, before the post-1970 rise of bioethics. He compares this to his earlier work on Listerism, where he suspended belief in germ theory to understand Victorian surgeons. The main findings challenge the uncritical use of bioethics discourse in historical writing, showing that it leads to terminological defensiveness, retrospective judgment, and an ahistorical imposition of today's moral standards.

The paper's significance lies in its forceful call for medical historians to reflect on their own methodological commitments. Connor does not advocate for moral neutrality but insists that genuine historical understanding requires temporarily suspending current ethical frameworks, just as historians suspend current scientific knowledge. The piece contributes to ongoing historiographic debates about presentism, especially in fields dealing with ethically charged topics like eugenics, and connects to broader discussions of how categories like "vulnerable human subject" are historically constructed.

## Key Concepts

- **[[concepts/presentism]]** — In Lynn Hunt's sense, interpreting the past through present-day moral frameworks, leading to "moral complacency and self-congratulation." Connor distinguishes between presentism as temporal focus (studying recent history) and presentism as moral imposition.
- **[[concepts/eugenics]]** — The primary historical case study (Newfoundland 1928) used to illustrate the problem of presentist interpretation. Connor situates the attempted sterilization within contemporary eugenic ideology (Buck v. Bell, Goddard's work).
- **[[concepts/historiography]]** — The paper is a methodological reflection on how historians should approach ethically charged topics, arguing for suspension of current moral frameworks as a principle of good historical practice.
- **Bioethics discourse** — The post-1970 framework centered on the Belmont Report and the "Georgetown mantra" (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice). Connor argues its categories (informed consent, vulnerable human subject) are historically specific constructs, not timeless truths.
- **Terminological presentism** — Applying modern connotations to historical diagnostic labels (e.g., "feeble-minded," "idiot," "moron"), leading either to apologetic disclaimers or politically motivated weaponization of language.
- **Made up people** (Ian Hacking) — Categories of people that emerge simultaneously with the classifications that describe them; used to support the claim that the "vulnerable human subject" is a recent invention.
- **Retrospective politics** (Berber Bevernage) — The problem of applying contemporary justice frameworks (e.g., compensation, truth commissions) to historical injustices without anachronism.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Medical historians are increasingly writing presentist history by uncritically applying modern bioethical categories (informed consent, vulnerable subjects) to past eras, especially when studying eugenics. This leads to "moral complacency and self-congratulation" rather than genuine historical understanding.
  - **Evidence**: Detailed analysis of a 1928 attempted sterilization in Newfoundland, contextualized through hospital records, the physician's training, and the post-Buck v. Bell eugenic climate. Comparison with the author's own earlier work on Listerism, where he suspended belief in germ theory.
  - **Strength**: Strong illustrative case that vividly demonstrates the tension. Moderate systematic evidence — the paper is a cautionary essay, not a systematic review. The critique of specific scholars (Malacrida, Stote) is persuasive but selective. Connor acknowledges the limits of the analogy between chemical substances and human subjects.

- **Core claim**: Terminological defensiveness in eugenics scholarship is a symptom of presentism. Excessive disclaimers and moral distancing undermine historical authenticity.
  - **Evidence**: Analysis of Dyck's and Malacrida's "Notes on Language" compared to Baker's more straightforward terminological discussion. Use of leprosy/Hansen's Disease analogy to show how terminological revisionism can distort historical meaning.
  - **Strength**: Well-argued with concrete examples. Weakness: may underestimate the genuine harm caused by historical labels; the author himself acknowledges this tension.

- **Core claim**: The "vulnerable human subject" is a historically specific construct that emerged around 1970 with the bioethics movement, not a timeless category. Applying it retroactively distorts historical actors' experiences.
  - **Evidence**: Citing Campbell & Stark's "Making up 'Vulnerable' People," which shows that human subjects before 1970 often saw their participation in experiments differently than after the bioethics framework recast them as "vulnerable."
  - **Strength**: Direct empirical support from a separate study. Limits: Campbell & Stark's work focuses on experimental settings, not clinical eugenics, which Connor acknowledges involves a different arena.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension**: Connor acknowledges his own "dual loyalty" — he teaches the Declaration of Geneva to medical students, aligning him with modern bioethical values, which conflicts with his call for ahistoricism. He cannot fully escape the presentist framework he critiques, creating a reflexive problem.
- **Limits of the analogy**: Connor admits that chemical substances (oxygen, germs) are easier to treat ahistorically than human subjects with moral agency, but does not fully resolve this tension. The "higher stakes" of human subjects make a purely historicist approach difficult to sustain.
- **Selective historicism**: Connor suspends judgment for the 1928 physician (seeing him as well-meaning in context) but seems less willing to afford the same suspension to Malacrida's or Stote's work, potentially revealing his own presentist commitments about what counts as "proper" historical scholarship.
- **Conflict with activist scholarship**: The paper positions itself against Stote's explicitly activist approach, which Connor argues sacrifices historical accuracy for political advocacy. This creates a tension between the wiki's potential social justice commitments and Connor's methodological strictures.

## Open Questions

- How do we balance historicist accuracy with the wiki's likely commitment to social justice? Connor's anti-presentism could be seen as a license for amoral relativism. Where is the line between "understanding" and "excusing" historical harm?
- Can the wiki adopt Connor's caution while still maintaining a critical stance toward eugenics? Perhaps yes, if the wiki distinguishes between *explaining* historical actors' rationales and *endorsing* them.
- Should the wiki include a methodological statement about its own position on presentism vs. historicism, especially for controversial topics like eugenics, forced sterilization, and psychiatric abuse?
- To what extent is the "vulnerable human subject" a useful category for historical analysis at all, given that it is a post-1970 construct? Does its application always represent presentism, or can it be used critically?
