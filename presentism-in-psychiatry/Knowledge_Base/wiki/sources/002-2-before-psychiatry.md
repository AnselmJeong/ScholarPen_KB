# Before Psychiatry

## Summary

This chapter reconstructs conceptions of mental illness in Western thought from ancient Greece through the early modern period, before the emergence of psychiatry as a dedicated profession in the late 18th century. It draws on a wide range of primary sources—Hippocratic texts, Platonic dialogues, Galen’s medical writings, Roman compilations, medieval religious treatises, and Robert Burton’s 17th-century encyclopedia—to show that pre-modern definitions of madness were remarkably stable and fundamentally contextual. The author argues that ancient Greek medicine and philosophy established three competing explanatory frameworks—supernatural (gods/demons), organic (humoral imbalance), and psychological (inner psychic conflict)—that persisted for over two thousand years.

The central finding is that the ancient criterion for diagnosing mental disorder was not a list of symptoms but the judgment that a person’s behavior lacked any rational or culturally understandable cause. This “without cause” principle, first articulated by Hippocratic physicians in the 5th century BCE, distinguished melancholia (prolonged fear and sadness without proportionate external reason) from normal grief. It reappears in Avicenna’s *Canon of Medicine*, in Felix Platter’s 16th-century case descriptions, and in English medical dictionaries of the 18th century. The chapter emphasizes that this contextual approach—looking at whether a symptom is proportional to its circumstances—is a direct ancestor of modern diagnostic criteria such as the DSM’s “clinical significance” and “distress/impairment” requirements.

The significance of this analysis is that it challenges the assumption that the biomedical model of mental illness is a purely modern invention. Hippocratic medicine already rooted madness in the brain and bodily fluids, blurring the line between physical and mental disease. At the same time, Platonic philosophy introduced the idea of internal psychic conflict, foreshadowing psychoanalysis. The chapter demonstrates that many core questions about definition, causation, and jurisdiction (medical vs. religious vs. legal) were already being debated in antiquity, and that the “without cause” criterion provided a remarkably durable conceptual anchor for diagnosing mental disorder across two millennia.

## Key Concepts

- **Contextual approach to diagnosis** — The ancient practice of judging mental disorder not by symptoms alone but by whether a behavior can be understood within shared cultural norms and circumstances. Madness was defined as action that had “no obvious reason” or exceeded the bounds of common sense.

- **“Without cause” criterion** — The diagnostic rule that melancholic sadness or fear is disordered only when it arises in the absence of an adequate external cause or is disproportionate in severity or duration. This is the ancient precursor to modern distinctions between endogenous depression and reactive grief.

- **Humoral theory** — The Hippocratic-Galenic model of health as balance among four bodily fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile). Mental disorder resulted from an excess or deficiency of a humor—e.g., too much black bile causing melancholia, too much yellow bile causing mania—and treatments aimed to restore equilibrium.

- **Mania** — An agitated form of madness characterized by frenzy, violence, or comic behavior that could not be accounted for within normal cultural categories. Sometimes linked to bipolar cycles when alternating with melancholia.

- **Melancholia** — A condition of prolonged fear, sadness, and social withdrawal “without cause.” The most historically continuous psychiatric concept, with descriptions from Hippocrates to Burton that closely resemble modern clinical depression.

- **Proportionality** — The idea that emotional reactions are normal when proportionate to their provoking events and disordered when excessive or enduring beyond reasonable bounds. This is the implicit logic behind the “without cause” criterion.

- **Platonic tripartite soul** — Plato’s model of the mind divided into reason, emotion, and instinct. Madness resulted when the irrational parts overcame rational control, anticipating psychodynamic theories of inner conflict.

- **Supernatural causation** — The belief that madness was imposed by gods, demons, witches, or divine punishment. This lay framework coexisted with medical and philosophical models and was especially prominent in pre-Classical Greece and the Christian medieval period.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Ancient Greek and pre-modern definitions of mental illness were fundamentally contextual—madness was judged by social incomprehensibility, not by symptom checklists.
  - **Evidence**: Quotations from Hippocrates, Aristotle, Herodotus, and Galen showing that inappropriate behavior “without obvious reason” was the mark of madness; examples of culturally acceptable cannibalism and Cynic provocations not considered insane.
  - **Strength**: Very well supported by multiple primary sources across centuries; the contextual criterion is consistently articulated from the 5th century BCE through the 17th century.

- **Core claim**: The “without cause” criterion for melancholia (fear/sadness disproportionate to events) was the central diagnostic rule from Hippocrates through Burton, persisting for over 2,000 years.
  - **Evidence**: Direct citations from Hippocrates (“If fear or sadness last for a long time it is melancholia”), Avicenna (“fear without cause”), Platter (“without any cause”), an 1745 medical dictionary, and Burton’s *Anatomy of Melancholy* (“dotage without a fever, having for his ordinary companions fear and sadness, without any apparent occasion”).
  - **Strength**: Extremely strong—the same formula recurs verbatim across cultures and languages; the argument is supported by a continuous textual tradition.

- **Core claim**: Three explanatory frameworks (supernatural, organic, psychological) coexisted and competed in pre-modern thought, with no single paradigm dominating.
  - **Evidence**: Homeric epics show divine intervention; Hippocratic texts reject supernatural causes and emphasize brain and humors; Plato describes reason versus passion; Christian era reinstates demonic possession.
  - **Strength**: Well-evidenced, though the chapter notes that the frameworks often blended (e.g., Burton included magic alongside humoral causes). The plurality is convincingly demonstrated.

- **Core claim**: No sharp line was drawn between mental and physical illness in Hippocratic medicine; both stemmed from disturbances of equilibrium and were treated with similar environmental and organic measures.
  - **Evidence**: Hippocrates stated “from the brain only arise our pleasures, joys, laughter, and jests, as well as our sorrows, pains, griefs and tears”; treatments for mental and physical disorders were identical (diet, rest, baths, opium).
  - **Strength**: Strongly supported; this finding challenges the notion that the biomedical model is a 19th-century invention.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The chapter claims a “substantial unity” between ancient and modern views of mental disorder, yet it also acknowledges that ancient nosology was far simpler (only two main categories) and relied on social context rather than symptom criteria. This raises a tension: is the similarity real or an artifact of selective reading? The emphasis on the “without cause” criterion may overstate continuity while downplaying ruptures, such as the medieval dominance of supernatural explanations.
- The source shows that the “without cause” criterion was used to distinguish disorder from normal grief, but it also admits that temperamental predispositions could produce intense emotional reactions. The boundary between personality variant and disorder was blurry then, and remains so today. This internal tension is noted but left unresolved.
- The chapter’s portrayal of Hippocratic medicine as already biomedical (brain-based, organic) contradicts a common narrative in the existing wiki (e.g., under [[concepts/biomedical-model]]) that the biomedical model emerged primarily in the 19th century. This suggests the need to nuance the timeline: the model existed in antiquity but was suppressed or transformed by religious and philosophical frameworks.

## Open Questions

- How directly does the ancient “without cause” criterion map onto the modern construct of “endogenous depression”? The source asserts continuity, but critics might argue that the similarity is superficial and that the underlying pathophysiology and social context are radically different.
- In what ways does the ancient contextual approach parallel or diverge from DSM-5’s requirement for “clinically significant distress or impairment” and the exclusion of culturally normative responses? A systematic comparison could clarify how much contemporary nosology has really changed.
