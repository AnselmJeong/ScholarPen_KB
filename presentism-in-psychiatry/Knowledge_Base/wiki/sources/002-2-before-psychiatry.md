# Before Psychiatry

## Summary
This source examines the conceptual history of mental illness prior to the formal establishment of psychiatry in the 18th and 19th centuries. It tracks the evolution of "sanity" and "madness" from Ancient Greece through the Renaissance, highlighting a persistent tension between three primary explanatory frameworks: the organic/physiological (Hippocratic/Galenic), the psychological/rationalist (Platonic), and the supernatural/spiritual (Homeric/Medieval Christian).

The author argues that while the *causes* of madness shifted—from humoral imbalances to demonic possession and back to physiological disturbances—the *definition* of madness remained remarkably stable. It was consistently viewed as a radical departure from social norms, characterized by behavior that was disproportionate to the environment or lacked an external trigger.

The analysis focuses heavily on the "without cause" criterion used to distinguish clinical disorders (like melancholia) from normal human suffering (such as grief). By analyzing figures from Aristotle and Galen to Robert Burton, the text demonstrates how pre-modern thinkers used context and duration to draw the line between a normal temperament and a pathological disease.

## Key Entities
- **Hippocratic School** (org) — Established the first naturalistic framework for mental illness rooted in brain physiology and bodily fluids.
- **Platonists** (org) — Developed rationalist theories of the soul as a battlefield between reason and irrational instinct.
- **Galenic Medical Culture** (org) — Synthesized Hippocratic and Aristotelian thought; dominated Western medicine for 1,500 years.
- **Early Christian Church** (org) — Shifted the paradigm toward spiritual explanations, emphasizing sin and demonic possession.
- **The DSM** (tool) — Modern diagnostic manual used as a point of comparison for Aristotle's contextual definitions.
- **The Canon of Medicine** (tool) — Avicenna's 11th-century work that preserved the Hippocratic tradition.
- **The Anatomy of Melancholy** (tool) — Robert Burton's 17th-century compendium synthesizing 2,000 years of thought on depression.

## Key Concepts
- **Contextualism in Diagnosis** — The theory that behavior is only "mad" if it lacks a rational motive or occurs in an inappropriate setting relative to cultural norms.
- **Humoral Theory** — The belief that health depends on the balance of four fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile); excess black bile causes melancholia.
- **Melancholia** — A broad category of mental disturbance characterized by prolonged fear and sadness "without cause."
- **Mania** — An agitated form of insanity marked by delirium and frenzy, categorized into violent or comic subtypes.
- **Psychic Conflict (Platonic)** — The view of the mind as a struggle where reason must contain irrational instincts and emotions.
- **Supernaturalism** — The belief that madness is caused by divine intervention, curses, or demonic possession.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The definition of madness has remained consistent across millennia as a failure of behavior to match its context.
  - Evidence: Comparisons between Aristotle's view of cannibalism (cultural vs. pathological) and modern DSM criteria; the persistent "without cause" or "prolonged" requirement for diagnosing melancholia from Hippocrates to Robert Burton.
  - Strength: Strong; supported by a wide range of primary historical texts across different eras.
- **Core claim**: Three theoretical templates for madness (Organic, Psychological, and Supernatural) co-existed and cycled in dominance.
  - Evidence: The shift from Hippocratic empiricism to Medieval spiritualism, then back to the Renaissance focus on humoral balance in Burton's work.
  - Strength: Strong; clearly documented transitions in cultural authority.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Empiricism vs. Faith**: A sharp conflict exists between the Hippocratic-Galenic tradition (brain/body focus) and the Medieval Christian view (soul/sin focus).
- **Internal vs. External Cause**: The phrase "without cause" creates a tension; it implies the absence of an *environmental* trigger, not the absence of a biological or spiritual cause.
