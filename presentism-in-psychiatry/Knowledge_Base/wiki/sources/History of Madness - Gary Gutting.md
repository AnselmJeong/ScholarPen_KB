# Foucault and the History of Madness

## Summary

This chapter by Gary Gutting provides a critical assessment of Michel Foucault's *Histoire de la folie* (*Madness and Civilization*) as a work of history. Gutting examines the sharply polarized reactions of professional historians, noting that praise focuses on Foucault's meta-level contributions—viewing madness as a social construct and embedding its history within the history of reason—while criticism targets his object-level factual claims and interpretations. Gutting argues that the consensus among historians (e.g., Porter, Scull) is that Foucault's work is seriously flawed in its specific assertions, even as its general approach is celebrated.

Gutting offers a close reading of Foucault's account of the Classical experience of madness, centered on the event of the "Great Confinement." He details Foucault's claims about the assimilation of madness into the broader category of unreason, the moral condemnation of idleness, the distinctive animality of madness, and the persistence of these Classical structures into modern psychiatry. He then evaluates major critiques, particularly those of Midelfort and Porter, finding Midelfort's objections largely misdirected but Porter's empirical challenges more substantial—especially regarding the scale and nature of confinement in England.

The central argument of the chapter is that the apparent split between Foucault's admirers and critics can be resolved by recognizing his method as "idealist" rather than empiricist. Foucault uses historical facts primarily as illustrations of an interpretative framework, not as evidence to support empirical generalizations. The strength of his account lies in its logical and imaginative power to organize data into intelligible configurations, not in strict correspondence with independently given facts. Gutting concludes that, despite this idealist bent, Foucault's work remains within the discipline of history, occupying one end of the idealist-empiricist continuum.

The significance of Gutting's analysis is twofold: it provides a rigorous philosophical framework for evaluating Foucault's historical project, and it offers a nuanced resolution to the long-standing debate between Foucault's defenders and detractors. By distinguishing meta-level from object-level claims and identifying Foucault's idealist methodology, Gutting enables a more productive engagement with Foucault's work that neither dismisses it as bad history nor exempts it from historical scrutiny.

## Key Concepts

- **Classical Age** — Foucault's term for roughly 1650–1800, characterized by the "Great Confinement" and a distinctive experience of madness as unreason.
- **Unreason (déraison)** — The broader Classical category encompassing madness alongside moral/sexual deviance, free-thinking, and idleness; madness is one division of unreason.
- **Great Confinement** — Foucault's claim of a sudden, massive internment of deviants (mad, poor, criminals) across mid-17th-century Europe, central to his analysis.
- **Animality of madness** — The Classical view that the mad are reduced to animal nature, at the "zero degree of humanity," justifying their exhibition as spectacle.
- **Idealist history** — Gutting's term for Foucault's method: uses facts as illustrations of an interpretative framework; primary support comes from coherence and explanatory power, not empirical correspondence.
- **Object-level vs. meta-level claims** — Distinction between specific factual assertions (e.g., scale of confinement) and broader interpretive frameworks (e.g., madness as social construct); explains the divergent historian reactions.
- **Experience (Foucault's usage)** — The anonymous, categorical perception of a period (e.g., the Classical experience of madness), not individual thoughts or beliefs.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Historians sharply split on Foucault: praised for meta-level insights (madness as social construct, reason/madness dialectic) but overwhelmingly reject his specific factual claims.
  - Evidence: Direct quotations from Mandrou, MacDonald, Goldstein, Porter, Scull, O'Brien.
  - Strength: Strong — well-documented scholarly consensus.

- **Core claim**: Midelfort's influential critique is largely misdirected; he misidentifies Foucault's basic contentions and misunderstands key positions (e.g., Foucault does not deny medical treatment existed, nor does he require Pinel to have no predecessors).
  - Evidence: Close reading of Foucault's text showing Midelfort's errors.
  - Strength: Moderate — persuasive philosophical analysis, but does not resolve all of Midelfort's empirical concerns.

- **Core claim**: Porter's critique of the "Great Confinement" is more serious: empirical evidence shows confinement in England was gradual, small-scale, and involved separation of deviants; the animality image was not dominant; the Lockean view of madness as mis-association was equally important.
  - Evidence: Porter's data (~5,000–10,000 confined by 1800 vs. 100,000 by 1900; evidence of "splitting" not "lumping" in institutions).
  - Strength: Strong — Gutting treats this as a substantial challenge to Foucault's central claims.

- **Core claim**: Foucault's method is "idealist" not empiricist; he uses facts as illustrations of an interpretative framework, not as evidence for generalizations. The primary support for his position is its logical and imaginative power to organize facts.
  - Evidence: Analysis of Foucault's use of evidence (single Descartes passage, limited citations, no quantitative data on confinement extent).
  - Strength: Strong — detailed textual analysis demonstrating a consistent pattern.

- **Core claim**: Foucault remains within the discipline of history, contra Goldstein and Megill. His work occupies one end of the idealist-empiricist continuum, not a position outside the discipline.
  - Evidence: Response to Goldstein (continuity is not essential for history) and Megill (ambiguity alone does not remove work from history).
  - Strength: Moderate — philosophically sound but may not persuade practicing historians.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Defense vs. concession**: Gutting defends Foucault against Midelfort's misreadings but largely concedes Porter's empirical critique, leaving Foucault's central claim about confinement in a fragile position—defended philosophically but not empirically salvaged.
- **History vs. not history**: Gutting argues Foucault remains within history (contra Goldstein/Megill) but characterizes his method as "idealist" in a way many historians would reject as insufficiently empirical. The defense is philosophically sound but may not persuade practicing historians.
- **Continuity vs. discontinuity**: Gutting notes that *Histoire de la folie* emphasizes continuities between Classical and modern periods, complicating the common view of Foucault as purely discontinuity-oriented. This tension affects how the wiki treats Foucault's periodization.
- **Porter vs. Foucault**: Porter's evidence of the Lockean counter-image of madness (as mis-association, not animality) directly contradicts Foucault's claim about the dominance of animality in the Classical experience. Gutting acknowledges this as unresolved.
- **European vs. national confinement**: Foucault's claim of a "European" phenomenon conflicts with Porter's evidence of significant English divergence; Gutting suggests Foucault's focus on categorical conditions may not be refuted by national variation, but this weakens the universality of Foucault's thesis.

## Open Questions

- Has subsequent scholarship resolved whether the "Great Confinement" was a European phenomenon or primarily French/continental? The wiki should track this.
- Is idealist history (as Gutting defines it) a valid approach within professional history, or does it represent something else (philosophy, literature)? This question is central to evaluating Foucault's legacy.
- How does Foucault's later self-criticism—that *Histoire de la folie* gave too much place to "experience"—affect our evaluation of the work?
- Does the full French text of *Histoire de la folie* actually address Porter's criticisms, as Colin Gordon suggests? The abridged English translation may have led to misreadings.
- Can the meta/object-level distinction be systematically applied to other works in the historiography of psychiatry to resolve similar debates?
