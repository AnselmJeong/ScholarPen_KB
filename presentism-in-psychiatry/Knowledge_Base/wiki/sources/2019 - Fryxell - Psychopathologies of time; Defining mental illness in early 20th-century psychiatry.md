# Psychopathologies of time: Defining mental illness in early 20th-century psychiatry

## Summary

This article by Allegra Fryxell examines the role of time as a central methodological and diagnostic tool in early 20th-century psychiatry and psychology. It focuses on the work of Eugène Minkowski and a network of European and American clinicians who developed a phenomenological approach to mental illness based on disturbances in the "time-sense." The study draws on published clinical case studies, theoretical works by Minkowski, Straus, Fischer, Lewis, and others, and the philosophical foundations laid by Henri Bergson's concept of *durée*.

The main finding is that depression, schizophrenia, and other disorders were understood as symptoms of a breakdown in the synthesis between subjective temporal experience (*Ichzeit*/ego-time) and objective clock-time (*Weltzeit*/world-time). Psychiatrists argued that healthy individuals continuously reconcile these two temporal modes, while patients exhibit a "generating disorder" (*trouble générateur*) that distorts their orientation toward the future. This "futurist" paradigm contrasted with Freud's emphasis on the past and Janet's focus on the present, offering a third way within psychiatric theory.

The significance of this work lies in recovering a phenomenological tradition that was influential in continental European and interwar Anglophone psychiatry but has been largely overlooked in histories dominated by psychoanalysis and biological psychiatry. It reveals that temporal experience was a legitimate object of scientific inquiry and that pre-DSM psychiatry contained rich theoretical alternatives for defining mental illness.

## Key Concepts

- **Ichzeit / Weltzeit dichotomy** — A dualistic model of temporal experience: *Ichzeit* (ego-time) is subjective, qualitative duration (Bergsonian *durée*); *Weltzeit* (world-time) is objective, quantitative clock-time or chronological frameworks. Psychopathology is a breakdown in the ongoing synthesis between these two modes.
- **Trouble générateur (generating disorder)** — Minkowski's concept that each psychiatric disorder has a specific underlying spatiotemporal disturbance that generates its symptomatic manifestations, enabling differential diagnosis based on temporal orientation.
- **Personal élan** — Minkowski's adaptation of Bergson's *élan vital*; a future-oriented vital force that bonds the individual to their becoming within the flow of time. Loss of personal élan underpins depressive and schizophrenic temporalities.
- **Syntony / schizoid temporality** — *Syntony* is the healthy ability to "vibrate in unison with the environment" by reconciling personal duration with external clock-time. *Schizoid temporality* is the inability to harmonize personal élan with external reality. Excess syntony = manic disorders; excess schizoid temporality = depression/schizophrenia.
- **Future-oriented paradigm** — A diagnostic framework emphasizing that mental illness results from an altered relation to the future, in contrast to Freud's emphasis on the past (trauma) or Janet's emphasis on the present. Represents a distinctively phenomenological turn.
- **Zeitsinn (time-sense)** — The faculty of temporal perception; clinicians argued that pathological states reveal the normally concealed structure of this sense.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Mental illness is fundamentally a temporal disorder—depression, schizophrenia, and obsessive-compulsive conditions are symptomatic of an altered "time-sense," specifically a breakdown in the synthesis between subjective time (*Ichzeit*) and objective time (*Weltzeit*).
  - **Evidence**: Clinical case studies (e.g., Charles's "interminable tunnel" of time; Minkowski's schizophrenic patient who daily announced execution), clinical observations of temporal disturbances (inability to date events, loss of future orientation, memory intrusions), and theoretical synthesis of Bergson's philosophy with clinical psychiatry.
  - **Strength**: Moderate. Relies on qualitative clinical observations and philosophical reasoning rather than controlled experiments. The author acknowledges experimental psychologists criticized Minkowski for rejecting quantifiable methods. Case studies are illustrative but not systematically collected.
- **Core claim**: Future orientation is central to psychiatric diagnosis—unlike Freud and Janet, phenomenological psychiatrists argued that psychopathologies arise from a diseased relation to the future (loss of personal élan, inability to conceive of an open future, or excessive orientation toward the past).
  - **Evidence**: Minkowski's *Lived Time* (1933) and subsequent research by Straus, Fischer, Lewis, and von Gebsattel; patient testimonies of temporal stasis and lack of futurity.
  - **Strength**: Moderate. The "futurist" framework is internally consistent and supported by multiple clinical reports, but it is culturally specific (assumes Western clock-time as normative) and was contested even within the phenomenological tradition (e.g., Blondel's emphasis on the present).
- **Core claim**: Phenomenological psychiatry offered a "third way" beyond experimental psychology (reductionist-empirical) and psychoanalysis (focus on the unconscious), using philosophy (Bergson, Husserl) to understand the totality of lived experience (*temps vécu*).
  - **Evidence**: The article traces the lineage from Minkowski through Binswanger, Straus, and others, showing how they positioned phenomenology as "intermediary" between psychophysics and pure philosophy.
  - **Strength**: Strong historical documentation that this was an explicit, self-conscious programme. However, the article notes that this tradition was largely marginalized in Anglophone psychiatry after the mid-20th century, limiting its long-term impact.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The Ichzeit/Weltzeit dualistic model is inherently unstable—Straus struggled with whether personal time is subordinate to clock-time or equal to it, never fully resolving the tension.
- Minkowski's concept of "personal élan" is acknowledged as "highly metaphysical," raising questions about empirical testability and compatibility with scientific psychiatry.
- Blondel's observation that depressed patients return to an "immeasurable" form of internal *durée* (treating this as evolutionarily authentic) contradicts Minkowski's focus on future orientation as the key diagnostic criterion, revealing disagreement within the same tradition.
- The framework assumes a Western conception of clock-time as normative—its applicability cross-culturally is questionable and was not addressed by the original authors.
- The source conflicts with the wiki's existing emphasis on DSM-III and the biomedical model by revealing a significant phenomenological tradition that was widespread in continental Europe but largely absent from Anglophone psychiatry after the 1950s.

## Open Questions

- How far did this phenomenological tradition persist after WWII in continental Europe, and did it influence later figures like Laing, Foucault, or Lacan?
- Is there evidence that this temporal framework was ever applied in treatment (e.g., therapeutic interventions targeting future orientation), or was it purely diagnostic/classificatory?
- Does the recent resurgence of interest in "lived time" in psychiatry (e.g., Fuchs, Van Duppen) represent a genuine revival of Minkowski's paradigm, or is it a parallel development?
- How did this tradition interact with or resist the rise of biological psychiatry and psychopharmacology in the 1950s–1970s?
- What explains the near-total disappearance of temporal nosology from Anglophone psychiatry, and what might its recovery contribute to current debates about psychiatric classification?
