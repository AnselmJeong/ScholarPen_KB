# Psychopathologies of time; Defining mental illness in early 20th-century psychiatry

## Summary
This article examines the emergence of a "futurist" paradigm in clinical psychiatry and psychology during the first half of the 20th century. The author analyzes how practitioners shifted away from reductionist physiological models of the brain to a phenomenological approach that viewed mental illness as a fundamental disturbance of the "time-sense."

The research focuses heavily on the influence of Henri Bergson’s philosophy of *durée* (duration) and its application by Eugene Minkowski. The core methodology involved clinical case studies and theoretical synthesis, arguing that health is characterized by the ability to synchronize one's internal, qualitative experience of time with the quantitative, linear time of the external world.

The findings demonstrate that specific pathologies were categorized by their unique temporal distortions: depression as a pathological deceleration and "usurpation" of the present by the past, and schizophrenia as a loss of "syntony" or vital contact with the temporal flow of reality. This framework positioned temporal disorder not as a symptom of delusion, but as the primary cause of it.

## Key Concepts
- **Ichzeit vs. Weltzeit** — The tension between subjective "ego-time" (internal duration/qualitative flux) and objective "world-time" (clock-time/quantitative chronology).
- **Duration (Durée)** — A Bergsonian concept describing time as a qualitative, non-linear flux of becoming, experienced intuitively rather than measured.
- **Temporal Synthesis** — The process by which a healthy psyche synchronizes internal subjective duration with the external temporal framework of society.
- **Personal Élan** — A future-oriented "vital force" that drives an individual toward self-realization and active becoming.
- **Syntony** — The state of "vibrating in unison" with the environment and world-time; the capacity for social and temporal resonance.
- **Phenomenological Psychiatry** — A clinical approach focusing on the totality of lived experience and the "structures of being-in-the-world" rather than isolated physiological symptoms.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Mental illness in the early 20th century was redefined as a failure of temporal synthesis, specifically a diseased relationship with the future.
  - Evidence: Minkowski's clinical observations of schizophrenic and depressive patients, and the adoption of Bergsonian duration in psychiatric theory.
  - Strength: Strong historical synthesis; effectively links philosophical shifts to diagnostic changes in the interwar period.
- **Pathology of Depression**: Characterized by a deceleration of *Ichzeit* where the past dominates the present, shrinking the perceived future into a state of stasis.
  - Evidence: Case studies of patients who felt "displaced" and described the past as "pushy" or "usurping" the present.
  - Strength: Well-supported within the phenomenological framework.
- **Pathology of Schizophrenia**: Defined as a loss of *syntony*, where the patient is unable to harmonize their personal *élan* with external reality, moving "out of sync" with society.
  - Evidence: Minkowski's 1923 case of a patient with a daily delusion of execution, interpreted as a basic temporal disorder.
  - Strength: High theoretical coherence, though relies on metaphysical assumptions.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Experimental vs. Phenomenological**: Tension between experimental psychologists demanding quantifiable statistics and phenomenologists viewing time as an intuitive, non-quantifiable flux.
- **Freud vs. Minkowski**: Freud focused on the *past* (trauma, memory) as the root of pathology, whereas Minkowski and the phenomenologists focused on the *future* (diseased anticipation, lost élan).
- **Metaphysical Validity**: The author notes that Minkowski's "personal élan" borders on social utopianism and metaphysical speculation, potentially limiting its scientific validity.
