# On the Limits of Presentism and Historicism in the Historiography of the Behavioral Sciences

## Summary
This editorial explores the philosophical tensions between "presentism" and "historicism" within the historiography of the behavioral sciences. The author examines how researchers often approach history with a utilitarian motive—searching for the origins of current theories or "founders" of the discipline—which frequently results in "Whig history," a linear narrative of cumulative progress that ignores the actual context and constraints of past thinkers.

The author argues that the behavioral sciences are particularly susceptible to these distortions because they are largely "pre-paradigmatic" in the Kuhnian sense, lacking a single consensual framework. This creates a vacuum where present-day academic competitions are projected onto the past.

The core thesis is a paradox: while the behavioral scientist's goal is often utilitarian (presentist), the only way to achieve a truly useful understanding of the past is to first employ an "affective" historicist method. By suspending judgment and reconstructing the specific questions to which past ideas were answers, the researcher can "recapture" lost perspectives and transcend current disciplinary fragmentation.

- **Journal of the History of the Behavioral Sciences (JHBS)** (org) — The publication venue and context for this methodological discussion.
- **The Structure of Scientific Revolutions** (tool/framework) — Thomas Kuhn's framework used to describe the "pre-paradigmatic" state of behavioral sciences.
- **The Whig Interpretation of History** (tool/framework) — Herbert Butterfield's critique of history written to ratify the present.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The study of the past for the sake of the present, often resulting in anachronism and the judging of past figures by modern standards.
- **Historicism** — The attempt to understand the past for its own sake, treating historical thoughts as answers to the specific questions of their own time.
- **Whig History** — A form of presentism that views history as a linear, inevitable progression toward current "truth" or "rationality."
- **Reasonableness vs. Rationality** — Rationality is viewed as timeless/abstract correctness; reasonableness is the quality of an idea being a valid answer to the specific questions of its era.
- **Pre-paradigmatic State** — A stage of science characterized by competing schools and the absence of a single unifying paradigm, making its history prone to presentist distortion.
- **Thinking vs. Thought** — The distinction between the history of static ideas (*thought*) and the history of the cognitive process of solving problems (*thinking*).

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The most effective way to derive utility from the past is to adopt a historicist approach.
  - Evidence: References to Kuhn's paradigms, Levenson's analysis of "reasonableness," and Dell Hymes' argument that recapturing lost perspectives helps transcend modern disciplinary fragmentation.
  - Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in established historiographical and philosophical frameworks (Butterfield, Kuhn, Collingwood).

- **Core claim**: Behavioral sciences are uniquely vulnerable to presentist distortion due to their "pre-paradigmatic" nature.
  - Evidence: The lack of a single framework leads researchers to use history to legitimize current points of view or search for "founders" to serve modern agendas.
  - Strength: High, as it provides a structural explanation for why "history of theory" courses in these fields are often sterile or partisan.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **The Presentist Paradox**: The author acknowledges an internal tension: the practitioner is motivated by the present (utilitarian), but must act as if they are not (affective/historicist) to avoid the pitfalls of anachronism.
- **Cumulative Progress**: The author challenges the "Sartonian" view that the history of science is a purely cumulative, ever-upward march of victory.

## Open Questions
- Who is best equipped to write these histories—the professional historian (who is naturally historicist) or the professional scientist (who has the technical background but is naturally presentist)?
