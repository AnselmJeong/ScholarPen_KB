# History of Madness

## Summary

Colin Gordon's essay provides a synthetic reading of Michel Foucault's *History of Madness* (HM), arguing that the book is primarily a history of exclusion—of how a culture defines its limits through the delineation and spatial containment of the Exterior—rather than a straightforward history of psychiatry or mental illness. Gordon situates HM within Foucault's broader intellectual trajectory, emphasizing its structural method, its analysis of the "great internment" of 1656, and its genealogy of how correctional internment mutated into the specialized therapeutic institution of the asylum. The essay draws on Foucault's 1961 preface, his later self-criticisms, and subsequent political contexts (including Soviet psychiatric repression) to illuminate HM's enduring significance.

Gordon's method is expository and interpretive: he closely reads Foucault's stated aims, conceptual architecture, and documentary appendices, while also reconstructing the adversarial intellectual field (Derrida's critique, Henri Ey's accusation of "psychiatricide") in which HM was received. Key findings include: (1) that the great internment was an administrative dragnet targeting the idle poor, not the insane specifically; (2) that HM's history of limits—leprosy, madness, the correctional world—reveals how spaces of exclusion are serially inherited and transformed; (3) that psychiatry's historical beginning is the purification of internment space into a therapeutic vocation where medical power ensures the production and avowal of truth; and (4) that HM anticipates Foucault's later analyses of governmentality, biopolitics, and the Christian genealogy of confession and power.

## Key Entities

- **Hôpital Général (Paris)** (org) — The institutional apparatus established by the 1657 French royal decree, central to the "great internment" that swept up the insane alongside the idle poor, venereal patients, and other deviants
- **Salpêtrière** (org) — A major Parisian hospital cited for its daily horarium (1721), exemplifying the quasi-monastic regimen inherited from leper houses
- **Saint-Alban** (org) — A psychiatric hospital that served as a wartime refuge for Resistance figures and a forerunner of the "institutional psychiatry" movement led by François Tosquelles and Félix Guattari
- **Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM)** (dataset) — Implicitly referenced through Gordon's discussion of psychiatry's defensive relationship to its own history and the ongoing question of diagnostic validity

## Key Concepts

- **Structural study** — Foucault's method of analyzing the historical ensemble of notions, institutions, judicial measures, and scientific concepts that "hold captive" madness; he distinguishes four components of the consciousness of madness: critical, practical, enunciative, and analytic
- **History of limits** — The study of how a culture defines itself through the rejection and delineation of the Exterior; madness is the "first and final" limit-experience alongside the Orient, dreams, and sexual prohibitions
- **The Great Internment** — The 1656 French royal measures that interned the idle poor, insane, and deviants in the Hôpital Général; not a specific targeting of the insane but an administrative dragnet that swept them up alongside other categories
- **Exclusion** — The spatial and symbolic demarcation of otherness, distinct from internment; leprosy constitutes the first Western space of exclusion, inherited by madness and later by the correctional world of the poor
- **Genealogy of psychiatry** — The tracing of how correctional internment mutated into a specialized therapeutic institution through a synthesis of exclusion, correction, and confession
- **Compelle intrare** — The Augustinian imperative "compel them to come in," used as the epigraph to Foucault's chapter on the Great Internment, framing the coercive benevolence of inclusionary hospitality and normative faith
- **Psychiatricide** — Henri Ey's accusation that Foucault's genealogical analysis was an act of violence against the psychiatric profession; reveals the profession's belief that legitimacy requires a positive, progressive genesis-narrative
- **Alterity/Othering** — The designation of an other does not inherently entail exclusion or internment; HM examines the intricate historical permutations of this relationship
- **Desacralization of the poor** — The Reformation theological shift that removed the pauper's status as the image of suffering Christ, making poverty an object of moral condemnation rather than religious veneration; a crucial precondition for the great internment

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: HM is a history of exclusion, not primarily of madness or psychiatry. Foucault interrogates how a culture defines its limits through the Exterior, with madness as the paradigmatic case. The book traces how leprosy exclusion spaces are inherited by madness, then by the correctional world of the poor, and finally by the psychiatric asylum.
  - Evidence: Close reading of Foucault's 1961 preface, the four-part structural schema of consciousness of madness, and the documentary appendix reproducing the Salpêtrière horarium of 1721
  - Strength: Strong textual evidence; Gordon is an established Foucault scholar. However, the interpretation is synthetic rather than based on new primary research.

- **Core claim**: The "Great Internment" was not targeting the insane specifically. They were swept up in an administrative dragnet for the idle, vagrant, and mendicant poor. The history is of multiple superposed limits and heterogeneous categories united in their space of exclusion.
  - Evidence: Foucault's chapter "The Correctional World" catalogs the diverse categories interned: venereal patients, sodomists, blasphemers, attempted suicides, sorcerers, alchemists, libertines
  - Strength: Well-supported by Foucault's text and documentary appendix; Gordon cites the 1657 royal decree preamble

- **Core claim**: Psychiatry's historical beginning is the purification of internment space into a therapeutic vocation. The asylum becomes an "other space" where medical power ensures production and avowal of truth. The genealogies of psychiatry, penality, and sexuality are thoroughly intertwined through exclusion-correction-confession synthesis.
  - Evidence: Foucault's 1981 interview outlining three series (exclusion-madness-truth; correction-prison-truth; sexual conduct-confession-truth); the structural homology between HM, Birth of the Clinic, and Discipline and Punish
  - Strength: Supported by Foucault's own later framing; Gordon demonstrates the continuity across Foucault's oeuvre

- **Core claim**: Psychiatry's defensive relationship to its own history is evident in the belief that professional legitimacy requires a progressive genesis-narrative. The Sérieux school and Henri Ey's "psychiatricide" accusation demonstrate this.
  - Evidence: Derrida's attack on HM as "totalitarian"; Ey's accusation at the 1971 national conference; Foucault's comment that nothing is more indicative of psychiatry's problematic epistemological status than this belief
  - Strength: Well-supported by historical evidence of professional reactions; Gordon notes the continuing relevance for psychiatric historiography

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Foucault's own self-critique vs. Gordon's defense: Foucault later distanced himself from HM's concept of "experience" as implying an "anonymous and general subject of history," but Gordon argues this autocritique was "largely unwarranted"
- The "impossible project" problem: Foucault's 1961 preface mentions wanting to write a history of "madness itself" before acknowledging its impossibility; Derrida seized on this as if it remained the actual project, while Gordon argues it was a methodological device
- Foucault deleting the 1961 preface in 1972 while leaving the body of the text unmodified: Gordon suggests this was a strategic refusal to be subjected to inquisitorial investigations, not a substantive repudiation
- Tension with existing wiki content on [[concepts/biomedical-model]]: Gordon shows Foucault was not "against medicine" and acknowledged forms of mental medicine existed before psychiatry; the critique in HM is more specific to how psychiatric power functions within institutional spaces
- Tension with [[concepts/anti-psychiatry]]: Gordon emphasizes HM "clearly acknowledges that forms of mental medicine exist outside of and before psychiatry" and is "not a book against medicine," complicating any straightforward anti-psychiatric reading
