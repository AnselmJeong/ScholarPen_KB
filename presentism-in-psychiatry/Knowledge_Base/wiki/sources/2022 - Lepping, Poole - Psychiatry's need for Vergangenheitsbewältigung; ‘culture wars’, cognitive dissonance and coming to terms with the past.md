# Psychiatry's need for Vergangenheitsbewältigung; ‘culture wars’, cognitive dissonance and coming to terms with the past

## Summary

This editorial argues that UK psychiatry’s self-identity as a socially progressive tradition makes it resistant to critical examination of its historical involvement in eugenics, racial science, and homophobia. The authors, both consultant psychiatrists, contend that defensive reactions to shameful episodes—such as Henry Maudsley’s racist anatomical claims, psychiatry’s collaboration with Francis Galton’s eugenics project, and the use of aversion therapy for homosexuality—reflect a broader British reluctance to confront the legacy of empire. They propose that the profession adopt a process of *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* (coming to terms with the past), a concept drawn from post-war German discourse, to achieve a more honest and integrated professional identity.

The piece is a commentary rather than an empirical study. It synthesizes historical evidence (primary sources from Galton, Pearson, Maudsley, and Tiedemann) with contemporary scholarship on Empire and structural racism (e.g., Sanghera, Olusoga, Barnett et al.). The authors emphasize that past values were not monolithic—contemporaneous critics like Tiedemann demonstrate that alternative ethical frameworks existed—thereby undermining the ‘different times’ excuse. They link historical eugenic ideology to modern disparities in mental health coercion, particularly the disproportionate detention of Black patients under the Mental Health Act.

The significance lies in reframing professional guilt as a constructive path toward self-awareness and improved practice. By honestly analyzing failures—not just successes—psychiatry can build a more authentic relationship with patients and society. The editorial directly challenges the Royal College of Psychiatrists’ self-congratulatory narrative (*Celebrating Our History*) and calls for curricular reform, public acknowledgment, and structural change.

## Key Entities

- **Royal College of Psychiatrists** (org) — The central professional body; its apologies for racism and homophobia are acknowledged but critiqued as insufficient, and its celebratory institutional history (*Celebrating Our History*) is identified as a barrier to genuine reckoning.
- **University College London (UCL)** (org) — Home of Galton’s Eugenics Laboratory; UCL’s apology and de-naming of spaces honoring Galton and Pearson are cited as a model of institutional acknowledgment.
- **Eugenics Laboratory (UCL)** (org) — Key institution for the development of eugenic concepts; mentioned in connection with Galton and Pearson.
- **Royal Bethlem Hospital** (org) — Site of collaboration between Galton and psychiatrists who used patient photographs to study hereditary degeneration.
- **British Psychological Society** (org) — Mentioned as an analogous institution struggling with its imperial legacy, drawing on the work of David Marks.

## Key Concepts

- **Vergangenheitsbewältigung** — The German term for “coming to terms with one’s past”; a process of honest analysis of failures, flaws, and moral ambiguities aimed at achieving authentic self-awareness and building a better future. The authors argue this should be a model for psychiatry.
- **Cognitive dissonance** — The psychological discomfort that arises when actions or beliefs conflict with self-image. Used to explain defensive reactions among psychiatrists when confronted with shameful history.
- **Eugenics** — The science/ideology of improving the human race through selective breeding. The source traces psychiatry’s direct involvement in eugenics (Galton, Pearson, Maudsley) and links it to modern structural racism.
- **Hereditary degeneration** — A 19th-century theory that mental illness is an untreatable genetic disorder that worsens across generations. Central to Maudsley’s thinking and to the therapeutic nihilism that justified coercive treatments.
- **Structural racism** — Systematic discrimination embedded in institutions and practices. The authors argue that the legacy of colonial racism is visible in modern disproportionate coercion of Black patients in mental health services.
- **Culture wars** — Contemporary political conflicts over national identity and history, often fueled by defensive pride in empire. The source links these to resistance against critical historical examination of psychiatry.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: UK psychiatry’s socially progressive self-image prevents honest engagement with its eugenic, racist, and homophobic past.
  - Evidence: Historical examples (Galton’s racial grading, Maudsley’s racist anatomy claims, Bethlem photo studies); recent apologies by the Royal College of Psychiatrists that remain incomplete; contemporary surveys showing British pride in empire.
  - Strength: Moderate. This is an opinion piece, not a systematic review. The historical examples are well-referenced but selective. The argument is logically coherent and supported by external scholarship (Sanghera, Olusoga, Barnett et al.).
- **Core claim**: Contemporaneous dissent proves that past values were not monolithic, which undermines the “different times” justification.
  - Evidence: Friedrich Tiedemann’s 1837 rebuttal of racial brain differences; British critics of the 1919 Amritsar massacre.
  - Strength: Strong. These examples demonstrate that alternative ethical frameworks existed at the time, making the defense of “everyone thought that way” historically inaccurate.
- **Core claim**: Adopting *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* can lead to a more authentic professional identity and improved practice.
  - Evidence: The authors draw on the philosopher Cioran’s idea that failures reveal the true self; they argue that honest reckoning can become a source of pride and integrity.
  - Strength: Weak to moderate. This is a normative argument without empirical evidence. The authors acknowledge the process is painful and triggers cognitive dissonance, but they do not provide examples of successful implementation in psychiatry.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The authors acknowledge their own position as “two male White psychiatrists,” which limits their ability to fully represent the experiences of non-White colleagues. This self-acknowledged limitation is a tension within the argument itself.
- There is an internal tension between the call for collective accountability and the recognition that individual psychiatrists may not be personally racist. The source does not fully resolve how to assign institutional versus individual responsibility.
- The concept of *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* is borrowed from German post-war discourse, but the source does not address potential differences between a defeated nation’s reckoning and a victorious nation’s reckoning—a gap that could be explored further.
- The source’s critique of the Royal College’s *Celebrating Our History* pamphlet may conflict with any existing wiki content that presents a more positive institutional history of the College.

## Open Questions

- How can psychiatry implement *Vergangenheitsbewältigung* in practical terms—curricular changes, public apologies, funding for community-led services—and how should the wiki track these as markers of genuine reckoning?
- Does the concept apply equally to other national contexts (e.g., US psychiatry’s relationship to slavery, eugenics, and mass incarceration)?
- What role should patients, especially those from racialized communities, play in shaping the historical narrative and the process of coming to terms?
- How can the profession preserve self-esteem and morale while honestly confronting shameful history—or is some loss of esteem an inevitable and necessary part of the process?
- To what extent do the “culture wars” in the UK over empire mirror similar dynamics in other post-imperial nations (e.g., France, Netherlands) and their psychiatric professions?
