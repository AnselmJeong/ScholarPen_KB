# History, Memory, and Profession; A View of American Psychiatry Through APA Presidential Addresses, 1883–2003

## Summary
This study examines the evolution of American psychiatry's professional identity by analyzing the retiring presidential addresses of the American Psychiatric Association (APA) from 1883 to 2003. The author employs a longitudinal analysis of these speeches, published in the *American Journal of Psychiatry* and its predecessors, to determine how leadership has used history not as a factual record, but as a rhetorical tool for self-definition and legitimacy.

The findings reveal a shifting narrative: early addresses focused on asylum administration and the reverence of founders; the interwar period emphasized "Mental Hygiene" and scientific grandeur; the mid-century shifted toward the "eternal" nature of psychotherapy and the influence of Freud; and the late 20th century focused on rapid change and neurobiological integration.

Significantly, the author identifies a "professionalizing purpose of memory," where the profession selectively remembers humanitarian successes while systematically forgetting or omitting "dark" periods, such as the era of eugenics, lobotomies, and racial segregation. This process allows the profession to maintain a consistent image of moral and scientific progress.

## Key Entities
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (type: org) — The central subject of study whose leadership narratives shaped the profession's identity.
- **Association of Medical Superintendents of American Institutions for the Insane** (type: org) — The original 1844 predecessor of the APA.
- **American Medico-Psychological Association** (type: org) — The intermediate name of the APA (1892–1921).
- **American Journal of Psychiatry** (type: tool) — The primary publication source for the dataset of presidential addresses.
- **American Journal of Insanity** (type: tool) — The predecessor journal to the *American Journal of Psychiatry*.
- **Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry** (type: org) — An activist group that challenged the APA's structure and traditionalism post-WWII.

## Key Concepts
- **Professional Self-Definition** — The process by which psychiatry defines its own boundaries, values, and identity, particularly in competition with psychologists and social workers.
- **Professionalizing Purpose of Memory** — The selective preservation of "positive" historical narratives and the erasure of problematic ones to enhance institutional legitimacy.
- **Mental Hygiene** — An early 20th-century movement transitioning psychiatry from custodial asylum care to proactive public health and scientific prevention.
- **Social Psychiatry** — The study of social factors in mental health, which was retrospectively framed by APA leaders as an inherent part of the profession's tradition.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: History in psychiatry is used as a rhetorical tool to justify current practices and defend the profession.
  - Evidence: Analysis of shifts in presidential addresses, such as the transition from celebrating asylum founders to celebrating the "timeless" doctor-patient relationship.
  - Strength: Strong; based on a consistent 120-year dataset of official leadership communications.
- **Core claim**: The profession employs selective forgetting to maintain a narrative of humanitarian progress.
  - Evidence: The absence of mentions of eugenics, lobotomies, and segregation in the vast majority of presidential addresses despite their historical prevalence.
  - Strength: Strong; the contrast between the "official" memory in addresses and documented historical fact is stark.
- **Core claim**: Psychiatry uses historical anchoring (e.g., referencing Hippocrates or Benjamin Rush) to assert its status as a medical specialty.
  - Evidence: Frequent invocations of the "medical lineage" to distinguish psychiatrists from non-physician practitioners.
  - Strength: Moderate to Strong; a recurring theme across multiple eras of addresses.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Narrative vs. Reality**: A fundamental tension exists between the "narrative of progress" promoted by APA presidents and the actual history of destructive psychiatric practices (e.g., eugenics).
- **Biological vs. Psychodynamic**: The tension between the profession's identity as a biological medical specialty versus a psychotherapeutic practice, often resolved by reinterpreting figures like Freud as "early neuroscientists."

## Open Questions
- To what extent do these elite narratives from presidential addresses reflect the actual beliefs and memories of the general psychiatric membership?
- How do the "forgotten" histories (e.g., eugenics) eventually re-enter the professional discourse, and through what mechanisms does this happen?
