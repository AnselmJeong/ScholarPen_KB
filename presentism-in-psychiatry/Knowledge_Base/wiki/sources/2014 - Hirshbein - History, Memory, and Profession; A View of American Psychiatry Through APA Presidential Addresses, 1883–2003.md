# History, Memory, and Profession: A View of American Psychiatry Through APA Presidential Addresses, 1883–2003

## Summary

This article examines how the presidents of the American Psychiatric Association (APA) used history in their annual presidential addresses from 1883 to 2003. The author analyzes the full corpus of published addresses (and later president-elect addresses) to trace shifts in how psychiatric leaders narrated the profession's past. The study treats these speeches as rhetorical acts that reveal how the profession defined itself, defended itself against critics, and projected a desired future — rather than as objective historical records.

The research method is a close reading of all presidential addresses published in the *American Journal of Psychiatry* and its predecessor, the *American Journal of Insanity*. The author organizes the analysis chronologically into six periods: the early years (1844–1882), 1883–1916, 1917–1940, 1941–1950, 1950–1969, and 1970–2003. Within each period, she identifies the dominant uses of history — from praising founders and defending asylum work to invoking grand historical lineages and asserting timeless values like the doctor-patient relationship.

The main finding is that professionally memory is a dynamic, purposeful construction. APA presidents consistently emphasized humanitarian motives, scientific progress, and continuity with medicine, while systematically omitting controversial episodes such as eugenics advocacy, lobotomy, racial segregation, and gynecological surgeries for psychiatric reasons. The addresses served to anchor professional identity during times of change — whether by reinventing Freud as a neuroscientific precursor or by tracing psychiatry's lineage back to Hippocrates. The significance lies in understanding how professions actively shape their own histories to maintain legitimacy and authority.

## Key Entities

- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (org) — the central organization; the addresses are its official presidential speeches, first delivered after the 1882 reorganization.
- ***American Journal of Psychiatry*** (dataset) — the primary publication venue for the addresses (and its predecessor, the *American Journal of Insanity*); the corpus of published speeches.
- **Group for the Advancement of Psychiatry (GAP)** (org) — activist subgroup that pushed for APA reorganization after World War II; referenced as a source of internal conflict.
- **Association of Medical Superintendents of American Institutions for the Insane** (org) — the predecessor organization to the APA, founded in 1844.

## Key Concepts

- **Professional memory** — The selective and socially constructed narrative of a profession's past, used to shape group identity, justify present actions, and defend against criticism. The author demonstrates how APA presidents actively constructed such memories.
- **Presentism (historiographical)** — The tendency to judge or interpret the past through the lens of present concerns. In this source, it is the method studied: presidents repeatedly used history to serve contemporary professional needs.
- **History as a professionalizing tool** — The rhetorical strategy of invoking a long, humanitarian, and scientific lineage to legitimize the profession's authority and scope.
- **Selective memory / amnesia** — The deliberate or unconscious forgetting of embarrassing or controversial past events (eugenics, lobotomy, racial segregation) to maintain a positive professional identity.
- **Mental hygiene** — A post-World War I movement to apply psychiatric principles to improve the mental fitness of the general population; this broadened focus shifted how presidents narrated history.
- **Timeless doctor-patient relationship** — A rhetorical figure repeatedly invoked to assert psychiatry's continuity with the entirety of medical tradition, often by citing Hippocrates.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The history of psychiatry as told by APA presidents is not a static record but a dynamic narrative that changes with the profession's self-definition, challenges, and aspirations.
  - Evidence: Systematic analysis of 120 years of addresses shows dramatic shifts in scope, emphasis, and content — from focusing on association founders to grand world-historical sweeps to recent-memory anchors.
  - Strength: Strong. Based on a comprehensive, consistent primary-source corpus; detailed chronological examples are provided.

- **Core claim**: The narrative serves a **professionalizing purpose**: it justifies current practices, defends against critics, and asserts authority within medicine.
  - Evidence: Presidents invoked Hippocrates, Benjamin Rush, and the doctor-patient relationship to claim timeless medical identity; they used history to respond to criticisms (e.g., S. Weir Mitchell) and to position psychiatry relative to neurology, psychology, and social work.
  - Strength: Strong. Direct quotations and specific episodes support this claim.

- **Core claim**: The profession has engaged in **selective memory**, systematically forgetting controversial episodes while recalling humanitarian and scientific achievements.
  - Evidence: The author explicitly lists omissions: eugenics, lobotomy, gynecological surgeries for psychiatric reasons, racial segregation in Southern asylums. Dorothea Dix's presence in early narratives and her later disappearance is a specific example.
  - Strength: Strong. The pattern of omission is documented across decades; the author notes it explicitly as a key finding.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The article itself highlights an internal tension within the APA: presidents urged colleagues to remember history to avoid past mistakes, yet they produced a highly sanitized version that omitted those very mistakes. This is a core contradiction between stated values and actual practice.
- Some presidents (e.g., Alan Stone in 1980) explicitly acknowledged racism, sexism, and pathologization of normal behavior, but these were exceptions. The overall pattern of omission contrasts sharply with the profession's self-image as humane and progressive.
- The author's claim that history is a "professionalizing tool" implicitly challenges any Whiggish (progress-focused) history of psychiatry, revealing it as a constructed narrative rather than an objective record.

## Open Questions

- How have APA presidents used history in the two decades after 2003 (the endpoint of this study)? Has the pattern of selective memory continued, or has there been a turn toward more critical self-reflection?
- Can the concept of "professional memory" be quantitatively measured (e.g., by tracking the frequency of references to eugenics, lobotomy, or other controversial topics over time)?
- To what extent do other professional medical organizations (e.g., the American Medical Association, American Psychological Association) engage in similar selective historical narratives in their presidential addresses?
- How do the omissions identified here relate to broader social movements (civil rights, feminism, disability rights) that challenged psychiatry from outside the profession?
