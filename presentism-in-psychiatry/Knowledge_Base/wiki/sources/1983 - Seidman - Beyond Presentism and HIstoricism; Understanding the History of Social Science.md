# Beyond Presentism and Historicism; Understanding the History of Social Science

## Summary
Steven Seidman examines the tension between two dominant historiographic approaches to the social sciences: presentism (the "Whiggish" view of cumulative progress) and historicism (the effort to understand the past "in its own terms"). He argues that while historicism provides a necessary corrective to the anachronisms of presentism, it suffers from flawed assumptions regarding textual interpretation and the nature of scientific development.

The author specifically critiques "New Historicism" for its reliance on intentionalism—the belief that meaning is identical to the author's original intent—and its insistence on total historical discontinuity. Seidman contends that these views ignore the "unintended meanings" of texts and the existence of universal social science problems that transcend specific historical contexts.

To resolve this, Seidman proposes a postpositivist, two-tiered model of science. He distinguishes between an "analytical realm" (hypotheses and empirical rules), which allows for cumulative progress, and an "ideological realm" (metaphysical and moral presuppositions), which provides stability and continuity through tradition. This framework allows for both the particularity of historical contexts and the enduring relevance of classic theories.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism (Whiggish History)** — A historiographic approach treating the past as a linear, cumulative progression toward the present, judging past ideas by current standards.
- **Historicism** — The attempt to understand a given period's science in its own terms, emphasizing historical discontinuity and the recovery of authorial intention.
- **Intentionalism** — The theory that the meaning of a text is identical to the author's original intention, recoverable via linguistic and historical context.
- **Contextualism** — An approach explaining ideas primarily through sociohistorical determinants (e.g., class), which historicists reject to protect the integrity of the text.
- **Postpositivist Theory of Science** — A multileveled view of science consisting of an analytical realm (logic/hypotheses) and an ideological realm (metaphysical/moral presuppositions).
- **Illocutionary Force** — The act performed by an author in issuing a statement, used by intentionalists to decode meaning.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Neither presentism nor historicism provides a sufficient model for the history of social science because both rely on flawed theories of scientific development and interpretation.
  - Evidence: The "contemporary resonance" of classic works (e.g., Marx, Weber) suggests they contain general claims that transcend their specific historical context, contradicting the historicist doctrine of total discontinuity.
  - Strength: Theoretically strong; synthesizes hermeneutics (Gadamer) and philosophy of science (Kuhn, Alexander).
- **Critique of Intentionalism**: Intentionalism is too narrow because it ignores "unintended meanings" and falsely assumes the interpreter can remain a "neutral" observer.
  - Evidence: References to Ricoeur and Gadamer regarding the semantic autonomy of the text and the dialogue between past and present.
  - Strength: High, supported by established phenomenological and hermeneutic theory.
- **The Two-Tiered Model**: Social science consists of a stable "ideological realm" (providing continuity/tradition) and a flexible "analytical realm" (allowing for cumulative progress).
  - Evidence: The observation that Marxist analytical components have varied wildly while the underlying ideological presuppositions remained relatively stable.
  - Strength: Provides a logical resolution to the tension between continuity and discontinuity.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension**: The tension between determining whether a text's meaning resides in the author's specific intention or in a general analytical claim.
- **Conflict with "New Historicism"**: Directly contradicts Quentin Skinner's intentionalist thesis, arguing that Skinner's approach is too restrictive and objectivist.
