# Beyond Presentism and Historicism; Understanding the History of Social Science

## Summary

This paper provides a critical methodological assessment of the two dominant approaches to writing the history of social science: presentism and historicism. Seidman argues that neither approach alone can adequately account for the complex development of social scientific thought, because each is built on an inadequate theory of science. The paper is structured into three main parts: a review of the principles of presentism and historicism, a detailed critique of the "new historicism," and a proposal for an alternative historiographic model.

Seidman defines presentism as a methodology that assumes continuity between past and present social science, often linked to a cumulative model of scientific progress and a positivist epistemology. Historicism emerges as a corrective, emphasizing discontinuity and aiming to understand the past "in its own terms." The author traces the historicist program primarily through the work of Quentin Skinner, Robert Alun Jones, and J. D. Y. Peel, focusing on their intentionalist theory of textual meaning and their empiricist, discontinuous model of scientific change.

The main findings of the paper are twofold. First, Seidman launches a three-pronged critique against historicist intentionalism: it ignores unconscious or unintended meanings (as psychoanalysis shows), it denies the semantic autonomy of texts (as hermeneutics and structuralism argue), and it rests on a false objectivism that assumes interpreters can bracket their own historical situation. Second, he criticizes the historicist theory of science for its radical discontinuity, which contradicts the rationalist premises of science and ignores the role of tradition and the continuity of general problems across historical contexts. The paper's significance lies in its systematic effort to synthesize insights from both presentism and historicism while avoiding their respective pitfalls, grounding a new historiography in a postpositivist, multi-level conception of science.

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism** — A historiographic methodology that assumes continuity between past and present, using contemporary standards and problems to organize, interpret, and judge past ideas. Linked to cumulative models of scientific progress and positivism.
- **Historicism** — A historiographic methodology that emphasizes the unique, context-bound character of past science, aiming to understand the past "in its own terms" by recovering authorial intention and situating texts in their original socio-linguistic context.
- **Intentionalist Theory of Interpretation** — The claim that textual meaning is identical to the author's intention, specifically the "illocutionary force" of what the author was doing in issuing a particular statement. Central to the new historicism of Quentin Skinner.
- **Textual Autonomy** — The idea that written discourse partially escapes the finite horizon of its author; the meaning of a text is not exhausted by authorial intention and can be determined in part by the historical situation of the interpreter.
- **Objectivism** — The epistemological position that it is possible to furnish a non-perspectivist, objective description of the past. Seidman, following Gadamer, argues this is impossible because interpretation is always conditioned by the interpreter's own traditions and prejudgments.
- **Postpositivist Theory of Science** — A multi-leveled conception of science that includes both an "analytical" realm (hypotheses, empirical statements, logical rules) and an "ideological" realm (reality-defining assumptions, epistemological presuppositions, moral and political commitments).
- **Tradition** — A heritage of common institutions, cultural commitments, and ideas that links successive generations. Seidman uses this concept, drawn from Gadamer and Shils, to argue for continuity in social scientific development against the historicist emphasis on radical discontinuity.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The existing historiographic dichotomy between presentism and historicism is inadequate for understanding the history of social science; a viable alternative must be grounded in a postpositivist, multi-level theory of science.
  - Evidence: The paper demonstrates that presentism leads to Whiggish, anachronistic interpretations, while historicism relies on an equally flawed intentionalist theory of interpretation and an empiricist, discontinuous model of scientific change. The alternative model, based on a two-tiered structure of science (analytical/ideological), accounts for both the cumulative (analytical) and non-cumulative (ideological) aspects of social scientific development.
  - Strength: Logically rigorous and well-supported by philosophical arguments from psychoanalysis, hermeneutics (Ricoeur, Gadamer), and the new philosophy of science (Kuhn, Lakatos). However, the paper remains at a theoretical/methodological level and does not test its model against detailed empirical case studies from the history of social science.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The paper acknowledges a tension within its own model: while the "ideological realm" of social science tends toward stability and non-cumulation, the "analytical realm" is susceptible to variation and cumulative progress. The precise mechanism by which these two levels interact over time is not fully specified.
- Seidman's reliance on "classic texts" to argue for continuity and general meaning is in tension with his own critique of objectivism, as the designation of a text as "classic" is itself a historically contingent and value-laden judgment.

## Open Questions

- How can Seidman's proposed two-tiered model be operationalized in concrete historiographic practice? What specific methods would allow historians to systematically analyze the interplay between the "ideological" and "analytical" realms in a given period of social science?
- Does the "dialogue" model of interpretation, drawn from Gadamer, provide sufficient criteria for adjudicating between competing interpretations of past texts, or does it risk collapsing into relativism?
- For the specific history of psychiatry (the focus of this wiki), what would be the content of the "ideological realm"? Does it primarily consist of the biomedical model, social control imperatives, or deeper cultural assumptions about rationality and personhood?
