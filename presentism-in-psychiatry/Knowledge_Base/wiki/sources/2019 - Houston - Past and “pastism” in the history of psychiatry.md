# Past and “pastism” in the history of psychiatry

## Summary

Rab Houston, a specialist in early modern British history (1500–1800), offers a synthetic critique of how the history of psychiatry is commonly misunderstood and misused. He introduces the term “pastism” to describe a pervasive historiographic bias: the automatic assumption that everything in the past was bad, weird, or confused (negative pastism) or, conversely, that things were uniformly better in bygone days (positive pastism). Houston argues that both variants distort accurate historical understanding and, consequently, impoverish modern clinical practice. The essay is rooted in decades of archival research on Scottish and English asylum records, but it is written as an accessible intervention aimed at mental health professionals and the general public rather than as a presentation of new primary data.

Houston identifies and systematically debunks five persistent myths: that mentally ill people were mistaken for witches, that trepanation was performed to release evil spirits, that asylums were mere “warehouses of the unwanted,” that husbands could easily have wives committed, and that unmarried mothers were institutionalized for having illegitimate children. In each case, he marshals counter-evidence from primary sources—showing, for example, that early modern people clearly distinguished witchcraft from madness, that trepanation was only used for head injuries, and that only a few dozen unmarried mothers appeared in Victorian Scottish asylum records, almost all with pre-existing or post-partum disorders.

The essay also challenges positive pastism by complicating triumphalist narratives about moral therapy and community care. Houston traces the roots of moral treatment not to the 18th-century Enlightenment but to earlier traditions of religious healing (“spiritual physic”), and he demonstrates that community care was the norm before 1800 and persisted in Scotland even during the asylum era. The significance of the piece lies in its dual argument: history is constantly being weaponized to legitimate or critique modern practice, but only accurate history—not myth—can serve either function constructively. Houston insists that mental health professionals are already historians in their daily work (compiling case histories) and that understanding historical contingency helps prevent the reification of contemporary diagnostic categories.

## Key Entities

- **Together (formerly Mental After-Care Association)** (type: org) — Oldest voluntary mental health group in Britain, founded in 1879 by the chaplain of Colney Hatch Lunatic Asylum to support patients discharged into the community; cited as evidence of early recognition of post-asylum care needs.
- **Colney Hatch Lunatic Asylum** (type: org) — North London asylum where the Mental After-Care Association was founded; serves as a peripheral institutional setting in Houston's narrative.

## Key Concepts

- **Pastism** — A historiographic bias in which the past is assumed to be uniformly bad (negative pastism) or uniformly better than the present (positive pastism). Houston coins the term as a more specific diagnostic for a pattern he sees as pervasive in both popular and professional discourse about psychiatric history.
- **Moral therapy / moral treatment** — A therapeutic approach emphasizing benign environment, useful occupation, and the verbal relationship between healer and sufferer. Houston argues its roots lie in 17th-century “spiritual physic” (religious healing), not the 18th-century Enlightenment as commonly claimed.
- **Teleological approach** — The practice of writing off the supposedly bad past while seeking only the roots of what is modern and good. Houston criticizes this as distorting historical understanding and reinforcing present-day complacency.
- **Biomedical reductionism** — The reduction of mental illness solely to organic brain disease, ignoring holistic and individualized approaches. Houston contrasts past holistic practices with modern bio-reductionism, arguing that accurate history can inform the current reaction against one-size-fits-all treatment.
- **Care in the community** — Non-institutional care for mentally disordered people. Houston shows it was the dominant mode before 1800 and persisted in Scotland (especially boarding-out) during the asylum era, challenging the narrative that community care is a modern invention.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim: Psychiatric history is systematically distorted by “pastism.”**
  - Evidence: Houston identifies five pervasive myths (witchcraft confusion, trepanation for spirits, asylums as warehouses, easy spousal commitment, punishment of unmarried mothers) and provides archival counter-evidence for each from early modern British sources.
  - Strength: Moderate to strong. Houston is a recognized specialist in early modern British history, and the counter-examples are drawn from his own and others' primary research. However, the essay is a synthetic intervention, not a presentation of new data; the claims are illustrative rather than exhaustive.

- **Core claim: Moral therapy has religious, not Enlightenment, origins.**
  - Evidence: Houston traces the emphasis on talking, spiritual comfort, and benign environment in 17th-century “spiritual physic,” arguing that the secularization of these practices in the 18th century obscured their religious roots.
  - Strength: Moderate. Houston provides a plausible genealogical revision, but the evidence is drawn from secondary literature and his own earlier work rather than a dedicated study of this claim.

- **Core claim: Accurate history is directly useful for clinical practice.**
  - Evidence: Houston argues that mental health professionals are already historians (compiling case histories) and that understanding the contingency of diagnostic categories prevents reification. He notes that terms like “dementia” and “neurasthenia” have shifted meaning, and that few current diagnoses existed before 1900.
  - Strength: Moderate. The argument is logical and well-illustrated but is a normative claim about the utility of history rather than an empirical finding.

- **Core claim: Community care is not a modern invention.**
  - Evidence: There were almost no institutional places before 1800; virtually all care was in the community. Scotland maintained a boarding-out system during the asylum era, suggesting different social and political ethos from England.
  - Strength: Moderate to strong. Houston provides specific historical examples, though the claim is more thoroughly supported in his other publications than in this synthetic essay.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Houston wants history to serve present practice (e.g., holistic therapies, community care) while warning against presentism. He resolves this by arguing that *accurate* historical understanding, not myth, can inform the present — but the tension between “history for the present” and the historicist ideal of understanding the past on its own terms remains partially unresolved.
- He criticizes teleological history (seeking roots of what is modern and good) while simultaneously arguing that moral therapy *is* a precursor to best modern practice. The tension is partially resolved by his claim that moral therapy's roots are in religious healing rather than Enlightenment rationalism, but the teleological structure of the argument persists.
- Houston's positive pastism (things were better in the past) overlaps with anti-progressivist historiography, creating a potential tension: if he debunks negative pastism, he may inadvertently open the door to romanticized views of the pre-asylum era that he also wants to avoid.
- The wiki's [[concepts/moral-treatment]] page may frame moral treatment as an 18th-century Enlightenment product — Houston explicitly challenges this, creating a direct contradiction that would need to be resolved in the concept page.

## Open Questions

- Is “pastism” a genuinely novel historiographic concept, or a specific instance of presentism with a negative valence? How should the wiki's taxonomy of historiographic biases (presentism, historicism, pastism) be structured?
- Houston's positive pastism (things were better in the past) seems to overlap with critiques of Whig history already present in the wiki. How does his formulation relate to existing work on progressivist versus declensionist narratives in psychiatric historiography?
- Houston argues that history is directly useful for clinicians, but how does this relate to the tension between “history for its own sake” (historicism) and “history for the present” (presentism) already debated in the wiki? Is there a principled way to distinguish between legitimate use of history and distortive presentism?
- The essay does not deeply engage with the eugenic, racist, and homophobic dimensions of psychiatric history that other sources (e.g., Lepping & Poole, Hilton) foreground. How should Houston's myth-busting approach be integrated with those more critical historiographic traditions?
- Houston's evidence is primarily from British (especially Scottish) sources. To what extent do his five myths and his corrective apply to other national psychiatric traditions (e.g., American, French, German)?
