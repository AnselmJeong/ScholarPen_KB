# Past and “pastism” in the history of psychiatry

## Summary
The author, an academic historian specializing in the early modern period (1500–1800), examines the prevalence of "pastism" within the history of psychiatry. Pastism is defined as the tendency to view historical medical practices as uniformly ignorant, cruel, or confused, often to legitimize current clinical methods or to support "anti-psychiatry" narratives that frame institutions as mere tools of social control.

Through a critical review of common historical tropes, the author refutes several myths, such as the idea that trepanation was used to release demons or that the mentally ill were routinely mistaken for witches. The author argues that early modern care was often more holistic and personalized than modern bio-reductionist models and notes that "care in the community" is an ancient norm rather than a modern invention.

The significance of this work lies in its call for clinicians to adopt a historian's mindset—recognizing that current diagnostic categories and "triumphant" biomedicine are contingent products of their own time and may eventually be viewed as historical artifacts.

## Key Entities
- **Together (formerly Mental After-Care Association)** (org) — An early voluntary group founded in 1879 to support patients transitioning from institutions to the community.
- **Colney Hatch Lunatic Asylum** (org) — A north London institution where the founder of the Mental After-Care Association served as chaplain.
- **In Our Time (BBC Radio Programme)** (tool/org) — Cited as a medium that has disseminated common psychiatric myths despite its intellectual reputation.

## Key Concepts
- **Pastism** — The historiographical bias of viewing the past as "bad, weird, or confused" to make the present seem more rational, or the opposite tendency to romanticize the past.
- **Anti-psychiatry** — A theoretical framework suggesting psychiatry serves as a mechanism for social control and capitalist interest rather than a humanitarian medical effort.
- **Moral Therapy** — A treatment approach focusing on benign environments, useful activity, and the therapeutic relationship; the author traces its roots back to "spiritual physic."
- **Bio-reductionism** — The modern tendency to reduce complex mental health issues to purely biological or genetic factors, contrasted with early modern holistic care.
- **Teleological Approach** — Interpreting history as a linear progression toward a "correct" modern end, which often dismisses past practices as mere failures.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Received wisdom regarding the "barbarism" of early psychiatry is often based on myths that serve current ideological needs.
  - Evidence: Analysis of legal archives and records showing that 16th/17th century people distinguished between witchcraft and madness, and that trepanation was used for intracranial pressure, not spirits.
  - Strength: Strong, based on historical archival evidence and comparative regional analysis.
- **Core claim**: The "warehouse" theory of asylums is an oversimplification.
  - Evidence: Evidence of the difficulty of admission (requiring petitions/affidavits) and the active agency of patients and families in negotiating their care.
  - Strength: Moderate; the author concedes the reality of involuntary admission but argues the "dumping ground" narrative is incomplete.
- **Core claim**: Community-based care is not a modern innovation.
  - Evidence: Historical data showing that before 1800, almost all care was community-based, with Scotland and Wales maintaining a stronger preference for "boarding out" than England.
  - Strength: Strong, based on regional comparative history.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Tension with Anti-psychiatry**: The author challenges the Foucault/Scull narrative that asylums were primarily "warehouses of the unwanted" for the purpose of capitalist social control.
- **Internal Caveat**: The author acknowledges that while "pastism" exists, there is a "measure of truth" in the warehouse theory regarding the longevity of stays and the involuntary nature of admission.
