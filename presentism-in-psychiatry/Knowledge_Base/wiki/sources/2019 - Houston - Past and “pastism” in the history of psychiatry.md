# Past and “pastism” in the history of psychiatry

## Summary

This essay by academic historian Rab Houston examines persistent myths in the history of psychiatry and proposes a balanced historiographical approach that avoids what he terms "pastism"—the tendency to view the past as uniformly worse (negative pastism) or uniformly better (positive pastism). Written as a synthetic reflection rather than an empirical study, the piece draws on Houston's expertise in early modern British history and his public engagement project "Promoting modern mental health through the lessons of history." It critiques both teleological narratives that dismiss past practices as inferior and anti-psychiatry accounts that reduce asylums to mere instruments of social control.

Houston identifies and debunks several specific myths: that madness and witchcraft were conflated in early modernity; that trepanation was performed to release evil spirits; that asylums were simple "warehouses of the unwanted"; and that moral therapy originated solely in the Enlightenment. He argues instead that early modern people clearly distinguished witchcraft from mental disorder, that trepanation in British history was always for head injuries, that asylum admission was competitive and involved family agency, and that moral therapy has deeper roots in 17th-century religious "spiritual physic."

The significance of the essay lies in its call for a nuanced history that neither legitimates modern practice by denigrating the past nor romanticises earlier eras. Houston maintains that understanding historical context—including the perennial presence of science, law, and community care—can inform current mental health debates. He urges practitioners to adopt an "informed scepticism" that recognises the contingency of knowledge and the value of accurate historical perspective for clinical judgment.

## Key Concepts

- **Pastism** — The historiographical bias that the past was either uniformly bad (negative pastism) or uniformly good (positive pastism); Houston's original term for the one-sided judgments that distort historical understanding of psychiatry
- **Moral Therapy** — 18th–19th century approach emphasising benign environment, useful occupation, and therapeutic relationship; Houston argues its origins lie in 17th-century "spiritual physic" rather than exclusively in the Enlightenment
- **Teleological approach** — Writing history as a progressive march toward the present, dismissing earlier ideas and practices as inferior or merely stepping stones to modern knowledge
- **Spiritual physic** — 17th-century term for religious healing through conversation; precursor to moral therapy, based on talking and shared religious frameworks for mental anguish
- **Bio-reductionism** — The tendency to reduce mental illness solely to biological or genetic factors; contrasted with historically holistic approaches that considered individual circumstances
- **Anti-psychiatry** — Critical perspective (Foucault, Dörner, Scull) viewing psychiatry as a mechanism for controlling deviant populations driven by capitalist interests; Houston revises some of its claims while acknowledging partial validity

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: "Pastism" (both negative and positive) is a fundamental historiographical error that distorts the history of psychiatry.
  - Evidence: Specific myth-busting examples show how common beliefs about witchcraft conflations, trepanation, asylum admissions, and the origins of moral therapy are inaccurate or oversimplified.
  - Strength: Moderate—supported by Houston's expertise and illustrative counterexamples, but the essay is synthetic rather than based on new quantitative data. Some claims (e.g., about asylum admissions) are acknowledged to have partial truth.

- **Core claim**: Historical myths serve to legitimate modern practice or invite change, but only accurate history can properly inform the present.
  - Evidence: The role of law in mental healthcare has deep historical roots; science has always been present in psychiatry (phrenology, physiognomy, nerve theory); community care existed long before deinstitutionalisation.
  - Strength: The argument is logically compelling and well-illustrated, though the author's own advocacy project creates a tension between scholarly neutrality and practical engagement.

- **Core claim**: The past is neither a "theatre of horrors" nor a golden age; historians and clinicians must resist the temptation to judge predecessors by contemporary standards.
  - Evidence: Examples of holistic therapies, family agency in admissions, and the diversity of care practices across regions and periods.
  - Strength: Well-supported by qualitative historical work; the call for epistemic humility is a methodological strength.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension**: Houston critiques the use of history to legitimate current practice but simultaneously runs a project that explicitly aims to "promote modern mental health through the lessons of history." He acknowledges this but does not fully resolve the conflict between scholarly neutrality and advocacy.
- **External tension**: The source directly challenges the anti-psychiatry canon (Scull, Foucault, Dörner) by arguing that asylums were not simply warehouses and that families had agency. However, Houston concedes "a measure of truth" in the warehouse thesis, creating a nuanced but contestable position.
- **External tension**: Against biomedical triumphalism, Houston argues that past practitioners were just as convinced of their science as modern psychiatrists—a position that may underestimate genuine advances in diagnosis and treatment.

## Open Questions

- How does "pastism" relate to the existing concept of "presentism"? Is it a subtype, a synonym, or a distinct analytical category? Houston uses the terms almost interchangeably, but further sources might clarify.
- Can Houston's revisionist account of asylum admissions (competitive, agency-driven) withstand systematic quantitative examination? His evidence is qualitative; future work (e.g., Wright 1997 cited in his bibliography) might provide numerical data.
- How should mental health professionals balance the "informed scepticism" Houston advocates with the practical need to use historical narratives to build therapeutic rapport or justify treatment decisions?
- Does the "spiritual physic" origin of moral therapy imply that secularization weakened the therapeutic relationship, or merely transformed its language?
