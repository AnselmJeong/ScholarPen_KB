# The New Focus on the Body

## Summary
This source provides a multidisciplinary look at the "somatic turn" in early 20th-century psychiatry, focusing on the intersection of medical treatment, patient experience, and state-sponsored biological control. It examines three distinct areas: the first-person experience of manic depression treated with hydrotherapy, the ideological framework of Swedish eugenics, and the clinical application of induced malaria to treat neurosyphilis.

The documents highlight a transition from purely theoretical psychiatry to an "empirical method" based on observed biological reactions. While some interventions, such as Wagner-Jauregg's malaria therapy, provided significant clinical breakthroughs for previously incurable diseases, others, like the compulsory sterilization programs in Sweden and the US, were driven by pseudo-scientific theories of "degeneracy" and "race-biology."

## Key Entities
- **Government Hospital for the Insane (Washington, DC)** (org) — Facility where the "Autopsychology" patient received hydrotherapy.
- **Institute for Race Biology (Uppsala, Sweden)** (org) — Research center led by Herman Lundborg dedicated to eugenic study.
- **Virginia Colony for Epileptics and Feeble Minded** (org) — Institution where Carrie Buck was held and sterilized.
- **U.S. Supreme Court** (org) — Judicial body that legalized compulsory sterilization in *Buck v. Bell*.
- **Hydrotherapy** (tool) — Use of continuous baths and wet sheet packs to relieve "cerebral congestion."
- **Malaria Inoculation** (tool) — Method of inducing high fevers to treat general paresis.
- **Salvarsan / Arsphenamine** (tool) — Anti-syphilitic drug used in combination with malaria therapy.

## Key Concepts
- **Manic Depression / Bipolar Disorder** — A condition characterized by "lightning-like" thought rapidity, delusions of grandeur, and intense physical restlessness.
- **Eugenics** — The social and scientific movement to improve human populations via controlled breeding and sterilization of the "unfit."
- **Degeneracy** — The theory that Western civilization was devolving due to the reproduction of "inferior" social strata.
- **General Paresis (Progressive Paralysis)** — Late-stage neurosyphilis resulting in dementia and paralysis.
- **Empirical Method (Psychiatry)** — A therapeutic approach based on imitating natural remissions (e.g., fevers) rather than relying solely on specific drug theories.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Induced high fevers can effectively treat progressive paralysis.
  - Evidence: Wagner-Jauregg reported an 84.8% full remission rate in early-stage cases when malaria inoculation was combined with neoarsphenamine.
  - Strength: Strong empirical/statistical weight for its era, leading to a Nobel Prize.
- **Core claim**: Compulsory sterilization is a justifiable exercise of "public welfare."
  - Evidence: The U.S. Supreme Court ruling in *Buck v. Bell* argued that preventing "three generations of imbeciles" outweighs individual bodily autonomy.
  - Strength: Based on biased ideological premises; legally authoritative at the time but scientifically discredited.
- **Core claim**: Mania involves a specific, overwhelming subjective experience of cognitive acceleration.
  - Evidence: First-person "Autopsychology" account describing thoughts "chasing one another" with "lightning rapidity."
  - Strength: High anecdotal validity, providing a rare professional (nurse's) perspective on the state of hypomania.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Somatic Relief vs. Clinical Method**: The patient in the "Autopsychology" report attempted to relieve "cerebral congestion" by beating her head against walls, while the doctors used hydrotherapy to achieve the same theoretical goal.
- **Autonomy vs. State Welfare**: The tension between the 14th Amendment's due process and the eugenicist claim that "public welfare" justifies the removal of reproductive rights.
- **Specific vs. Empirical Therapy**: Wagner-Jauregg's tension between the "rational path" (specific anti-syphilitic drugs) and the "empirical path" (induced fever).
