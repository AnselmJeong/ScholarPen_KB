# Presenteeism: A hidden phenomenon with many shades of gray

## Summary

This guest editorial by Amitav Banerjee, published in the *Industrial Psychiatry Journal* (2025), examines the concept of sickness presenteeism — the act of continuing to work while ill — as a largely hidden workplace phenomenon. The author contrasts presenteeism with the more visible and traditionally studied sickness absenteeism, noting that presenteeism may impose an even greater economic burden through reduced productivity. The piece is a commentary rather than an empirical study, drawing on selected literature and the author's observations of the Indian medical education context.

The editorial highlights a recent conceptual shift away from framing presenteeism solely as a negative behavior. It introduces the Health-Performance Framework of Presenteeism (HPFP), which balances health limitations against performance demands and categorizes workers into subtypes: functional, dysfunctional, overachieving, average, and therapeutic presentees. This approach accounts for heterogeneity in how illness affects work output and acknowledges that presenteeism can be functional under supportive workplace conditions. The HPFP was tested in a proof-of-concept study by Biron et al. (2022), which identified four profiles and found that dysfunctional presentees experienced more job stressors, while absenteeism did not differ across profiles.

A significant portion of the editorial addresses cultural and regulatory factors. The author notes that in many Asian workplaces, employees stay late when their boss is present, which may not increase output but harms well-being. More specifically, Banerjee describes a phenomenon he terms "absenteeism-presenteeism" in Indian medical colleges: faculty who are physically absent but digitally present through biometric attendance systems (Aadhar-linked, with face recognition), mandated by the National Medical Commission to combat "ghost faculty." This hybrid form eludes simple classification and introduces unique measurement and morale challenges. The editorial's significance lies in broadening the understanding of presenteeism beyond a purely negative lens and drawing attention to context-dependent and culturally embedded manifestations.

## Key Entities

- **National Medical Commission (NMC)** (org) — Indian regulatory body that mandated biometric attendance (Aadhar + face recognition) to enforce full-time faculty presence in medical colleges; created the conditions for "absenteeism-presenteeism."
- **Health-Performance Framework of Presenteeism (HPFP)** (tool) — Conceptual model that balances health limitations against performance demands to categorize presenteeism subtypes (functional, dysfunctional, overachieving, average, therapeutic).
- **Aadhar** (tool) — Indian biometric identity system used as part of the NMC's attendance tracking mechanism.

## Key Concepts

- **Presenteeism** — Continuing to work while ill; opposed to sickness absenteeism. Recognized as a hidden phenomenon with economic and health consequences.
- **Health-Performance Framework of Presenteeism (HPFP)** — An approach that evaluates presenteeism by considering both the worker's health status and their work output, allowing for a nuanced classification beyond the traditional negative framing.
- **Absenteeism-presenteeism** — A hybrid phenomenon where a worker is physically absent from the workplace but digitally present via biometric attendance systems; uniquely identified in Indian medical colleges under NMC regulations.
- **Sickness absenteeism** — Absence from work due to illness; the traditional focus of occupational medicine.
- **Therapeutic presenteeism** — A profile within the HPFP: good health but poor work output.
- **Functional presenteeism** — HPFP profile: good health and high work output.
- **Dysfunctional presenteeism** — HPFP profile: poor health and poor work output.
- **Overachieving presenteeism** — HPFP profile: high performance despite poor health.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Presenteeism is not uniformly negative; its impact depends on context, worker health, job demands, and workplace support. The HPFP provides a framework for disaggregating presenteeism into meaningful subtypes.
  - **Evidence**: Cites Biron et al. (2022) who identified four distinct profiles using the HPFP and found that dysfunctional presentees experienced more job stressors but no difference in absenteeism across profiles.
  - **Strength**: Moderate — based on a single proof-of-concept study; the editorial is a commentary, not an original analysis.
- **Core claim**: A novel form of "absenteeism-presenteeism" has emerged in India due to biometric attendance mandates in medical colleges, where faculty are physically absent but digitally present.
  - **Evidence**: Observational — describes NMC regulations and the resulting loophole (signing in and out twice daily while attending private practice in between).
  - **Strength**: Weak to moderate — anecdotal; no systematic data provided.
- **Core claim**: Presenteeism's economic impact may exceed that of absenteeism, but it is harder to quantify because the same days of presenteeism can yield different work outputs.
  - **Evidence**: Cites Hemp (2004) estimating a one-third or greater reduction in productivity; notes the same days of presenteeism leading to different outputs across HPFP profiles.
  - **Strength**: Weak — relies on a single estimate; no original cost data.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The editorial advocates for a neutral/contextual view of presenteeism while simultaneously emphasizing its hidden costs and negative productivity impacts, creating a tension between positive and negative framings.
- Short-term productivity gains from presenteeism may exacerbate long-term health damage and lead to future absenteeism, an internal caveat the source acknowledges.
- The quantification problem — "same days of presenteeism, different work outputs" — undermines any simple prevalence-based measurement approach.
- External tension: The term "presenteeism" may be confused with the historiographic concept "presentism" (as used elsewhere in this wiki), which concerns judging the past by present standards. The two are conceptually unrelated despite the shared etymology.

## Open Questions

- How generalizable are the HPFP profiles beyond the single study cited? Do they hold across different industries, cultures, and types of illness?
- Is the "absenteeism-presenteeism" phenomenon uniquely Indian, or might similar dynamics exist in other countries with biometric attendance mandates?
- What are the long-term health consequences of different presenteeism profiles (especially overachieving vs. dysfunctional presentees)?
- How can researchers reliably measure presenteeism's productivity impact given the heterogeneity within the same number of presenteeism days?
