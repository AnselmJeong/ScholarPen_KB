# Presenteeism; A hidden phenomenon with many shades of gray

## Summary
This editorial examines the complexities of "presenteeism"—the act of continuing to work while ill—and argues that it represents a more significant hidden economic burden than traditional sickness absenteeism. The author synthesizes existing research to move beyond a purely negative view of presenteeism, suggesting that its impact varies based on the type of illness, the specific requirements of the job, and the available workplace support.

The text introduces the Health-Performance Framework of Presenteeism (HPFP) to categorize workers based on the intersection of their health status and their actual work output. Furthermore, the author highlights a modern systemic paradox in India, where biometric attendance systems intended to curb absenteeism have instead created a hybrid "absenteeism-presenteeism" where faculty are digitally present but physically absent.

## Key Entities
- **National Medical Commission (NMC)** (org) — Indian regulatory body implementing biometric tracking to prevent "ghost faculty."
- **Industrial Psychiatry Journal** (org) — The publishing venue for this editorial.
- **Health-Performance Framework of Presenteeism (HPFP)** (tool) — A worker-centric model used to categorize presenteeism profiles based on health and performance.
- **Aadhar / Biometric Systems** (tool) — Digital identity and attendance tools used by the NMC.

## Key Concepts
- **Sickness Presenteeism** — The practice of attending work despite being ill or incapacitated.
- **Sickness Absenteeism** — Absence from the workplace due to illness; the traditional metric for occupational health.
- **HPFP Profiles** — A classification system comprising Functional (good health/high output), Dysfunctional (poor health/poor output), Overachieving (poor health/high output), Average (average health/output), and Therapeutic (good health/poor output) presentees.
- **Absenteeism-Presenteeism** — A behavioral loophole where a worker is "digitally present" (via biometric log) but "physically absent" (e.g., attending private practice).
- **Cultural Presenteeism** — The tendency in certain Asian cultures to remain at the workplace until superiors leave, regardless of actual productivity.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Presenteeism is not a monolith and its impact is determined by health status, performance, and context.
  - Evidence: Reference to the HPFP model and Biron et al. study showing different output levels for people with similar days of presenteeism.
  - Strength: Moderate; primarily an editorial synthesis of other studies rather than new empirical data.
- **Core claim**: Presenteeism is economically more damaging than absenteeism.
  - Evidence: Estimates suggesting productivity cuts of one-third or more, which are harder to quantify than binary absence.
  - Strength: High (widely accepted in occupational medicine literature cited).
- **Core claim**: Technological solutions for attendance can create new forms of deceptive presence.
  - Evidence: The NMC's biometric mandate allowing faculty to log in/out while spending the day at private practices.
  - Strength: Anecdotal/Regulatory report based.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Measurement Gap**: There is a fundamental tension between the ease of measuring absenteeism (binary) and the difficulty of quantifying the "shades of gray" in presenteeism productivity.
- **Technological Paradox**: Biometric tools designed to eliminate "ghost faculty" (absenteeism) have inadvertently legitimized "absenteeism-presenteeism" (digital presence without physical presence).
- **Functional vs. Dysfunctional**: While typically seen as negative, the HPFP suggests presenteeism can be "functional" if appropriate workplace support exists.

## Open Questions
