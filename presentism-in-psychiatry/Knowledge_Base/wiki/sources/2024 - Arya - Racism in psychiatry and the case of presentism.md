# Racism in psychiatry and the case of presentism

## Summary
This guest editorial examines the intersection of racism, colonial power structures, and the historiography of psychiatry. The author argues that a complete critical history of the field requires two simultaneous efforts: the "recovery" of marginalized figures from the Global South and the nuanced contextualization of influential but morally problematic historical figures.

The author utilizes historical examples, such as the contributions of Juliano Moreira and Frantz Fanon, to demonstrate how colonial hegemony has suppressed non-Western psychiatric knowledge. Conversely, the piece addresses the tension regarding figures like Emil Kraepelin, whose scientific contributions are foundational but whose personal views were rooted in eugenics and antisemitism.

The central thesis is a rejection of "presentism"—the act of judging historical figures solely by modern moral standards—in favor of a "syncretic approach." This approach maintains that the past cannot be purged; instead, it must be layered, allowing practitioners to learn from historical failures to build more inclusive, reflexive current practices.

Finally, the author asserts that the modern psychiatric profession must engage in critical reflexivity regarding "White privilege" to avoid both the blindness of institutional racism and the pitfall of oversimplified accusations.

## Key Concepts
- **Presentism** — The historiographical error of applying current-day moral and social standards to judge individuals from the past without accounting for the historical context.
- **Decolonization** — The process of identifying and undoing the impact of colonialist power structures on the production and distribution of psychiatric knowledge.
- **White Privilege** — The unearned advantages associated with being White, male, and heteronormative, which the author argues requires active reflection and accountability in clinical practice.
- **Cultural Psychiatry** — A specialized field focusing on the intersection of cultural context and mental health, which the author argues has been shaped by both racist origins and subsequent corrective recoveries.
- **Syncretic Approach** — A method of historical analysis that integrates a multi-layered understanding of the past (including its flaws) to drive the development of more inclusive future knowledge.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The recovery of marginalized voices is essential for a complete psychiatric history.
  - Evidence: The overlooked clinical work of Juliano Moreira (African-Brazilian) and Frantz Fanon (Martinique-born).
  - Strength: Strong historical precedent, though primarily a theoretical argument for historiographical change.
- **Core claim**: Erasure of "problematic" figures via presentism is counterproductive.
  - Evidence: The case of Emil Kraepelin, whose eugenicist views coexist with his foundational scientific work.
  - Strength: Medium; relies on the philosophical distinction between contextualization and excuse.
- **Core claim**: Clinical effectiveness requires a nuanced understanding of privilege.
  - Evidence: Argument that lacking nuance leads to "reading racism everywhere" and creates therapeutic friction.
  - Strength: Theoretical; proposed as a path toward a more positive therapeutic environment.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Context vs. Erasure**: There is a tension between the drive to "decolonize" (which challenges the dominance of White, Western figures) and the warning against "presentism" (which argues against erasing those same figures due to their flawed views).
- **Nuance in Privilege**: The author notes a tension where a lack of a nuanced understanding of White privilege can lead to "unhelpful accusations" of oversensitivity.

## Open Questions
- How can psychiatry practically implement a "syncretic approach" in medical education without appearing to excuse historical racism?
- What specific mechanisms of "recovery" are most effective for integrating Global South psychiatric histories into Western curricula?
