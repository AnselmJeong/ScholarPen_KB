# War and Neurosis

## Summary
This document presents two contrasting approaches to the treatment of "war neuroses" (shell shock) during World War I, featuring the work of Fritz Kaufmann and W.H.R. Rivers. The text explores the tension between somatic, aggressive interventions aimed at rapid military return and psychological, analytical methods aimed at resolving internal conflict.

Fritz Kaufmann advocated for a "symptomatic treatment" known as the "surprise attack," which utilized strong electrical shocks and harsh military commands to forcibly remove motor dysfunctions. His primary objective was the efficient restoration of human labor power and the reduction of the military budget's burden.

Conversely, W.H.R. Rivers developed a theory based on military hierarchy. He argued that the blind obedience required of private soldiers increased their suggestibility, leading to hysterical symptoms. Officers, however, suffered from anxiety neurosis caused by the need to repress emotion and the psychological weight of responsibility for their subordinates. Rivers proposed a "re-education" method to make patients conscious of the mental basis of their disorders.

## Key Entities
- **Fritz Kaufmann** (tool: "The Surprise Attack") — Developed a high-intensity electrical and suggestive treatment to rapidly cure soldiers of motor disorders.
- **W.H.R. Rivers** (tool: Psychoanalytic Re-education) — Developed a psychological approach to shell shock emphasizing talking cures and understanding the etiology of neurosis.
- **Erlanger Pantostat** (tool) — A portable electrical device used to deliver alternating currents during "surprise attack" treatments.
- **Craiglockhart War Hospital** (org) — The facility where Rivers treated officers using psychoanalytic methods.
- **Erbs Clinic** (org) — The site where Kaufmann initially practiced faradic brushing.

## Key Concepts
- **Traumatic Neuroses / Shell Shock** — Psychogenic disorders manifesting as motor or mental dysfunction following war trauma.
- **The Surprise Attack** — A treatment combining strong electric shocks, harsh military commands, and a demand for an immediate cure to "shock" the nervous system.
- **Anxiety Neurosis** — A state of persistent anxiety caused by the repression of emotion and the weight of responsibility, particularly among officers.
- **Suggestibility** — The susceptibility to external influence, which Rivers argues is heightened in privates by military drilling.
- **Sublimation and Repression** — Psychological mechanisms used by officers to maintain a calm exterior, which Rivers argues leads to internal conflict.
- **Re-education** — The process of making a patient understand the mental basis of their disorder to reduce suggestibility and resolve conflict.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Complicated psychogenic motor disorders can be cured in a single session through a "mental shock."
  - Evidence: Clinical application of the "surprise attack" (electric current + military orders) on soldiers.
  - Strength: Anecdotal/Clinical; limited by lack of long-term data and admitted risks of relapse.
- **Core claim**: War neurosis differs by rank due to the nature of military training and duty.
  - Evidence: Comparative observation of officers (repression/responsibility) vs. privates (suggestion/obedience).
  - Strength: Theoretical/Observational; provides a strong etiological framework but lacks long-term "after-history" data.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Tension (Kaufmann)**: Kaufmann admits his method may only remove symptoms while the "illness inclination" remains, risking relapse if the patient is released too early.
- **Inter-Author Conflict**: Kaufmann views the physician as a dominant will who forces a cure upon the patient; Rivers views the physician as a guide in a process of re-education and consciousness.
- **Socio-Economic Tension**: Kaufmann's motivation is explicitly tied to military budget efficiency and labor power, contrasting with Rivers' focus on the patient's psychological resolution.
