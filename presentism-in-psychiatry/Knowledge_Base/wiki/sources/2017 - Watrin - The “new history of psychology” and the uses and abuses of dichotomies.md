# The "new history of psychology" and the uses and abuses of dichotomies

## Summary

Watrin (2017) offers a sustained methodological critique of the "new history of psychology" – a self-described historiographical movement that defines itself in symmetrical opposition to an "old history" through a set of standard dichotomies: Whig/prig, presentism/historicism, great men/Zeitgeist, internalism/externalism, primary/secondary sources, and psychologist/professional historian. Rather than examining actual historical narratives, Watrin analyzes the justifications and manifestos of this rhetoric, focusing on how its proponents (notably Furumoto and Leahey) have used these dichotomies to distinguish critical from celebratory history.

The article proceeds by examining each dichotomy in turn, drawing on established historiography of science to show that the poles are complementary rather than mutually exclusive. For example, Whig and anti-Whig (prig) attitudes both imply a progressive narrative – the prig stance is simply Whiggish about historiography itself. Similarly, presentism is an unavoidable perspective when writing from the present, and the rigid distinction between presentism and historicism collapses into what Teo and Goertzen call "presentist historicism" and "historicist presentism." Watrin then explores the implications of this dichotomization for both critical historiography and the history of psychology as a discipline.

The central finding is that an over-reliance on dichotomies as prescriptive rules produces several undesirable consequences: a misleading sense of unity among critical historians, an uncritical attitude toward the new history's own assumptions, isolation of the history of psychology from philosophy and from psychology itself, and threats to thematic and methodological breadth. Watrin argues that the solution lies in replacing prescription with critical reflection on assumptions, and in recognizing that a balanced view of dichotomies preserves both rigor and inclusiveness. The significance of this work lies in its challenge to a widely taught and often unexamined framework, and its call for a more nuanced historiographical practice.

## Key Concepts

- **New history of psychology** — A self-described historiographical movement defined in opposition to "old history" through dichotomies such as presentism vs. historicism and internalism vs. externalism; Watrin argues it is a misleading rhetorical construct.
- **Old history of psychology** — A straw-man category representing Whig, presentist, internalist, great-man, secondary-source reliant, and amateurish history; used as the opposing pole to define the new history.
- **Whig history** — Narratives that emphasize progress and ratify the present (Butterfield); Watrin argues anti-Whiggism (prig) is equally problematic.
- **Prig history** — An anti-Whig attitude that rejects progress narratives but paradoxically implies the superiority of present historians and becomes Whiggish about historiography.
- **Presentism** — Judging the past by present standards; Watrin distinguishes between necessary presentism (inevitable perspective) and distorting presentism (naïve anachronism).
- **Historicism** — Understanding the past on its own terms; Watrin argues it is complementary to presentism, not its opposite.
- **Great men / Zeitgeist** — Dichotomy between individual genius and contextual forces as drivers of historical change; Watrin dissolves it by noting that individuals are agents of context.
- **Internalism / Externalism** — Opposition between forces inside science (reason, evidence) and outside (social, political, cultural) that shape scientific development; Watrin argues they are complementary.
- **Compensatory history** — Over-correction that inflates contributions of marginalized groups to compensate for past neglect; Watrin critiques this as potentially reducing thematic breadth.
- **Textbook historiography** — A simplified, dichotomous view of history writing taught in textbooks; Watrin warns the new history may become exactly what it criticizes.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The "new history of psychology" is a misleading rhetorical construct built on historiographical dichotomies that do not hold in practice.
  - Evidence: Logical critique of each dichotomy showing their poles are complementary; examples of actual histories (e.g., Blumenthal, Danziger, DeCarvalho) mixing styles; admissions by proponents Leahey and Samelson that they do not fully follow the framework.
  - Strength: Moderate to strong — logically rigorous but relies on selective examples rather than systematic empirical analysis of historical narratives.

- **Core claim**: Dichotomies are abused when treated as mutually exclusive prescriptions and prohibitions rather than as complementary tools.
  - Evidence: Demonstrates that the anti-Whig (prig) stance is itself Whiggish about historiography; shows that presentism is unavoidable and not inherently distorting; notes that even proponents like Furumoto and Leahey do not produce pure "new history."
  - Strength: Well-supported by logical argumentation and direct citations from the literature.

- **Core claim**: The rhetoric of new history produces undesirable consequences: a misleading sense of identity among critical historians, an uncritical attitude toward its own assumptions, isolation from philosophy and psychology, and threats to thematic and methodological breadth.
  - Evidence: Observes the scarcity of critiques of new history; points to Furumoto's (2003) overcompensation for marginalization; notes Samelson's (1999) criticism of fragmentary products; argues that emphasis on primary sources and social context devalues intellectual history and textbook writing.
  - Strength: Moderate — plausible and well-argued, but the evidence is largely anecdotal and based on a limited number of examples from the new history literature.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension**: Watrin critiques dichotomization but himself uses dichotomies such as "critical/uncritical" and "balanced/abused." He implicitly acknowledges this by stating that dichotomies are not inherently problematic, only their abuse is.
- **Potential conflict with [[concepts/presentism]]** — If the wiki currently defines presentism as simply "bad history," Watrin's argument that necessary presentism is unavoidable and distinct from distorting presentism would require revision.
- **Potential conflict with [[sources/2016 - Brock - The new history of psychology; Some (different) answers to Lovett’s five questions.|Brock (2016)]]** — Watrin directly counters Brock's claim that the new history represents a fundamental and coherent methodological shift.

## Open Questions

- How should historians of psychology actually teach historiography, given the shortcomings of both textbook historiography and the new history framework? Watrin criticizes both but offers no concrete pedagogical alternative.
- Is there a "third way" beyond the new/old dichotomy, such as the "philosophical history of psychology" proposed by Araujo (2017), which Watrin cites approvingly?
- Does Watrin's critique of dichotomous historiography apply equally to the history of psychiatry, given that the same dichotomies (presentism vs. historicism, internalism vs. externalism) appear in that field?
- What counts as "abuse" of dichotomies versus "use"? Watrin draws a conceptual line but does not operationalize it, leaving an unresolved tension in the argument.
