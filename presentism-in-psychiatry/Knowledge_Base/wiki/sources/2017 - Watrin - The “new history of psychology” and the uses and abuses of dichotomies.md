# The “new history of psychology” and the uses and abuses of dichotomies

## Summary
This article provides a meta-critique of the "new history of psychology," a movement that emerged in the late 20th century as a professionalized, critical alternative to "old" (traditional) history. The author examines the theoretical framework used by proponents of the new history, which relies on a series of binary oppositions (e.g., historicism vs. presentism, externalism vs. internalism) to define its superiority over traditional narratives.

Through a theoretical analysis of these dichotomies and a review of the practices of "new historians," Watrin argues that these binaries are complementary rather than mutually exclusive. The author finds a significant gap between the rhetorical prescriptions of the "new history" and the actual work produced by its advocates, who often employ a mix of both "old" and "new" methods.

The significance of this work lies in its warning against "textbook historiography"—the tendency to treat these dichotomies as dogmatic rules. The author suggests that this rigid framing leads to "overcompensation for marginalization," a fragmentary focus on narrow topics, and a detrimental isolation of historians from the very psychologists and philosophers they study.

## Key Concepts
- **Whiggism / Anti-Whiggism ("Prig")** — Whiggism is the portrayal of history as a progressive march toward the present; "prig" is the rigid rejection of such narratives, which the author argues can paradoxically become a new form of Whiggism regarding historiographical progress.
- **Presentism vs. Historicism** — The tension between interpreting the past through the lens of the present (presentism) versus understanding the past for its own sake (historicism).
- **Internalism vs. Externalism** — Focus on the internal logic and intellectual development of a science (internalism) versus its social, political, and cultural contexts (externalism).
- **Great Men vs. Zeitgeist** — Attributing change to influential individuals versus the "spirit of the times" or collective contextual factors.
- **Critical Historiography** — A general approach to questioning traditional historical narratives, which the author distinguishes from the specific, more rigid "new history" framework.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "new history of psychology" is a rhetorical device rather than a description of actual critical practice, built on artificial dichotomies.
  - Evidence: The author demonstrates that poles like internalism/externalism are reciprocal; citations show that prominent "new historians" (e.g., Leahey) admit their work does not strictly follow "new history" prescriptions.
  - Strength: Strong; logically rigorous and uses the internal contradictions of the movement's proponents.
- **Core claim**: The "new history" rhetoric produces a "Procrustean bed" that restricts the thematic and methodological scope of the field.
  - Evidence: The "overcompensation for marginalization" leads to a fragmented history of disparate pieces rather than a coherent "big picture" of the discipline.
  - Strength: Moderate; relies on the analysis of current scholarship trends and the observation of thematic fragmentation.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Internal Paradox**: The "new history" promotes critical thinking and the questioning of assumptions, yet its own foundational dichotomies have become "totemic dogmas" that are rarely questioned by its adherents.
- **Professional Tension**: Proponents argue the "new history" professionalizes the field; Watrin argues this professionalization is a rhetorical claim to legitimacy that creates a divide between professional historians and practicing psychologists.

## Open Questions
- How can the field move toward a "post-dichotomy" framework that maintains a critical edge without relying on artificial oppositions?
- To what extent has the "new history" rhetoric actually shifted undergraduate psychology curricula versus remaining an academic discourse among specialists?
