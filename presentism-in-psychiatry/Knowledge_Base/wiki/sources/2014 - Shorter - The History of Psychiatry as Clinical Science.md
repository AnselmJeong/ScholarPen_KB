# The History of Psychiatry as Clinical Science

## Summary

Shorter argues that historians of psychiatry have largely avoided studying actual disease entities, treating diagnosis instead as intellectual or cultural history. He identifies three causes for this neglect: infection with antipsychiatry ideology (Foucault's legacy), lack of technical medical knowledge, and residual attachment to the history of ideas. He advocates "presentism with a vengeance"—using historical knowledge not to justify the present but to correct current diagnostic and therapeutic practice.

The essay contrasts two historiographic approaches: psychiatrist-historians (exemplified by Werner Janzarik) who produce clinically useful analyses of past diagnoses, and PhD-historians (exemplified by Eric Engstrom) who produce culturally insightful but clinically irrelevant work. Shorter's central claim is that current DSM classification—especially "major depression" and "schizophrenia"—is artifactual because it lumps distinct disease entities that nature separates, while historically validated diagnoses like melancholia and catatonia are neglected.

Shorter provides several concrete examples of clinically useful historical work: Howard Kushner's use of historical sources to confirm infectious triggers in Tourette syndrome and revise Kawasaki syndrome diagnostic criteria; collaborative research on self-injurious behavior (SIB) in autism as a treatable catatonic syndrome; and the revival of melancholia as an independent diagnosis through historical scholarship. He introduces the "symptom pool" concept—the culturally and historically variable set of symptoms from which patients unconsciously select—as a theoretical contribution bridging biological realism and social construction.

## Key Entities

- **Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM)** (tool) — Primary target of Shorter's nosological critique; he argues that DSM-5 classification is full of artifacts that misrepresent natural disease boundaries
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (org) — Publisher of DSM; institutional entity responsible for the diagnostic classification Shorter criticizes
- **Heidelberg University Psychiatric Hospital** (org) — Premier historical center for psychopathology under Kraepelin, Nissl, and Wilmanns; site of Janzarik's clinically useful historical research
- **Kennedy Krieger Institute** (org) — Research site for collaborative work on catatonia and self-injurious behavior in autism
- **Johns Hopkins Medical Institutions** (org) — Affiliated institution for collaborative autism/catatonia research
- **Emory University** (org) — Howard Kushner's institutional base for pioneering clinically engaged historical research
- **University of California San Diego** (org) — Collaborative site for Kawasaki syndrome historical research

## Key Concepts

- **Presentism (defended)** — Approaching the past not to justify the present but to aid it; Shorter advocates using historical knowledge to solve current clinical problems
- **Clinical science** — The practice of writing psychiatric history with the explicit goal of contributing to patient care and diagnostic accuracy
- **Symptom pool** — A culturally and historically variable set of symptoms from which patients unconsciously select to present as "organic" to their clinicians; explains changing presentations of psychosomatic illness
- **Catatonia** — A motor syndrome treatable with benzodiazepines and ECT; Shorter argues it underlies many cases of self-injurious behavior in autism
- **Melancholia** — A distinct form of depressive illness that Shorter argues should be an independent diagnosis, not merely a specifier of "major depression"
- **Artifact** — A diagnostic category in the DSM that lumps distinct disease entities together (e.g., major depression conflating melancholia and non-melancholic depression; schizophrenia lumping multiple psychotic conditions)
- **Iron triangle** — A hypothesized subset of autism involving the co-occurrence of autism, catatonia, and psychosis; proposed by Shorter and Wachtel
- **Antipsychiatry virus** — Shorter's term for the skeptical stance that psychiatric diagnoses are social labels rather than real disease entities; he argues this "infected" historians and discouraged study of actual illness
- **Nosology** — The classification of psychiatric illness; Shorter critiques the current DSM nosology as historically uninformed and artificially constructed
- **Disease entity realism** — The epistemological position that some diagnoses "cut nature at the joints" and correspond to actual biological disease, as opposed to being purely social constructions

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Historians of psychiatry can and should function as clinical scientists.
  - Evidence: Kushner's work on Tourette syndrome (historical sources confirmed streptococcal trigger) and Kawasaki syndrome (history used to revise diagnostic criteria); Janzarik's analysis of 100 nineteenth-century case histories revealing diagnostic patterns; collaborative SIB-catatonia research identifying eight historical cases with catatonic features
  - Strength: Moderate—Kushner's work is peer-reviewed and replicated, but Shorter's own contributions (symptom pool, iron triangle) are more speculative; the anti-Engstrom characterization is opinion rather than data

- **Core claim**: Current DSM classification (especially "major depression" and "schizophrenia") is artifactual.
  - Evidence: Historical data show melancholia and non-melancholic depression have always been recognized as distinct; "schizophrenia" lumps multiple psychotic conditions into one category; diagnostic change (e.g., neurasthenia replaced by depressive neurosis) was driven by psychoanalytic ideology, not science
  - Strength: Moderate to strong—supported by clinical researchers (Parker, Taylor, Fink) but contested by DSM framers; the argument relies partly on Shorter's own research program

- **Core claim**: Psychiatrist-historians produce clinically useful work while PhD-historians often produce culturally insightful but clinically irrelevant work.
  - Evidence: Comparison of Janzarik's clinically engaged Heidelberg centennial volume with Engstrom's "militantly antipsychiatric" account of Imperial German psychiatry
  - Strength: Weak—this is an interpretive claim based on a single comparison; it is contradicted by Shorter's own praise of Howard Kushner (a PhD-historian, not a physician) as a pioneer of clinically engaged history

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Shorter valorizes psychiatrist-historians (Janzarik) over PhD-historians (Engstrom), yet praises Howard Kushner (a PhD-historian, not a physician) as a pioneer of clinically engaged history. This creates an unresolved tension: is the relevant distinction disciplinary background or specific methodological approach?
- Shorter advocates using history to inform the present, but acknowledges that past treatments were often "unwise" (ovariectomies, lobotomy). The selection criteria for which past wisdom to recover remain unclear.
- Shorter argues disease entities are "real" (realist position), yet acknowledges diagnostic change can be socially driven (neurasthenia → depressive neurosis). The boundary between real and socially-constructed diagnoses is not clearly defined.
- Shorter's characterization of Engstrom as "militantly antipsychiatric" is an ad hominem claim presented without direct evidence; it may reflect personal or professional conflict rather than scholarly analysis.
- The essay relies heavily on Shorter's own work (15+ self-citations), creating potential confirmation bias; his claims about having "completed the basic science courses of a rigorous medical program" are self-reported and unverified.

## Open Questions

- How should the field distinguish between diagnostic categories that are "real" (cutting nature at the joints) and those that are socially constructed? Shorter provides historical examples but no clear epistemological criterion.
- Can the "iron triangle" hypothesis (autism + catatonia + psychosis as a distinct subset) be validated through prospective clinical studies, or does it remain a speculative framework?
- To what extent can Shorter's critique of DSM classification be reconciled with the pragmatic goals of psychiatric epidemiology and clinical communication?
- Is the dichotomy between psychiatrist-historians and PhD-historians a useful heuristic, or does it obscure the possibility that both can contribute to clinical science (as Kushner's work suggests)?
- How should the history of psychiatry balance its clinical-science mission with its intellectual-history and cultural-history missions, given that Shorter's "presentism with a vengeance" may neglect historical context that is valuable for other scholarly purposes?
