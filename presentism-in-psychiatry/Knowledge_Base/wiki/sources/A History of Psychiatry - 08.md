# The Second Biological Psychiatry

## Summary
This source documents the mid-20th-century paradigm shift in psychiatry, termed the "Second Biological Psychiatry." This era marked the displacement of psychoanalysis and psychogenic theories—such as the "schizophrenogenic mother"—by a medical model viewing psychiatric illnesses as disorders of brain chemistry and genetic inheritance.

The transition was supported by two primary pillars: rigorous quantitative genetics and the advent of effective psychopharmacology. The author details the move from anecdotal evidence to controlled studies, specifically twin and adoption studies, to isolate nature from nurture. Simultaneously, the introduction of chlorpromazine and lithium transitioned the field from a "branch of social work" to a specialized medical discipline focused on neurotransmitters and chemical substrates.

The text highlights the role of pharmaceutical companies in the rapid adoption of these treatments and the tension between academic discovery and commercial interests. It concludes that while these developments did not "cure" the underlying brain disorders, they successfully abolished cardinal symptoms, allowing patients to lead more normal lives outside of institutions.

## Key Entities
- **German Research Institute for Psychiatry (Munich)** (type: org) — Intellectual ancestor of the second biological psychiatry; established early labs for inheritance patterns.
- **National Institute of Mental Heights (NIMH)** (type: org) — Shifted the trajectory of US psychiatric research from psychoanalysis toward basic science.
- **Rhône-Poulenc** (type: org) — French pharmaceutical company that synthesized chlorpromazine (4560 RP).
- **Smith Kline & French** (type: org) — Commercialized chlorpromazine as "Thorazine" in the United States.
- **Chlorpromazine (Thorazine)** (type: tool) — The first antipsychotic; triggered the "pharmacologic revolution" by abolishing psychotic symptoms.
- **Lithium** (type: tool) — An element proven effective for the treatment of mania, though its adoption was delayed by a lack of corporate backing.
- **Danish Adoption Study** (type: dataset) — A large-scale longitudinal study providing definitive evidence that biological kinship, not home environment, predicts schizophrenia.

## Key Concepts
- **The Second Biological Psychiatry** — A paradigm shift in the 1970s viewing mental illness as a result of disordered brain chemistry and development.
- **Monozygotic vs. Dizygotic Concordance** — The comparison of illness rates between identical and fraternal twins to determine the genetic contribution to a disorder.
- **Genetic Anticipation** — The theory that illness-causing genes expand in size across generations, increasing the severity of the disease (modern equivalent of "degeneration").
- **Neurotransmitter** — Chemicals (e.g., acetylcholine) that transmit impulses across synapses, forming the basis for chemical psychiatry.
- **Extrapyramidal Symptoms** — Involuntary muscle movements (e.g., tardive dyskinesia) occurring as side effects of antipsychotic medication.
- **Psychogenic** — The theory that mental illness arises from psychological factors, faulty parenting, or environmental stress.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Major psychiatric illnesses are primarily rooted in genetic and chemical substrates rather than environmental "psychogenic" causes.
  - Evidence: The [[Danish Adoption Study]] showed schizophrenic adoptees had biological relatives with the illness, while the adoptive environment showed no correlation. Twin studies by Rosanoff and Kallmann showed vastly higher concordance in monozygotic vs. dizygotic twins.
  - Strength: High; the transition from anecdotes to large-scale, quantitative, controlled data provided a robust empirical basis.
- **Core claim**: The "pharmacologic revolution" transformed the clinical reality of psychiatry.
  - Evidence: The rapid remission of psychotic symptoms in patients treated with chlorpromazine by Delay, Deniker, and Lehmann.
  - Strength: Very High; the observable "abolition" of symptoms led to mass deinstitutionalization and a shift toward medical specialization.
- **Core claim**: Commercial interests, rather than academic curiosity alone, drove the adoption of psychiatric drugs.
  - Evidence: Lithium's 20-year delay in the US due to its status as a natural substance without a corporate patent-holder, contrasted with the aggressive marketing of Thorazine by Smith Kline & French.
  - Strength: Medium; while industry played a role, the clinical efficacy was the primary catalyst.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Symptomatic vs. Curative**: A central tension exists between the "abolition" of symptoms by drugs (e.g., chlorpromazine) and the failure to "cure" the underlying biological disorder.
- **Industry vs. Science**: The delay of lithium adoption highlights a tension where scientific evidence of efficacy was ignored because it lacked a commercial "backer."
- **Ethics of Genetics**: The text notes the historical tension where early genetic methodologies were adopted by Nazi "racial hygiene" researchers, though it argues the methods (twin studies) were not intrinsically racist.
