# Desperate Remedies (Chapter Ten)

## Summary
This source examines the evolution of psychiatric treatment during and after World War I, focusing on the transition from custodial care to aggressive physical interventions. It analyzes how the phenomenon of "shell shock" forced a confrontation between theories of biological degeneracy and psychological trauma, often resulting in brutal "will-based" electrical treatments designed to return soldiers to the front.

The text further explores the "crisis of legitimacy" within psychiatry, where practitioners, envious of the success of the bacteriological revolution in general medicine, attempted to "medicalize" madness. This led to the rise of somatic therapies—ranging from Henry Cotton's surgical removal of organs to Wagner-Jauregg's malarial fever therapy and Sakel's insulin shock therapy. The author argues that these high-risk experiments were made possible by the total lack of agency and legal protection afforded to asylum patients.

## Key Entities
- **Craiglockhart Hospital** (org) — A facility where war poets like Wilfred Owen received sympathetic, Freudian-inflected treatment.
- **Queen Square, London** (org) — Site of authoritarian electrical treatments for shell shock.
- **Trenton State Hospital** (org) — Center for Henry Cotton's "surgical bacteriology" experiments.
- **Worcester State Hospital** (org) — Training ground for scientific psychiatry under Adolf Meyer.
- **Battle Creek Sanitarium** (org) — Institution supportive of Cotton's methods via John Harvey Kellogg.
- **Lichterfelde Hospital** (org) — Where Manfred Sakel first utilized insulin for addicts.
- **Harlem Valley State Hospital** (org) — Site where Sakel continued insulin shock therapy in the US.
- **Kaufmann cure** (tool) — A treatment combining painful electric shocks with military drills.
- **Torpillage** (tool) — Faradic electrical treatment used by Clovis Vincent.
- **Surgical Bacteriology** (tool) — The surgical removal of "foci of infection" (teeth, tonsils, colons) to treat mental illness.
- **Malarial Therapy** (tool) — Injection of malaria to induce high fevers to treat General Paralysis of the Insane (GPI).
- **Insulin Shock Therapy** (tool) — Use of high-dose insulin to induce hypoglycemic comas.
- **Metrazol / Camphor** (tool) — Chemicals used to induce convulsions to treat schizophrenia.

## Key Concepts
- **Shell Shock** — Mental breakdown caused by war trauma, oscillating between being viewed as a physical brain injury, psychological hysteria, or a sign of biological weakness.
- **Schreckneurose** — The German term for "terror neurosis," the equivalent of shell shock.
- **Degeneration Theory** — The belief that mental illness is a manifestation of innate biological or hereditary inferiority.
- **General Paralysis of the Insane (GPI)** — A late-stage neurosyphilitic condition that provided a rare example of a biologically "curable" psychiatric condition.
- **Focal Sepsis / Auto-intoxication** — The theory that chronic infections in the body produce toxins that poison the brain.
- **Somatic Treatment** — Physical interventions (shocks, surgeries, drugs) used to treat mental disorders, marking a move toward "hard" medicine.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Psychiatry's shift toward somatic "shocks" was driven more by a professional desire for medical legitimacy than by scientific evidence.
  - Evidence: The adoption of "surgical bacteriology" and "insulin shock" despite high mortality rates and lack of controlled trials.
  - Strength: Strong; the author cites specific clinical outcomes (e.g., Cotton's 45% mortality rate) and the professional suppression of these figures by figures like Adolf Meyer.
- **Core claim**: The "captive" status of asylum patients allowed for sadistic experimentation.
  - Evidence: The use of "torpillage" and forced colons removals on patients who had no legal or moral standing to refuse.
  - Strength: Strong; detailed accounts of the power imbalance between doctors and institutionalized patients.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Humanitarianism vs. Barbarism**: The tension between the stated goal of "curing" patients and the use of torture-like methods (electric shocks to testicles/tongues) to achieve it.
- **Heredity vs. Infection**: The conflict between the "Degeneration" school (illness is innate) and the "Bacteriological" school (illness is caused by external germs).

## Open Questions
- How did the medical community justify mortality rates as high as 45% in surgical bacteriology without facing systemic legal repercussions?
- To what extent did the "success" of malarial therapy influence the later, less scientific "shock" therapies of the 1930s?
