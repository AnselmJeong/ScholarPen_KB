# Madness in Civilization: Chapter Twelve - A Psychiatric Revolution?

## Summary
This chapter examines the mid-20th-century transition from mass confinement in state asylums to "community care," a process known as deinstitutionalization. The author analyzes the collapse of the asylum system across the Anglophone world and continental Europe, highlighting the stark contrast in timing and the eventual fate of the displaced mentally ill.

The core of the analysis challenges the "Drug Myth"—the widely held belief that the introduction of chlorpromazine (Thorazine) in the 1950s enabled the emptying of hospitals. Instead, the author argues that deinstitutionalization was a product of political and economic pressures, including the high cost of maintaining "total institutions" and the introduction of federal funding (Medicare/Medicaid) in the US that incentivized states to shift patients to cheaper, unregulated private care.

The author concludes that the "revolution" was largely a shell game. While some patients benefited, many with chronic illnesses were shifted from state hospitals to homelessness, squalid "board and care" homes, or the prison system, effectively returning to a state of "care in custody" similar to the pre-asylum era.

## Key Concepts
- **Deinstitutionalization** — The systemic discharge of patients from large state asylums toward community-based care.
- **Total Institutions** — Environments where all aspects of life (work, sleep, play) are conducted in the same place under a single authority, leading to dehumanization (via Erving Goffman).
- **Institutional Neurosis** — Mental deterioration and infantile behavior caused specifically by the structure of long-term confinement.
- **Anti-Psychiatry** — Intellectual movement (Szasz, Laing) that viewed mental illness as a social construct or a response to a "mad world" rather than a medical disease.
- **Care in Custody** — The phenomenon where the mentally ill are managed by the penal system (jails/prisons) instead of health services.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: The "Drug Myth" obscures the true causes of deinstitutionalization.
  - Evidence: Comparative data shows continental Europe had the same drugs but kept hospitals longer; studies in California showed Thorazine was associated with *longer* stays.
  - Strength: High; uses global comparative analysis to decouple drug availability from discharge rates.
- **Core claim**: Fiscal policy was the primary driver of US deinstitutionalization.
  - Evidence: Medicare/Medicaid paid for nursing homes but not state hospitals, allowing states to transfer costs to the federal government.
  - Strength: Strong; explains the specific timing and demographics (elderly first) of discharges.
- **Core claim**: The asylum's failure was structural, not financial.
  - Evidence: Drawing on Goffman's "total institutions," the author argues that no amount of funding could fix the inherent pathology of confinement.
  - Strength: Theoretical/Sociological; relies on the framework of "institutional neurosis."
