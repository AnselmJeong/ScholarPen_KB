# A Psychiatric Revolution? The End of Asylumdom

## Summary

This chapter examines the dramatic collapse of the asylum system in Western societies during the second half of the twentieth century, challenging the dominant narrative that psychotropic drugs like chlorpromazine (Thorazine) were the primary cause. The author argues that deinstitutionalization was instead driven by a confluence of social policy shifts, fiscal incentives, and ideological critiques of the mental hospital as a "total institution." The analysis draws on cross-national comparisons, historical data on hospital censuses, and policy documentation from the United Kingdom, United States, Italy, and other European countries.

The chapter methodically dismantles the technological determinist account by showing that hospital population declines began in some localities before drugs were introduced, that drug-treated patients sometimes had longer stays, and that continental European countries lagged decades behind Anglophone nations despite access to the same medications. Instead, the author identifies specific policy mechanisms—particularly the US Medicare/Medicaid programmes and Supplemental Security Income, which created incentives for states to transfer costs by discharging patients—as the primary drivers. In the UK, Enoch Powell's 1961 declaration that mental hospitals were "doomed institutions" and deliberate underfunding accelerated closures.

The chapter concludes with a sobering assessment of the consequences. Community care remained largely rhetorical and chronically underfunded, while discharged patients were frequently relocated to unregulated nursing homes, board-and-care facilities, or prisons—a pattern the author calls transinstitutionalization. The homeless mentally ill and the swelling population of psychotic inmates in jails stand as indictments of the policy's failure. The significance lies in reframing deinstitutionalization not as a medical triumph but as a fiscal and ideological transformation with deeply ambiguous outcomes.

## Key Entities

- **San Clemente Asylum (Venice)** (institution) — Former female asylum converted into a luxury hotel; emblematic of the erasure of asylum history.
- **Colney Hatch Asylum (Princess Park Manor)** (institution) — Second county lunatic asylum for Middlesex, converted into luxury apartments; its history as a mental hospital was actively concealed by developers.
- **Central State Hospital (Milledgeville, Georgia)** (institution) — Once the largest mental hospital in the world, housing 12,000 patients; now largely abandoned.
- **Trenton State Hospital (New Jersey)** (institution) — Site of Henry Cotton's focal sepsis experiments; now largely empty and decaying.
- **St Elizabeth's Hospital (Washington, DC)** (institution) — Federal mental hospital where Erving Goffman conducted fieldwork for *Asylums*.
- **National Institute of Mental Health (NIMH)** (organization) — Funded Goffman's Laboratory of Socio-Environmental Studies and his fieldwork at St Elizabeth's.
- **American Psychiatric Association (APA)** (organization) — Granted honorary membership to journalist Albert Deutsch for his exposés of asylum conditions.
- **Citizens Commission on Human Rights** (organization) — Anti-psychiatry group co-founded by Thomas Szasz and the Church of Scientology.
- **US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)** (organization) — Approved Thorazine for sale in 1954.
- **Thorazine/Chlorpromazine/Largactil** (tool) — First modern antipsychotic drug, introduced in 1954; the chapter argues its causal role in deinstitutionalization has been oversold.
- **Medicare/Medicaid** (policy program) — US federal programmes that created incentives for states to discharge patients by covering costs in private facilities.
- **Supplemental Security Income (SSI)** (policy program) — Extended federal benefits to the disabled, including those with mental disabilities, further accelerating discharges.
- **Legge 180 (Basaglia Law, Italy 1978)** (policy) — Italian law banning new admissions to traditional mental hospitals and construction of new ones; named after psychiatrist Franco Basaglia.

## Key Concepts

- **Deinstitutionalization** — The process of discharging patients from mental hospitals and closing asylums; the chapter argues this was driven by fiscal policy and ideological critique, not drugs.
- **Total institution** — Erving Goffman's concept designating places where work, sleep, and play occur in the same constricting environment; used to argue that mental hospitals were structurally, not just financially, damaging.
- **Institutional neurosis** — Russell Barton's term for the damaging psychological effects of long-term confinement; supported the anti-institutional critique.
- **Anti-psychiatry** — Movement (associated with Szasz and Laing) arguing that mental illness is a myth or a social product; provided ideological ammunition for deinstitutionalization.
- **Community care** — The policy of treating mentally ill persons outside hospitals in community settings; the chapter argues this remained largely rhetorical and underfunded.
- **Transinstitutionalization** — The movement of patients from mental hospitals to prisons, nursing homes, and homeless shelters; the chapter documents this as the hidden consequence of deinstitutionalization.
- **Focal sepsis theory** — Henry Cotton's discredited theory that mental illness was caused by hidden infections; provides historical context for the brutality of asylum medicine.
- **Psychopharmacological revolution** — The idea that drugs like Thorazine transformed psychiatry; the chapter seeks to debunk this as the primary cause of deinstitutionalization.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Deinstitutionalization was not primarily caused by psychotropic drugs but by social policy and fiscal incentives.
  - Evidence: Cross-national comparison showing continental Europe lagged decades behind Anglophone countries despite access to the same drugs; local declines in hospital populations predating 1954; the Brill and Patton (1957) admission that no quantitative correlation could be shown; a California study linking drug treatment to longer hospital stays.
  - Strength: Strongly supported by multiple lines of evidence, though the chapter acknowledges drugs did transform psychiatric practice in other ways.

- **Core claim**: US Medicare/Medicaid and SSI programmes created perverse incentives that drove deinstitutionalization.
  - Evidence: The elderly were discharged first (when federal dollars became available for nursing homes), followed by younger patients (when SSI extended benefits to the disabled); states realized they could transfer costs to the federal government.
  - Strength: Strong—explains the specific temporal pattern of discharges better than the drug hypothesis.

- **Core claim**: Community care was systematically underfunded and remained largely rhetorical.
  - Evidence: In 1973–74, the UK spent £300 million on institutional care versus £6.5 million on community services; official inquiries described community care as "a poor relation"; governments deliberately avoided systematic data collection.
  - Strength: Moderate—the chapter relies on scattered data points and acknowledges that some patients benefited.

- **Core claim**: Deinstitutionalization has produced new forms of confinement (transinstitutionalization).
  - Evidence: 15% of US state prisoners and 24% of jail inmates meet criteria for psychotic disorders; the Los Angeles County Jail houses the largest concentration of seriously mentally ill in the US; the UK prison population with mental illness rose seven-fold between the late 1980s and 2002.
  - Strength: Moderate—data is acknowledged as approximate and systematic studies were avoided by governments.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The chapter directly contradicts the widely accepted narrative (still common in psychiatry textbooks and pharmaceutical marketing) that Thorazine enabled deinstitutionalization—a major historiographical tension that may conflict with other sources in the wiki that assume a "psychopharmacological revolution."
- While arguing drugs were not the primary cause, the chapter acknowledges they "did nonetheless transform psychiatry, and broader cultural conceptions of madness besides"—creating an internal tension between rejecting causal primacy and recognizing significance.
- Italian deinstitutionalization (1978) occurred much later than Anglo-American, yet Italy's ban on new admissions was more absolute than the gradual run-down elsewhere—suggesting multiple pathways to deinstitutionalization, not simply a lag effect.
- The critique of community care as underfunded exists alongside the acknowledgement that some patients "unambiguously benefited," creating nuance that could be lost in a simplistic reading.
- Japan's continued high institutionalization is presented as an exception, but the explanations offered (stigma, family shame, cultural preferences) remain somewhat speculative and under-evidenced.
