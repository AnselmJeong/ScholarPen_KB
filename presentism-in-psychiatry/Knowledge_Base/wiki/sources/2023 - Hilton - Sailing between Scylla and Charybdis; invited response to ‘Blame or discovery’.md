# Sailing between Scylla and Charybdis; invited response to ‘Blame or discovery’

## Summary
This commentary by Claire Hilton responds to a critique by George Ikkos regarding the motivations and biases inherent in the history of psychiatry. Hilton addresses the tension between the desire for "fairness" toward practitioners and the academic drive for "discovery."

The author argues that historians of psychiatry face two primary perils, metaphorically described as Scylla and Charybdis. On one side are hagiographic representations—idealized accounts often produced by institutions and colleagues (such as *Festschriften* and overly positive obituaries) that erase flaws. On the other side are critical historical frameworks (citing Foucault and Scull) that frequently portray psychiatrists primarily as agents of oppression and self-interest.

Hilton asserts that the goal of the historian should be impartial understanding and objective explanation rather than the assignment of praise or blame. She suggests that by adhering to a rigorous, objective methodology, historians can actually protect the profession from unfair criticism by providing a truthful account of the past.

## Key Concepts
- **Hagiographic Representations** — The tendency in professional history to create idealized, "saint-like" accounts of figures, often seen in obituaries and commemorative volumes.
- **Critical Historical Analysis** — A school of historical thought (e.g., Foucault) that views psychiatric institutions as tools for social control and the oppression of the vulnerable.
- **Scylla and Charybdis (Metaphor)** — The precarious balance required to avoid both blind institutional praise and overly cynical systemic condemnation.
- **Historical Objectivity** — The methodological approach of treating "praise and blame" as by-products of research rather than the primary motivation.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Historians must avoid the binary of "hero vs. villain" to achieve a truthful understanding of psychiatric history.
  - Evidence: Contrasts the "nothing but good" approach of professional obituaries with the systemic critiques of Foucault, Scull, and Showalter.
  - Strength: Theoretical/Philosophical; based on historiographic observation rather than empirical data.

- **Core claim**: Objectivity is the best defense against unfair criticism.
  - Evidence: Argument that an impartial account based on discovery prevents the field from being unfairly maligned while still acknowledging failures.
  - Strength: Moderate; it relies on the premise that "truth" is discoverable and can be distinguished from "unfair blame."

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Institutional Position vs. Academic Independence**: There is an explicit tension between Hilton and Ikkos regarding whether Hilton's role within the Royal College of Psychiatrists colors her desire for "fairness," while Hilton maintains her critical drive predates her institutional role.
- **Fairness vs. Discovery**: The tension between the moral value of being "fair" to past actors and the academic requirement to uncover potentially damaging truths.
