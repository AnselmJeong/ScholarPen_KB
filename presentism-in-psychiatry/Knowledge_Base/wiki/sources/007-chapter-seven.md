# The Great Confinement

## Summary

This chapter traces the rise of the asylum as the dominant institutional response to mental disorder across Europe, North America, and European colonies during the nineteenth century. It draws on parliamentary inquiries, reformist reports, and the writings of key figures such as Esquirol, Pinel, Tuke, and Dorothea Dix to document the transition from family- and community-based care to state-funded asylum systems. The narrative is organized thematically, covering the exposure of madhouse horrors, the ideological content of moral treatment, the political campaigns that drove asylum construction, and the uneven global spread of the asylum model through imperialism.

The chapter's central finding is that the asylum movement was Janus-faced: it represented a genuine humanitarian advance over the brutal madhouses of the *ancien régime*, yet moral treatment functioned as a sophisticated technology of control. Reformers like Dix deliberately exaggerated cure rates—the "cult of curability"—to persuade legislatures to fund asylums, while superintendents such as W. A. F. Browne openly acknowledged that moral treatment aimed to extend "discipline and inspection...into the very dreams of the insane." Pinel himself insisted that kindness must be backed by "an imposing apparatus of repression."

The significance of this account lies in its global scope and its critical edge. The chapter shows that asylum-building was a mark of civilization in Western self-understanding, exported through settler colonialism, missionary work, and cultural emulation in places as diverse as Argentina, China, Japan, and Algeria. Yet it also reveals the persistent tension between medical and religious authority over asylum management, and the limited success of Western psychiatry in displacing indigenous healing traditions. The chapter thus complicates any simple narrative of progressive reform, positioning the asylum as both a therapeutic innovation and a instrument of social discipline.

## Key Concepts

- **Moral treatment** — A therapeutic approach emphasizing kindness, work, self-restraint, and a carefully designed environment rather than physical coercion; the chapter highlights both its humanitarian promise and its controlling, disciplinary function.
- **Great confinement** — The massive wave of institutionalization of the insane in asylums across Europe, North America, and globally from the early nineteenth century onward.
- **Cult of curability** — Exaggerated claims of 70–100% cure rates for recent cases made by early American asylum superintendents (e.g., William Awl) to justify asylum construction.
- **Nervous vs. mad** — A social distinction between milder nervous disorders (depression, ennui) and full-blown insanity (loss of reason); the wealthy preferred "nervous" labels to avoid stigma.
- **Moral entrepreneurship** — The activity of reformers like Dorothea Dix who campaign to change social norms, invent or embroider horror stories, and drive legislation to create institutions.
- **Imperial psychiatry** — The export of Western asylum models to colonies via settler states, administration, or missionaries; often coexisted uneasily with indigenous healing traditions.
- **Janus-faced moral treatment** — The insight that moral treatment combined kindness (*douceur*) with an "imposing apparatus of repression," making it both humane and a technology of control.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: The asylum movement was a global phenomenon driven by horror exposés and utopian promises, not by proven therapeutic efficacy.
  - Evidence: Detailed descriptions of abuses from Esquirol's report, Henry Alexander's testimony, Godfrey Higgins's account of York Asylum; Dix's fabricated stories.
  - Strength: High — extensive primary-source quotations; consistent across multiple countries and institutions.

- **Core claim**: Moral treatment was the ideological core of the new asylums, but it was inherently disciplinary and controlling, not simply humane.
  - Evidence: W. A. F. Browne's statement that control should "penetrate into the very dreams of the insane"; Pinel's acknowledgment of the need for an "appareil of repression"; Foucault's critique.
  - Strength: Moderate — relies on selected quotes; the chapter itself acknowledges the paradox.

- **Core claim**: Asylums became a mark of civilization and spread through imperialism, cultural emulation, and the work of moral entrepreneurs.
  - Evidence: Dix's rhetorical claim that asylums are "so general among all civilized and christianized nations"; Paget's quote calling the asylum "the most blessed manifestation of true civilization"; colonial examples from Canada, Australia, India, Algeria, China, Japan, Argentina.
  - Strength: Moderate — broad survey but limited depth on non-Western cases; acknowledges resistance from local populations.

- **Core claim**: Medical authority over the asylum was contested, especially by religious orders and lay administrators, and physicians had to justify their centrality.
  - Evidence: French tensions between *médecins aliénistes* and Catholic sisters; Pinel learning from lay keepers Pussin and Marguerite; the question of "why physicians were best placed to administer" moral treatment.
  - Strength: Strong — shows internal professional struggle documented across multiple settings.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- The chapter endorses the humanitarian narrative (reformers exposed horrors, built better institutions) while simultaneously arguing that moral treatment was a "gigantic moral imprisonment." This internal paradox is acknowledged but not fully resolved.
- If the wiki's existing [[concepts/moral-treatment]] page presents moral treatment as purely humane or as a benign precursor to modern psychosocial therapy, this chapter challenges that by foregrounding its disciplinary function and its use of control.
- The "cult of curability" is presented as propaganda that inflated cure rates to drive asylum construction, undercutting any simple narrative of asylum effectiveness.
