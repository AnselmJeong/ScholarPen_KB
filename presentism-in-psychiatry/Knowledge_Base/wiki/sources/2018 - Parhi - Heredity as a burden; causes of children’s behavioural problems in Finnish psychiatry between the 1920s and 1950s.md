# Heredity as a burden; causes of children’s behavioural problems in Finnish psychiatry between the 1920s and 1950s

## Summary

This article examines how Finnish child psychiatrists at Pitkäniemi Hospital interpreted the causes of children's severe behavioural problems from the 1920s to the 1950s, a period predating psychodynamic influences. Using patient records of approximately sixty children diagnosed with psychopathy, the author adopts a present-centred methodological lens by foregrounding socioeconomic factors—a question the historical actors themselves did not ask. The study reveals that despite routinely documenting details such as family background, parental occupations, and material deprivation, psychiatrists consistently interpreted these data through a hereditarian framework rather than as environmental causes.

The primary finding is that psychiatrists viewed psychopathy as an innate, permanent condition rooted in hereditary degeneration or constitutional defects. A child's improvement in the structured hospital environment served as a diagnostic test: those who responded were deemed "corrigible" and returned home or to foster care; those who did not were confirmed as constitutionally psychopathic and sent to reform schools. This circular logic reinforced biological explanations and obscured the role of socioeconomic adversity. The diagnosis was applied unevenly by class—working-class and underclass children predominated, while middle-class children were rarely sent to reform schools.

The significance of the article lies in its demonstration of how a present-centred research question can expose gaps between documented information and professional interpretation in historical psychiatry. It also contributes to historiographic debates about silence, agency, and the limits of archival inference, particularly regarding topics like sexual abuse which were systematically overlooked. By showing that socioeconomic factors were acknowledged but not causally integrated, Parhi challenges teleological narratives of progress in psychiatric knowledge and highlights the persistence of interpretive frameworks that can render obvious connections invisible.

## Key Entities

- **Pitkäniemi Hospital** (org) — Primary research site; housed Finland's first dedicated children's psychiatric ward from 1927, where the patient records analysed originate.
- **Mannerheim League for Child Welfare** (org) — Finnish-speaking NGO that promoted child guidance and mental hygiene, providing preventive care alongside hospital treatment.
- **Samfundet Folkhälsan** (org) — Swedish-language public health association in Finland; counterpart to the Mannerheim League for child guidance services.
- **Finnish National Board of Health** (org) — Government body whose mandated nosology shaped the diagnostic categories used in Pitkäniemi, including psychopathy.
- **Pitkäniemi Patient Records (National Archives, Hämeenlinna)** (dataset) — Primary source material; detailed case files of children diagnosed with psychopathy from the 1920s–1950s.
- **Torma & Vuoristo Follow-up Study (1950)** (dataset) — Outcome survey of 30 former child patients diagnosed with psychopathy, providing data on later adjustment.

## Key Concepts

- **Hereditarian explanation of child behavioural problems** — The assumption that severe behavioural problems in children stem primarily from hereditary or constitutional defects, not environmental adversity. This framework dominated Finnish child psychiatry in the study period, shaping diagnosis and institutional placement.
- **Constitutional psychopathy (Constitutio psychopathica)** — A permanent, innate form of psychopathy distinguished from temporary psychogenic reactions. The diagnosis encoded assumptions about corrigibility: children with constitutional psychopathy were seen as incapable of lasting improvement.
- **Present-centred selection criteria** — The methodological choice to use contemporary research questions (here, the role of socioeconomic factors) to guide historical inquiry, despite risks of presentism. Parhi defends this approach as productive for revealing gaps between documentation and interpretation.
- **Corrigibility as prognostic criterion** — The practical logic whereby a child's behavioural improvement in hospital determined whether they were deemed treatable (corrigible) or sent to reform school. Improvement confirmed a favourable environment; failure confirmed constitutional defect.
- **Silences in patient records** — The absence of discussion around sensitive topics such as sexual abuse, even when behavioural clues were present. Parhi treats these silences as meaningful interpretive choices that reflect the limits of the psychiatric framework.
- **Degeneration theory applied to children** — The view that psychopathy resulted from hereditary degeneration, detectable through family history of alcoholism, mental illness, or deviance. This concept transmitted degeneration theory from adult to child psychiatry.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Hereditarian explanations dominated Finnish child psychiatry in the 1920s–1950s; psychiatrists interpreted children's severe behavioural problems primarily as results of constitutional/hereditary defects, not socioeconomic conditions.
  - Evidence: Patient records show consistent documentation of parental pathology (alcoholism, mental illness, "abnormality") and family history of deviance, used to identify "hereditary taint." Socioeconomic details like poverty and family breakdown were recorded but not cited as causes.
  - Strength: Strong for the interpretive claim; supported by 60+ cases and a follow-up study. Weaker for causal claims about actual etiology.
- **Core claim**: The hospital environment functioned as a diagnostic test for corrigibility; failure to improve confirmed constitutional psychopathy, creating self-reinforcing circular reasoning.
  - Evidence: Multiple case examples where children who improved were sent home; those who did not were labelled permanently antisocial and sent to reform schools. The hospital was assumed a "good environment" by default.
  - Strength: Moderate; logic is clear from records but sample size is small and institutional perspective may bias documentation.
- **Core claim**: The diagnosis of psychopathy was applied unevenly by class—working-class and underclass children predominated, while middle-class children avoided reform school placement.
  - Evidence: Descriptions of parental occupations show mostly workers, farmers, maids. One middle-class boy's parents expressed distress at his placement; no middle-class children in the sample were sent to reform school.
  - Strength: Moderate; class patterns are suggestive but the study lacks systematic comparison or statistical analysis.
- **Core claim**: Sexual abuse of girls was systematically silenced; promiscuity was interpreted as innate moral defect rather than a possible response to abuse.
  - Evidence: Only one case file mentions sexual violence, and it is not pursued. Descriptions of "frantic eroticism" co-occur with fear of undressing, yet psychiatrists attributed behaviour to premature sexual drive.
  - Strength: Moderate; the argument rests on inference from silence and a single explicit mention. Supported by wider historical context (Erkkilä 1952).
- **Core claim**: Present-centred selection criteria can reveal historiographically valuable insights that the historical actors' own questions could not.
  - Evidence: By asking about socioeconomic factors, Parhi exposes a gap between what psychiatrists documented and how they interpreted it—a gap invisible within the hereditarian framework.
  - Strength: Strong as a methodological argument; the article itself demonstrates the payoff of this approach.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- **Internal tension between documentation and interpretation**: Psychiatrists recorded socioeconomic details extensively (poverty, family breakdown, single parenthood) but never used them as causal explanations. This gap is the article's central finding but raises the question of whether it reflects absence of causal thinking or genre conventions of record-keeping.
- **Circular logic of the hospital as "good environment"**: The assumption that hospital was a neutral or beneficial environment meant failure to improve confirmed constitutional pathology. This logic was not recognised at the time, creating a self-reinforcing diagnostic system.
- **Class awareness without class analysis**: Psychiatrists implicitly distinguished working-class from middle-class children through placement decisions (reform school vs. home) but did not theorise class as a causal factor, even when documenting class-linked adversities.
- **Tension with anti-presentist historiography**: The article explicitly defends present-centred selection criteria, directly challenging the strong anti-presentism of sources like Stocking (1965). This creates a methodological tension within the wiki that should be held open rather than resolved.
- **Limited generalisability**: The study focuses on a single institution in Finland; findings may not extend to other countries, time periods, or diagnostic categories. The author acknowledges this but does not systematically compare with child psychiatry in Germany, the UK, or Scandinavia.

## Open Questions

- How much did class bias affect diagnosis and placement at Pitkäniemi, and to what extent was this implicit vs. conscious?
- What would a systematic comparison of Pitkäniemi with similar institutions in other Nordic countries reveal about the role of hereditarian thinking in child psychiatry?
- Can the "silences" methodology be extended to systematically recover children's experiences from patient records, or is it limited to identifying what the institution chose not to discuss?
- How did the gradual shift away from the psychopathy diagnosis in the 1950s relate to changes in socioeconomic interpretation, and was this shift driven by evidence, professional pressures, or broader social changes?
- Does Parhi's defence of present-centred criteria apply only to cases where the historical actors overlooked a factor, or is it a general methodological stance that could be applied more broadly? What are its limits?
- How did the class-stratified placement patterns documented here compare to patterns in reform schools, children's homes, and other institutions beyond the hospital? Was the hospital simply reinforcing existing class hierarchies?
