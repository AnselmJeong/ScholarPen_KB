# Heredity as a burden; causes of children’s behavioural problems in Finnish psychiatry between the 1920s and 1950s

## Summary
This study examines the conceptualization of children's behavioral problems in Finland from the 1920s to the 1950s, focusing on the transition from biological to psychodynamic perspectives. The author analyzes patient records from the children's ward at Pitkäniemi Hospital, specifically targeting children diagnosed with "psychopathy." The research utilizes a "present-centered" historical approach to identify the gap between the socioeconomic realities of the patients (poverty, instability) and the clinical interpretations of the psychiatrists.

The findings reveal a dominance of biological psychiatry, where behavioral deviance was viewed as an immutable hereditary "burden" or constitutional flaw. While psychiatrists recorded socioeconomic data, they rarely treated it as a cause, instead viewing environmental stressors only as catalysts that triggered an existing innate pathology. The study highlights a systematic "silence" in records regarding child sexual abuse, where girls' "promiscuity" was medicalized as a psychopathic trait rather than a reaction to trauma.

The research is significant for illustrating how medical diagnoses like psychopathy functioned as mechanisms of social control, particularly for working-class children. It demonstrates how the hospital environment served as a diagnostic tool: children who did not improve in a "curative" structured environment were deemed "incorrigible" and often diverted to reform schools.

## Key Concepts
- **Psychopathy (Constitutio psychopathica / Degeneratio psychopathica)** — A broad diagnostic category for children with severe behavioral problems, viewed as a permanent, innate, and hereditary character defect.
- **Biological Psychiatry** — A medical approach prioritizing hereditary factors, physical constitution, and nervous system abnormalities over social or psychological drivers.
- **Presentism (in Historical Research)** — The methodological approach of asking questions relevant to the present (e.g., socioeconomic determinants) to uncover what contemporaries ignored.
- **Mental Hygiene** — Preventive psychiatric care focused on early intervention to avoid the need for institutionalization.
- **Degeneration** — The theory that mental and physical defects are passed down and worsen across generations, providing a biological justification for "tainted" lineages.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Hereditary factors were prioritized over environmental causes in the diagnosis of behavioral problems.
  - Evidence: Patient records documented poverty and parental alcoholism but framed these as background details rather than causes of the pathology.
  - Strength: Strong, based on a direct contrast between clinician notes and later patient self-reports.
- **Core claim**: The hospital environment acted as a litmus test for "corrigibility."
  - Evidence: Children who improved in the ward were seen as having a manageable condition; those who did not were labeled "permanently antisocial" and sent to reform schools.
  - Strength: Well-supported by the administrative flow between the hospital and reform schools.
- **Core claim**: Medicalization was used to obscure child sexual abuse, particularly in girls.
  - Evidence: "Promiscuous" or "erotic" behavior in young girls was diagnosed as a "sick-minded" psychopathic drive rather than a result of abuse.
  - Strength: Based on "silences" in the archives; high inferential value but limited by the lack of explicit records.

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Environmental vs. Hereditary Tension**: A contradiction exists between the *recorded* causes (heredity) and the *experienced* causes (poverty, death of parents) as reported by former patients in follow-up surveys.
- **Clinical Shift**: Tension between the "old" biological school (represented by Kalpa) and the "new" psychodynamic influences (introduced by Vuoristo) in the late 1940s.

## Open Questions
- Does the Finnish pattern of ignoring socioeconomic factors in favor of hereditary "burden" mirror the psychiatric practices of other Nordic or European countries during the interwar period?
- To what extent did the shift from "Degeneratio" to "Constitutio" in the 1930s change the legal or institutional treatment of children?
