# The Fate of the Dead

## Summary

This doctoral dissertation in political theory examines the moral relationship between living people and the dead perpetrators of historical injustice. Hoerger addresses a growing objection to redress efforts (e.g., statue removal, reparations, curriculum revision): that such actions harm or slander past wrongdoers. The central thesis is that redress does not harm past perpetrators but rather redeems them. By drawing on Walter Benjamin’s concept of "weak messianic power" and Saidiya Hartman’s work on harming the dead, Hoerger argues that living people can alter the moral significance of past actions through present-day efforts at correction.

The study is primarily philosophical and conceptual, with historical and genealogical components. Hoerger traces the term "historical injustice" from its 19th-century usage (slander against individuals) to its contemporary meaning (past violence against peoples), and situates the rise of historical-injustice discourse within what he calls the "Guilted Age" (post-WWII era of apologies, reparations, and commemoration). He systematically refutes deflationary accounts offered by critics such as John Torpey (collapse of progress) and Wilfred McClay (incomplete secularization), proposing instead that multiple legitimate factors—heightened moral awareness, trauma theory, path dependence, and human rights discourse—explain the turn to the past.

Hoerger's most distinctive contribution is the concept of "left conservatism," a political stance that combines attachment to historical figures (including flawed ones) with a commitment to redressing their wrongs. He argues that if past perpetrators could know what the living now know, they would choose for the living to redress their actions rather than preserve an unjust legacy. This framework aims to show that those who care about past perpetrators have a powerful reason to support, rather than oppose, redress measures. The dissertation engages seriously with counterarguments but remains primarily normative rather than empirical, leaving open important questions about application to specific historical contexts.

## Key Concepts

- **Historical Injustice** — Two definitions: (1) slander against historical figures (older usage); (2) past acts of violence or exploitation inflicted on entire peoples (contemporary usage). The shift from first to second meaning underpins the dissertation's entire argument.
- **Redemption** — The possibility that living people can alter the moral significance of past suffering or wrongdoing through present action. Hoerger argues past perpetrators are redeemable through redress, not despite it.
- **Weak Messianic Power** — Walter Benjamin's concept that the living have a limited capacity to "help" or "save" the dead by attending to their suffering and memory. Foundational for Hoerger's theory of how present actions affect past moral meaning.
- **Guilted Age** — Term for the post-WWII era characterized by increased efforts to address historical injustice (reparations, apologies, monuments, curriculum changes). Hoerger critically examines both its label and its explanatory factors.
- **Left Conservatism** — Hoerger's own term for a political orientation that balances attachment to past figures (including flawed ones) with progressive efforts to redress their wrongs, reconciling respect for tradition with transformative justice.
- **Path Dependence** — Social-science theory that "history matters" in shaping present political, economic, and social structures. Hoerger uses it as one legitimate explanation for the rise of historical-injustice discourse.
- **Trauma Theory** — Idea that terrible past events shape future mental states and actions at both individual and collective levels. Another factor Hoerger cites to legitimate, rather than deflate, concern with historical injustice.
- **Secularization Thesis (McClay's Version)** — Argument that incomplete secularization leaves Western societies with Judeo-Christian guilt reflexes but without the metaphysical framework to manage them. Hoerger critiques this as a deflationary account.

## Main Arguments & Findings

- **Core claim**: Past perpetrators of historical injustice are redeemable — but this demands redressing their wrongs, not preserving the status quo.
  - Evidence: Philosophical argumentation from Benjamin (weak messianic power) and Hartman (harming the dead); conceptual genealogy of "historical injustice"; historical analysis of the Guilted Age with seven explanatory factors; engagement with counterarguments from Torpey, McClay, Epstein, and Waldron.
  - Strength: Logically coherent and seriously engages objections, but primarily normative rather than empirical; depends on accepting the Benjaminian framework about helping/harming the dead; historical claims rely on plausible synthesis rather than systematic data.

- **Core claim**: The worry that redress efforts (statue removal, curriculum revision, reparations) harm past perpetrators is unfounded.
  - Evidence: If living people can help or harm dead victims (as Benjamin and Hartman argue for victims), the same logic applied to perpetrators does not protect them from critique — it demands redress. Counterfactual: if past perpetrators knew what the living now know, they would choose for the living to redress their actions.
  - Strength: Strong logical extension of existing philosophical commitments; vulnerable to epistemic objections about knowing what historical figures would have wanted.

- **Core claim**: "Left conservatism" offers an underappreciated political stance that reconciles attachment to the past with transformative justice.
  - Evidence: Synthesized from the overall argument; applied to show that those who care about past perpetrators have a powerful reason to support redress. Connects to "never again" normativity (Chapter VI) to align perpetrator redemption with victim attention.
  - Strength: Conceptually novel but abstract; not tested against concrete historical cases. Remains a suggestion rather than a fully worked-out methodology.

Both see moral engagement with the past as possible without "canceling" historical figures.

## Contradictions & Tensions

- Internal tension: Hoerger's argument depends on a counterfactual — that past perpetrators, if they knew what the living now know, would choose redress. This is epistemically fraught; we cannot know what historical figures would think if transported into our moral framework. Hoerger acknowledges but does not fully resolve this.
- Internal tension: The scope of "redemption" remains unclear. Does it apply to all perpetrators? Those who committed genocide? What about those who died unrepentant? The dissertation sets a general principle but does not specify boundaries.
- Internal tension: The focus on perpetrators risks being perpetrator-centric. Hoerger attempts to use "never again" (Chapter VI) to align perpetrator redemption with victim attention, but the balance remains precarious.
- Tension with existing wiki sources: Sources emphasizing contextual understanding (e.g., Hilton 2023, Houston 2014) warn against presentism; Hoerger's project explicitly uses present moral frameworks. However, his "left conservatism" offers a middle path not yet captured in the wiki.

## Open Questions

- Can Hoerger's redemption framework be applied to specific psychiatric controversies (e.g., how should we relate to eugenicist psychiatrists of the early 20th century)? The wiki's existing sources (Lepping/Poole, Fryxell, Kendler) provide test cases.
- Does "left conservatism" offer a workable methodological stance for historians of psychiatry, or does it remain too abstract for practical historiography?
- How does Hoerger's project relate to the "new history of psychology" debates (Brock 2016, Watrin 2017) about presentism and historicism?
- What implications does Hoerger's argument have for the question of whom we memorialize and how — a question directly relevant to ongoing controversies in psychiatric history (e.g., statues of Emil Kraepelin, naming of hospitals after eugenicists)?
