# The Fate of the Dead

## Summary
This doctoral dissertation investigates the moral and political implications of redressing historical injustices, specifically focusing on the perceived harm such actions (e.g., removing statues, renaming buildings) cause to the perpetrators of those injustices. Hoerger challenges the "Right-wing" narrative that these measures "defame heroes" or "erase history," arguing instead that if past perpetrators are viewed as redeemable, redress is the only way to actually assist them by aligning their legacy with current moral truths.

The research employs a multi-disciplinary approach, combining linguistic analysis via the Google Books archive to trace the evolution of the term "historical injustice," theoretical synthesis of political philosophy, and a historical survey of the post-WWII "Guilted Age." The author bridges the gap between the desires of victims to seek justice and the attachments of the living to past figures.

The primary finding is the proposal of a "politics of redemption." By leveraging Walter Benjamin's concept of "weak messianic power," Hoerger argues that the living can alter the "web of meaning" surrounding the dead. This leads to the conceptualization of "Left Conservatism," a framework where the living maintain an attachment to the past but seek to conserve it by actively correcting its injustices.

## Key Concepts
- **Historical Injustice** — Evolved from a biographical meaning (mischaracterization/slander of an individual) to a systemic meaning (violence/exploitation of entire groups).
- **The Guilted Age** — The post-WWII era characterized by a proliferation of official apologies, reparations, and memory politics.
- **Weak Messianic Power** — A theoretical capacity of the living to "help" the dead by changing the significance of their suffering or errors through remembrance and action.
- **Politics of Redemption** — A framework where redressing a perpetrator's wrongs is viewed not as an attack, but as a way to redeem the perpetrator's legacy.
- **Left Conservatism** — A philosophical stance that supports redress of the past while maintaining a conservative attachment to historical figures and structures.
- **Path Dependence** — The theory that current political and social structures are fundamentally shaped by original historical trajectories and injustices.

## Main Arguments & Findings
- **Core claim**: Redress efforts enhance rather than threaten the legacy of past perpetrators.
  - Evidence: Theoretical synthesis of Benjamin's messianism and Hartman's harm theory; linguistic evidence showing "historical injustice" once meant "correcting the record" for the dead.
  - Strength: Logically rigorous; provides a novel philosophical alternative to the "erasure" vs. "justice" dichotomy.
- **Core claim**: The rise of the "Guilted Age" is a legitimate moral evolution rather than a psychological pathology.
  - Evidence: Synthesis of factors including heightened moral awareness, path dependency, human rights discourse, and the "collapse of the future" (Torpey).
  - Strength: Strong, though acknowledges internal tensions between different drivers (e.g., guilt vs. hope).

## Contradictions & Tensions
- **Guilt vs. Action**: The author notes a tension where "guilt" is often the driver of the Guilted Age but is an unreliable reactive attitude that can lead to counterproductive "virtue signaling."
- **External Conflict**: Directly contradicts Wilfred McClay's view that the "Guilted Age" is a symptom of failed secularization and a "Manichean culture of hyper-victimization."
- **Justice vs. Redress**: Notes that for some mass atrocities ("evil beyond repair"), traditional "justice" may be impossible, necessitating a broader category of "redress."

## Open Questions
- How does the "Left Conservatism" framework apply to "unredeemable" perpetrators—those who would actively oppose the redress of their actions even with modern knowledge?
