# John Conolly and the Ambiguity of Reform: Between Humanitarianism and Surveillance

## 1. The Paradox of a Psychiatric Hero

John Conolly (1794–1866) occupies an uneasy position in the historiography of psychiatry. To his Victorian contemporaries, he was nothing less than a secular saint: the man who abolished mechanical restraint at Hanwell Asylum, completing the work begun by Philippe Pinel and William Tuke. Lord Shaftesbury, the era's most prominent lunacy reform advocate, called Conolly's work "the greatest triumph of skill and humanity" the world had ever known (Scull, 1984, p. 240). Sir James Crichton-Browne went further, asserting that "no member of his profession—except Jenner and Lister—has done a tithe as much as he to ward off and alleviate human suffering" (quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 240). A marble bust was commissioned, Oxford awarded him an honorary doctorate, and his colleagues presented him with allegorical silver plate two feet high, valued at £500 — a sum exceeding an ordinary laborer's lifetime earnings.

Yet contemporary scholarship has all but inverted this judgment. Conolly has been reinterpreted not as a liberator but as an architect of more sophisticated control. The non-restraint system, in this reading, did not free patients; it merely replaced visible chains with invisible ones — seclusion, constant surveillance, architectural manipulation, and the disciplinary apparatus of moral management. Conolly becomes a transitional figure in the genealogy of psychiatric power: not the man who humanized the asylum, but the man who perfected its techniques of subjection.

This chapter argues that both positions, taken alone, are inadequate. The Victorian hagiography erased the ambiguities and appropriations that marked Conolly's career; the Foucauldian critique, conversely, strips his actions of their historical context, reducing a situated reformer to a symbolic perpetrator. What is needed — and what Conolly's case exemplifies — is a reading that neither excuses nor condemns, but instead reconstructs the problem-space within which he operated: the suffering he confronted, the institutional alternatives available, and the limits of what even a reform-minded alienist could imagine and achieve in early Victorian England.

## 2. The Making of a Reluctant Alienist

Conolly was born in 1794 in Market Rasen, Lincolnshire, to a genteel but impoverished Irish family. His father "died young" leaving his wife with three children (Scull, 1984, p. 241). At the age of five, Conolly was boarded out with an elderly widow in the decaying borough of Hedon — an experience he later described as "barren" and "wretched," a descent from "even a shabby gentility" into surroundings "where nothing was tasteful, and nothing was beautiful, and nothing was cheerful" (Conolly, quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 241). This early fall in status, as Scull (1984) observes, left Conolly with a lifelong anxiety about his gentlemanly standing — a need for recognition that would shape his professional choices in significant ways.

His path to psychiatry was circuitous. After a brief stint as an ensign in the Cambridgeshire militia during the Napoleonic Wars, he studied medicine at Glasgow and Edinburgh, receiving his M.D. in 1821 with a dissertation on mental disorders. A visit to the Glasgow Lunatic Asylum during his student years proved formative: "The impression made in that and several other visits by the conversation of the patients, and by the several forms and degrees of eccentricity and unreason there witnessed, has never been effaced" (Conolly, 1860, quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 243).

But Conolly's early career was a chronicle of failure. He lacked the means to purchase an established practice and had no family connections to metropolitan medical elites. His attempts to establish himself as a general practitioner in Chichester and then Stratford-upon-Avon produced only the most modest results, his best year yielding an income "not to have exceeded 400 pounds" (Scull, 1984, p. 245). As Maudsley (1866, p. 172) later observed, Conolly was a poor practical physician, "with little talent or ability to inspire confidence in the exact investigation of disease." He had little faith in medicines and "the actual practice of his profession was not agreeable to him."

An unexpected appointment as professor of medicine at the newly founded University of London in 1827 seemed to promise escape from provincial obscurity. But within three years, Conolly was drowning in debt, quarrelling with administrators, failing to attract private patients despite aristocratic patronage from Lords Russell, Auckland, and Brougham, and ultimately forced to resign in humiliation (Scull, 1984, pp. 248–256). He returned to Warwickshire — "a very rustic physician," as he wrote to a friend, "buried" (Conolly to Coates, 1834, quoted in Scull, 1988).

It was in this context — desperate, past forty, his career a "suicide of reputation," as Maudsley (1866) put it — that Conolly applied for the superintendency of the Middlesex County Lunatic Asylum at Hanwell in 1838. His first application was rejected, apparently because of his progressive politics (Scull, 1988). The second, the following year, succeeded — ironically, because the asylum had descended into chaos under his predecessor, J.R. Millingen, whose inexperience and quarrelsome disposition had brought conditions to "the verge of anarchy" (Scull, 1984, p. 262).

## 3. The Non-Restraint System: Achievement and Appropriation

At Hanwell, Conolly unexpectedly found his métier. Within weeks, the magistrates reported that "a remarkable change for the better had already taken place in the discipline and order of the establishment" (Scull, 1984, p. 264). The man who had failed at almost everything else turned out to be an able and effective administrator of what was already England's largest and most visible asylum.

His signature innovation was the policy of total non-restraint. Beginning with his first report, Conolly asserted "that the management of a large asylum is not only practicable without the application of bodily coercion to the patient, but that, after the total disuse of such a method of control, the whole character of the asylum undergoes a gradual and beneficial change" (quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 265). Restraint, he argued, was not merely unnecessary but actively harmful: "restraints and neglect, may be considered as synonymous" (Conolly, 1856, p. 323). In their place, Hanwell relied "wholly upon constant superintendence, constant kindness, and firmness when required."

The effects, by contemporary accounts, were dramatic. The *Times* devoted close and sympathetic attention to the experiment over a four-year period, reporting on New Year's festivities where "the utmost tranquility prevailed" among 400 assembled patients (quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 267). The Duke of Cambridge visited and proclaimed himself "highly delighted" (Scull, 1984, p. 268). Samuel Tuke, the venerable author of the English version of moral treatment, bestowed his benediction. Within a few years, non-restraint became the ruling orthodoxy of British asylumdom.

Two facts, however, complicate this triumphalist narrative. The first concerns intellectual property. Non-restraint was not Conolly's invention. It was introduced by Robert Gardiner Hill, a twenty-four-year-old house surgeon at the provincial Lincoln Asylum, who had announced the system in a public lecture in 1838 — two years before Conolly assumed his duties — with the bold claim: "I wish to complete that which Pinel began. I assert then in plain and distinct terms, that in a properly constructed building, with a sufficient number of suitable attendants, restraint is never necessary, never justifiable, and always injurious, in all cases of lunacy whatever" (Hill, 1839, quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 269). It was, as Conolly himself periodically acknowledged, a visit to Lincoln that prompted him to try the new system (Scull, 1988).

Yet Hill received none of the recognition accorded to Conolly. Forced to resign from Lincoln amid institutional machinations, unable to obtain another asylum post — he was rejected even for a position under Conolly at Hanwell — he was thrust into general practice by default (Scull, 1984, p. 270). His subsequent attempts to reclaim priority proved counterproductive. As Scull (1988) notes, Hill's "shrill and strident claims of priority, his wearisome marshaling of minutiae" were "not only boring, but repellent." He proved chronically unable to grasp that one who exhibited "such boorish and ungentlemanly qualities could never hope to be accorded a place of honour in a profession desperate to dissociate itself from all that smacked of lower-class, tradesmanlike behaviour" (Scull, 1984, p. 271).

Conolly's own conduct in this affair was less than admirable. With whatever motives, "he consistently declined to give Hill his due. That he had borrowed the idea of non-restraint from Lincoln he could not deny; that the discovery was Hill's he sought constantly to obscure" (Scull, 1984, p. 271). This was not innocent oversight but sustained evasion.

The second complication concerns the substance of what Conolly achieved — or, more precisely, what the abolition of mechanical restraint actually meant for patients. Here, later scholarship has been far less generous than Victorian opinion.

## 4. The Foucauldian Critique: Liberation or More Refined Control?

The most influential challenge to the heroic narrative of non-restraint comes from historians working within a broadly Foucauldian framework. Their central contention is that the elimination of chains, straitjackets, and leg-locks did not constitute liberation; it constituted a shift from one mode of control to another — more subtle, more pervasive, and arguably more effective precisely because it was less visible.

As Lisa Topp's research has documented, seclusion and solitary confinement became prominent substitutes for mechanical restraint in the post-Conolly asylum. These practices raised concerns even among contemporaries, who recognized their resemblance to the solitary confinement regimes deployed in prisons. The transparency that mechanical restraint had afforded — a patient in chains was visibly restrained — was replaced by the opacity of the seclusion room, behind whose closed door no independent observer could verify what occurred (Topp, referenced in paper_plan).

The architectural dimension of control is similarly significant. The Victorian asylum was not merely a container for non-restrained patients; it was itself a technology of management. Corridor layouts, ward classifications, the positioning of attendants' stations, the regulation of movement between spaces — all these constituted what Markus (1993) has termed a "spatial syntax of power." Conolly's patients may have been free of mechanical bonds, but they were embedded in an environment designed to govern their conduct at every point.

Most pervasive of all was the system of moral management itself — "constant superintendence," in Conolly's phrase. This required a staff trained to observe, report, and intervene; a regime in which every patient action, every mood, every interaction fell under the gaze of attendants who were themselves supervised by medical officers who answered to the superintendent. Foucault's (1975) analysis of the disciplinary society, with its emphasis on surveillance, normalization, and the production of docile bodies, finds in the post-Conolly asylum an almost paradigmatic case.

From this perspective, Conolly appears not as a humanitarian hero but as a transitional figure in the genealogy of psychiatric power: the man who, under the banner of reform, perfected techniques that would define the asylum for the next century. The non-restraint system was, in this reading, an Orwellian apparatus avant la lettre — the abolition of visible coercion enabling the installation of a more comprehensive, because less resistible, regime of control.

## 5. Between Presentism and Historicism: Reconstructing the Problem-Space

The Foucauldian critique illuminates real structures of power that the Victorian narrative of humanitarian progress obscured. It is a necessary corrective. Yet when applied without historical discrimination, it produces its own distortions — a presentism that judges past actors by standards, conceptual frameworks, and institutional possibilities that did not exist in their time.

To assess Conolly historically, we must reconstruct the problem-space he inhabited. What was the actual condition of the insane in England in the 1830s? The parliamentary inquiries of 1807 and 1815 had documented conditions of "appalling degradation, inhuman treatment and neglect" in both public and private institutions (Dubois, 2023). In private madhouses, patients were "transmitted, like stock-in-trade, from one member of a family to another" — a "free trade in lunacy" that attracted, besides a few "respectable, well-educated, and humane individuals," the "ignorant and ill-educated" and those "capable of no feeling but a desire for wealth" (Conolly, 1830, pp. 13–14). In workhouses, parish authorities regularly detained the mentally ill in conditions indistinguishable from those of ordinary paupers — despite explicit legal prohibitions (Dubois, 2023).

Physical restraint, when Conolly entered Hanwell, was not an abstract disciplinary technique. It was a concrete, brutal, and ubiquitous practice. Chains, manacles, iron collars, straitjackets, coercion chairs, and leg-locks were standard equipment. Samuel Tuke, the very founder of moral treatment, had declared in 1813 that he could not "anticipate that the most enlightened and ingenious humanity, will ever be able entirely to supersede the necessity of personal restraint" (Tuke, 1813, p. 163). This was the consensus of the most progressive opinion.

What were Conolly's alternatives? He could maintain the existing system — chains and straitjackets, with their attendant brutalities — or he could attempt to abolish them. There was no third option in which patients would be released from the asylum altogether, for this was the era in which institutional confinement was understood, by reformers and conservatives alike, as the only viable framework for managing the mentally ill. Even Conolly's own earlier critique of the asylum, in his 1830 *Inquiry Concerning the Indications of Insanity*, had not proposed abolition; it had argued, rather, for more discriminating admission criteria and for domiciliary care of milder cases — positions that, as Scull (1984) documents, were simply ignored by the profession.

The question, then, is not whether the non-restraint system was free of coercive elements — it plainly was not — but whether it represented, in its historical context, a genuine improvement over what it replaced. On this point, even Scull (1988), whose analysis of Conolly is far from hagiographic, acknowledges the significance of the reform. The replacement of chains with supervision, of straitjackets with seclusion, was not liberation in any absolute sense. But it was a meaningful reduction in the most directly brutal forms of control then practiced. To dismiss it as mere "repackaging" is to apply a standard of purity that no historical reform could meet — and that our own era's psychiatric practices would equally fail.

## 6. The Trajectory of a Career: Principle and Interest

No historically honest assessment of Conolly can ignore the uncomfortable correspondence between his intellectual positions and his material circumstances. Scull (1984, 1988) documents this trajectory with precision. In 1830, as an outsider critic of the asylum system, Conolly denounced indiscriminate confinement and argued that "complete restraint is very rarely required" (Conolly, 1830, p. 6). A decade later, as superintendent of Hanwell, he became the most effective proselyte for the expansion of the county asylum system. By the 1850s, having left public employment and entered the private "trade in lunacy" — operating his own licensed houses at Lawn House, Wood End, and Hayes Park — he produced a Panglossian defense of private asylums that contradicted his earlier denunciations of profit-driven madhouse care.

The irony is sharp. Conolly had once argued that "every lunatic asylum should be the property of the State, and should be controlled by public officers" (Conolly, 1830). He ended his career as exactly the kind of private proprietor he had anathematized. His earlier insistence on the need for careful discrimination in certifying lunacy — to prevent wrongful confinement — gave way to an extraordinarily expansive definition of who belonged in an asylum: not only the dangerous but those exhibiting "excessive eccentricity," "perversions of the moral feelings," or conduct "inconsistent with the comfort of society" (Conolly, 1849, quoted in Scull, 1984, p. 275).

When Conolly himself remarked, in his early career, that "when men's interests depend upon an opinion, it is too much to expect that opinion always to be cautiously formed, or even in all cases honestly given" (Conolly, 1830, p. 14), he unwittingly supplied the epigraph for his own intellectual biography. His contemporary, Sir John Charles Bucknill — whose intellectual development ran in precisely the opposite direction, from enthusiastic advocacy to scathing critique of the asylum system — lamented that "advancing years and personal interests had made [Conolly] indulgent to the evils he had denounced" (Bucknill, quoted in Scull, 1988, p. 69).

Charles Reade's best-selling novel *Hard Cash* (1863) delivered an even harsher verdict, satirizing Conolly — thinly disguised as the bumbling Dr. Wycherly — as the epitome of psychiatric venality: a man who received commissions from asylum keepers for every patient he certified, who "found facts on theories instead of theories on facts," and whose "vast benevolence of manner" concealed a "second-rate mind blinded by self-interest" (Reade, 1863, quoted in Scull, 1984, pp. 276–278).

These criticisms have merit. Conolly was not a disinterested scientist whose views evolved purely through the accumulation of clinical experience. His positions tracked his interests with a fidelity that strains any charitable interpretation.

But the recognition of this fact need not — and should not — entail the dismissal of every position he held on the grounds of their suspect origins. The non-restraint policy at Hanwell, regardless of Conolly's subsequent compromises, represented a genuine advance in the treatment of the institutionalized insane. The fact that its author later abandoned his principles does not retroactively nullify what was achieved. Historical judgment requires the capacity to hold these contraries in view simultaneously: that Conolly's career exhibits a pattern of self-interested ideological adjustment, and that his most significant reform, whatever its limitations, reduced the sum of physical brutality in the institutions under his charge.

## 7. Implications: What Conolly's Case Teaches About Reading Psychiatric History

Conolly's case illuminates a larger methodological problem in the historiography of psychiatry: the tendency toward moral binary — liberator or oppressor, reformer or perpetrator — that flattens the complexity of historical actors who were, in most instances, neither.

The Foucauldian critique has performed an invaluable service by exposing the coercive dimensions of practices that presented themselves as purely therapeutic. The non-restraint system did indeed replace one form of control with others, and the humanitarian rhetoric that accompanied it served, in part, as legitimation for the continued expansion of asylum power. These are genuine insights that the Victorian hagiography — in which Conolly figured as an uncomplicated hero — could not generate.

But the Foucauldian framework, when applied as a totalizing lens, produces its own form of presentist reduction. It transforms situated actors making constrained choices into mere instantiations of an abstract power structure. The specificity of Conolly's context — the actual alternatives he faced, the actual suffering of chained and manacled patients, the actual constraints of early Victorian institutional possibilities — dissolves into a narrative in which every reform is always already a refinement of domination.

A more adequate reading would acknowledge that both the improvement and the control were real, and that they were inseparable. Conolly's patients at Hanwell were indisputably better off without chains than with them — a fact that should not be obscured by the subsequent recognition that they remained subject to other forms of coercion. At the same time, the humanitarian justification of non-restraint did serve to legitimize an institutional system whose net effects, over the longue durée, proved deeply problematic. Both truths must be held together.

The deeper lesson concerns not Conolly but ourselves. Historicism, properly understood, is not an apology for the past. It is not the claim that past psychiatrists should be excused because they were "people of their time." Rather, it asks us to reconstruct the problem-space in which they acted: the suffering they confronted, the institutional alternatives available to them, the limits of medical knowledge, and the moral imagination of their age. This reconstruction does not produce acquittal; it produces understanding of a kind that presentist condemnation cannot yield — an understanding of how harm can emerge not only from cruelty or indifference, but also from benevolence, scientific confidence, institutional necessity, and therapeutic desperation.

Such a perspective may be more ethically demanding than condemnation, because it reminds us that we too act within a specific problem-space, under conditions of ignorance whose contours will be visible only to those who come after. Future generations may judge our pharmacological maintenance, our involuntary admissions, our risk-assessment algorithms, and our neurotechnological interventions with a severity comparable to that with which we now regard the straitjacket and the coercion chair. If Conolly's case teaches anything, it is that the question is not whether our practices contain coercive elements we cannot yet see — they almost certainly do — but whether we possess the historical humility to recognize this possibility in advance.
