Empiricist history and idealist history represent two fundamentally different approaches to understanding the past, focusing respectively on external facts and internal, mental processes. Empiricism argues that history is built on observable evidence and sensory experience, while idealism posits that history is fundamentally the study of past thoughts and ideas. [[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism), [2](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1541-0064.1959.tb01825.x), [3](https://www.biblicaltraining.org/learn/foundations/th310-essentials-of-worldview-analysis/th310-01-philosophy-of-history-part-1), [4](https://www.slideshare.net/slideshow/empericism-vs-idealism-positivism/106676972), [5](https://philosophy.institute/research-methodology/berkeley-empirical-insight-existence-perception/)]

Empiricist History (Positivist Approach)

Empiricist history often aligns with positivism, focusing on "facts" and "evidence" gathered from primary sources. It holds that the past can be known by collecting evidence and constructing a narrative based on empirical data rather than interpretations or preconceived ideas. [[1](https://files.commons.gc.cuny.edu/wp-content/blogs.dir/1358/files/2019/02/History-and-Idealism-2-PUBLIC.pdf), [2](https://methods.sagepub.com/hnbk/edvol/sage-handbook-of-historical-theory/chpt/2-implications-empiricism-history), [3](https://thenewdigest.substack.com/p/two-versions-of-history-and-tradition), [4](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1541-0064.1959.tb01825.x), [5](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/370822544_What_is_Empiricism_Critically_Evaluate_its_Value_When_Writing_History)]

- **Focus:** External, tangible events, "facts," and causality.
- **Methodology:** Inductive reasoning—moving from specific, observed events to general conclusions, similar to the natural sciences.
- **Key Tenet:** The historian's job is to "let the facts speak for themselves" by documenting what happened using primary sources.
- **Critique:** Critics argue that "facts" are rarely final or entirely neutral, as the selection and arrangement of evidence involve subjective choices. [[1](https://jnnielsen.medium.com/oakeshotts-idealist-conception-of-historical-experience-204374c0f7de), [2](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/370822544_What_is_Empiricism_Critically_Evaluate_its_Value_When_Writing_History), [3](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/hith.70019), [4](https://researchportal.murdoch.edu.au/esploro/fulltext/journalArticle/Oral-history-critical-theory-and-politics/991005542386307891?repId=12135789940007891&mId=13136899250007891&institution=61MUN_INST), [5](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1541-0064.1959.tb01825.x)]

Idealist History

Idealist history, most famously associated with philosophers like R.G. Collingwood, argues that history is not just about recording external events, but understanding the thoughts and intentions behind them. [[1](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1541-0064.1959.tb01825.x)]

- **Focus:** The internal thought processes of historical actors; "all history is the history of thought".
- **Methodology:** Re-enactment or re-thinking (re-constructing the thoughts of historical agents in the historian's own mind).
- **Key Tenet:** Historical events are unique and cannot be studied simply as instances of a general law, as empirical, scientific methods might suggest.
- **Critique:** Idealism can lead to a form of subjective interpretation, where the past is only what the historian imagines it to be, potentially ignoring the material, physical constraints of the time. [[1](https://www.academia.edu/79359508/_All_history_is_the_history_of_thought_competing_British_idealist_historiographies), [2](https://www.ceeol.com/search/article-detail?id=220556), [3](https://jnnielsen.medium.com/oakeshotts-idealist-conception-of-historical-experience-204374c0f7de), [4](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1541-0064.1959.tb01825.x), [5](https://files.commons.gc.cuny.edu/wp-content/blogs.dir/1358/files/2019/02/History-and-Idealism-2-PUBLIC.pdf)]

Modern Synthesis

Most modern historians, rather than adhering strictly to one, often employ a blended methodology. They use rigorous empirical evidence to understand the context, while using idealist methods to interpret the motives and internal lives of historical actors, often adopting a "conceptual idealism" that rejects the idea that history can be reduced to pure "facts".
